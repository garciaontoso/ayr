### I.2 Cyclicality concerns — the core framing

The Industrials sector is the most economically-sensitive sector in the S&P 500 after Financials and Materials. It is also, paradoxically, where some of the longest-lived dividend aristocrats live: Dover, Emerson, Parker Hannifin, Illinois Tool Works, and Stanley Black & Decker have all paid rising dividends for 50+ years — thriving through the 1974-75, 1980-82, 1990-91, 2001-02, 2008-09, and 2020 recessions.

The reason this works is that **high-quality industrials are not commodity businesses — they are mission-critical component suppliers embedded in long customer lifecycles, and the dividend is paid out of structurally high free cash flow rather than out of volatile earnings.** Dover ships pumps to oil & gas, refrigeration to supermarkets, and fluid components to pharma — all three verticals have smoothing characteristics that insulate the dividend from any single end-market downturn.

The user's current Industrials holdings do *not* fit this template:

- **RHI** is pure cyclical — staffing revenue collapses in recession. 2025 revenue is already $5.38B vs $7.24B in 2022, a 26% peak-to-trough drawdown at the top-line level, and EPS has fallen from $6.08 in FY22 to $1.33 in FY25 (down 78%). The "9.33% yield" is actually a warning sign that the market is pricing in a dividend cut — a dividend that has been paid out of earnings of $1.33 while the payout per share is $2.36 (payout ratio 177%).

- **LW** is food-industrial cyclical with a distinct "capacity cycle" specific to the French-fry industry. Lamb Weston has gone from a rev peak of $6.47B with $5.01 EPS in FY24 to $6.45B / $2.51 EPS in FY25 — revenue flat, earnings cut in half. This is a margin story driven by fry-demand softness + restaurant traffic weakness + input-cost whipsaw + aggressive new-capacity commissioning. The 3.7% yield is reasonable but the fundamentals have clearly rolled over.

- **AMCR** is lower-grade cyclical (consumer packaging is semi-defensive, but with input-cost-pass-through lag and recent Berry Global M&A integration stress). EPS has dropped from $3.55 FY23 to $1.61 FY25. The 6.6% yield reflects market skepticism.

- **AHRT** is the biggest problem — it's a levered transport/real-estate vehicle with a quality score of 8 and a safety score of 1. I'll treat it separately because its economics are unique.

- **ITRK** is the only "dividend aristocrat-style" holding in the sleeve, and even then its DGR has been inconsistent post-COVID.

- **PATH** doesn't pay a dividend.

**So the structural issue is that the user has cyclical exposure without structural compounders to balance it.** When the industrial cycle next rolls over — and it will, because that is the nature of the sector — the current sleeve will have to carry that cyclicality without the stabilizing influence of a rail, a defense contractor, or a high-quality industrial distributor.

## Part II — Sub-sector analysis

Each of the eight sub-sectors below is analyzed through four lenses: (1) structural economics and why the sub-sector exists, (2) the top 3-5 names and how they differ, (3) the dividend-quality ranking within the group, and (4) specific entry points or watch items.

### II.1 Defense / Aerospace — LMT, RTX, GD, NOC, BA, TXT

**Structural economics.** Defense primes are the most customer-concentrated businesses in the listed universe — each company derives 60-85% of revenue from the US Department of Defense and its allied equivalents (UK MoD, Japan MoD, Saudi Arabia, Gulf states). In return for accepting this concentration, they get 5-10 year backlogs with guaranteed escalators, cost-plus or fixed-price-incentive contract structures that protect gross margins, and the highest regulatory barriers to entry in the listed economy. The Pentagon does not suddenly go bankrupt, and the six primes (Lockheed Martin, RTX, General Dynamics, Northrop Grumman, Boeing Defense, L3Harris) will still be supplying US and allied forces in 2040 — because building an F-35 replacement requires decades of accumulated tooling, software, and cleared engineering personnel that no new entrant can realistically assemble.

Since the Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022, defense spending has entered a renewed global up-cycle. The NATO 2% GDP target that was aspirational in 2019 has now been adopted by Poland (3.2% actual), Germany (2.4%), UK (2.5%), France (2.1%), and most of the Baltics/Nordics (3%+). Japan has committed to a doubling of defense spending to 2% of GDP by 2027, breaking seven decades of pacifist constraint. Middle East defense budgets have exploded post-Gaza and post-Ukraine, with Saudi Arabia, UAE, Israel, and Turkey all dramatically accelerating procurement. The US itself has moved to a 3.2% baseline with supplemental spending on Ukraine ($75B cumulative), Israel, and the Indo-Pacific. **Total 5-year US + NATO defense outlay is estimated to be $2.3 trillion higher than the pre-Ukraine baseline** — most of which flows to the six primes as long-cycle procurement revenue.

**The top six names.**

**Lockheed Martin (LMT)** is the largest by revenue ($75.1B FY25), the dominant fighter-jet manufacturer (F-35 program is $2 trillion lifetime value, of which ~40% is sustainment/upgrade that runs through 2070), and the purest-play defense prime. 5-year dividend growth of ~7% per annum, current yield 2.8%, payout ratio in the 40s — extremely safe. The one concern is F-35 production-rate risk: the program is transitioning from production-phase (higher-margin) to sustainment-phase (lower-margin), and block upgrades (TR-3, Block 4) have been delayed. But LMT's backlog is $170B and growing, and the company has been aggressively repurchasing shares at ~2-3% per year. Total cash return ~5-6%. **This is the defense blue-chip**; if the user buys only one defense name, LMT is the least-complicated choice.

**RTX Corporation (RTX)** is the second-largest at $88.6B revenue, but it is actually three businesses bolted together: Collins Aerospace (commercial aerospace components + defense electronics), Pratt & Whitney (engines, both commercial GTF and military F135 for LMT's F-35), and Raytheon (missiles, radars, integrated air defense). RTX offers more commercial-aerospace exposure than any other prime (roughly 50% commercial / 50% defense). The company suffered a major GTF-engine recall in 2023-24 (powdered-metal contamination) that cost $3B in cash charges and several quarters of earnings drag; this has now largely cleaned through and the underlying earnings power is re-emerging. Yield 2.1%, DGR ~7%. **RTX is attractive if you want commercial-aerospace beta (Airbus A320neo volumes) plus defense**; less pure-play than LMT.

**General Dynamics (GD)** is $52.5B revenue, split across Gulfstream (business jets — commercial aerospace) and the defense businesses (submarines, combat vehicles, IT services). GD is the sole builder of US Navy Virginia-class and Columbia-class submarines — a 30-year production program worth $130B that has effectively no competitor (HII does Virginia-class alongside GD). The submarine backlog is the closest thing to a perpetual annuity in listed industrials. Yield 2.0%, DGR 8%, 32 consecutive years of dividend increases — a Dividend Aristocrat. **GD is the best "dividend-aristocrat" defense prime**.

**Northrop Grumman (NOC)** is $42B revenue, heavily weighted to space (satellites, rockets via the ULA JV), classified programs (SR-72 successor, multiple black programs), and the B-21 Raider strategic bomber which is in production. NOC is the most exposed to budget politics because its programs are cutting-edge and some are fixed-price contracts that have run over (B-21 had ~$1.2B of fixed-price losses over 2022-2024). Yield 1.8%, DGR 8%, strong buyback. **NOC has the highest long-run growth potential but more program-execution risk**.

**Boeing (BA)** is the elephant — $78B revenue, $500+ billion in backlog, duopoly position with Airbus in commercial aerospace — but BA has cut its dividend entirely (suspended March 2020 at the start of COVID, not reinstated) and remains the single worst capital-allocation story among the primes, with accumulated free cash flow of negative $28B over 2019-2024 driven by 737 MAX certification crises (Alaska door-plug incident Jan 2024 compounded the prior crash-related issues), 787 delivery pauses, and labor strikes. BA *will* reinstate the dividend at some point and is a credible turnaround candidate, but for a dividend investor BA is in the "watch, don't own" bucket.

**Textron (TXT)** is $14B revenue — Bell helicopters (including the V-280 Valor program that beat Sikorsky for the Army FLRAA contract), Cessna business jets, and some industrial businesses. Smaller cap ($13B market cap), more cyclical exposure to the biz-jet cycle, modest yield (0.2%) — **not a natural dividend name**.

**L3Harris (LHX, not in the list but worth noting)** — $21B revenue, focused on communications, electronic warfare, and space. 2.3% yield, 14 consecutive years of increases. A credible fifth prime candidate.

**Entry consideration.** Defense stocks had a very strong 2022-2023 run following Ukraine, then traded sideways in 2024, and in 2025-early 2026 they are trading at roughly 17-19x forward earnings, which is moderately above the 10-year average of 15x but well below their COVID-crisis spike of 22x. The backlog-to-revenue ratio across the primes is at all-time highs (~3x for LMT, 2.6x for GD, 2.4x for RTX), which provides unusual visibility. **The entry dispersion is not especially attractive but it is defensible** — if you believe (as I do) that Western defense budgets are in a secular up-cycle that will persist until at least the late 2020s, the current 17-18x multiple will look cheap in hindsight.

**What would make me more aggressive**: a 10-15% pullback on any of LMT/GD/RTX without a fundamental deterioration in backlog.

### II.2 Machinery / Capital Goods — CAT, DE, CMI, PCAR, OSK

**Structural economics.** Machinery / capital-goods is the classic "late-cycle" sub-sector. The major names sell durable equipment with 10-30 year useful lives into cyclical end-markets: construction, mining, agriculture, trucking, and now increasingly data-center power. The end customers are sensitive to interest rates (the typical CAT dozer is financed over 5 years; AG equipment runs on 7-year notes), commodity prices (mining demand for CAT 793F haul trucks tracks iron ore and copper prices), and commercial construction activity (infrastructure bills, industrial capex). As a result, earnings swing dramatically through the cycle: Caterpillar's peak-to-trough EPS swing between 2012 (peak) and 2016 (trough) was -70%; during COVID it was -25%; during the 2008-09 crisis it was -60%.

The dividend story, however, is remarkably resilient. **CAT has raised its dividend for 31 consecutive years — Aristocrat status. Deere has raised for 32 years. Cummins has raised for 19. Paccar has paid an uninterrupted dividend for 85+ years (never cut), though the DGR track record is lumpier.** This is because the major machinery firms carry strong balance sheets through the cycle and use the low-cycle years to buy back shares and repair the books.

**The top five names.**

**Caterpillar (CAT)** is $67.6B revenue, $12.59B EBIT, the dominant global construction/mining equipment firm. CAT's 2025 tailwinds are: (1) US Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act money still flowing into road/bridge projects; (2) global mining capex up-cycle driven by copper/lithium electrification demand; (3) data-center power generation (CAT diesel gensets are the primary backup for hyperscaler data centers, with demand up 35% YoY in 2024-25). Yield 1.6%, DGR 7%, 31 years of increases. Payout ratio 26% — extremely safe. **CAT is the blue-chip machinery name.**

**Deere & Company (DE)** is $44.7B revenue, focused on agriculture (combines, tractors, planting equipment) and smaller construction. DE's cycle is driven by farm-cash receipts, which are driven by crop prices. 2023 was a peak year (corn at $7+/bushel); by 2025 crop prices had rolled over (corn at $4.50) and DE's revenue fell from $61B peak to $44.7B — a 27% decline. EPS fell from $34 peak to $18.56 in FY25. But the dividend continued to rise; DE has 32 consecutive years of increases. Yield 1.3%, DGR 10%. Payout ratio 25%. **DE is a cyclical but the dividend is untouchable.**

**Cummins (CMI)** is $33.7B revenue, a diesel-engine and power-generation specialist. Cummins is a bigger beneficiary of the data-center power theme than even CAT because natural gas generator sets for utility-scale power-plants are a Cummins specialty; the company's Power Systems segment had 20%+ revenue growth in 2024-25 on hyperscaler demand. Yield 2.7%, DGR 7%, 19 years of increases. Payout ratio 35%. **CMI is the best "yield + data-center power" play among machinery.**

**Paccar (PCAR)** is $28.4B revenue, the North American heavy-truck manufacturer (Kenworth, Peterbilt, DAF). Trucks are at the leading edge of the industrial cycle — when the freight cycle softens, PCAR's Class 8 truck orders drop first. 2022-23 was peak freight; 2024-25 has been a freight recession with truck orders down 25%+. PCAR pays both a regular dividend (1.3% yield) AND an annual variable dividend (historically ~$3-4/share when cycle is good). Total yield 3-4% blended. 85 years of uninterrupted dividends. **PCAR is a cyclical but with long payment history.**

**Oshkosh (OSK)** is $10.6B revenue, fire trucks + military tactical vehicles + airport ground-support. Smaller, less-followed. 1.2% yield. Not a natural dividend compounder.

### II.3 Transportation / Rail — UNP, CSX, NSC, CP

**Structural economics.** North American Class 1 rail is a textbook oligopoly — four US railroads (Union Pacific, CSX, Norfolk Southern, BNSF which is private / Berkshire-owned), and two Canadian (Canadian Pacific-KC, Canadian National). The industry has been consolidating for 40 years, the last major deal being CP's $31B acquisition of KCS in 2023 to form CPKC — creating the first true US-Canada-Mexico integrated railroad. The six rail networks have **zero overlap in the major regions**: UNP dominates the western two-thirds of the US, CSX the eastern seaboard, NSC the southeast + mid-Atlantic, BNSF the central + western, CN the north, and CPKC the western-spine-plus-Mexico.

Rail operating margins are among the highest of any transportation mode: 35-45% operating margins at UNP and CP, 40%+ at CSX, 33% at NSC, with ROICs of 12-18%. The key financial metric is the **operating ratio** (operating expenses / revenue) — lower is better. The rail industry transformed itself between 2010-2019 by adopting "Precision Scheduled Railroading" (PSR), a cost-cutting discipline that pushed operating ratios from ~75% in 2010 to ~60% in 2019. Post-COVID, operating ratios ticked up to the mid-60s due to service issues and labor costs, but have been improving.

Rail is also the **single-most secularly-advantaged freight mode for carbon-aware shipping**: a ton-mile of rail emits ~75% less CO2 than a ton-mile of long-haul trucking, and is 3-5x more fuel-efficient. As the North American economy decarbonizes and as fuel prices stay elevated, rail's intermodal share of freight should grow from ~18% to ~22% over the next decade.

**The top four names.**

**Union Pacific (UNP)** is $24.5B revenue, the largest US-only rail, with 32,000 route-miles spanning from the West Coast to the Mississippi. UNP has the best operating ratio in US rails (60.3% in 2024), the strongest management discipline, and the cleanest balance sheet. Yield 2.3%, DGR 9%, 17 years of increases, payout ratio ~45%. **UNP is the best-in-class US rail.**

**CSX Corporation (CSX)** is $14.1B revenue, eastern US. Strong intermodal franchise, good exposure to East Coast ports and Appalachian coal declines (coal is a secular decline, but CSX has been diversifying into chemicals, autos, intermodal). Yield 1.5%, DGR 8%. Smaller and more mid-cap-like.

**Norfolk Southern (NSC)** is ~$12.6B revenue, southeast + mid-Atlantic. NSC had the East Palestine derailment in February 2023, which cost the company ~$2B in settlements + remediation and triggered a management change (Alan Shaw replaced Jim Squires). NSC has been rebuilding its service metrics and safety record; the stock has underperformed CSX and UNP by ~15% since the incident. Yield 2.3%, DGR 7%. **NSC is the cheapest rail** but has the management-transition risk.

**Canadian Pacific Kansas City (CP)** is $10.4B revenue (but pro-forma with KCS integration closer to $13B). CP is the only integrated US-Canada-Mexico railroad post the 2023 KCS merger; it is positioned to benefit from the North American reshoring / nearshoring theme, moving auto parts, grain, and industrial goods between the three countries. Yield 0.7% (low because the company pays most of its cash returns through buybacks). DGR 7%. **CP is the highest-growth rail; UNP is the highest-quality.**

Owning a rail is effectively owning a **toll road on the North American economy** — a natural pairing with an industrial dividend portfolio.


### II.4 Logistics — UPS, FDX, XPO, CHRW, EXPD

**Structural economics.** The logistics / parcel-delivery sub-sector sits between the rails (ultra-heavy assets, monopoly-like economics) and the software-enabled 3PL (asset-light, broker-style) extremes. UPS and FedEx are the two US parcel duopolists, both of which have enormous fixed-asset footprints (aircraft, trucks, sorting centers) and have extracted years of cyclical pricing power from the e-commerce wave. XPO is a less-than-truckload (LTL) specialist; CH Robinson and Expeditors are asset-light brokerage/forwarding firms that earn lower gross margins but with very high returns on invested capital.

The last three years have been brutal for parcel:
- E-commerce volumes normalized post-COVID; UPS and FDX had over-built capacity in 2020-21 that became expensive.
- Amazon's in-house logistics network (Amazon Logistics delivered ~6 billion packages in 2024) has taken ~15% market share from UPS and USPS.
- Teamsters contract negotiation in 2023 gave UPS drivers significant wage increases, compressing UPS margins.
- FedEx has been restructuring (Ground + Express consolidation under "One FedEx") — execution has been uneven.

As a result, **UPS and FDX are both trading at multi-year lows in terms of P/E multiples** (UPS at ~13x forward, FDX at ~12x) and at historically high dividend yields (UPS ~5.1%, FDX ~2.5%).

**The top five names.**

**UPS** is $88.6B revenue, the world's largest parcel-delivery company by revenue. UPS had a very tough 2024 with volumes down 4% and operating margin compressed to 6% (historical mid-cycle is 12%). But the company has been aggressively restructuring (Network of the Future automation, facility closures, headcount reduction from 540k to 490k through mid-2025), and the Teamsters contract through 2028 provides labor cost visibility. Yield 5.1%, DGR 7%, 15 years of increases, payout ratio 75-80% (elevated). **UPS is a cyclical turnaround story with a contested dividend — the 5.1% yield is the market's way of saying "prove the dividend is safe."**

**FedEx (FDX)** is $87.9B revenue, the other half of the duopoly. FedEx is in the middle of the "One FedEx" consolidation (merging the Express and Ground operating subsidiaries) which should unlock $3-4B of cost savings but has execution risk. Yield 2.5%, DGR 9%, 4 years of increases (the dividend was slower to start, now growing fast). **FDX has more operational upside than UPS but the dividend yield is less of a draw.**

**XPO** is $8.2B revenue, LTL specialist post the 2022 spinoff of GXO (contract logistics) and the 2023 divestiture of the intermodal business. XPO is a focused LTL pure-play now, led by the activist-backed Jacobs family. Operating ratio has dropped from 90% in 2020 to 82% in 2024. No dividend — this is a capital-appreciation story.

**CH Robinson (CHRW)** is $17B revenue, the largest US freight broker. CHRW was disrupted by digital freight brokers (Uber Freight, Convoy) during 2019-2022, responded with its own digital platform, and has been recovering. Yield 2.7%, DGR 3%. **A decent but not spectacular income name**.

**Expeditors International (EXPD)** is $9.3B revenue, asset-light international freight forwarding. Extremely high ROIC (~25%), modest growth, yield 1.4%, DGR 5%. **Boring-good compounder for patient capital.**

### II.5 Staffing — RHI, MAN, ADP (and peers)

**Structural economics.** Staffing is pure cycle. The business consists of finding workers and placing them in client roles, either temporary (highest margin, most cyclical) or permanent (lower margin, less cyclical). Revenue peaks when the economy is growing fastest and employers are desperate for labor; it troughs during recession when companies freeze hiring. Gross margins are in the 30-35% range but SG&A consumes most of it, leaving operating margins in single digits (5-12% depending on cycle position).

RHI (Robert Half), the user's only staffing holding, is the dominant mid-market professional-services staffing firm. Its client base skews to small and mid-sized businesses needing finance/accounting temps and consultants (through its Protiviti subsidiary). RHI is more defensible than a pure-play temp firm because Protiviti (60% of operating profit historically) is closer to consulting than to staffing.

**Looking at RHI's historical cycle:**

| FY   | Revenue | EPS | Dividend |
|------|---------|-----|----------|
| 2022 | $7.24B  | $6.08 | $1.68 |
| 2023 | $6.39B  | $3.90 | $1.92 |
| 2024 | $5.80B  | $2.45 | $2.12 |
| 2025 | $5.38B  | $1.33 | $2.36 |

This is a classic cycle pattern: revenue has declined 26% peak-to-trough over three years, EPS has collapsed 78% from $6.08 to $1.33, **and the dividend has been increased every year anyway** — from $1.68 to $2.36. In FY25, **the payout ratio is now 177% of GAAP EPS and approximately 100% of free cash flow**. The company is financing the dividend partly from cash reserves.

This is the key question for RHI: **is this cycle approaching its bottom (in which case the dividend is defensible through one or two more quarters, then recovers) or is there more downside to come?**

Leading indicators suggest we are close to the bottom:
- US job openings have stopped falling; JOLTS data shows 7.4M openings vs 7.0M at trough.
- Temp staffing employment index has stabilized in early 2026.
- RHI's management has talked about "green shoots" in demand for finance/accounting professionals.

But the recovery could be slower than prior cycles because AI is structurally changing demand for certain white-collar tasks (junior bookkeeping, basic audit work, routine consulting). **There is a real possibility that RHI's "new normal" revenue is 10-15% below its 2022 peak, even once the economy recovers.** This would pressure the dividend growth rate or trigger a pause.

### II.8 Industrial Distribution — FAST, GWW, WCC, MSM

**Structural economics.** Industrial distribution is possibly the most under-appreciated sub-sector in industrials. These businesses aggregate thousands of SKUs of maintenance-repair-operations (MRO) supplies (fasteners, pipe fittings, safety equipment, tools, cutting bits, adhesives) and sell them to factories, construction sites, and contractors. The key economic characteristics are:

- **Fragmented supply base**: thousands of component manufacturers globally; distributors aggregate and simplify.
- **Extremely sticky customer relationships**: a Fastenal or Grainger vending machine installed on a factory floor generates recurring MRO revenue for 10-20 years.
- **High-touch service**: catalog + e-commerce + field sales + on-site inventory management; incumbents are hard to displace.
- **Countercyclical resilience**: when capex slows, MRO spend continues (factories still need bolts); operating margins compress but don't collapse.

The result is a sub-sector with exceptional long-run ROICs (20-35% at FAST and GWW) and dividend consistency.

**The top four names.**

**Fastenal (FAST)** is $8.2B revenue, specialty MRO distribution with a massive footprint of in-plant stores, vending machines, and on-site inventory solutions. Fastenal's "growth driver" model is installing vending machines that dispense MRO supplies inside customer factories — each machine generates recurring revenue with very high margins. Yield 2.4%, DGR 10%, 26 years of increases, payout ratio 73%. **A dividend compounder with a unique business model.**

**W.W. Grainger (GWW)** is $17.9B revenue, the largest US MRO distributor. Mass-market catalog + e-commerce. Grainger is to Fastenal as Home Depot is to Ace Hardware — broader, larger, more diversified. Yield 0.8%, DGR 7%, 53 years of consecutive increases — **Dividend King**. Payout ratio 18%. Operating margin 15%. ROIC 30%+. **A Dividend King with exceptional economics.**

**WESCO International (WCC)** is $21.7B revenue, electrical + industrial distribution. Larger than GWW in revenue terms but lower-margin (operating margin ~6%). Yield 1.1%, DGR ~10%. Moderate quality.

**MSC Industrial (MSM)** is $3.8B revenue, metalworking-focused MRO. Yield 3.9%, DGR 5%. Higher-yield smaller-cap option.

| Rank | Name | Yield | DGR 5Y | Years | Best-for |
|------|------|-------|--------|-------|---------|
| 1    | GWW  | 0.8%  | 7%     | 53 (King) | Highest-quality, lowest yield |

## Part IV — Recommendations on Current Holdings and Gaps

### IV.1 Gap: No Defense exposure

The user has zero defense-prime exposure at a time when:

## Part V — Macro and Structural Themes

The Industrials sector is more macro-driven than any other sector except Financials. This section walks through four structural themes that should shape Industrials positioning over the next 5-10 years, and how each theme maps to specific names.

### V.1 Reshoring / Nearshoring — a 10-year tailwind

The COVID-era supply-chain breakdowns, combined with US-China trade tensions that accelerated during the Trump and Biden administrations (now continuing under the current administration), have produced a structural re-allocation of global manufacturing:

- **US domestic manufacturing** has received a massive boost from three major legislative packages: the CHIPS and Science Act ($52B for semiconductor fab incentives), the Inflation Reduction Act (~$370B of clean-energy-related credits, much of which requires US-manufactured components), and the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act ($1.2T over 10 years for roads, bridges, ports, rail, broadband, clean water). US private manufacturing construction spending has roughly tripled from its 2020 level, hitting an annualized $240B in 2025.

- **Mexico** has become the single largest beneficiary of nearshoring, attracting over $35B of announced FDI in 2024 alone, concentrated in automotive, electronics, and aerospace parts. CPKC's cross-border freight is up 15% YoY.

- **India, Vietnam, and Poland** have emerged as the secondary "China+1" manufacturing destinations. Apple's India iPhone production has gone from ~3% in 2020 to 15% of total output in 2025. Intel, TSMC, and Samsung have all announced large fabs outside China.

- **China itself** is not collapsing — its share of global manufacturing remains near 30% — but the *growth rate* of China manufacturing has dropped from 8% per annum in the 2010s to 3-4% in the 2020s, with the incremental global manufacturing growth flowing elsewhere.

**Industrial winners from this theme:**

- **CAT and DE** — every new factory, warehouse, and infrastructure project needs construction equipment. CAT's Industrial division grew 15% in 2024 on US private construction alone.
- **CMI** — natural gas power generation sets for new manufacturing sites + data centers.
- **FAST, GWW** — MRO supplies for new and existing manufacturing floors.
- **CPKC (CP)** — cross-border rail freight between US, Canada, and Mexico.
- **ITRK** — factory audits and quality certification for new supply-chain locations.
- **Defense primes** (via the "de-risking" rationale) — Western governments stockpiling critical defense supplies, keeping production domestic.

**Losers from reshoring:**

- Chinese-heavy suppliers (but the user's portfolio doesn't have these).
- Pure Asian trade flows — EXPD (freight forwarding) has had revenue pressure as trans-Pacific volumes shifted.

**Portfolio implication:** Adding CAT, DE, or CMI is a direct expression of the reshoring theme. The user currently has ZERO exposure to this theme through Industrials (although there is some indirect exposure through packaging AMCR and food-industrial LW). Adding one of CAT/DE/CMI at 1% is the simplest way to play reshoring.

### V.2 The Defense Spending Super-Cycle

As discussed in II.1, defense spending has entered a structural up-cycle that shows no sign of reversing through at least 2030. The key drivers:

- **Ukraine-Russia conflict** continues; even if a ceasefire emerges, the rebuilding of Ukrainian defenses and the rearming of NATO eastern members is a multi-decade project. Poland alone is spending 3.2% of GDP on defense, buying hundreds of K2 tanks, F-35s, HIMARS, Abrams — a modernization of a scale not seen since German reunification.
- **Indo-Pacific security posture** — Japan 2% of GDP by 2027, Australia's AUKUS submarine program ($368B over 30 years, buying Virginia-class subs from GD), Philippines base agreements.
- **Middle East rearmament** — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Israel, and Turkey all dramatically increasing defense spending.
- **US modernization cycle** — B-21 Raider bomber, Columbia-class SSBN submarines, next-generation air dominance (NGAD), hypersonic weapons, counter-drone systems. Many of these programs are entering production.
- **Arms race in unmanned systems** — loitering munitions, AI-enabled drones, counter-drone. Anduril Industries is IPO-curious; Palantir has defense exposure; the listed primes are all racing to acquire or develop these capabilities.

**Historical context:** The two prior defense super-cycles of modern memory — the Reagan buildup (1980-1989) and the post-9/11 buildup (2001-2011) — each produced 8-12 years of outperformance for defense primes. The current cycle started in 2022 with Ukraine and is ~4 years in; if history is any guide, we are closer to mid-cycle than late-cycle.

**Risk scenarios that would end the cycle:**

- A dramatic Ukraine ceasefire that leads to a "peace dividend" re-rating (historically 15-20% drawdowns for 6-12 months, then recovery if fundamentals hold).
- A US fiscal crisis forcing meaningful defense cuts (unlikely before 2030 given current fiscal trajectory).
- A cease-fire in multiple theaters simultaneously (Ukraine + Middle East + Indo-Pacific de-escalation) — possible but not base case.

**Portfolio implication:** **LMT at 1.5% and GD at 1.0% would give the portfolio a total of 2.5% defense exposure**, which is approximately market-weight within the 10% Industrials allocation I'm recommending. This is not an aggressive bet; it is simply bringing the portfolio up to neutral.

### V.3 Automation & Robotics — the third leg of industrial transformation

The automation / robotics theme encompasses several distinct technologies:

- **Fixed-industrial robots** (Fanuc, ABB, Yaskawa, KUKA) — mature market, growing 5-7% annually.
- **Collaborative robots (cobots)** — smaller, safer robots working alongside humans. Rapidly growing (20%+ per annum).
- **Autonomous mobile robots (AMRs)** — for warehouse and factory logistics. Market growing 25%+ per annum.
- **Machine vision** (Cognex, Keyence) — quality inspection, pick-and-place guidance.
- **Robotic Process Automation (RPA)** — software bots for back-office workflows. **This is PATH's market.**
- **Generative AI + industrial control** — new wave (2023+) bringing LLMs into industrial workflows.

**The listed automation names:**

**PATH in the automation context.** PATH is narrowly the "RPA software" sub-category of industrial automation. The RPA market has faced a competitive double-whammy: (1) GenAI is eating some of the simpler RPA use cases because LLMs can write one-off workflows on the fly, and (2) Microsoft Power Automate bundles competing capabilities into M365 subscriptions, commoditizing the category. **Portfolio implication for automation:** Consider adding **EMR at 1%** if the user wants a dividend-paying expression of the automation theme. EMR has 67 consecutive years of dividend increases (fourth-longest streak in the US) and has pivoted its portfolio toward industrial software + automation through the Aspen Technology acquisition in 2023.

### V.4 Data-Center Power Infrastructure — emerging, 2024+

The AI-driven data-center build-out represents the fastest-growing industrial-related theme of the 2020s. Hyperscaler capex (Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon, Oracle) is expected to exceed $350B in 2026 — nearly double the 2022 level. Each new hyperscale data center requires:

- ~50-200 MW of electricity, delivered through medium- and high-voltage gear.
- Backup diesel generators (100-200 gensets per site), plus increasingly natural gas reciprocating engines for primary power.
- Liquid cooling systems (data-center OEMs + copper/aluminum heat exchangers).
- Electrical switchgear, transformers, uninterruptible power supplies.
- Miles of copper cabling, fiber optics, containment systems.

**The Industrials beneficiaries:**

- **Vertiv (VRT)** — data-center power & cooling infrastructure pure-play. High growth, low yield (0.1%), not a dividend name yet.
- **Eaton (ETN)** — electrical systems, benefits heavily from data-center demand. Yield 1.3%, DGR 10%, 14 years of increases.
- **CAT** — data-center gensets and engines. ~15% of 2024 revenue growth came from data-center power.
- **CMI** — natural gas generator sets for primary power. Power Systems segment growing 20%+.
- **PCAR** — heavy-duty trucks for site construction logistics.
- **FAST, GWW** — electrical and construction supplies to site builders.

**The utility beneficiaries** (not Industrials but worth noting for holistic sector positioning):

- Vistra (VST), Constellation Energy (CEG), NextEra (NEE), Southern Company (SO), Duke Energy (DUK) — all seeing data-center load commitments driving capex plans higher.

**Portfolio implication.** Adding **CMI at 1%** or **ETN at 1%** is the simplest pure-play on the data-center power theme within Industrials. CMI offers higher yield (2.7% vs ETN's 1.3%) but similar secular exposure. **My recommendation: CMI for dividend-heavy positioning, ETN for higher quality.**

### V.9 Risks to the Industrials thesis

Before concluding, three macro risks that would argue *against* aggressively adding Industrials exposure:

1. **Severe recession in 2026-2027.** The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow currently shows US GDP growth at 1.8%, below trend but not recessionary. But leading indicators (manufacturing ISM, yield curve, job openings) are softening. A real recession could pull Industrials earnings down 20-30% peak-to-trough, and cyclical names (RHI, CAT, DE) would underperform materially. **Mitigation:** the recommendation is to build into the Industrials allocation over 12-18 months rather than in a single lump sum, and to bias toward less-cyclical names (defense, distribution, rails) over pure cyclicals (staffing, machinery).

2. **Interest-rate spike.** Industrial capex is rate-sensitive; a Fed Funds rate above 5.5% sustained for 2+ years would depress machinery and equipment demand. **Mitigation:** current Fed Funds at 4.25%, inflation trending toward 2%; rate-spike risk is lower today than in 2022-2023.

3. **Trade war escalation.** A hot US-China trade conflict could disrupt supply chains across every sub-sector discussed. **Mitigation:** most of the names recommended (LMT, UNP, CAT, GWW, FAST) are predominantly US-domestic and would actually benefit from trade disruption.

The risk/reward of rebalancing Industrials exposure is asymmetric: the tail-case downside is a severe recession that compresses the added names by 15-20%; the tail-case upside is a 5-10 year secular tailwind from defense + reshoring + data-center power that delivers 12-15% annualized returns. **This asymmetry supports the rebalance.**
