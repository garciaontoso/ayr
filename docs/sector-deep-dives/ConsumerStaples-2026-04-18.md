### II.C — PACKAGED FOOD

This is the weakest sub-sector in US staples and the most over-represented in your portfolio. Six of your 11 staples names are in packaged food; none of them qualify as best-in-class. The structural issues are well-documented:

- **Organic volume is negative across almost every category.** Cereal, soup, frozen vegetables, frozen meals, yogurt, bread, cheese, processed meats, condiments — all showing multi-year volume declines in US and Europe. Only snacking, confectionery, pet food, and premium natural/organic show positive volume.
- **Private label share is structurally gaining.** Retailers (Walmart, Kroger, Aldi, Lidl, Costco) have upgraded their private-label offerings from "cheap alternative" to "premium value." In categories like frozen vegetables, canned soup, yogurt, and bread, private label now holds 25-40% unit share and is still gaining.
- **Pricing power has broken.** After three years of +5-10% annual price increases, 2025 is the year consumers cracked. Volume elasticity is now punishing further price increases. Net: companies cannot raise prices without losing volume, and cannot grow volume without cutting prices.
- **Tariff pressure** on imported ingredients and packaging is real and uneven. CPB's Q2 FY2026 gross margin compression of 230 bps is the canary.
- **GLP-1 drug impact** hits the most consumer-discretionary food categories (snacks, frozen meals, cereal) hardest. Not existential but an incremental drag of 1-3% on category volumes over the next 3-5 years.

### V.A — Inflation pass-through: who can and who cannot

The four-year inflation cycle (2021-2024) separated staples into two clear camps based on their ability to pass through costs without destroying volume:

**Winners (full pass-through, volume intact):**
- **KO** — concentrate pricing mechanism is incidence-based; pricing captures as inflation works through bottler system. 2025 price/mix +4%. Operating margin *expanded* during the inflation cycle (from ~27% to 28.7%).
- **PG** — premium-tier HPC brands. Gross margin bottomed at 47.4% in FY2022, fully recovered to 51.2% by FY2025 — a 12-18 month lag but complete recovery.
- **HRL** — premium protein brands (Spam, Applegate) passed through cost inflation with only modest volume elasticity.
- **DEO** — premium spirits are highly price-inelastic. Don Julio +42% US despite tequila price increases.
- **MO** — five price increases in 2025 on Marlboro; pricing captured 100% of the 10% volume decline.

**Middle (partial pass-through, some volume damage):**
- **PEP** — PFNA volumes down 2-2.5% for two years straight. Price/mix did the work but ceiling was reached.
- **KMB** — diapers and tissue have moderate elasticity; price/mix offset volume declines but margins compressed modestly.

**Losers (failed pass-through, volume destruction):**
- **KHC** — FY2025 9M volume -3.9%, pricing only +0.8%. Category-leading brand (Heinz) maintains some power; lower-tier brands (Kraft, Velveeta, Maxwell House) lost.

**Sector implication:** The inflation cycle has already been a selection event. Companies that passed through cleanly (KO, PG, HRL, MO, DEO) showed structural pricing power. Companies that failed (KHC, CAG, CPB, FLO) revealed the weakness was there all along. This data is now three years old and decisive. ## APPENDIX A — Methodology notes

1. **Dividend safety scoring** is author-assigned on a 1-10 scale, based on: (a) reported vs normalized FCF coverage, (b) payout ratio trajectory, (c) Net Debt/EBITDA vs historical range, (d) management's demonstrated willingness to prioritize dividend over other uses of capital, (e) category-level structural growth vs decline, (f) historical dividend behavior under stress (2020 COVID, 2022 inflation, 2019 trade wars).

2. **5-year DGR** is computed from annual DPS in the single-name reports; uses either calendar-year cash dividends paid or declared DPS depending on what the 10-K reports. Slight methodological differences across tickers (~10-30 bps).

3. **Net Debt/EBITDA** as reported in each single-name report; cross-verified to FMP where possible. Some figures are adjusted for one-time items (KO normalized for fairlife + IRS).

4. **Moat scoring** is qualitative, derived from the single-name report's assessment of pricing power, brand equity, distribution scale, and private-label vulnerability.

5. **Cross-sector comparisons** assume mid-April 2026 prices; small daily fluctuations do not change the verdict.

6. **Portfolio math** uses the positions table from the A&R worker API as of 2026-04-17 10:46 UTC. The cost basis for MO at -$69.69 reflects net of covered-call premiums received, which is a known accounting artifact in this user's book.

7. **Tier rankings** are relative within each sub-sector, not absolute scores. A Tier 2 beverage name (DEO) may have higher intrinsic quality than a Tier 1 packaged food name (HRL), but they compete in different categories.

## APPENDIX E — Deeper sub-sector commentary (expansion)

### Beverages — why the soft drink category is the best defensive franchise in equities

The most profitable business model in consumer staples is the concentrate-to-bottler franchise, and Coca-Cola perfected it. Unlike food companies that must bear the capital cost of manufacturing, logistics, and retail distribution, KO sells concentrate at 60%+ gross margin to bottlers who absorb 80%+ of the capital intensity. This asset-light model produces ROIC of 14-16% on a mature revenue base, free cash conversion above 90% of operating income, and — most importantly — the ability to capture pricing automatically through incidence-based contracts. When a Mexican bottler raises the retail price of Coca-Cola by 10% to offset peso depreciation, KO captures a proportional share of that pricing through the concentrate invoice, with no renegotiation required. This is why KO's operating margin expanded 800 bps over the 2016-2025 decade despite stagnant unit volumes.

Pepsi's business model is more integrated and therefore more capital-intensive. The bottling investments, the Frito-Lay manufacturing footprint, the Gatorade production infrastructure — all sit inside the P&L and drag operating margin to 14.4% vs KO's 28.7%. This is why PEP generates a 3.8% yield and KO generates a 2.7% yield at similar payout ratios: the market pays a premium for KO's capital structure. For a dividend investor, the PEP discount is partial compensation for the additional operational risk, not a mispricing.

Spirits (DEO) is a different animal. Premium aged spirits benefit from structural tailwinds (tequila +18% global, whiskey +3%, gin category growth) that sugared beverages do not have, and the pricing power is arguably stronger than KO's because a $50 bottle of Don Julio has pricing elasticity characteristics closer to luxury goods than to staple consumables. But DEO carries three disadvantages: (1) exposure to China and Asia Pacific weakness that KO largely sidesteps through its US focus, (2) M&A-driven goodwill burden ($20B+ on the books) that creates impairment risk on category shifts, and (3) a less cash-efficient distribution model that requires DEO to hold more inventory on its own balance sheet. DEO's 6.87% yield is compensation for these issues, and it is a fair compensation — but only after three years of thesis validation under the new permanent CEO.

Monster and KDP are growth-flavored staples that do not belong in a dividend-first portfolio. Monster pays no dividend at all; KDP pays a 2.5% yield with only 2 years of consecutive raises. Neither is a replacement for KO or PEP. Anheuser-Busch InBev (BUD) is structurally damaged: Bud Light boycott, European consumer weakness, balance sheet stretched from the SAB Miller deal. BUD's 1.7% yield is inadequate compensation and the dividend was effectively reset in 2020. Skip all three.

**The conclusion for the beverage sub-sector is simple:** KO and PEP are the only two investment-grade dividend holdings. KO is missing from the user's portfolio. PEP is adequately sized. DEO is a speculative stressed-value bet that happens to currently sit at 28% of the staples book — more than I would recommend for a new dollar, but the 18.6% unrealized loss argues against crystallizing exit here. The natural rebalancing action is to add KO on weakness (bringing DEO's relative weight down without forcing a sale at a loss) and let time heal the DEO overweight.

### Personal Care — where the moat is actually durable

HPC (Household & Personal Care) is the most underappreciated sub-sector in staples. The reason is that US investors lump it into the same bucket as packaged food and tobacco — "consumer staples" — and apply the same narrative. But the structural dynamics are opposite.

Consider the key categories: shampoo, soap, toothpaste, razors, diapers, laundry detergent, cleaning products. These share three features that food does not have:

**1. Performance-driven purchase decisions.** Consumers pick Tide because it removes stains better than private label, not because it is familiar. A bad detergent shows up in a ruined shirt. A bad shampoo shows up in unmanageable hair. This is why PG brands (Tide, Crest, Gillette, Pampers) have held share through two decades of private-label assault in ways that KHC brands (Oscar Mayer, Velveeta, Kraft Singles) have not.

**2. Replacement-cycle predictability.** A household buys approximately 150-200 units of PG products per year (counting individual items across all categories), and the purchase is behavioral rather than discretionary. You do not "skip" shampoo the way you might skip cereal. This makes HPC volumes the most stable sub-sector of consumer staples.

**3. Innovation-driven premium tiers.** PG's premium lines (Pampers Pure, Tide Pods, Gillette Labs) have been able to extract 20-40% price premiums over base versions with meaningful margin expansion. The equivalent does not exist in cereal or canned soup, where "premium" just means a bigger box.

These three factors are why PG's gross margin expanded from 49.6% to 51.2% over the decade despite cumulative commodity inflation of approximately 30% in resins, pulp, and energy. KMB's gross margin did not expand materially because KMB's brands are in the commoditized end of HPC (diapers, tissue) rather than the performance-differentiated end (laundry, grooming, oral care). This is the structural reason KMB trades at 12-15x forward P/E and PG trades at 22-25x — the moat is genuinely narrower.

**CL (Colgate-Palmolive)** sits between PG and KMB in terms of quality. Hill's Pet Nutrition is growing 5-7% organically and is the crown jewel. The core toothpaste business (Colgate, Sensodyne) has pricing power but less category innovation than PG's categories. At 2.3% yield and 3.5% DGR, CL is a steady-compounder that never has the drama KMB does and never trades at the multiple PG does. For a dividend portfolio, CL is an 8/10 safety holding at a reasonable multiple.

**CLX (Clorox)** is in the third tier of HPC quality but still materially better than anything in packaged food. Cleaning products (Clorox bleach, Pine-Sol, Glad) have been resilient through pandemic spikes and post-pandemic normalization. The 2023 cyberattack was a one-time operational disaster that depressed earnings for 4-6 quarters but did not damage brand equity. Glad JV termination with PG (January 2026) releases $500M of cash. 3.8% yield, 5.8% DGR, 47-year streak — this is an Aristocrat with genuine free cash flow. For investors who want Personal Care exposure without PG's premium multiple, CLX is the best entry.

**Church & Dwight (CHD)** — not in the comparable set — is the fourth HPC name worth noting. Arm & Hammer, OxiClean, Trojan, Waterpik, Nair. 28 consecutive years of raises. 1.1% yield (low). 2-3% DGR. Quality is strong but the yield is too modest for an income-first portfolio. Skip.

### Packaged Food — why this is the weakest sub-sector and what to do

Packaged food is experiencing a 10-15 year structural adjustment that started with Amazon/Walmart's private-label push in 2015-2018 and has accelerated through COVID (2020-2022), the inflation shock (2022-2023), and now the GLP-1 drug adoption curve (2024-2027). The sub-sector had a golden decade from 2005-2015 when private label was primitive, retailer consolidation was moderate, and consumer brand loyalty was strong. Since 2015, every one of those tailwinds has reversed.

**Private label economics have fundamentally shifted.** Aldi, Lidl, Costco Kirkland Signature, Trader Joe's, and Amazon's 365 Everyday Value have proven that private label can be premium quality at 20-30% lower retail prices. Consumers used to buy Kraft Macaroni & Cheese because it was better than any private-label alternative. Today, Aldi's "Simply Nature" mac & cheese is essentially indistinguishable in blind taste tests. The retailer consolidation has given the big three US grocers (Walmart, Kroger, Costco) procurement leverage that lets them squeeze pricing from the big brands. KHC's 21% of sales to Walmart is not an asset; it is a liability.

**The 2022-2023 inflation cycle was a consumer revolt.** When food inflation hit 11% in mid-2022, shoppers traded down across every category. Private-label share gains accelerated from 100 bps/year pre-COVID to 250-300 bps/year 2022-2024. Branded food companies passed through pricing aggressively (KHC +8.9% in 2023; CAG +7.5%) but the consumer response was to switch to private label, not to pay up. By 2025, the big brands tried to reverse — KHC's pricing was +0.8% in 9M FY2025 — but the damage was done. Consumers who tried private label and liked it did not come back.

**GLP-1 drugs are a slow-motion category earthquake.** The affected categories are specifically snacking (down 35% per GLP-1 user), sugared beverages (down 25%), and frozen meals (down 20%). At 14-18% US adult adoption by 2027, the aggregate category impact is roughly 5-8% volume drag by 2028 — enough to turn low-single-digit growth categories into low-single-digit decline categories. The drag is not evenly distributed: premium snacking (Frito-Lay, Mondelez) is hit harder than value snacking (private label); sugared CSDs (KO, PEP) are hit harder than water or Zero Sugar variants. Defensive pivots are possible (KO's zero-sugar portfolio is already 35% of volume and growing) but the aggregate category is in structural pressure.

**Which packaged food brands have durable pricing power, and which do not?**

Durable pricing power (can raise prices 3-5% annually without losing volume):
- Heinz ketchup (75%+ category share in UK/Netherlands/Germany, "Heinz or nothing" consumer preference)
- Hellmann's mayonnaise (not in user's portfolio; owned by Unilever)
- Hershey chocolate (60%+ share, category loyalty)
- Cadbury / Oreo / Ritz (MDLZ — global scale + emotional brand equity)
- Blue Buffalo premium pet food (GIS — premium tier with minimal private-label threat)
- Spam, Applegate organic meats (HRL — premium protein tier)
- Rao's premium pasta sauces (CPB — $6.50 jar is 3x private label at same retail)
- Don Julio tequila (DEO — luxury tier)

Broken pricing power (cannot raise prices without losing volume):
- Oscar Mayer hot dogs (KHC — processed meats category under GLP-1 + health pressure)
- Kraft Singles cheese (KHC — commodity cheese)
- Lunchables (KHC — declining school lunch category)
- Hamburger Helper (GIS — commodity meal kit)
- Progresso soup (GIS — commodity soup)
- Campbell's condensed soup (CPB — category in structural decline)
- Birds Eye frozen vegetables (CAG — private label at 35%+ share)
- Hungry-Man frozen meals (CAG — GLP-1 exposure + price-elastic category)
- Wonder bread (FLO — commodity bread)
- Nature's Own whole wheat (FLO — still has some pricing power)
- Fairlife ultra-filtered milk (KO — growing category but dairy pricing pressure)

**The brand-level view matters more than the company-level view.** KHC owns Heinz (durable) and Oscar Mayer (broken) in the same portfolio; the spin-off is the market's attempt to separate these into two distinct risk profiles. CPB's Rao's is durable; its Pepperidge Farm cookies are moderate; its condensed soup is broken. CAG is almost entirely broken — Pinnacle was the acquisition that bet on frozen being durable, and that bet has failed. GIS has Blue Buffalo durable + everything else declining. FLO is almost entirely broken except for Nature's Own and Dave's Killer Bread.

### Tobacco — why pricing power is still working but the capital allocation is the problem

Tobacco's investment case is fundamentally about pricing elasticity. The sub-sector has a clear math: if volumes decline 4-5% annually and pricing rises 7-9% annually, revenue grows 3-4% and operating income grows 5-7% (because COGS fall with volume). If volumes decline 7-10% (current MO rate) and pricing rises only 4-6%, revenue is flat-to-down and operating income stagnates. The 50-year history of the US tobacco industry shows pricing has generally offset volume; the question is whether that continues.

MO's 2025 data is the canary. Cigarette volume fell 10.0% (worst year ever). Management raised Marlboro list prices five times. Adjusted OCI for smokeable products rose 1.3%. This is still pricing winning — but barely. If volume declines become 8%+ annually and pricing ceiling is 7-9%, the model begins to break. This is the single most important thing to monitor at MO.

**The bright spot in tobacco is reduced-risk products (RRPs).** Nicotine pouches (Zyn, on!, Velo), heated tobacco (IQOS, Glo, Ploom), and premium e-vapor have all grown at 20-40% annually while combustible cigarettes decline. The problem for MO is that it has executed poorly on RRPs: JUUL $12.55B destroyed, NJOY $2.1B written down, on! losing share to Zyn (15.4% vs 18.8% year-over-year). The company that has executed well on RRPs is Philip Morris International (PM):

- **Zyn (PM)** — global pouch leader, 40%+ annual growth, premium pricing, no impairment history
- **IQOS (PM, now including US rights returned from MO)** — 15%+ annual growth, 40%+ of PM revenue from RRPs
- **Swedish Match acquisition (PM, 2022)** — fully integrated, accretive to earnings, brought Zyn in-house

PM's structural advantage over MO is not about cigarettes — both have strong cigarette franchises (Marlboro-US for MO, Marlboro-International for PM plus Parliament, L&M, Bond Street) — but about the speed and discipline of RRP execution. For the next 10 years, RRPs will be the growth engine of tobacco. If the user wants full tobacco exposure rather than just US cigarettes, the right split is ~60% PM / ~40% MO, not 100% MO.

BTI (British American Tobacco) is in a different place. The 2023 dividend cut reset the yield higher but signaled that management believed category transition required capital reallocation. BTI's Vuse e-vapor and Velo pouch brands have not caught Zyn. The 7.5% yield is high but the DGR is 1-2% and the streak is short. Not a replacement for MO or PM; skip.

**For the user's tobacco allocation:** The current 100% MO positioning ($6,580) at 3.4% of staples is appropriate. The recommended rotation to add $5-10K of PM (bringing tobacco allocation to ~6% of staples, 0.7% of total portfolio) would diversify the category and add the RRP growth exposure. PM's 4.5% yield is lower than MO's 6.44%, but 2-3% higher DGR compensates.
