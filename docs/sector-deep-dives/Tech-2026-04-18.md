But there are three issues worth flagging before going further:

Tech is the single-largest cash generator in the global economy right now; being this light is a defensive stance that has cost opportunity cost over the last 5 years.
2. **There is a mega-cap gap.** The portfolio has **zero exposure** to any of the seven names that between them account for over 50% of the market cap of the tech sector: MSFT, AAPL, GOOGL, AMZN (non-payers), AVGO, ORCL, META (non-payer). Of the dividend-paying subset of that list, the absence of **Microsoft, Apple, Broadcom, Oracle, Texas Instruments, Qualcomm, and Cisco** is notable. These are the firms that write the dividend checks for the dividend-growth ETFs like SCHD, DGRO, VIG — they are the tech lane of dividend compounding.
3. **PATH and XYZ are the wrong kind of tech.** Neither pays a dividend, neither is GAAP-profitable on a full-year basis (PATH has flirted with profitability; XYZ is volatile), and both are deeply underwater (PATH -50% cost basis, XYZ -63%). They are "speculative growth" positions that got into the portfolio for different reasons and should be handled differently from the cash-compounding core.

### I.2 The dividend-tech vs growth-tech tradeoff

There is an important framing question every dividend investor faces when looking at technology:

**Do you buy the cash-return machines (MSFT, AAPL, AVGO, TXN) that pay a modest 1-3% yield but grow the dividend 8-12% per year, or do you buy the slower services compounders (ACN, ADP, PAYX) at 3-5% yield with slightly lower growth?**

The answer, for a long-horizon portfolio, is **both** — but the mix matters. Historically, the blended cash-return (dividend + buyback) yield has been:

| Group                                    | Starting div yield | 5Y DGR | Buyback yield | Total cash return |
|------------------------------------------|--------------------|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Mega-cap tech dividend (MSFT, AAPL, AVGO)| 0.8-2.0%           | 9-12%  | 2-4%          | ~5-6%             |
| Services compounders (ACN, ADP, PAYX)    | 2.5-4.5%           | 7-10%  | 1-2%          | ~5%               |
| Payments (V, MA)                         | 0.6-0.8%           | 14-17% | 3-4%          | ~5%               |
| Semi dividend (TXN, QCOM, ADI)           | 2.5-3.5%           | 5-12%  | 2-3%          | ~6-8%             |
| Data monopolies (SPGI, MSCI, MCO)        | 0.8-1.8%           | 10-14% | 2-3%          | ~5%               |

The striking thing is that **total cash return is remarkably similar across the subgroups** — the market is roughly efficient here. The difference is in the *composition*: mega-cap tech is more capital-appreciation-and-buybacks, services are more cash-in-the-bank-today.

For a Spanish-resident investor using US-China treaty 10% WHT on US dividends, a higher-yield mix is slightly more tax-efficient (because buybacks defer the tax event to sale). But that is a second-order consideration; the first-order question is **capital quality**, and most of the names above pass the test.

### I.3 The "sell" case for PATH and XYZ

Before talking about what to add, let's address the elephants:

**PATH (UiPath).** The position is down 50%. UiPath sells RPA (robotic process automation) software — historically a defensible niche, but two problems have emerged: (1) generative AI is eating the lower-end of the RPA use case because LLMs can script workflows on the fly, and (2) competition from Microsoft Power Automate and other hyperscaler-bundled automation tools is commoditizing the market. UiPath's net dollar retention has drifted down from 133% in 2022 to roughly 110% in 2025, and the company has been through layoffs. It does not pay a dividend. It has no clear path to joining the dividend portfolio.

**Verdict on PATH:** This is a growth/turnaround bet, not a dividend position. Hold or exit based on one's view of the RPA market under GenAI, but treat it as a non-core satellite, not part of the income thesis.

**XYZ (Block).** Down 63% from cost. Block has two businesses: Square (merchant payments for SMBs) and Cash App (consumer P2P + investing + bitcoin). Both are respectable standalone businesses, but the company has struggled with stock-based compensation dilution, Bitcoin treasury exposure volatility, and a CEO (Jack Dorsey) whose attention is famously divided. No dividend. Operating margins improving in 2024-2025 but the stock has not rewarded it.

**Verdict on XYZ:** Same as PATH — this is a speculation, not a dividend position. The loss is real but the position is small (~$3k) so it is not urgent. If conviction has evaporated, tax-loss-harvest and redeploy into one of the dividend-tech names below.

Both PATH and XYZ *could* recover, of course — that is what "down a lot" positions frequently do. But from the specific lens of *this framework* (sustainable long-term dividend investing), they do not belong in the core sleeve. We will set them aside for the rest of this report.

### I.4 The recommended direction

The core recommendation of this report is:

> **Shift the tech allocation from 5.7% toward 10-12% over 12-18 months by adding one mega-cap tech dividend compounder (MSFT or AVGO), one semiconductor dividend aristocrat (TXN), and one mission-critical data monopoly (SPGI or MSCI). Keep ACN, ADP, PAYX, WKL, FDS, LSEG, CNSWF as the compounders of the services sleeve. Re-evaluate PATH and XYZ outside this framework.**

The analytical case for each of those names is made in Parts III and IV. First, let's map the landscape.

## Part II — Sub-sector analysis

The "technology" label is wildly heterogeneous. For a dividend investor, it is more useful to think in sub-sectors, each with its own economics, moat sources, and capital-return norms.

### II.1 Mega-Cap Tech Dividend — the hyperscale cash machines

These are the largest firms in the S&P 500 technology sector that pay a dividend. As a group they are characterized by: massive recurring revenue bases, enormous free cash flow generation, material buyback programs, and dividend yields in the 0.4% to 3.5% range.

| Ticker | Company    | Mkt cap | Yield | Div streak | 5Y DGR | Payout (FCF) | Net debt / EBITDA |
|--------|------------|---------|-------|------------|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| MSFT   | Microsoft  | ~$3.8T  | 0.80% | 22 yrs     | 10.2%  | 34%           | Net cash           |
| AAPL   | Apple      | ~$3.4T  | 0.50% | 13 yrs     | 5.3%   | 16%           | Slight net debt    |
| AVGO   | Broadcom   | ~$1.4T  | 1.60% | 14 yrs     | 12.8%  | 41%           | 1.6x               |
| ORCL   | Oracle     | ~$600B  | 1.15% | 17 yrs     | 9.1%   | n/a (negFCF)  | 2.5x               |
| CSCO   | Cisco      | ~$290B  | 2.80% | 14 yrs     | 4.4%   | 48%           | Net cash           |
| IBM    | IBM        | ~$250B  | 2.95% | 30 yrs     | 1.5%   | 54%           | 2.1x               |
| TXN    | TX Instr.  | ~$180B  | 3.00% | 21 yrs     | 11.3%  | 190% (!)      | 1.1x               |
| QCOM   | Qualcomm   | ~$190B  | 2.15% | 22 yrs     | 5.9%   | 30%           | 0.6x               |
| INTC   | Intel      | ~$120B  | 0.00% | SUSPENDED  | -100%  | n/a           | 3.2x               |

A few observations:

**Microsoft is the anchor.** The best large-cap compounder of the decade. In FY2025 Microsoft generated $281.7B of revenue (+15% YoY from $245B in FY24), $101.8B of net income (33% net margin), $136B of operating cash flow, and $71.6B of free cash flow — this after $64.6B of capital spending, which is the single-largest hyperscaler data-center buildout in history. Microsoft returned $42.5B to shareholders in FY25 ($24.1B dividends + $18.4B buybacks) while simultaneously ramping capex from $29B to $65B in three years to fuel the Azure + AI buildout. This is unprecedented. The dividend has been raised 22 consecutive years at a 10%+ CAGR. **This is the single most obvious miss in the user's portfolio.**

**AAPL pays a modest dividend but returns cash primarily through buybacks.** FY2025: $416B revenue, $112B net income, $98.8B FCF, and $90.7B of buybacks against only $15.4B in dividends. If you want the buyback-dominant cash-return model, Apple is it. If you want dividend growth specifically, Apple's 5Y DGR of 5.3% is mediocre versus peers; the market prefers Apple's stance of reducing share count.

**AVGO is the hidden superstar.** Broadcom has been one of the best-performing large-cap tech stocks of the last decade, and most dividend investors still don't own it. FY25 (Oct YE): $63.9B revenue (+24% YoY partly VMware-driven), $23.1B net income, $26.9B FCF, $11.1B dividends paid. The dividend streak is 14 years but the growth is exceptional — **from $0.32/yr to $6+/yr in 10 years** — and Broadcom's custom-silicon business is one of the two biggest beneficiaries of the AI buildout (alongside Nvidia). The counterargument is debt: AVGO carries ~$70B of debt post-VMware ($69.4B net), which means a 12-15x EBITDA leverage on a pro-forma basis — but the cash flow services it comfortably. Dividend payout ratio on FCF is ~41%. **This is the second most obvious miss.**

**ORCL has great headline growth but a FCF problem.** Larry Ellison is going all-in on AI infrastructure: FY25 capex was $21.2B, which actually *exceeded* operating cash flow, producing negative FCF. Oracle's OCI (Oracle Cloud Infrastructure) is legitimately growing 50%+ y/y and the firm has massive multi-year RPO (remaining performance obligations) from big-ticket AI customers. But right now the dividend is not fully covered by free cash flow, and until the capex cycle rolls off and the OCI revenue flows through, the dividend safety is notably worse than MSFT/AVGO. Hold if you already own, but not a first-time buy for this specific portfolio.

**TXN is a puzzle.** Texas Instruments is widely regarded as the *highest-quality* analog semiconductor business in the world — wide moat (thousands of SKUs, long design cycles, sticky customers across industrial/auto/comms), 40+ year history of returning cash, and the longest dividend streak in the group at 21 years. But there is a *current* problem: TXN is in the middle of its own massive capex cycle (building new 300mm fabs in Texas + Utah, capitalizing on CHIPS Act subsidies) that has temporarily compressed free cash flow. FY25: OCF $7.2B, capex $4.5B, FCF only $2.6B — against $5.0B of dividends paid. **The dividend-to-FCF ratio is 190%.** This is alarming on its face but contextually acceptable because: (a) TXN explicitly guided shareholders that 2023-2027 would be a heavy capex period that suppresses short-term FCF in exchange for 2028+ earnings power, (b) the firm has ample balance sheet capacity (net debt/EBITDA only 1.1x, $10B+ liquidity), and (c) the dividend increases have been modest (5-7%, not the 11%+ of the pre-capex era) specifically to preserve flexibility. Still, if you're allergic to payout ratios above 100%, TXN is a 2027-2028 re-entry rather than a 2026 buy. **I lean toward "the medium-term setup is exceptional but monitor capex roll-off."**

**CSCO is boring but reliable.** Back to a 2.8% yield, 14-year streak, modest 4.4% DGR. Cisco is transforming from a networking box seller to a software + services company (Splunk acquisition, big step up in recurring revenue). FY25 revenue grew 5% to $56.7B and FCF was $13.3B — comfortably covering $6.4B of dividends and $7.2B of buybacks. Low growth but very durable. Fair-yield play for diversification.

**IBM is a special case.** 30 consecutive years of dividend increases — the only Dividend Aristocrat among mega-tech. But the DGR is terrible (1.5% over 5 years), essentially token increases because the business has been in a 15-year slow-growth phase. FY25 revenue $67.5B is only slightly above FY20 levels. Operating margin 15% (vs. 30%+ for MSFT). The Red Hat + hybrid cloud strategy + recent AI/Watson repositioning have helped; revenue CAGR of 3.7% over the last 3 years is much better than the 0-1% seen from 2014-2022. Payout ratio is high (54% on FCF). **For pure dividend safety + status, IBM is acceptable; for total return, it is mediocre. I would not add but would hold if already there.**

**QCOM is cyclical.** Qualcomm is the handset modem leader with a powerful IP-licensing moat (CDMA, 5G essentials). But handset volumes are ex-growth, Apple is slowly building its own modem, and the automotive + IoT businesses, while growing, are not yet large enough to offset. FY25 revenue $44.3B (+12% from low base of FY24 $40B — recovery from handset downturn), but 3Y CAGR is effectively zero. The dividend has been raised 22 years but the growth has slowed sharply (5.9% 5Y DGR). Payout 30% on FCF, net debt/EBITDA 0.6x — dividend is safe. But this is a no-growth-plus-yield thesis, not a growth-plus-yield thesis. Acceptable but not inspired.

**INTC SUSPENDED the dividend in 2024.** Intel is in the middle of the most brutal turnaround of a US industrial company in a generation — Pat Gelsinger ousted in late 2024, massive capex commitments, Foundry business bleeding cash, CHIPS Act dependence. FY25 FCF was negative $4.9B. The dividend was cut to zero. **Avoid for dividend purposes** until there is a credible 2+ year track record of reinstatement. This is a turnaround/special-situation name, not a dividend name.

**ACN is the king of dividend IT services.** Revenue $64B+, operating margin 15-16%, FCF margin 17%. The company is navigating the GenAI transition better than peers — FY25 GenAI bookings reached roughly $4B, up from $1B a year earlier, and the new "Reinvention Services" strategy positions Accenture to sell AI transformation to Fortune 500 clients. The stock has been volatile (concerns about consulting industry margins, AI disintermediation risk), but the dividend track record speaks for itself: 20 years of increases, 10% 5Y CAGR, $3.7B paid out FY25 plus $4.6B of buybacks against $10.9B of FCF (payout 34%). **Net cash balance sheet.** This is a best-in-class name to hold.

**ADP is a 50-year Dividend King.** One of only ~50 companies on earth to have raised the dividend 50 consecutive years. Payroll is as sticky a business as you will find — switching costs are extreme because it touches every employee every two weeks. Revenue grew 6-7%/yr, margins expanding, 2.5%+ of the payroll-tax float is also a low-cost capital source. 11.7% 5Y DGR is strong. The only knock is valuation — ADP routinely trades at 28-32x earnings, reflecting the quality. At the user's cost basis of $210 with stock at $204, the position is slightly underwater but fundamentally fine. **Hold & accumulate on 15%+ drawdowns.**

**PAYX is the #2 payroll, but it over-distributes.** Paychex pays the highest yield in the group at 4.71% — which reflects both a higher payout ratio (78% of FCF in FY25) and lower growth than ADP. 18-year streak, 8.9% 5Y DGR. The business is solid, but with operating margin already at peak ~40% and a more SMB-concentrated book (small business is more economically sensitive than ADP's enterprise mix), PAYX is slightly lower quality than ADP. Still a very good dividend holding; the 4.71% yield does most of the work. **Hold at current size; do not add aggressively above 4.5% yield threshold because the implied risk is that growth slows.**

**WKL is Europe's answer to Thomson Reuters.** Wolters Kluwer sells professional information (legal, tax, health, compliance) on subscription. Recurring revenue ~80%. FY25 revenue ~€5.8B, operating margin ~26%, FCF €1.4B, dividend €0.6B (43% of FCF). 20+ year dividend track record with a 13% 5Y CAGR. **This is one of the best compounders in European dividend equities** — very hard to find comparable US-listed companies at the same combination of moat, growth, and yield. Keep.

**FDS is a smaller SPGI analog.** FactSet provides financial data + analytics to buy-side asset managers. Smaller ($8.5B) than SPGI/MSCI but similar economics. 26 years of dividend increases, 9.4% DGR, FCF margin ~35%. The concern is whether buy-side clients will continue to pay for FactSet over Bloomberg terminals + Aladdin/BlackRock solutions. Revenue growth has slowed to 4-5% vs 8-10% in the 2010s. Payout is only 34% so safety is fine, but this is a slower-growth compounder. **Hold but don't add aggressively — mid-cap with some competitive pressure.**

**CNSWF (Constellation Software) is a different beast.** Constellation is a roll-up of vertical-market software — they buy hundreds of small VMS businesses (niche software for dentists, breweries, municipalities, etc.) and compound them at 15-20%/year. Dividend is a token CAD$1/quarter ($4 USD/yr) with no growth — the company's return on incremental capital is so high (25%+ IRR on acquisitions) that Mark Leonard explicitly chooses to reinvest rather than pay more dividends. **This is a "growth + optionality" name that happens to pay a tiny dividend. Correct framing: own for growth, not yield.** 5-year total return has outperformed every other name in this table.

**CTSH (Cognizant) is an ACN-lite.** Indian-heritage IT services, smaller scale, lower margins (operating margin 16% vs ACN's 15% — similar now), 12-year dividend streak, 7.4% DGR. The firm has underperformed vs ACN for a decade as it lost share in digital transformation deals. Net cash balance sheet. Yield 1.6% is fine but not special. **Not a first-choice add for the user who already owns ACN.**

**LSEG is "Refinitiv + the LSE."** Post the $27B Refinitiv acquisition (2021), LSEG is now primarily a data + analytics business (~70% of revenue), with the exchange operation only ~30%. Similar economics to SPGI, with a 60%+ recurring revenue mix and an index franchise (FTSE Russell) that competes with MSCI/S&P indices. FCF £2.7B FY25, dividend payout ~30% — plenty of room. The GBP FX exposure adds some volatility for a USD-reporting investor but nothing pathological. 1.54% yield is low but the total return compounding is the story. **Keep.**

**V and MA are the gold standard.** Payments is arguably the single best business model in the S&P 500: a toll road on every dollar of consumer spending, with pricing power, network effects, and near-zero incremental cost per transaction. The numbers are stunning:

- Visa FY25: $40B revenue, 60% operating margin, $21.6B FCF, $4.6B dividends + $13.4B buybacks.
- Mastercard FY25: $32.8B revenue, 59% operating margin, $16.9B FCF, $2.8B dividends + $11.7B buybacks.

Both have 14-17 years of dividend streaks and **the fastest DGR of any large-cap in this report (16-17% five-year)**. Payout ratio is only 17-21% of FCF — dividends could 4x from here without any problem, which is why they keep growing the dividend so aggressively. Yield is the catch — only 0.6-0.75% starting yield — but the yield-on-cost after 15 years compounds to 5-8%.

For a dividend portfolio that expects to hold 20+ years, **V and MA are among the five or six best possible core holdings.** The user has zero exposure.

**XYZ and PYPL are "fintech" not "payments oligopoly."** These are the growth-focused fintechs — Block runs Square + Cash App; PayPal runs its namesake platform + Venmo + Braintree. Neither pays a dividend. Both have had dramatic drawdowns since the 2021 highs (PYPL -80%, XYZ -70%). They are potentially interesting as growth/recovery stories but **are not dividend holdings.** XYZ in the user's portfolio should be understood as a speculation, not as a dividend position.

**FI (Fiserv) and FIS are B2B payment infrastructure.** These firms provide the back-end rails for banks (Fiserv more so), card issuing, merchant processing, etc. FI has no dividend; FIS has a 2.8% yield with 24 consecutive years of dividend increases. FIS has been restructuring — the Worldpay sale and subsequent strategic review have made the story more complex. Payout is high at ~80% and the 3Y revenue CAGR is only 3.2%. Net debt/EBITDA ~2x. **Not a high-conviction add.**

This is a very high-quality subgroup. **Every semi on this list has a meaningful moat:** TXN (analog breadth + cost), AVGO (custom ASICs + software), QCOM (CDMA/5G IP), ADI (analog, post-Linear Tech acquisition), LRCX/AMAT/KLA (capex oligopoly for leading-edge fabs).

**The semi cycle is the key risk.** Over 5+ years, semi dividends compound at 10-12% — among the best growth rates in mature tech. But in any given 12-18 month window, semi revenue can swing ±30% and FCF ±50%. The firms listed here have all survived multiple cycles while paying and raising the dividend; the balance sheets are generally sound.

**For this specific user,** the most interesting candidates to add are:

1. **TXN** — purest dividend-aristocrat semi. 21 years. Wide analog moat. Temporarily high payout but capex cycle winds down 2027-2028. **Start small, add on weakness.**
2. **AVGO** — already discussed. Anchor position of the AI infrastructure trade, plus the software business (VMware + legacy CA). Dividend growth exceptional.
3. **ADI** — analog #2 behind TXN, more industrial-exposed, similar aristocrat-like profile. Solid alternative if TXN is too concentrated.

Lam/Applied/KLA (the "WFE" — wafer fab equipment — oligopoly) are also excellent but have higher cyclicality. Good 5-10% positions; might be overkill for an already-diversified book.

### II.5 IT Hardware — the ex-growth commodity

**Generic takeaway:** this sub-sector offers yield without quality. **Skip** for dividend purposes. The exception would be Dell if you specifically want AI-server exposure with a nascent dividend — but even then, pure-play semis (AVGO, TSM) capture more of the value.

## Part V — AI impact: who wins, who loses

The single most important secular shift in technology over the next decade is generative AI — both as a capex supercycle and as a potential disruption of entire business models. For a dividend investor, the question is not "will AI grow?" (obviously yes) but "will AI *preserve or enhance* the dividend-paying business models I care about?"

### V.1 Clear winners (own these)

**1. Hyperscaler infrastructure: MSFT (+AVGO as a supplier)**

Microsoft, Google (GOOGL), Amazon (AMZN), and Meta (META) are collectively spending roughly **$320B in 2026 on AI infrastructure** (up from ~$180B in 2024 and ~$110B in 2023). Of these four, only Microsoft pays a meaningful dividend — making it the cleanest dividend-investor vehicle for the AI infrastructure theme.

The bull case: Azure + OpenAI + Copilot monetizes this capex at an ROIC of 20%+ over time, vindicating the investment and sustaining margins. The bear case: the capex is too large, too fast, and margins compress for 2-3 years before revenue catches up.

MSFT's FCF growth has clearly slowed: $65B in FY22 → $60B in FY23 → $74B in FY24 → $72B in FY25. **Five-year FCF CAGR is only ~5%**, far below revenue CAGR of 14%. This is the capex-ingestion phase. If AI Copilot revenue scales as expected (the bull case: by FY28 Copilot + Azure AI adds $50B+ of incremental revenue at ~70% gross margin), FCF growth reaccelerates. If it doesn't, MSFT is essentially a 10% top-line grower with a tepid FCF story for the rest of the decade. **I side with the bulls but acknowledge the uncertainty.**

Broadcom is the cleanest supplier-side bet. Custom AI ASICs for Google (TPUs) and other hyperscalers are AVGO's fastest-growing segment. FY25 AI networking + custom silicon revenue was ~$15B+, up from ~$4B in FY23. This is a direct beneficiary of the capex cycle regardless of which hyperscaler "wins" — as long as *someone* is buying chips, AVGO sells them.

**2. Specialty analog / automotive semis: TXN (+ADI)**

Even if generative AI itself turns out to be a bubble, the physical-world build-out of electric vehicles, industrial automation, factory robotics, and edge-computing requires vast quantities of analog silicon. TXN and ADI are the two largest suppliers in that market. Automotive semiconductor content per vehicle is projected to rise from ~$500 (ICE vehicle, 2020) to ~$1,400 (EV, 2030). That is a 2.8x growth in unit demand even before any AI.

**3. Mission-critical data monopolies: SPGI, MSCI, MCO**

AI may automate away some analyst research roles, but it *increases* the value of the underlying proprietary data that models need to train and operate on. S&P's ratings, Moody's credit data, MSCI's index constituents and calculations — none of these are reproducible by an LLM. If anything, data vendors may be able to monetize AI-driven productization by selling clean, curated data feeds to AI companies.

**4. Payment oligopoly: V, MA**

Completely orthogonal to AI. Payment rails don't care about LLMs. Long-term thesis unchanged. Short-term, V/MA may invest in AI-based fraud detection (which would benefit merchants and issuers) but it's a cost line, not a disruption.

### V.2 Uncertain (monitor carefully)

**1. IT services / consulting: ACN**

This is the most nuanced case. The bear case: generative AI lets Fortune 500 companies do their own code generation + IT modernization without needing $100M Accenture contracts. Entry-level consulting jobs get eliminated. ACN's 742,000 headcount has to shrink, which pressures revenue.

The bull case: AI creates more IT complexity (agents, data pipelines, custom models, governance), not less. Accenture's "Reinvention Services" mission is exactly to help enterprises navigate that — and ACN has been converting: **GenAI bookings reached $4B+ in FY25, up from $1B in FY24.** The GenAI practice is small relative to ACN's $65B total revenue, but it is growing triple-digit and is taking share from competitors like TCS/Infosys that have been slower to pivot.

My read: **ACN is adapting successfully, but the next 24-36 months are a show-me period.** Watch (a) GenAI bookings growth, (b) consolidated revenue growth (needs to stay above 4-5%), and (c) operating margin stability (the firm has guided to 10-30 basis points of margin expansion per year — any wobble there is a canary).

**Sub-thesis for CTSH (Cognizant), Infosys, TCS, Wipro:** more exposed than ACN. Their offshore-labor-arbitrage model is more directly threatened by code generation. Reason I would not recommend CTSH as a first-time add.

**2. Enterprise SaaS: WKL, FDS, LSEG**

These are mostly AI-neutral. The data they sell is still needed, the workflow software is still sticky. Some upside from AI-enabled product features (WKL has "AI Co-Pilot" for tax and legal workflows). Main risk is that a new entrant uses AI to build a cheaper competitor in a specific vertical — but WKL/FDS/LSEG are so deeply embedded in customer workflows that switching costs dominate.

**3. ADP, PAYX (payroll)**

AI-neutral. Payroll is a compliance + reliability business, not an information business. The switching costs are tied to integration with HR, benefits, and tax systems — none of which are disrupted by LLMs. **These are durable regardless of AI outcomes.**

### V.3 Losers (avoid / trim)

**1. Legacy IT services with offshore-labor-arbitrage models**

Without naming specific non-US tickers, the "body shop" model of Indian IT services is under structural threat. CTSH is the largest US-listed example.

**2. PC / printer hardware: HPQ**

Not because of AI specifically, but because these businesses have been in secular decline for 15 years and AI doesn't rescue them. PC unit growth is 0% long-term. Printing is -3%/yr volume.

**3. Some traditional software (without AI pivot)**

Any firm whose product is "a form that humans fill out" is at risk of being automated away. This is why CRM, ERP, and verticalized software leaders (Salesforce, Oracle NetSuite, Workday) are rapidly adding "AI Agent" features — and why firms that *don't* will lose pricing power. In the user's portfolio, WKL and LSEG are actively adding AI features; the risk is lower. FDS has been slower to publicly show AI integration — monitor.

**4. Robotic Process Automation (RPA): PATH**

This is precisely the thesis I outlined earlier. RPA was a very good business in 2018-2022 — screen-scraping + workflow automation for legacy systems. But LLMs can now do many of the same tasks more flexibly, often without the expensive RPA platform. UiPath is fighting back (AI agents, LLM integration) but the original product advantage is eroding. **Not a dividend position regardless of outcome.**

### V.5 Putting it together — how to tilt the portfolio toward AI winners without over-risking
