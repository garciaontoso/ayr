## PART I — Executive Summary

Yuexiu Transport Infrastructure (1052.HK, "YXT") is the listed expressway platform of Guangzhou Yuexiu Group, the state-owned conglomerate of the Guangzhou Municipal Government. The company owns, operates and, where permitted, invests in toll road and bridge concessions across the People's Republic of China, with 15 assets in the portfolio as of year-end 2021 disclosure (Guangdong, Guangxi, Tianjin, Hubei, Hunan, Henan) and total attributable toll length of approximately 416.6 kilometres. Revenue is generated almost entirely from toll collection on concession agreements issued by provincial transportation authorities, with asset lives capped at the regulatory concession period — typically 20 to 30 years.

The dividend case is simple and brutal. Toll road cash flow is highly visible, interest cover is adequate, and the 6.2 % headline yield is one of the highest in the HK-listed infrastructure universe. The problem is what happens after the concession runs off. Yuexiu's largest legacy asset, Humen Bridge, already expired in 2019 (subsequently rolled into a replacement concession), and several Hubei-Hunan expressways from the post-2012 acquisition wave have 8–15 years of remaining life. Unlike a regulated utility, there is no rate base that survives the concession — at expiry, the asset reverts to the grantor and the toll stream ends. Management's answer has been to (i) acquire new expressways with cash and debt and (ii) maintain a ~50 % payout ratio. Both strategies depend on parent Yuexiu Group continuing to use YXT as its listed infrastructure vehicle, and on Chinese provinces being willing to sell concessions at reasonable IRRs — neither is certain.

The single most important change in FY2025 is the reappearance of margin compression. Revenue rose 9.1 % in CNY terms (to CNY 4.22bn reported; stockanalysis.com cites CNY 5.61bn on a more consolidated basis reflecting the 2024-acquired Daguangnan expressway), but EBIT margin fell from 40.3 % to 37.7 %, EBITDA margin remained elevated at ~82 % (non-cash D&A absorbs the bulk of cost), and net income dropped 21 % to CNY 519m. EPS fell to CNY 0.31 from CNY 0.39. At the FY2025 DPS of HKD 0.26, the payout ratio on reported HKD-equivalent earnings is now well above 70 % — a material tightening of the cushion that underpinned the dividend through the 2020–2022 COVID disruption. Management cut the full-year DPS from HKD 0.28 to HKD 0.26 (-7.1 %), a small but unambiguous signal that the 2021 special distribution of HKD 0.41 is not coming back and that the baseline dividend is under pressure.

Final verdict. We keep the 20,000-share position (≈0.60 % of portfolio at HKD 4.14) but do not add. The yield is attractive and the parent is a well-capitalised SOE, but the combination of shrinking concession runway, a 4.87x debt/EBITDA ratio, declining EPS, and the start of DPS cuts means this is not a compound-and-forget holding. We would trim at HKD 5.00+ and exit entirely if (a) DPS is cut a second consecutive year by more than 5 %, (b) net debt/EBITDA moves above 5.5x, or (c) a major concession (Guangzhou Western Second Ring, Guangdong Qinglian) is not renewed at reasonable economics.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield / Payout / Streak: HKD 4.14 / 6.22 % / ~70 % (EPS basis) / 0 years unbroken growth (cut in 2022 special, reduced again 2025)
- Last dividend change: FY2025 DPS HKD 0.26 vs FY2024 HKD 0.28 (-7.1 %). Ex-date ~16 June 2026.
- 5y dividend CAGR (ordinary): roughly -3 % [approx, excludes 2021 HKD 0.41 special]
- Net debt / EBITDA: 4.87x (FY2025)
- Free cash flow coverage of dividend: ~3.0–3.5x OCF to common dividend [approx, on CNY-translated basis]

## PART III — Historical Review (last 10 years)

Yuexiu Transport's history maps directly onto three phases: (a) the pre-COVID "mature cash machine" period 2016–2019, when a concentrated Guangdong portfolio generated 50 %+ operating margins and stable net income around CNY 1.0bn; (b) the 2020–2022 disruption, combining COVID toll waivers, a first-half 2020 national toll holiday mandated by the State Council (free passage for passenger cars to support pandemic mobility), and goodwill write-downs as management reassessed cash-generating units; and (c) the 2023–2025 post-pandemic phase, characterised by large asset injections from parent, margin normalisation, and the start of what looks like structural earnings pressure from (i) intermodal competition, (ii) higher interest expense, and (iii) re-acquiring traffic lost to substitute routes.

Shares outstanding have been essentially unchanged at 1.673bn since at least 2016 — management does not issue equity for acquisitions, preferring debt plus parent injections. This is a positive from a dilution standpoint but has left the company with debt/EBITDA creeping toward 5x.

**Inflection points:**

1. **2019 — EBIT margin compression from Humen Bridge concession expiry.** The original Humen Bridge concession expired on 30 March 2019 after 22 years of operation. The asset did not revert fully to the grantor — a replacement concession structure was negotiated — but the economics of the renewed arrangement are materially worse than the original BOT (build-operate-transfer) contract. This is why 2019 EBIT margin fell to 53.2 % vs 75.3 % the prior year: the new Humen arrangement absorbs a larger share of tolls as cost of revenue.

2. **2020 — national toll holiday + COVID mobility collapse.** The PRC State Council suspended all expressway tolls from 17 February 2020 to 6 May 2020 (79 days) to support pandemic economic activity. The revenue loss was not reimbursed. Net income collapsed 86 % to CNY 160m, the lowest in a decade. The DPS was maintained (HKD 0.24 ordinary) only by drawing on retained earnings — a key stress-test data point.

3. **2021 — Hanxiao disposal gain + HKD 0.41 special dividend.** A partial disposal of a Hanxiao Expressway joint venture generated a one-time gain that boosted reported net income to CNY 1.465bn (EPS 0.88). The board declared a special dividend of HKD 0.41 per share, which investors incorrectly extrapolated as a new run-rate — the 2022 DPS reverted to HKD 0.26 ordinary, creating a substantial "yield trap" impression for first-time buyers at 2021 valuations.

4. **2022 — post-special normalisation + margin reset.** With the Hanxiao disposal gone, reported net income dropped to CNY 453m and EBIT margin compressed to 39.6 %. This was the first post-COVID "clean" year and suggested the mid-50 % EBIT margin structure of 2017–2019 was not coming back. Investors accustomed to the pre-COVID profile had to reset.

5. **2023 — Daguangnan acquisition announcement + traffic rebound.** Revenue recovered strongly (+31 % YoY) driven by China's 2023 post-COVID reopening and the announcement of the Daguangnan Hubei corridor acquisition from parent. This was funded primarily with CNY-denominated debt, pushing interest expense up.

6. **2024 — Daguangnan consolidation begins + Shenzhen-Zhongshan Bridge opens.** The Shenzhen-Zhongshan Link (海中大桥) opened to traffic on 30 June 2024. This 24km cross-PRD connection directly substitutes for some Humen Bridge traffic, with early reports suggesting 15–25 % of eastbound Humen volume [approx] diverted in H2 2024. YXT management acknowledged traffic softness but quantified it narrowly.

7. **2025 — margin compression accelerates + DPS cut.** EBIT margin fell to 37.7 %, net income declined 21 %. Management reduced the final dividend, taking full-year DPS from HKD 0.28 to HKD 0.26 (-7.1 %). This is the first clear signal that the ordinary dividend is not insulated from the underlying cash-flow deterioration.

## PART IV — Dividend Deep Dive

- **Current yield:** 6.22 % at HKD 4.14 (semi-annual pay schedule).
- **Payout ratio (EPS basis):** Using FY2025 EPS CNY 0.31 ≈ HKD 0.34 (at CNY/HKD 1.10 approx), DPS HKD 0.26 = **77 % payout**. In pre-2025 years: 60–70 %. The 2022 payout was ~130 % on very depressed EPS — covered from retained earnings.
- **Payout ratio (FCF basis):** FY2024 FCF CNY 2.63bn; dividends paid (including minority-interest distributions) approximately CNY 800m on common + CNY ~250m to minorities. Dividend coverage by FCF for common remains ample at 3.0–3.5x, but this only works because D&A (CNY 1.8bn in 2025) is a non-cash charge that reverses out of OCF. The crucial test is: what is the true maintenance capex / concession fee pressure that eats into FCF over time? See Part VIII.
- **Dividend streak:** Zero years of uninterrupted growth. The ordinary DPS has oscillated between HKD 0.24 and HKD 0.32 over the last decade, with a special distribution of HKD 0.41 in 2021 (not repeated) and now a -7 % cut in 2025. This is NOT a dividend aristocrat — it is a capital-return vehicle that pays out what it can, when it can, given the concession runoff.
- **5y dividend CAGR (ordinary):** Approximately -3 % [approx, excluding the 2021 special]. Including the special, the FY2021 "total distribution" was HKD 0.67; on that basis the 4-year CAGR to FY2025 (HKD 0.26) is -21 % — but that comparison is misleading.
- **10y dividend CAGR (ordinary):** Roughly flat in HKD terms. This is a yield holding, not a dividend-growth holding.
- **Last hike:** FY2021 special of HKD 0.41 was the last material increase. The last ordinary increase was FY2019 from HKD 0.28 to HKD 0.32 (subsequently cut back to HKD 0.26).
- **Pattern of hikes:** Non-pattern. The board declares interim (typically HKD 0.13 with H1 results in August) and final (typically HKD 0.13–0.15 with FY results in March) dividends based on annual distributable profits.
- **Dividend coverage by FCF:** 3.0–3.5x on CNY cash flow basis. This is above the 1.0x minimum but is eroding.
- **Currency of payment:** Hong Kong dollars. The underlying earnings are RMB/CNY. Currency risk: in a PRC policy-driven CNY depreciation cycle, HKD dividends would fall proportionally.
- **Withholding tax for non-US holders (your situation, China tax resident):** Hong Kong imposes **zero withholding tax** on dividends paid by Hong Kong-registered companies. However, YXT is Bermuda-registered and Hong Kong-listed. Bermuda imposes no withholding tax. As a PRC tax resident, you technically should self-declare this dividend income on your PRC personal tax return, but in practice, HK-sourced dividends from non-PRC-registered issuers are not subject to PRC withholding at source. Net yield to you approximates the gross 6.22 % — a significant advantage vs any A-share or US-listed equivalent. [Confirm with tax advisor — the PRC-HK CDTA (Comprehensive Double Tax Arrangement) applies but specific personal tax application depends on residency status and whether income is remitted into mainland accounts.]

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The dividend has survived three genuine stress tests in the last 15 years: (1) the 2008–2009 Global Financial Crisis (Guangdong GDP growth decelerated but toll traffic recovered within 18 months); (2) the 2020 national toll holiday and COVID mobility collapse (DPS maintained at HKD 0.24 on 86 % lower net income, covered by retained earnings); (3) the 2022 post-COVID normalisation when EPS fell 70 % and DPS was maintained at HKD 0.26 (payout ratio ~130 %). In each case the board accepted an elevated payout ratio rather than cut the ordinary dividend.

What changed in 2025 is that, unlike 2022, the earnings pressure appears **structural** rather than cyclical. Shenzhen-Zhongshan Bridge is a permanent fixture of PRD geography. High-speed rail competition on Wuhan-Changsha corridors is permanent. Interest expense at CNY 414m on CNY 17.2bn of debt implies a 2.4 % average cost of funds [approx, weighted against multi-year fixed-rate notes] — refinancing in 2026–2028 at PRC 10-year yields of 1.8 % could be neutral, but a tightening cycle would bite. The board's response — a -7.1 % DPS cut — is rational, but it also signals that the prior DPS of HKD 0.28 was not fully sustainable.

For FY2026, we assume the DPS holds at HKD 0.26 (management is unlikely to cut two consecutive years absent a catastrophe). Beyond that, the DPS level depends on whether new concession acquisitions from parent can offset the ongoing erosion of legacy Guangdong assets. The base case is a slow decline in real HKD dividend purchasing power; the bear case is a further 10–20 % cut if Humen traffic continues to deteriorate through 2027.

## PART V — Business Quality & Moat

Toll road businesses have two moat dimensions that operate in opposite directions.

**The temporary moat (concession exclusivity).** During the concession period, the operator has a government-granted monopoly on toll collection along a specific alignment. This is economically similar to a very-long-duration utility franchise, with the critical difference that the franchise has a defined expiry — typically 20–30 years for Chinese expressways. Within the concession period, pricing is regulated (toll rates are set by provincial transportation authorities and adjusted rarely, if ever, to track inflation), but volume upside (traffic growth) flows to the operator. This creates a monopoly on a depleting asset.

**The structural lack of moat (post-concession).** At concession expiry, the asset reverts to the grantor — usually at zero compensation to the listco, since the original BOT contract anticipated full cost recovery during the concession term. There is no residual asset value, no rate base, no terminal cash flow. This is why valuations should structurally be discounted cash flow models rather than multiples — a P/E multiple implicitly assumes perpetual earnings, which a toll road concession simply does not have.

- **Moat type:** Regulatory (concession-based) with limited duration. **Not comparable to a regulated utility rate base.**
- **Moat trajectory:** **Narrowing.** Each year that passes, the remaining economic life of the portfolio decreases. The Humen renewal economics are worse than the original. The Shenzhen-Zhongshan bridge is a permanent substitute. Yuexiu Group's ability to continue injecting fresh concessions depends on the Guangzhou municipal government's willingness to monetise assets through the listed vehicle versus alternative channels (REITs, direct sale to CR Expressway, etc.).
- **Pricing power evidence:** Effectively none. PRC expressway tolls are regulated to ensure public affordability; the last major rate change on most YXT assets was during the original concession negotiation. Revenue growth must come from volume (traffic) and mix (higher-margin commercial vehicles vs passenger).
- **Return on invested capital trend:**
  - FY2021: ~7.5 % (depressed 2020 base recovery)
  - FY2022: ~4.5 %
  - FY2023: ~6.5 %
  - FY2024: ~5.5 %
  - FY2025: 4.2 % (stockanalysis.com reported)

ROIC is below YXT's weighted cost of capital (estimated 6.5–7.0 % blended HKD/CNY). The business is destroying shareholder value at the margin — a classic feature of infrastructure assets where the incremental acquired concession is priced at a premium that absorbs the returns.

## PART VI — Capital Allocation

The capital allocation priority for FY2024 (in approximate CNY terms) was:

1. **Dividends (ordinary):** ~CNY 920m including minorities — approximately 35 % of OCF.
2. **Buybacks:** CNY 0. YXT has not repurchased shares in the last 10 years despite trading below book value consistently since 2017.
3. **Capex (maintenance):** ~CNY 30m. Minuscule. Expressway concession maintenance capex is largely absorbed through the concession agreement (concession fees paid to grantor cover highway resurfacing cycles).
4. **M&A (via parent injections):** Daguangnan acquisition completed 2024 [approx CNY 3–4bn consideration]. This is the primary growth vector — every 3–5 years, parent injects a new expressway at a negotiated price that is theoretically "independent" but is governed by PRC SOE asset injection rules.
5. **Debt paydown:** Marginal. Net debt has stayed within a CNY 14–15bn band since 2019.

**Capital allocation grade: C.** The board consistently pays ~50 % of earnings as dividends regardless of acquisition opportunities or debt levels; does not buy back stock at sub-book prices (a missed opportunity when P/B fell to 0.39x in FY2024); and accepts acquisition premiums from parent that depress ROIC. This is rational behaviour for an SOE-controlled vehicle — the parent's interest is in collecting its 66 % of the dividend and recycling capital into new assets — but it is not shareholder-optimal from a minority perspective.

The missed buyback opportunity is the single clearest signal that management is not operating in the minority-shareholder interest. With a P/B of ~0.40 and distributable reserves likely above HKD 3bn on a BVPS of HKD 7.17, a modest HKD 300m buyback (4 % of market cap) at HKD 4.00 would increase intrinsic value per share by roughly 6 %. It has not happened in 10 years.

## PART VIII — Financial Analysis

- **Cash & equivalents (FY2025):** CNY 2.97bn (up 50 % YoY) — reflects H2 2025 working-capital build ahead of expected acquisition payments and debt refinancing.
- **Total debt:** CNY 17.2bn. Composition: approximately CNY 12.1bn long-term (mix of RMB bank loans and CNY-denominated corporate bonds), CNY 5.1bn current portion of long-term debt. Weighted average interest cost: ~2.4 %.
- **Net debt:** CNY 14.3bn.
- **Net debt / EBITDA:** 4.87x (FY2025).
- **Interest coverage (EBIT/Interest):** CNY 1.59bn / CNY 414m = **3.84x**. Adequate but not comfortable for an infrastructure business.
- **Working capital:** Current ratio 0.53 — structurally low because toll receivables are collected in days (electronic ETC system) and current-portion-of-long-term-debt inflates current liabilities. This is normal for HK-listed Chinese toll operators but worth monitoring.
- **Intangible assets:** CNY 30.77bn — this is the single largest balance sheet item and represents the capitalised cost of the concession rights themselves. As concessions run off, this balance is amortised through the income statement (CNY 1.85bn of D&A in FY2025 — substantially higher than the CNY 30m of maintenance capex). This is the accounting embodiment of the "depleting moat" — every year, the carrying value of the right to collect tolls is ground down.
- **Goodwill:** Minimal on a standalone basis.
- **Pension obligations:** PRC social security scheme participation — no material defined-benefit deficit.
- **Off-balance-sheet:** Minority interests in jointly-controlled expressway JVs account for ~CNY 3.5–4bn of equity-method investment. These are the Guangxi and Hubei assets held as 50 %+ JVs rather than wholly-owned. Distribution receivables from these JVs are a recurring working-capital item.
- **Accounting quality:** Generally clean under HKFRS. The only aggressive-adjacent item is the useful-life assumption on concession intangibles — a small change (e.g., switching from straight-line amortisation over concession life to unit-of-traffic amortisation) can materially swing reported EPS.

**Balance sheet stress test.** If PRC 10-year yields rise from 1.8 % to 4.0 % (a stress that would require a shift in PRC monetary regime), and YXT's debt is refinanced at +200 bps, interest expense rises by ~CNY 340m, which at current EBIT of CNY 1.59bn would cut pre-tax income by 21 % and interest coverage from 3.84x to 2.55x. Dividend coverage becomes tight but not negative. This is a low-probability scenario in the 12–24 month horizon given PRC central bank policy stance.

## PART X — Risks & Stress Tests

1. **Risk #1: Concession runoff without adequate replacement.** The cash flows are finite by regulatory construction. If parent Yuexiu Group does not continue to inject replacement concessions at reasonable IRRs (the Daguangnan injection was the last one; nothing announced for 2026), the portfolio depletes mechanically over 15–20 years. **Probability:** Medium-high (parent has incentives to extract cash to consolidated SOE budget rather than reinvest). **Severity:** Very high — terminal value of the equity trends to zero. **Mitigant:** HK-listed SOE subsidiaries have typically been refreshed with new assets, though at declining frequency.

2. **Risk #2: Intermodal and intramodal competition on core Guangdong corridors.** Shenzhen-Zhongshan Link (opened June 2024) diverts Humen-Bridge traffic. High-speed rail continues to take passenger share from expressway travel (though intercity passenger-car is still growing). EV adoption reduces per-vehicle toll friction (lighter cars, lower per-vehicle toll revenue if tolls shift to distance+weight basis). **Probability:** High. **Severity:** Medium — each substitution event reduces one asset's cash flow by 10–25 %, but the portfolio diversification dilutes impact. **Mitigant:** Parent can inject new corridors in more defensible geographies.

3. **Risk #3: PRC policy risk on toll road economics.** The State Council has repeatedly intervened in toll road economics — 2020 toll holiday, mandatory ETC installation, promoting "free-flow" tolling that erodes evasion-prevention margins. A political-economy shift toward "free public infrastructure" during a recession would compress EBIT. **Probability:** Low-Medium. **Severity:** Medium. **Mitigant:** Concession contracts are provincial, not central, and Guangdong has been relatively business-friendly.

4. **Risk #4: Interest rate and refinancing risk.** CNY 5.1bn of debt is current-portion. Refinancing at higher rates directly cuts EPS (already 3.84x interest coverage is not comfortable). **Probability:** Low in 12–24 months given PRC monetary policy. **Severity:** Medium.

5. **Risk #5: Parent cash-extraction pressure.** Yuexiu Group is heavily levered at holding-co level (Yuexiu Property has been under balance sheet stress). If parent needs cash, YXT dividend becomes the ATM — which could force payout to exceed sustainable levels. **Probability:** Medium. **Severity:** Medium — this argues for higher dividend but lower quality of earnings.

6. **Risk #6: EV and autonomous-vehicle disruption on traffic volume (long-term).** Unclear impact. Autonomous trucking might increase commercial vehicle volume (cheaper trucking → more goods moved). EV adoption reduces fuel-station-adjacent toll revenue. Net effect is uncertain and distant (10+ years). **Probability:** Certain but slow. **Severity:** Low-Medium. **Mitigant:** Adaptive tolling schemes under pilot in Guangdong.

**Stress test scenarios**

### Deep-dive: Guangdong GDP and toll road volume linkage

Guangdong Province is the dominant economic engine in YXT's portfolio, accounting for the plurality of attributable toll revenue through three major asset clusters: the Greater Guangzhou ring roads (Northern Second Ring, Western Second Ring, Northern Ring Road), the Pearl River Delta crossings (Humen Bridge renewal, Shantou Bay Bridge), and the provincial trunk (Qinglian Expressway linking Guangdong to Hunan). Guangdong's 2024 GDP was approximately RMB 14.2 trillion (USD 2.0 trillion equivalent), placing it above the national GDPs of South Korea and Russia as a standalone economy. The province has consistently grown at 4.5–5.5 % in real terms since 2018, above the national PRC average.

However, the linkage between provincial GDP and toll revenue has weakened materially since 2020 for three reasons. First, Guangdong's growth mix has shifted from manufacturing-heavy (which generates truck traffic) toward services and high-value-added electronics (which does not). Second, the Greater Bay Area planning framework — a policy push to integrate Hong Kong, Macau, Shenzhen, Zhuhai and Guangzhou into a single metropolitan system — has generated multiple substitute infrastructure projects (HKZMB, Shenzhen-Zhongshan, Zhuhai-Zhongshan rail) that dilute traffic across YXT's original east-west Pearl River corridor assets. Third, high-speed rail buildout has captured an increasing share of intercity passenger-car travel — a Guangzhou-Shenzhen HSR ticket at RMB 82 economy-class is competitive with the all-in cost of toll + fuel for a small passenger car over the same distance.

The net effect is that a 5 % Guangdong GDP growth year no longer translates into a 5 % toll volume growth year. Our estimate of the current "GDP beta" of YXT Guangdong-assets toll volume is approximately 0.4–0.5, down from 0.8–1.0 pre-2020. This is the quantitative root of the earnings pressure management is now discussing in qualitative terms.

### Hubei cluster: the 2024 acquisition and its economics

The Daguangnan Expressway acquisition, announced mid-2024 and consolidated in H2 2024 YXT results, deserves specific attention because it is the single largest capital deployment by YXT in the last 5 years and it sets the template for how future parent-injection transactions will be priced. Daguangnan connects the Hubei capital Wuhan with the eastern industrial clusters around Huangshi and is a wholly-owned BOT concession with approximately 18 years of remaining life at acquisition. The transaction price of approximately RMB 3.9bn implies an EV/EBITDA purchase multiple of approximately 7.5x — higher than YXT's own trading multiple of 6.6x at the time of acquisition, which means YXT paid a premium to its own stock-market valuation for the asset.

From a strategic perspective this makes sense only if (a) Daguangnan's traffic growth profile is materially higher than the existing YXT portfolio (partially true — Hubei manufacturing cluster is growing faster than mature Guangdong corridors), or (b) parent Yuexiu Group is exerting SOE pricing discipline and YXT minority shareholders are effectively subsidising the broader SOE system. The actual answer is likely a mix of both, with the second factor dominant. This is the single clearest demonstration that YXT is not operated as an independent value-maximising entity — it is a capital-allocation vehicle for the broader Yuexiu Group conglomerate.

The IRR implied by the Daguangnan transaction, on consensus traffic and toll-rate assumptions, is approximately 6.0–6.5 %. This is below YXT's weighted cost of capital of 6.5–7.0 %. The acquisition is marginally value-destructive for YXT shareholders on an incremental basis.

### The Humen Bridge renewal — anatomy of a concession rollover

The original Humen Bridge BOT concession expired on 30 March 2019. This was the first major concession expiry faced by YXT in its post-2011 rebrand, and it established the template for how the renewal economics are expected to work across the rest of the portfolio.

Under the original 1997 concession, YXT (then GZI Transport) had the right to collect 100 % of Humen Bridge tolls after grantor fees for 22 years. The renewal arrangement — disclosed in 2019 interim results — restructures the economics materially. Under the renewed structure, YXT shares a higher proportion of toll revenue with the grantor (Guangdong Provincial Expressway Development Co), and the economic life of the remaining YXT entitlement is approximately 15 years (to 2034). The cost-of-revenue ratio on Humen Bridge tolls under the renewal is approximately 55–60 %, compared to 30–35 % under the original concession. This is the accounting source of the 2019 EBIT margin compression from 75 % to 53 % at the consolidated level — Humen alone accounted for 60 % of the revenue mix at the time.

This template is the single most important data point for projecting YXT's long-term earnings trajectory. As each legacy Guangdong concession rolls over (Northern Second Ring in 2029, Western Second Ring in 2031, Qinglian in 2033, etc.), we should expect a similar step-down in the economic share retained by the listco. Under base-case assumptions, the weighted average "YXT retention ratio" on Guangdong toll revenue falls from approximately 70 % today to 45 % by 2035. This is the underlying mechanical reason why the dividend cannot be compounded — the share of toll revenue flowing to YXT shareholders is structurally shrinking.

### Greater Bay Area integration — the bull case that has stalled

A bull-case argument for Chinese toll road operators in 2015–2019 was the Greater Bay Area (GBA) integration thesis: as Guangdong-HK-Macau became a more integrated metropolitan system, cross-border and intra-regional traffic would grow above GDP rates, lifting all toll operators. This thesis has only partially materialised.

The HKZMB (Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge), which opened in October 2018, has carried below-expectations traffic — closed-border effects during COVID were part of it, but even in post-COVID 2024 traffic, the HKZMB is running at approximately 40 % of design capacity. The Shenzhen-Zhongshan Link opened June 2024 is tracking closer to design capacity but at the direct expense of Humen Bridge (which lost 18 % of directional eastbound traffic in H2 2024 per industry estimates [approx]). The net effect on YXT is negative — the GBA integration has created substitute infrastructure faster than it has generated net new demand.

The remaining planned GBA infrastructure includes a Shenzhen-Huizhou-Shanwei HSR completion (passenger competition), a Zhuhai-Zhongshan second crossing (parallel-road competition), and a Shenzhen outer ring expansion (bypasses Guangzhou cluster). Each is a potential future headwind for YXT's specific asset base. There is no major GBA infrastructure project that is additive to the YXT portfolio.

### Electric vehicle traffic impact — first-order and second-order effects

EV adoption in China has been the fastest globally. Guangdong Province has approximately 30 % new-vehicle EV penetration in 2025, with target of 60 % by 2030 per provincial policy. The first-order impact on toll revenue is ambiguous: EV vehicles pay the same toll as ICE vehicles per passage, so revenue per passage is unchanged. The second-order effects are more subtle.

First, EV vehicles have reduced range anxiety only for short-haul trips; long-distance EV travel (Guangzhou-Beijing 2,200km, Guangzhou-Shanghai 1,500km) still faces charging infrastructure friction that favours air or rail. This is a mild positive for toll roads — EVs may shift some long-distance travel away from roads. Second, EV vehicles are on average lighter than comparable ICE vehicles, which reduces road wear and therefore maintenance capex requirements — a minor positive for toll operators. Third, some PRC provinces are piloting differential toll rates based on vehicle weight and emissions, which could materially shift the toll revenue per vehicle depending on formula design. The regime has not yet been adopted in Guangdong but is being trialled in Sichuan.

Net effect on YXT over a 10-year horizon: ambiguous, slight negative tilt, but small relative to the concession-runoff dynamic.

### Foreign exchange exposure — the hidden risk

YXT reports in CNY (RMB). Dividends are declared and paid in HKD. The pegging mechanism between the two — the HKMA USD peg through which HKD effectively tracks USD, while CNY floats in a managed band — means there is a genuine currency mismatch between earnings (CNY) and distributions (HKD).

Over the last 5 years, CNY/HKD has moved from approximately 1.17 (CNY weaker, 2019) to approximately 1.10 (CNY stronger vs HKD at some points) and back. A 5 % depreciation of CNY vs HKD effectively reduces the HKD-equivalent coverage of the dividend by 5 %. If PRC policymakers allow or engineer CNY depreciation to support export competitiveness in a 2026–2028 trade war scenario, YXT's HKD dividend-paying capacity weakens at the margin.

Additionally, the CNY 17.2bn of debt is primarily RMB-denominated, matching the currency of operating cash flows — this is prudent. The equity market cap, however, is HKD-denominated. For a HK-based investor measuring returns in HKD, a CNY weakening directly hits both the dividend AND the value of the underlying earnings power.

For the user's context (PRC resident receiving HKD dividends into a HK brokerage account), this currency structure is actually slightly advantageous — the HKD proceeds can be held offshore without triggering PRC onshore-RMB conversion. But from a total-return perspective, a CNY weakening hurts.

### Counter-narrative: the bull case I am rejecting but articulating

A thoughtful bull case for YXT, which I am not persuaded by but which a reasonable investor could hold, runs as follows. (1) The Guangdong economy is structurally the most dynamic in China and will continue to grow at 4–5 % real for another decade. (2) Parent Yuexiu Group has every incentive to continue using YXT as its listed infrastructure platform — it has done so for 25 years and the SOE privatisation incentive is not going away. (3) The dividend is 6.2 % in a world of 4 % US 10-year yields and 1.8 % PRC 10-year yields — the relative yield attraction is significant. (4) At P/B 0.39, the stock is clearly trading below intrinsic value if concession renewals continue at reasonable rates. (5) The -7 % DPS cut in FY2025 is a one-time adjustment to a sustainable level; further cuts are not inevitable.

Each of these points has merit. The problem is that even granting all five, the expected total return over a 10-year horizon is dividend-yield + modest capital appreciation — approximately 6 % + 1–2 % = 7–8 %. This is not bad. But the compounding profile is fundamentally weaker than a US dividend aristocrat at 3 % yield + 7 % dividend growth + 2 % multiple expansion = 12 %. The bull case is "this is a decent yield holding" — not "this is a compounding franchise". Our HOLD rating reflects this: the security is well-priced for what it is, but what it is, is not a long-duration compounder.
