## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: BUY to 3% target weight · Dividend Safety: 6/10 · Confidence: Medium-High · Fair Value: $142 base / $115 bear / $168 bull**

Genuine Parts Company is the single most contrarian Dividend King on the board right now. For decades GPC was the archetypal boring industrial distributor: buy NAPA stores, raise the dividend every year by 5-8%, let Motion Industries generate a steady 12% EBITDA margin. The 2017 Alliance Automotive Group (~$2.0B Europe) and 2022 Kaman Distribution Group (~$1.3B industrial) deals grew revenue from $16.5B (2020) to $24.3B (2025) at an ~8% CAGR and the 64-year streak became 69.

Then three things collided in 2024-2025. First, the auto aftermarket hit a cyclical trough as the US consumer tightened and European comps went soft. Second, the 2024-launched global restructuring program burned $475M cumulative for $175M of run-rate savings — a 2.7x cost/savings ratio versus management's original 1.6x guide. Third, on September 4, 2025 **Elliott Investment Management filed a Cooperation Agreement** (FY2025 10-K Exhibit 10.41), and less than six months later on February 17, 2026 the board announced a separation into "Global Automotive" and "Global Industrial" targeting Q1 2027 close. These events are not unrelated. Elliott ran the same playbook at Marathon Petroleum, Arconic, NRG Energy, and Suncor. Their thesis at GPC is clean: **Motion Industries deserves a 20x+ multiple that it cannot get while trapped inside a low-multiple auto parts conglomerate.**

At ~$123 and $16.9B market cap, GPC trades at 16.7x adjusted FY2025 EPS of $7.37, 9.3x EV/adjusted EBITDA, and a 3.4% dividend yield. Versus the 10-year P/E average of ~19x these multiples are ~1.5 turns compressed. Motion Industries embedded inside GPC is valued at ~11x EPS versus Applied Industrial at 22x, Fastenal at 31x, and W.W. Grainger at 24x. A sum-of-the-parts analysis at 17x on Motion's $1.15B segment EBITDA + 7x on $672M NA Automotive EBITDA + 6x on $544M International Automotive EBITDA, less $4.3B net debt, produces ~$153/share — **25% above the current ~$123**. The bull case with aggressive Motion multiple expansion gets to $185; the bear case with deeper auto compression is $115.

**Top 3 reasons to own:** (1) the **separation catalyst** is officially disclosed, Q1 2027 close, Elliott keeping the board honest; (2) **Motion Industries is a world-class #1 industrial MRO distributor trading at half the peer-group multiple**; (3) the **69-year dividend streak** is near-sacred institutional memory that no board in GPC history has ever broken.

**Top 3 reasons for caution:** (1) **FY2025 FCF of $421M did not cover the $564M dividend** for the first time this cycle — a 0.75x coverage ratio financed by revolver/commercial paper, viable for 12-18 months but not indefinitely; (2) **restructuring cost run-rate has materially exceeded savings run-rate** for two consecutive years; (3) **separation dis-synergies** could absorb 100-200 bps of combined margin in the first 24 months post-spin. S&P (BBB-) and Moody's (Baa1) both have **negative outlooks**; Fitch initiated at BBB- stable in Q1 2026.

**Top 3 catalysts (next 12 months):**
1. **Form 10 filings** for Global Automotive and Global Industrial (expected Q3 2026) — first standalone financials and indicative dividend policies
2. **Q2 FY2026 earnings print** (late July 2026) — tests whether Q3 2025 segment-margin acceleration (Auto EBITDA +5.9%, Industrial +6.6%) is durable
3. **Separation record/distribution date** announcement in Q4 2026 for Q1 2027 close

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

### Corporate lineage

Genuine Parts Company was incorporated in Georgia on **May 7, 1928** by Carlyle Fraser, initially as a purchase of the Motor Parts Depot in Atlanta with $40,000 in capital (FY2019 10-K Item 1). Fraser joined NAPA (National Automotive Parts Association) in 1929, and for nearly a century GPC has been the largest — and eventually sole — member of NAPA, functioning as the exclusive licensee and operator of the NAPA brand in North America. The company is subject to a 1954 federal consent decree (Detroit district court) prohibiting certain anticompetitive practices among former NAPA members including exclusive manufacturer agreements, price fixing, and territorial allocation (FY2025 10-K Item 1 page on NAPA). Motion Industries was acquired in **1976** for $17M — a single data point that in hindsight is one of the greatest industrial M&A deals of the 20th century. Motion generated roughly $10M of EBITDA in 1976 and is now projected to generate ~$1.15B in FY2025 — a **115x increase over 49 years, a ~10% CAGR** entirely organic-plus-bolt-on.

The modern geographic footprint was built through four transformational deals:
- **2017** — Alliance Automotive Group (AAG), UK-based European auto parts distributor, acquired for ~$2.0B; entered France, UK, Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, Poland
- **2019** — Inenco Group, Australian industrial distributor, acquired (tuck-in to Motion Asia Pacific)
- **2020** — Business Products Group (S.P. Richards / SSE) **divested** for total proceeds ~$400M (FY2022 10-K Note 10) — a strategic simplification
- **2022** — Kaman Distribution Group (KDG), acquired January 3 for $1.31B (FY2022 10-K Business Combinations Footnote) — folded into Motion Industries, adding 220 branches and ~$1.1B of annual revenue
- **2019-2025** — Continuous bolt-on acquisitions of independent NAPA stores; in FY2025 alone GPC completed **"over 50 strategic acquisitions resulting in over 250 additional locations globally"** (FY2025 10-K MD&A)

## PART III · THE 10-YEAR ARC

The story falls into four chapters visible in the filings:

**Chapter 1 (2016-2019): European build + Business Products overhang.** Paul Donahue became CEO in 2016 and did the ~$2.0B Alliance Automotive Group (AAG) deal in October 2017, vaulting GPC to #2 European auto parts distributor behind LKQ. The thesis half-worked: AAG grew revenue but European margins ran 200-300 bps below US Automotive given fragmented country-level banners. Meanwhile the legacy S.P. Richards / Business Products Group was dying from Amazon Business and Staples competition; a 2018 spin-off attempt failed (management took a $12M termination fee), and Q4 2019 brought an **$82M goodwill impairment** on BPG (FY2019 10-K non-GAAP reconciliation). FY2019: revenue $19.4B, adjusted EPS $5.69, OCF $892M, dividend $3.05.

**Chapter 2 (2020-2021): COVID trough, BPG divestiture, recovery.** FY2020 revenue dropped 15% to $16.5B. GPC took a **$507M goodwill impairment** on the International Automotive (AAG Europe) segment — the first crack in the AAG thesis — and a final $192M loss on BPG disposal. The Business Products Group was fully divested by H2 2020. Despite the revenue crater, the board raised the dividend from $3.05 to $3.16 (+3.6%). **This was the moment the streak became institutionalized psychology rather than financial calculation.** FY2021 snapped back to $18.9B revenue with adjusted EPS $6.23 and a conservative +3.2% dividend hike to $3.26.

**Chapter 3 (2022-2023): The KDG acquisition and peak earnings.** January 3, 2022: **Kaman Distribution Group closed for $1.31B** (FY2022 10-K Note 10). KDG added 220 North American Motion branches and ~$1.1B of annual revenue. FY2022 Industrial revenue grew **33%** to $8.4B on the combined effect. **FY2023 was the peak year**: revenue $23.1B, GAAP net income $1.32B, diluted EPS $9.33 (all-time high), OCF $1.44B, FCF $923M, dividend coverage 1.75x. The stock peaked at $166 in November 2023.

**Chapter 4 (2024-2025): The unraveling.** Four things went wrong in sequence:

1. **Consumer pressure on DIY** — high rates, soft consumer, deferred maintenance. NA Automotive comps went flat ex-acquisitions by FY2025.
2. **European stagnation** — Int'l Automotive EBITDA margin fell from ~11% (2023) to 9.3% (2025), a 170 bps compression destroying the AAG thesis.
3. **Restructuring cost overrun** — $221M spent in 2024 + $254M in 2025 = **$475M cumulative** for only $175M of run-rate savings, a 2.7x cost/savings ratio.
4. **Triple whammy of discrete charges in FY2025**: (a) **$742M pension settlement charge** (Dec 19, 2025 termination of US DB plan, $1.5B obligation transferred to insurer); (b) **$151M First Brands credit loss allowance** after that top-10 supplier filed Chapter 11 in September 2025; (c) **$103M asbestos-related product liability remeasurement** on adverse actuarial trends. Combined $996M pre-tax charge.

**GAAP EPS collapsed 93% from $6.47 to $0.47. Adjusted EPS dropped 9.7% from $8.16 to $7.37.** On **September 4, 2025** Elliott filed the Cooperation Agreement. Five months later, the separation was announced.

## PART V · DIVIDEND DEEP DIVE — THE 69-YEAR STREAK

The entire bull case for patient GPC ownership rests on one question: will the streak survive the separation? **Yes, but the post-spin dividend math changes permanently.**

### The streak verified

FY2025 10-K Item 5 ("Dividend Information"): **"We have paid a cash dividend to shareholders every year since going public in 1948 and increased the annual dividend for 69 consecutive years through 2025."** The streak began in 1956, has compounded through ten US recessions including 2008-09 and COVID 2020, and now at 69 years puts GPC alongside Parker Hannifin and P&G in the middle of the Dividend King tier. The peer group with streaks ≥65 years is tiny — AWR (71), DOV (70), PG/NWN/GPC/PH (69), EMR (68), MMM (66), CINF (65) — and every single one of these boards treats the streak as non-negotiable covenant.

### The 2025 hike was the smallest of the modern era

From 2005-2015 the typical hike was 5-7% annually; 2016-2023 averaged 6.2%. The 2024 hike to $4.00 was +5.3%. **The 2025 hike to $4.12 is only +3.0% — the smallest percentage increase of the last 25 years outside the 2020 COVID year.** The board is preserving cash in advance of the restructuring charges, the pension settlement, the First Brands writeoff, and the separation. The 3% hike is a signal that the streak continues but the flexibility is gone.

### Post-separation dividend math

When GPC splits into Global Automotive and Global Industrial in Q1 2027, each shareholder will receive shares in both entities. The existing $4.12 dividend will NOT split 50/50. Historically in industrial spin-offs, the parent dividend gets reallocated based on post-tax EBITDA contribution. On FY2025 segment EBITDA:

| Entity | Adj Segment EBITDA ($M) | % of Total | Implied Initial Annual Div |
|---|---|---|---|
| Global Automotive (NA Auto + Int'l Auto) | 1,216 | 51.5% | ~$2.18 |
| Global Industrial (Motion) | 1,146 | 48.5% | ~$2.06 |
| Combined | 2,362 | 100% | **$4.24** |

The more likely outcome, driven by Elliott's preference for ringfencing Motion as the premium asset, is that **Global Industrial initiates at a lower payout ratio (~35% of EPS) to support multiple expansion** while **Global Automotive initiates at a higher payout ratio (~60%) to attract yield buyers**. Combined initial dividend: roughly $4.00-4.40.

**Can the streak survive the spin?** Technically yes. The parent entity GPC will continue to exist as one of the two split companies (most likely Global Automotive, keeping the NAPA brand and 1928 incorporation); the other will be the "new" spin-co. For screening purposes, the parent GPC keeps the streak only if its standalone post-spin dividend is ≥ its pre-spin dividend. I expect management to mechanically thread this needle: parent dividend ~$2.50 annualized, spin-co dividend ~$1.50, combined ≥ prior $4.12.

### Stress test: can the dividend survive another 2020-style recession?

The real question is whether the current $4.12 (implied $4.24 in 2026) can be paid through a real recession. Let me model a -15% revenue scenario (matching FY2020):

| Metric | FY2025 Actual | -15% Rev Scenario |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 24.3 | 20.7 |
| Adj EBITDA @ -200 bps margin | 2.01 (8.3%) | 1.31 (6.3%) |
| Interest | 164 | 170 |
| D&A | 538 | 520 |
| EBIT | 1,310 | 620 |
| Taxes @ 24% | 314 | 149 |
| Adj Net Income | 996 | 471 |
| Capex | 470 | 350 (cut) |
| OCF | 891 | 550 (est) |
| FCF | 421 | 200 |
| Dividend need | 564 | 564 |
| **FCF Coverage** | **0.75x** | **0.35x** |

In a real recession, FCF would cover only 35% of the dividend. Management would have to choose between cutting capex to ~$200M (maintenance only, destroying the supply chain modernization program), drawing on the revolver aggressively (currently $1.1B available at year-end 2025), or cutting the dividend. History suggests they cut capex and draw the revolver first. The revolver was expanded from $1.5B to $2.0B on March 20, 2025 (FY2025 10-K Credit Facility section) — **this is not coincidental timing; management was preparing the balance sheet liquidity to defend the dividend through the restructuring/separation period**.

The streak is safer than it looks **because management has explicitly prioritized it over capital investment**, but that prioritization comes at a cost: capex-as-a-percentage-of-revenue has fallen from 2.4% in 2023 to 1.9% in 2025, and is likely to stay compressed through 2027. In a distribution business that requires continuous technology and DC investment, underinvesting for three years is a real competitive disadvantage versus O'Reilly (capex ~5% of revenue), AutoZone (~3.5%), and Fastenal (~2.2%).

**Dividend sustainability verdict:** 6/10. Safer than the market thinks on a 12-month view, but structurally at risk if the 2024-2025 cyclical trough turns out to be a secular plateau.

## PART VI · SEGMENT DEEP DIVE — NAPA vs MOTION

### North America Automotive: the franchise under pressure

NA Automotive (39% of revenue, $9.5B in 2025) runs a two-tier model: 2,471 company-owned stores + 4,317 independently-owned stores supported by distribution centers. The DIFM/DIY split is ~80/20, better than Advance Auto Parts and comparable to O'Reilly. Margins are compressing for four reasons: (1) labor/healthcare/rent inflation cited explicitly in FY2025 10-K MD&A, (2) O'Reilly's superior Hub-and-Spoke DIFM execution eating share, (3) the First Brands bankruptcy forcing scramble for replacement SKUs at higher cost, and (4) integration overhead from the 50+ FY2025 bolt-on NAPA store deals costing 100-150 bps in the first 12-18 months.

**But NA Automotive is not broken.** DIFM commercial demand is not a channel Amazon disrupts — repair shops need parts now and NAPA's 30-minute delivery density is a genuine moat. The average US vehicle age is 12.6 years and rising, vehicle miles driven is growing, and EVs are still only ~1.5% of the US car parc. My read: **this is a 7-8% EBITDA margin business going forward, not the 9-10% of 2022-2023.** The structural reset is largely done. At $9.5B × 7.5% = ~$712M of EBITDA, it is still a highly cash-generative franchise.

### International Automotive: the problem child

Int'l Automotive (AAG Europe + GPC Asia Pacific Australasia) is 24% of revenue ($5.9B) and 9.3% EBITDA margin. The trajectory is terrible: 11.0% (2023) → 10.2% (2024) → 9.3% (2025) — **170 bps compression in two years and steepening**. The business is structurally complex with seven different European national banners (GROUPAUTO, Precisium, UAN, Alliance Germany, PartsPoint, Hennig, Lausan) plus Repco/NAPA Australasia. Europe is a harder aftermarket than North America: Norway is 80%+ EV, Netherlands 30%+, Germany 15%+; diesel is being regulated out; UK car parc is declining absolute. The **~$506M accumulated goodwill impairment** on Int'l Automotive (FY2025 10-K Note 3) is a reminder AAG has already been written down once in 2020. Another impairment is plausible.

**This is the weakest piece of GPC.** If I'm Elliott, the Global Automotive spin-co announces a portfolio review of AAG within 12 months of the Q1 2027 separation and either sells it to LKQ or to European private equity. Fair value for Int'l Automotive embedded: ~$4-5B EV on 7-8x the $544M EBITDA.

### Industrial (Motion Industries): the crown jewel

Motion is 37% of revenue ($8.9B) and **12.9% EBITDA margin (+30 bps YoY)** — the only segment simultaneously growing margins and generating peer-leading returns. Motion is the **#1 industrial MRO distributor in North America**: 180,000 customers across 900,000 locations, 10M+ replacement parts from 40,000 suppliers, 45% national-account revenue, 755 locations with 24-hour fulfillment standard.

## PART VII · MOAT, CAPITAL ALLOCATION & MANAGEMENT

### Moat: scale, switching costs, brand, distribution density

GPC's moat is 95 years of accumulated distribution density. NAPA has the **broadest parts availability** in North America (>500,000 SKUs, 30 minutes to most metros). Motion has the **broadest industrial MRO catalog** (10M SKUs) and deepest vendor relationships (40K suppliers, 45% from top 50). Amazon Business has been trying to crack industrial MRO for six years and has penetrated <5% of the enterprise market. The moat is narrowing on Automotive (O'Reilly catching up in the US, LKQ in Europe) and stable-to-widening on Industrial (MRO customers are sticky with years-long switching costs).

**Pricing power evidence:** Gross margin expanded 50 bps in FY2025 (36.3% → 36.8%) despite volume headwinds — GPC retained pricing power on sourcing. But SG&A rose 7.6% against revenue of +3.5%, eroding the gross margin gain at the operating line. The ability to raise prices is intact; the ability to hold cost inflation is failing.

**ROIC trend:** ~11.4% in FY2025 (down from ~14% in 2022-2023) but still above WACC of ~7.5%. Motion's standalone ROIC is likely 18-20%; Automotive's is likely 8-9%.

### Capital allocation priority stack

Dividends first, M&A second, capex third, buybacks fourth. This is a classic Dividend King pattern. Buybacks have been the first thing cut — **GPC bought back $0 of stock in FY2025** (vs $150M in 2024 and $261M in 2023), saving $150-250M that went into dividend defense. The 2017-authorized 15M-share repurchase has 7.5M remaining and is effectively frozen.

**Capex intensity trajectory:** 1.5% (2019) → 0.9% (2020 COVID) → 1.1% (2021) → 1.8% (2022) → 2.2% (2023) → 2.4% (2024 peak) → **1.9% (2025, cut)**. The 50 bps cut in 2025 is a deliberate choice to preserve the dividend. Defensible short-term, but a leading indicator of under-investment if sustained beyond 2026 in a business that needs supply-chain modernization to keep pace with O'Reilly's capex of ~5% of revenue.

**M&A track record:** Mixed. AAG (2017, $2B) is roughly break-even — the accumulated $506M impairment suggests GPC overpaid. KDG (2022, $1.3B) is a clear winner — Motion's EBITDA has risen from ~$800M to $1.15B in three years with KDG contributing $300-400M of that. Tuck-in NAPA store acquisitions (~$100-300M annually) are mildly accretive but integration-heavy.

**Capital allocation grade: B-.** Dividends prioritized correctly. M&A batting average ~50%. Buybacks and capex both being sacrificed to defend the dividend is a warning sign.

### Detailed workforce and operational context

GPC's workforce of over 65,000 employees across 17 countries is one of the largest in the industrial distribution sector. The company operates more than 10,800 locations globally, with North America Automotive alone running 6,864 stores (approximately 2,471 company-owned and 4,317 independently-owned). This hybrid ownership model -- unique in the auto parts industry -- creates both advantages and complexity. The independent NAPA stores are not franchisees in the traditional sense; they are independently owned businesses that purchase inventory from GPC's distribution network and operate under the NAPA brand. This model generates lower margins than company-owned stores but requires less capital investment and shifts real estate and labor risk to the independent owners.

Motion Industries operates 755 locations including distribution centers, branches, and service centers. The 180,000+ customer count served across approximately 900,000 locations gives Motion the broadest reach in North American industrial MRO. The 45% national-account revenue share is a critical metric: national accounts are multi-year contracts with Fortune 500 manufacturers that are extremely sticky (typically 95%+ retention) and provide a recurring revenue floor that smooths cyclical volatility.

International Automotive runs seven different European national banners -- GROUPAUTO (France), Precisium (France), UAN (Netherlands), Alliance Germany, PartsPoint (Benelux), Hennig (Germany), and Lausan (Spain) -- plus Repco and NAPA Australasia. This multi-brand complexity is the legacy of the 2017 AAG acquisition and is a persistent source of operating inefficiency. Each banner maintains separate IT systems, supplier relationships, and marketing programs. The planned separation offers an opportunity to rationalize this structure under a single Global Automotive entity, though the $506M accumulated goodwill impairment on International Automotive (FY2025 10-K Note 3) is a warning that the platform may require further write-downs.

The 1954 federal consent decree restricting certain NAPA practices (mentioned in the FY2025 10-K Item 1) is a notable historical artifact. This Detroit district court order prohibits exclusive manufacturer agreements, price fixing, and territorial allocation among former NAPA members. While it has not been materially constraining in the modern era, it represents a legal overhang that any acquirer of Global Automotive would need to evaluate.

## PART VIII · RISKS — WHERE THIS GOES WRONG

**Risk 1: EV transition accelerates post-2030 (Medium probability, Medium severity).** EVs have 30-50% fewer moving parts. Real risk but slow-motion: US car parc 289M vehicles end-of-2025, EVs ~1.5%. Even at 50% new-sales penetration by 2030, the installed ICE base stays >200M through 2040 — a 15-year tailwind for NAPA. European exposure is worse, which is part of why Int'l Automotive margins are compressing. GPC has been stocking EV parts since 2020; Motion is actively selling into EV battery manufacturing plants (FY2025 10-K Item 1).

**Risk 2: Separation dis-synergies exceed guidance (Medium-high probability, Medium severity).** Standing up two standalone companies typically costs 100-200 bps of combined EBITDA margin for 2-3 years. The FY2025 10-K Item 1A separation risk factor discloses separation costs "expected to be significant." This is the biggest single risk to the IRR of the separation thesis. Base case dis-synergies: $200M annualized. Bear case: $500M.

**Risk 3: Recession / PMI contraction deepens in 2026 (Medium probability, High severity).** Industrial PMI has been sub-50 for most of 2024-2025. A real manufacturing recession in 2026 could hit Motion EBITDA 10-15% and turn Auto comps -3% to -5%. Probability ~30%. Fed rate cuts in 2026 would help, but consumer balance sheets remain stressed.

**Risk 4: Asbestos liability escalation (Low probability, Medium severity).** The FY2025 10-K CAM (Critical Audit Matter) by Ernst & Young calls out the $317M asbestos-related product liability. The $103M FY2025 remeasurement came from adverse actuarial trends. Another $100-200M charge is possible but would not cut the dividend.

**Risk 5: Credit downgrade to BB+/BB (Low-medium probability, High severity).** S&P BBB- Negative, Moody's Baa1 Negative, Fitch BBB- Stable. **S&P is already at the bottom of investment grade.** A downgrade would force selling from IG-only mandates, widen spreads 150-250 bps on the $4.8B debt stack, and push interest expense from $164M to $230-280M. The separation should actually help ratings over the medium term (Motion alone deserves a stronger rating than the combined entity); the risk is the 12-month transition period.

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

What does the market think that I disagree with?

**Market view #1: "The 2025 guidance miss means the business is structurally broken."**

*My view: The FY2025 guidance miss was driven 70% by one-time items (pension, First Brands, asbestos) and 30% by genuine cyclical pressure. Adjusted EPS of $7.37 is only 10% below 2024's $8.16, and 20% below the 2023 peak of $9.33. This is a cyclical trough, not a structural break. The Q3 2025 segment EBITDA acceleration (Auto +5.9%, Industrial +6.6%) suggests the trough is behind us.*

**Market view #2: "The EV transition will kill NAPA over 10 years."**

*My view: The EV transition will compress but not destroy NAPA. The installed base of ICE vehicles in the US is ~290M today and will be >200M in 2040 even at aggressive EV adoption assumptions. DIFM commercial repair is not going to zero — it's going to grow from $55B today to $70B in 2035 per IMR Market Research estimates. NAPA's share of DIFM is ~25%. The math says NAPA's DIFM revenue grows modestly through 2035.*

**Market view #3: "The separation is a Hail Mary by an activist who doesn't understand the business."**

*My view: Elliott's track record on structural transactions is one of the best in activist investing. Marathon Petroleum / Speedway created $20B+ of shareholder value. Arconic separation from Howmet created multi-hundred percent returns. The Motion / Automotive separation is structurally similar — a high-multiple business trapped inside a low-multiple conglomerate. Elliott is not winging this; they've run this playbook four or five times successfully.*

**Market view #4: "GPC's dividend is safe because of the 69-year streak."**

*My view: The dividend is safer than it looks **because management has explicitly prioritized it over investment**, but that is not the same as "safe because the streak is sacred." The 2025 FCF coverage of 0.75x is a genuine warning. Another 15% revenue decline would force hard choices. The streak survives on willpower and revolver capacity, not on operating cash flow.*

**Market view #5: "Motion Industries is just another industrial distributor."**

*My view: Motion is the #1 industrial MRO distributor in North America, period. It has 10M SKUs, 180K customers, 40K suppliers, and 45% national-account revenue. The only reason it doesn't trade at Fastenal multiples is the conglomerate discount. Remove that and the business prints 18-20% ROIC with 12-13% EBITDA margins and mid-single-digit organic growth. That's a $20B+ standalone business.*

## PART XI · VERDICT

**Final verdict: BUY · Target weight 3% · Fair value $142 base / $168 bull / $115 bear**
