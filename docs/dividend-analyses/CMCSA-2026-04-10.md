## TL;DR

Comcast at $28 is the single most mispriced large-cap dividend grower in the Communication Services sector. The stock trades at roughly 5.2x 2025 Adjusted EBITDA of $37.4B and ~5.2x trailing diluted EPS of $5.39. The market is treating Comcast as if cord-cutting will annihilate the entire business, when in reality the Connectivity & Platforms segment alone -- $81B in revenue, $32.4B in EBITDA, 40% margins -- is a quasi-regulated broadband toll-road generating more free cash flow than 90% of the S&P 500. The Versant spinoff completed January 2, 2026 surgically removes the lowest-growth cable networks (USA, CNBC, MSNBC, SYFY, etc.) and leaves RemainCo with NBC broadcast, Peacock (44M paid subs, closing on breakeven), Universal Studios, and the theme parks that just opened Epic Universe in Orlando. The 17-year dividend streak is bulletproof: FY2025 FCF of $21.9B covers the $4.9B dividend payout 4.5x. Annualized dividend of $1.32/share yields ~4.7% at $28.11, with a payout ratio under 25% of net income. This is a generational value setup disguised as a legacy media problem.

## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: STRONG BUY below $30. Accumulate aggressively below $28. -- Dividend Safety: 9.5/10 -- Confidence: Very High**

The disconnect between Comcast's intrinsic value and its market price is extraordinary. FMP's DCF model spits out $341.87 -- I think that is too generous because it capitalizes current earnings growth rates into perpetuity -- but even a conservative sum-of-the-parts yields $50-65, implying 80-130% upside from current levels. The sell-side consensus target is $32.19 (median $31.50, range $23-$40), which itself looks timid. The stock has traded between $24.13 and $34.36 over the past 52 weeks.

Here is what the market is missing:

First, post-Versant Comcast is a structurally different company. The cable network portfolio that was the biggest drag on sentiment -- the USAs and MSNBCs that hemorrhaged viewers and advertising dollars -- is gone. What remains is: (a) the broadband/wireless connectivity franchise, which is functionally a utility with 31.3M domestic broadband subscribers and 47.6% penetration of 65.0M homes passed; (b) NBC broadcast + Peacock, where paid subs grew from 36M to 44M in 2025 and the loss gap is narrowing ($5.4B revenue vs $6.5B costs, improving from $4.9B/$6.7B); (c) Universal Studios, which delivered 1.7% revenue growth even without a mega-hit theatrical slate; and (d) Theme Parks, where Epic Universe in Orlando opened May 2025 and drove 14.2% revenue growth to $9.8B with $3.1B in EBITDA. This is not your father's cable company.

Second, the capital return machine is ferocious and accelerating. In 2025, Comcast returned $11.7B to shareholders: $6.8B in buybacks (205M shares retired, reducing the float from 3.9B to 3.6B shares -- a 5% reduction in a single year) plus $4.9B in dividends. The January 2025 raise to $1.32/share annualized was the 17th consecutive increase. Over the past five years, Comcast has repurchased approximately 1.1 billion shares -- nearly a quarter of the shares outstanding at the start of the period. This is genuine per-share value creation, not stock-comp offset.

Third, the balance sheet is battle-tested but not overleveraged. Total debt of $110.4B against $9.5B cash looks alarming on paper, but net debt/EBITDA of ~2.7x is entirely manageable for a business with $33.6B of operating cash flow and $21.9B of free cash flow. The weighted-average maturity of the debt portfolio is long (Comcast has notes extending to 2055), and the May 2025 issuance at 5.51% weighted-average rate is the marginal cost of debt. Rating agencies have Comcast at A-/A3 -- investment-grade with comfortable headroom.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**
1. **Broadband is a toll road, not a media business.** 31.3M domestic broadband customers generate $25.8B in annual revenue at ~38% EBITDA margins with minimal churn risk -- these are structurally recurring subscribers who need internet regardless of whether they watch linear TV. The 711K net broadband losses in 2025 are manageable: 65M homes passed at 47.6% penetration means Comcast still has ~34M untapped passings. DOCSIS 4.0 deployment in select markets enables multigigabit symmetrical speeds over existing HFC plant, extending the competitive life of the network without full fiber overbuilds.
2. **Capital return yield is ~11.5% and growing.** $11.7B returned on a $102B market cap = 11.5% total shareholder yield. The buyback alone is retiring 5%+ of the float annually. At $28/share, every dollar of buyback retires more shares, creating a virtuous accretion cycle. The payout ratio on dividends is under 25% of net income, leaving enormous room for continued raises.
3. **Peacock is inflecting toward profitability.** The loss narrowed from $1.8B ($6.7B costs minus $4.9B revenue) in 2024 to $1.1B ($6.5B costs minus $5.4B revenue) in 2025. 44M paid subscribers represents meaningful scale. Management is scaling back content spend relative to revenue growth. Breakeven is plausible by 2027. Once Peacock stops being a drag, the Media segment's $3.2B EBITDA becomes a floor, not a peak.

**Top 3 reasons to be cautious:**
1. **Broadband subscriber losses are real and accelerating.** 711K net domestic broadband losses in 2025 vs 411K in 2024. Fixed wireless access (T-Mobile, Verizon) and fiber overbuilders (AT&T, Frontier) are chipping away at the base. If losses accelerate to 1M+ annually, ARPU increases cannot fully offset the volume decline, and the broadband revenue growth engine stalls.
2. **$110B gross debt is a structural constraint.** While leverage is manageable at current cash flow levels, interest expense of $4.4B in 2025 consumes roughly 20% of Adjusted EBITDA. A ratings downgrade would increase refinancing costs and could restrict capital return flexibility. The dual-class share structure gives Brian Roberts (CEO, controlling Class B shareholder with 33.3% voting power) unchecked governance control -- minority shareholders have no mechanism to force capital allocation discipline.
3. **Theme Parks concentration risk from Epic Universe.** The $9.8B theme parks segment now represents a meaningful earnings contributor ($3.1B EBITDA), but the heavy capex behind Epic Universe (significant spending through 2025, plus a UK park projected for 2031) creates execution risk. If attendance disappoints, the capex-heavy profile becomes a drag rather than a growth driver.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- **Post-Versant RemainCo financials clarity** (Q1 2026 earnings will be the first clean quarter without cable network revenues/costs -- market should re-rate the multiple once the segment mix is visible)
- **Epic Universe ramp** (first full year of operations in 2026 -- attendance and per-cap spending data will be closely watched)
- **Peacock path to breakeven** (management has not given a specific date, but the trajectory suggests 2027; any acceleration would be a positive catalyst)
- **Dividend raise in January 2027** (near-certain -- 18th consecutive increase)
- **Continued aggressive buyback** (if management maintains the ~$7B annual pace, shares outstanding will decline below 3.5B by year-end 2026)
- **DOCSIS 4.0 deployment milestones** (competitive response to fiber overbuilders -- material for broadband narrative)

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

## PART III -- HISTORICAL REVIEW: 10-YEAR STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 -- Cable Empire Building (2016-2018).** Comcast entered this period as the dominant US cable operator, having acquired NBCUniversal in 2011. The strategic focus was expanding the HFC network footprint, upgrading speeds (DOCSIS 3.1 rollout), and bundling video/broadband/voice. Revenue grew from ~$80B (2016) to ~$95B (2018). The transformational event was the September 2018 acquisition of Sky plc for approximately $39B -- the largest cross-border media deal in history at that point. Sky brought 23M customers in the UK, Ireland, Germany, Austria, Italy, and Spain, plus premium sports rights (English Premier League, Serie A, Formula One). The deal was expensive and leveraged-up the balance sheet, but it gave Comcast a genuine international platform and diversified the business away from US cord-cutting dynamics. Brian Roberts personally led the bidding war against Fox/Disney, eventually paying GBP 17.28/share -- a significant premium over 21CF's prior offer. The market hated the price at the time. In hindsight, Sky's broadband business in the UK became a meaningful growth driver while the German and Austrian operations were eventually wound down.

**Phase 2 -- COVID Disruption and Peacock Launch (2019-2020).** Revenue fell from ~$109B to ~$103.6B in 2020 as theme parks closed, theatrical releases were delayed, and advertising dried up. Management responded by launching Peacock in July 2020 -- significantly later than Disney+ (November 2019) and HBO Max (May 2020). The late launch meant Peacock had to fight for subscriber attention in an increasingly crowded field. But Comcast made a critical strategic decision: Peacock would be tightly integrated into the Xfinity ecosystem, offered free or at a discount to broadband subscribers. This reduced churn, increased broadband stickiness, and created a flywheel -- even if Peacock as a standalone P&L bled cash, it functioned as a retention tool for the $26B broadband franchise. Free cash flow remained resilient: $13.1B in 2020, only modestly below the $13.3B of 2019.

**Phase 3 -- The Streaming Wars and Broadband Peak (2021-2023).** This was the critical inflection period. Domestic broadband subscribers peaked in 2021 at approximately 32.2M and began a structural decline as fixed wireless access (FWA) from T-Mobile and Verizon offered a "good enough" alternative in many markets, and AT&T/Frontier accelerated fiber overbuilds into Comcast territory. Net broadband losses began in 2022 and accelerated each year. Simultaneously, video subscriber losses continued at 1.3-1.6M per year as cord-cutting became a cultural norm. The market narrative shifted from "Comcast is a broadband monopoly" to "Comcast is losing subscribers to wireless and fiber." Revenue stagnated at $121-$124B. However -- and this is the crucial detail the market missed -- ARPU increases fully offset subscriber losses in broadband, and the video losses were actually margin-accretive because video is a low-margin pass-through business (programming costs decline roughly in proportion to subscriber losses). EBITDA held in the $37-$39B range throughout. Free cash flow improved from $13.0B to $15.5B as capex discipline tightened.

**Phase 4 -- Simplification and Capital Return Acceleration (2024-2025).** Management recognized that conglomerate complexity was destroying equity value. In November 2024, they announced the Versant spinoff -- USA Network, MSNBC, CNBC, SYFY, E!, Oxygen, Golf Channel, Fandango, Rotten Tomatoes, GolfNow, and SportsEngine would be spun into an independent publicly traded company. The separation completed January 2, 2026. This was a ruthlessly rational move: these cable networks were structurally declining businesses facing secular viewership erosion, but they were generating enough cash flow to be viable as a standalone entity. Removing them from Comcast eliminates the "media discount" that had compressed the entire conglomerate's multiple.

Simultaneously, Comcast sold its interest in Hulu for a $9.4B pre-tax gain in June 2025 (having originally been forced to sell to Disney under a 2019 put/call agreement). And the buyback machine went into overdrive: 205M shares in 2025 (212M in 2024), $6.8B in 2025 ($8.6B in 2024). The share count dropped from 3.91B (weighted average diluted, 2024) to 3.71B (2025) -- a 5.1% reduction. Combined with the January 2025 dividend raise to $1.32/share (from $1.24), total capital return was $11.7B against a $102B market cap.

## PART VII -- COMPETITIVE POSITIONING AND MOAT ANALYSIS

Comcast's moat is layered and durable, but not invulnerable:

**Layer 1: Physical infrastructure.** The domestic HFC network passes 65.0M homes and businesses. Building a competing wireline network requires billions in capex and years of construction. This is the most durable moat -- no competitor can replicate it quickly. The network supports DOCSIS 3.1 (gigabit-plus speeds across nearly the entire footprint) and is being selectively upgraded to DOCSIS 4.0 (multigigabit symmetrical) and fiber-to-the-premises. The $8.7B in annual Connectivity & Platforms capex is the cost of maintaining and extending this moat.

**Layer 2: Customer stickiness and bundling.** The Xfinity ecosystem bundles broadband, wireless, video, home security, and streaming (Peacock, Disney+, Netflix integrations) into a single relationship. Multi-product customers churn at significantly lower rates than single-product subscribers. The addition of Xfinity Mobile (9.3M lines, growing 1.5M/year) strengthens the bundle -- offering a free wireless line to broadband subscribers is a margin sacrifice that reduces churn.

**Layer 3: Content rights.** Post-Versant, Comcast retains NBC broadcast (200+ affiliates reaching virtually all US households), Telemundo (96% of US Hispanic households), and a portfolio of long-dated sports rights: NFL (through 2033-34), NBA/WNBA (through 2035-36), Summer and Winter Olympics (through 2032), English Premier League, PGA Tour, Big Ten, Formula One, and Serie A. These rights are the economic moat for Peacock and the advertising business. The NFL and NBA contracts alone are worth billions in annual ad revenue.

**Layer 4: Theme parks as experiential brand.** Universal theme parks are effectively un-replicable physical assets. Epic Universe in Orlando -- the largest park expansion in Universal's history -- adds capacity, pricing power, and a new reason-to-visit in a market where Disney has been raising prices aggressively. The UK park (projected 2031) extends the geographic footprint.

**Vulnerabilities:**
- Fixed wireless access from T-Mobile and Verizon is a real competitive threat in mid-tier broadband markets. FWA offers 200-400 Mbps at competitive prices without truck rolls or installation appointments. Comcast's response (higher speeds, bundling, free wireless lines) is rational but costly.
- Fiber overbuilds by AT&T Fiber and Frontier (post-bankruptcy) are accelerating into Comcast's footprint. Fiber offers symmetrical speeds that HFC cannot fully match without DOCSIS 4.0 upgrades. Comcast is responding by extending fiber-to-the-premises selectively and upgrading the HFC backbone, but this is an ongoing capex commitment.
- Federally subsidized broadband (BEAD program and successors) may enable competitors to build in areas where Comcast had monopoly or duopoly status. The ACP (Affordable Connectivity Program) expiration in 2024 also reduced broadband adoption among lower-income households.

## PART VIII -- MANAGEMENT AND GOVERNANCE

**Brian L. Roberts** (Chairman and CEO) is Comcast. He has led the company since 2002 and controls approximately 33.3% of voting power through Class B common stock despite owning a much smaller economic interest. This dual-class structure is the single biggest governance risk for minority shareholders -- Roberts cannot be removed by a shareholder vote, and his strategic decisions (the Sky acquisition, the NBC Universal deal, the Versant spinoff) are functionally unilateral.

That said, Roberts's track record is mixed but ultimately positive for shareholders. The NBCUniversal acquisition in 2011 (~$30B including assumed debt) was initially controversial but created enormous value -- Universal Studios and the theme parks are now worth more than the entire purchase price. The Sky acquisition at GBP 17.28/share was widely criticized as overpaying, and the subsequent impairment of Sky goodwill in 2022 confirmed that the premium was too high in an absolute sense -- but Sky's broadband business in the UK has performed well, and the overall Connectivity & Platforms international segment has been a stable contributor. The Versant spinoff decision was strategically correct and well-timed.

Roberts's compensation is substantial but performance-linked. The family's economic alignment with Class A shareholders is real -- the Roberts family's wealth is overwhelmingly tied to Comcast stock. This is a benevolent dictatorship, not an agency-cost disaster. But the lack of shareholder recourse is a permanent discount that the market applies, and should apply, to the governance structure.

The executive team includes Mike Cavanagh (President) and a deep bench of segment-level operators. Succession planning for Roberts is the elephant in the room -- he is 66 and has no publicly designated successor. The market will eventually need to price in a post-Roberts Comcast, and the uncertainty around that transition is a long-term governance overhang.

## PART IX -- VERSANT SPINOFF: IMPACT ON REMANCO

The January 2, 2026 separation of Versant Media Group (VSNT) is the most important structural change in Comcast's history since the NBCUniversal acquisition. Each Comcast shareholder received 1 share of Versant for every 25 shares of Comcast owned as of December 16, 2025.

What Versant took:
- Cable networks: MS NOW (formerly MSNBC), CNBC, USA Network, SYFY, E!, Oxygen, Golf Channel, CNBC World, CNBC International
- Digital assets: Fandango, Rotten Tomatoes, GolfNow, SportsEngine
- Associated sports rights: all NASCAR television rights, WWE Smackdown television rights

What remains at Comcast (RemainCo):
- All broadband, wireless, video, and voice (Xfinity, Sky, NOW)
- NBC broadcast network + 11 owned stations
- Telemundo broadcast network + 30 owned stations
- Peacock streaming service
- Bravo, Universo, NBC Sports Network (launched Nov 2025)
- Regional sports networks (~9M households)
- Sky Sports (UK, Italy)
- Universal Studios (film and television production)
- Universal Theme Parks (Orlando, Hollywood, Osaka, Beijing + UK in development)
- All international Sky operations (UK, Italy broadband/wireless/video)

**The dividend math does not change.** The Versant businesses were part of the Media segment and contributed approximately $2-3B of the segment's $27.1B revenue and probably $400-600M of its $3.2B EBITDA (these are cable networks in secular decline -- viewership and advertising were falling). Comcast's FCF coverage of the dividend is so overwhelming (4.5x on reported, 3.3x normalized) that losing $400-600M of EBITDA has zero impact on dividend safety. Management has not announced any change to the $1.32/share dividend rate post-separation.

The more important effect is on the multiple. The market should value post-Versant Comcast at a higher multiple because: (a) the fastest-declining businesses are gone; (b) the remaining Media segment is higher-quality (NBC broadcast, Peacock with growth optionality, Sky Sports); and (c) the revenue mix tilts further toward the high-margin, high-visibility Connectivity & Platforms segment.

## PART XI -- RISK FACTORS

**Risk 1: Broadband subscriber erosion accelerates beyond ARPU offset (PROBABILITY: MEDIUM).** The 711K net domestic broadband loss in 2025 (up from 411K in 2024) is the trend that matters most. If losses accelerate to 1M+ annually -- plausible if FWA improves and fiber overbuilds penetrate deeper into Comcast territory -- then domestic broadband revenue ($25.8B in 2025) could begin declining even with ARPU increases. At 47.6% penetration of 65M passings, the math still works: Comcast can lose 1M subs/year for a decade and still have 20M+ broadband customers. But the equity narrative would shift from "stable utility" to "secular decliner," and the multiple would compress further. **Mitigant:** DOCSIS 4.0, fiber-to-the-premises extensions, free wireless line bundling, and the NOW prepaid tier all expand the addressable market and reduce churn.

**Risk 2: Peacock losses persist or widen (PROBABILITY: LOW-MEDIUM).** Peacock generated $5.4B revenue against $6.5B costs in 2025 -- a $1.1B loss. If subscriber growth stalls and content costs escalate (the NBA deal starting Q4 2025 is expensive), Peacock could remain a cash drain for years. Management has not committed to a specific breakeven date. **Mitigant:** The loss trend is clearly improving ($1.8B in 2024 to $1.1B in 2025). The 44M paid sub base provides scale for advertising revenue growth. The integration with Xfinity gives Peacock a distribution advantage that pure-play streamers lack.

**Risk 3: Dual-class governance and Roberts succession (PROBABILITY: LOW but HIGH IMPACT).** Brian Roberts's unchecked control means minority shareholders cannot influence capital allocation, strategic direction, or management succession. If Roberts makes a large, value-destructive acquisition (as some argue Sky was), there is no shareholder recourse. The succession question -- who leads Comcast after Roberts? -- is entirely at his discretion. **Mitigant:** Roberts's economic alignment through Comcast stock ownership and his historical track record (more value creation than destruction over 24 years as CEO) reduce but do not eliminate this risk.

**Risk 4: Regulatory and political risk.** Net neutrality, broadband competition mandates, local franchise regulations, and potential antitrust scrutiny of the broadband + content combination are ongoing regulatory overhangs. The international operations face regulatory price controls on network access in the UK and Italy. **Mitigant:** Comcast has navigated regulatory changes for decades; the diversified geographic and business-line exposure limits concentration risk.
