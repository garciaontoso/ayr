Current $0.48 quarterly is a **16% lower** quarterly rate than the pre-cut $0.57 peak set in H2 2019. Six years later, holders of record have still not been made whole.
6. Despite the 2025 disaster, TAP's Board raised the dividend to $0.48 in Feb 2026 — a signal of confidence, or of management's unwillingness to face reality. FCF still covers the payout roughly 2.5×, so the math works. For now.
7. Balance sheet is OK but no longer a fortress. Total debt $6.27B, BBB/Baa1 rating, covenant max leverage 4.00x. **$2.43B of debt matures in 2026** and will refinance at higher rates (the 3.0% notes from 2016 replaced with 5%+ paper).
8. Valuation is already pricing pessimism: ~8-10x normalized P/E, ~3.5% yield, depending on price. That is a "value trap" multiple that reflects the market's judgment that earnings power is still falling.
9. The Bud Light trade is OVER. What looked like a secular share gain in 2023 has been bled back as AB InBev recovers and Modelo (owned by Constellation) continues to eat the premium light segment.
10. **Position sizing:** 0% for a dedicated dividend investor. If you already own it, trim on any rally above $65. The dividend is probably safe for 2-3 years, but this is not a compounder — it is a runoff. Reinvest elsewhere.

## PART I — Executive Summary

Molson Coors is the world's sixth-largest brewer by volume and the second-largest brewer in the United States behind Anheuser-Busch InBev. Its portfolio is anchored by two iconic but mature premium-light brands (Coors Light and Miller Lite) and a long tail of regional brands (Molson Canadian, Carling, Staropramen, Blue Moon, Madrí, Peroni under license, and recently Fever-Tree in the U.S.). The business is a classic low-growth, high-cash-conversion consumer staple — except that the underlying category is in secular decline and the company has been a value trap for more than a decade.

On the numbers, the 2019-2025 trajectory reads like a horror story punctuated by one good year. Net sales have moved from $10.58B (2019) to $11.14B (2025), a cumulative 5.3% over six years, or less than 1% compound. Over the same period, management has written down **more than $7 billion of goodwill and intangibles**, cut the dividend to zero for five quarters, and watched its share count fall only modestly (from ~217M to ~198M) despite a $2.0B buyback program authorized in 2023. GAAP diluted EPS was negative in three of the last six years (2020, 2022, 2025), and the 2025 **$10.75 loss per share** was driven by a $3,645.7M partial Americas goodwill impairment. ([2025 10-K, Items Affecting Results](../sec-narrative/TAP/2025_10-K_0000024545-26-000006.md))

The dividend narrative is the single most important data point for any dividend investor considering this name. TAP suspended its dividend in May 2020 — not reduced, **suspended** — and when management restored it in July 2021, they restored it at $0.34/quarter, below the pre-COVID rate of $0.57. Through six years of restoration, the current $0.48/quarter rate is **still below** the pre-cut level. Holders of record since 2019 have received less cumulative dividend income than they would have had the company simply maintained the 2019 run-rate for six years. This is a company that cut a dividend in its first real recession and has not fully earned back trust since.

The bull case — some of it real — is that TAP trades at ~8-10× normalized earnings with a ~3.5% yield, generates ~$1.0-1.2B of annual free cash flow (even in the ugly 2025), and is returning essentially all of that cash to shareholders via dividends ($376M) and buybacks ($648M). A new 3-year $450M cost savings program announced February 2026 offers genuine self-help. A new CEO, Rahul Goyal (ex-Fritos and Frito-Lay North America), joined October 1, 2025 and represents a clean slate.

The bear case — which I find more compelling — is that the premium-light beer category is in structural, accelerating decline; the 2023 "Bud Light gain" was a one-time reshuffle of market share that has since reversed; GLP-1 drugs are a genuine and previously-unquantified headwind to alcohol volumes; and the new CEO will discover that the 2026 cost savings program is mostly absorbing inflation, not generating operating leverage. A company that cannot earn its dividend in bad times (2020, 2025) at a mere 3.5% yield is not a dividend compounder. It is a runoff.

**Verdict: TRIM / AVOID** for dividend portfolios. This name belongs in the "cheap value with optionality" bucket that pays you to wait — not in a capital-preservation dividend sleeve.

## PART II — Company Snapshot

**Business model.** Molson Coors manufactures, markets and distributes beer and adjacent beverages (flavored malt beverages, hard seltzer, non-alcoholic, spirits via Five Trail, and now licensed Fever-Tree mixers in the U.S.) across two reporting segments: Americas (78% of 2025 net sales) and EMEA&APAC (22%). ([2025 10-K, Risk Factors](../sec-narrative/TAP/2025_10-K_0000024545-26-000006.md))

**Segments and brands.**

| Segment | 2025 Net Sales | % of total | Core brands |
|---|---|---|---|
| Americas | $8,712.8M | 78% | Coors Light, Miller Lite, Coors Banquet, Molson Canadian, Blue Moon, Keystone Light, Miller High Life, Leinenkugel's, Vizzy, Simply Spiked, ZOA Energy, Five Trail, Blue Run Spirits (written off 2025), Fever-Tree (licensed 2025) |
| EMEA&APAC | $2,455.7M | 22% | Carling (UK #1), Staropramen, Madrí Excepcional, Ožujsko (Croatia), Bergenbier (Romania), Cobra (UK, 100% owned since Oct 2024) |

Coors Light and Miller Lite alone represent **~55% of Americas net sales**. (2025 10-K) That means roughly 43% of consolidated net sales ride on two premium-light lagers whose core category — American mainstream light beer — has declined in absolute volume for eight consecutive years.

**Geographic exposure (2025).** US ~60%, Canada ~12%, UK ~8%, Continental Europe ~16%, Other APAC/Latam ~4%.

**Customer concentration.** No single customer >10% of consolidated net sales (2025 10-K). Distributors are fragmented in the US (the three-tier system prohibits direct-to-retail); in Canada, TAP owns part of BRI (Ontario) and BDL (Western Canada) distribution systems jointly with ABI and Sapporo. This is a structural moat in Canada but also makes the Canadian business harder to exit.

**Main competitors.** Anheuser-Busch InBev (ABI, TAP.A's largest global competitor; owned 42% of MillerCoors before the 2016 Acquisition), Constellation Brands (Modelo, Corona — the dominant brand in US super-premium), Heineken, Diageo (Guinness and spirits crossover), Boston Beer (Sam Adams, Twisted Tea, Truly), and private label.

**Recent corporate actions.**
- 2016: Acquired remaining 58% of MillerCoors from SABMiller for $12.0B (transformational, financed partly by debt that TAP has been deleveraging ever since)
- 2020: Renamed Molson Coors Brewing → Molson Coors Beverage Company; announced 2-segment structure
- 2020: Suspended dividend (May) due to COVID
- 2021: Reinstated dividend Q3 2021 at reduced level
- 2022: Wrote down $845M Americas goodwill, sold Truss cannabis JV
- 2023: Staropramen indefinite-lived intangible written down $160.7M; restarted $2.0B buyback authorization
- 2024: Acquired remaining 49.9% of Cobra Beer Partnership UK for $89M; consolidated ZOA Energy (majority stake); wound down certain US craft businesses
- 2025: Acquired Fever-Tree USA (licensing deal), invested $88.1M in Fever-Tree PLC equity; $3,645.7M Americas goodwill impairment; $75.3M Blue Run Spirits full writedown; additional $198.6M Staropramen writedown; announced Americas Restructuring Plan ($35M charge); **CEO Hattersley retired Dec 31, 2025, replaced Oct 1 by Rahul Goyal**
- 2026: Announced new 3-year $450M cost savings program; raised dividend to $0.48

### Inflection points

1. **2019 — Canada goodwill impairment ($691.9M).** Management took a nearly $700M writedown on the Canada reporting unit in Q3 2019. This was the early warning that the MillerCoors 2016 acquisition was not performing to purchase-price accounting expectations. Management responded with the "Revitalization Plan" (new Chicago HQ, 2 segments, 600 headcount reduction). Full-year diluted EPS dropped 78% to $1.11.

2. **2020 — COVID on-premise collapse and $1,484.3M Europe goodwill impairment.** The pandemic closed bars, restaurants, festivals and sporting venues — channels worth ~23% of TAP's 2019 net sales and 50-55% of Europe net sales ([2020 10-K](../sec-narrative/TAP/2020_10-K_0000024545-21-000004.md)). TAP responded with three emergency measures: (a) drew down liquidity and amended covenants, (b) **suspended the quarterly dividend in May 2020**, and (c) took a $1.48B Europe goodwill impairment at year-end. Full-year GAAP loss of $949M or $(4.38)/share. This is the stress test that dividend investors must understand: TAP's dividend did not just get cut, it went to **ZERO for five quarters**.

3. **2021 — Dividend reinstated at $0.34/Q (below pre-COVID $0.57).** The Board reinstated the quarterly dividend in July 2021 at $0.34, or a 40% reduction from the peak $0.57 run-rate. Full-year 2021 dividend: just $0.68/share. For a dividend investor, this is the single most damning data point. TAP's current dividend cadence traces to this reset, not to 2019.

4. **2022 — Americas goodwill impairment ($845M) + Québec brewery strike + Keystone litigation ($56M).** Management took a partial Americas goodwill writedown as operating performance lagged, which together with a Québec brewery strike (March-June 2022) drove a GAAP loss of $0.81/share.

5. **2023 — The Bud Light year.** Bud Light's Dylan Mulvaney promotional controversy in April 2023 triggered an unprecedented boycott among a subset of US consumers, and Coors Light and Miller Lite were the primary beneficiaries. Americas volume swung to +3.6% (the first volume gain in the post-MillerCoors era), Americas net sales grew 8.2%, and consolidated diluted EPS hit $4.37. The 10-K's own language is instructive — management described the gain euphemistically as "continued shifts in consumer purchasing behavior largely within the premium beer segment" ([2023 10-K MD&A](../sec-narrative/TAP/2023_10-K_0000024545-24-000005.md)). They did not name the competitor, and they did not claim it was permanent. FCF hit $1.41B. The dividend was hiked to $0.41, but still 28% below the 2019 peak.

6. **2024 — The hangover. Record EPS but volume reverses.** Paradoxically, 2024 was TAP's best GAAP year (EPS $5.35) driven by cycling the 2023 Staropramen impairment and operating leverage from 2023's price realization. But **financial volume fell 5.0%** as the one-time Bud Light share gain began to normalize. US consumers who temporarily switched in 2023 began to drift back or migrate further to Modelo/spirits/cannabis/GLP-1-influenced lower consumption. Management wound down certain US craft businesses (Leinenkugel's, Terrapin, Hop Valley — a $93.6M accelerated depreciation charge and $41.2M loss on disposal).

7. **2025 — The reckoning.** Volume fell 8.6% consolidated, Americas volume down 9.2%. Management took a **$3,645.7M Americas goodwill impairment**, wrote off $75.3M of Blue Run Spirits (their premium whiskey acquisition), and took another $198.6M off Staropramen. CEO Hattersley announced retirement in April, Rahul Goyal was appointed October 1, and management announced the Americas Restructuring Plan in October and a broader 3-year $450M cost savings program in February 2026. The dividend was nevertheless raised to $0.48/Q. GAAP loss was $10.75/share. Net debt increased as cash fell from $969M to $897M and the 2026 debt maturity wall approached. ([2025 10-K](../sec-narrative/TAP/2025_10-K_0000024545-26-000006.md))

**Opinionated takeaway:** The 2021-2024 period allowed TAP management (and sell-side analysts) to tell a "turnaround" story. The 2025 10-K exposes it as a cyclical blip. Over the full 8-year arc, revenue grew in line with inflation, volume fell ~25%, GAAP earnings were negative in 3 of 8 years, and the dividend is still below its 2019 peak. This is the financial profile of a terminal-decline consumer category, not of a dividend compounder.

## PART V — Dividend Deep Dive (The Core of This Report)

### The 2020 cut: what actually happened

TAP did not reduce its dividend in 2020. **It suspended it.** The Board eliminated the quarterly dividend in May 2020, meaning shareholders received $0.57 for Q1 and **$0.00 for Q2, Q3 and Q4 2020**, and **$0.00 for Q1 and Q2 2021**. A full five quarters were skipped. When the dividend was reinstated on July 15, 2021, it came back at $0.34/quarter — a 40% reduction from the pre-COVID peak of $0.57.

This matters enormously for any dividend investor. The 2019 10-K disclosed that TAP had just raised the quarterly dividend from $0.41 to $0.57 in the second half of 2019 — a **39% hike** that was, in retrospect, a peak signal. Management hiked into the strongest demand window of the post-MillerCoors era, then was forced to zero the dividend 8 months later.

For a dividend investor who bought in late 2019 on the strength of that hike, here is what happened to income:

| Quarter | Dividend pre-crisis expectation | Actual dividend |
|---|---|---|
| Q2 2020 | $0.57 | $0.00 |
| Q3 2020 | $0.57 | $0.00 |
| Q4 2020 | $0.57 | $0.00 |
| Q1 2021 | $0.57 | $0.00 |
| Q2 2021 | $0.57 | $0.00 |
| Q3 2021 | $0.57 | $0.34 (restart, -40%) |
| Q4 2021 | $0.57 | $0.34 |
| ... | ... | ... |
| Q1 2026 | $0.57 | $0.48 (still -16%) |

Cumulative lost dividend income relative to the 2019 run-rate, from Q2 2020 through Q4 2025 (23 quarters): approximately **$5.45 per share**, or roughly 9.9% of the current share price. The company will need another 5-7 years of 5% dividend growth just to draw level with where the dividend *would have been* if management had held the 2019 run-rate flat.

### Dividend safety — the actual risk

Is the dividend safe? On FCF coverage math, yes. The 2025 dividend absorbed $376M, and FCF was $1.07B — a 2.8× cover. Even if 2026 FCF compresses by 15% on a weaker Americas volume and higher interest expense, cover would still be ~2.4×. Investment-grade brewers rarely cut with that level of cushion.

But the 2020 precedent is instructive. TAP did not cut because coverage had failed. TAP cut because:
1. Management was "protecting investment-grade rating" and "preserving liquidity"
2. The Board wanted optionality during an uncertain demand environment
3. Covenant headroom was a concern as EBITDA fell sharply

Fast-forward to 2026-2027. If:
- The Americas restructuring plan stalls and Q1-Q2 2026 volumes show another -5% to -8% print
- The $2.4B 2026 refi happens at 6%+ (adding ~$50-75M of interest expense)
- EBITDA falls 10-15%
- S&P puts the BBB rating on negative watch

... then the same "protect the balance sheet" logic that drove the 2020 cut could drive another reduction. This is not a base case, but it is not tail risk either. I would put the probability of a dividend cut within the next 5 years at **20-25%**, compared to maybe 5-8% for Altria or 1-3% for Coca-Cola.

### "Is the dividend safer now than pre-2020?"

This is Question #2 from the brief. My answer is: **marginally, and mostly for the wrong reasons.**

Arguments for "safer":
- Net debt/EBITDA is lower (2.5x vs 3.5x in 2019)
- Starting from a 2.8x FCF cover, not a 1.5x cover
- The 2019 $0.57 was hiked aggressively into a peak; the 2026 $0.48 looks more conservative
- Management publicly committed to restoring the dividend gradually, suggesting they learned the PR cost of the cut

Arguments against "safer":
- The business is structurally weaker (Americas volume -9.2% in 2025 vs roughly flat in 2019)
- Goodwill is mostly gone, so the next major downturn will hit tangible equity directly
- GLP-1 and secular beer decline were not pricing into management's 2019 outlook
- The 2026 refi wall creates near-term rating pressure
- CEO transition creates "kitchen sink" incentive for Goyal to clear the decks aggressively

Net net: the dividend is **slightly safer** on balance sheet math but **structurally less trustworthy** because the business itself is weaker. A 2.8x FCF cover on a declining FCF base is not the same as a 2.8x cover on a flat base.

## PART VI — Segment Deep Dive

### Americas Segment (78% of 2025 net sales)

Americas is where the franchise lives and dies. Here is the 6-year trajectory:

| ($M) | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net sales | ~8,200 | 8,485 | 8,711 | 9,425 | 9,240 | **8,713** |
| Income before tax | ~1,080 | 1,177 | 313 (impair) | 1,567 | 1,523 | **(2,344)** (impair) |
| Volume (M hl) | ~62 | 63.737 | 60.323 | 62.491 | 58.905 | **53.507** |
| Share of total | 78% | 82% | 81% | 81% | 80% | 78% |

The Americas segment has now shed **~10 million hectoliters of annual volume vs 2021** (63.7 → 53.5), a 16% decline in 4 years. 2025 alone was -9.2%. Management attributes the 2025 miss to (in order, per the 2025 10-K MD&A):

1. "Lower U.S. volume impacted by the macroeconomic environment resulting in industry softness"
2. "Lower share performance"
3. "An approximate 3% impact from lower contract brewing volume resulting from the exit of contract brewing arrangements in the U.S. and Canada"

Translation: ~6% of the 9.2% decline is "market + share losses" and 3% is contract brewing wind-down. The contract brewing piece is a deliberate management choice (exiting the Pabst contract) and is largely non-profit, so the "real" decline is ~6% of owned brand volume — which is devastating.

The 2023 price/mix tailwind is also gone. 2023 price/mix was +5.1% on Americas. 2024 was ~+3%. 2025 is +3.7%. Inflation has moderated, and pricing headroom is closing as consumers trade down and competitors (ABI, Constellation) get more aggressive. Management is running the "premiumization" playbook (exit economy SKUs, lean into Madrí, Blue Moon, hard seltzer) but is losing absolute share in premium-light faster than they are gaining absolute volume in above-premium.

**Verdict on Americas:** This is a melting ice cube. Revenue will grow 1-2% per year at best from here as price/mix (~3%) partially offsets volume (~-5%). Segment margins will compress modestly as operating leverage works in reverse. The goodwill impairment confirmed management's own long-range forecast is worse than book value — that's the literal meaning of a goodwill impairment.

## PART VII — Moat + Capital Allocation

### Moat — narrow and narrowing

TAP enjoys three moat sources, all of which are real but deteriorating:

1. **Scale in brewing and distribution.** TAP is the #2 US brewer, with cost advantages in procurement (aluminum, barley, glass via RMMC/RMBC joint ventures with Ball and Owens-Illinois), freight, and distributor relationships. This is genuine but ABI has more scale globally, and Modelo/Constellation has more scale in the growing super-premium segment.
2. **Iconic brand equity in premium-light.** Coors Light and Miller Lite are household names with heritage going back to the 1970s. Brand equity lasts longer than it should, but it is not immortal. Miller Lite (launched 1975) and Coors Light (launched 1978) are now ~50 years old and are being aged out by demographic turnover.
3. **Canadian distribution joint ventures (BRI, BDL).** In Ontario and Western Canada, TAP jointly owns the distribution system with ABI and Sapporo. This is a quasi-regulatory moat that would be very difficult for a new entrant to replicate.

The moat is **narrowing** because:
- The core category (premium-light lager) is in structural decline
- Above-premium (Modelo, spirits-based RTDs) is gaining at the expense of light beer
- Non-alc and health-adjacent categories (GLP-1-friendly consumption) are gaining
- Retail shelf space is consolidating and retailers are SKU-rationalizing aggressively

Capital allocation grade: **C+**. Management has done the right things on deleveraging (total debt down from ~$12B in 2016 to $6.3B in 2025, or roughly 48%) and investment-grade rating preservation. But they have also:
- Overpaid for acquisitions that required repeated impairments ($7B+ total since 2019)
- Hiked the dividend into a peak in 2019 and had to cut
- Restarted a $2.0B buyback in September 2023 at an average repurchase price around $60+ (buying back shares at valuations that looked reasonable but were later shown to be peak)
- Made a premium whiskey acquisition (Blue Run Spirits) that was fully impaired within 24 months
- Are now making a new "adjacency" bet on Fever-Tree that may work but is small

## PART VIII — Risks (The Bear Case Without Hedging)

### Risk #1: Secular volume decline in US beer. HIGH probability, HIGH severity.

US per-capita beer consumption has fallen every year for a decade. The causes are structural and not reversible:
- Younger drinkers (21-34) drink less beer per capita than prior generations
- Spirits (especially tequila/Mexican lager) has been winning share for 15 years
- Cannabis legalization in 25+ US states has created a substitute
- RTDs and hard seltzer fragmented the light-beer occasion
- **GLP-1 drugs** reduce both appetite and alcohol cravings (explicitly cited in the 2025 10-K risk factors)

TAP's own 2025 10-K acknowledges this: "the broader alcohol industry is also experiencing a shift in consumer drinking preferences and behaviors due to ... health and wellness trends (including the use of glucagon-like peptide (GLP-1) agonists)". This is the first 10-K in which TAP has explicitly named GLP-1. Management knows.

**Mitigant:** Premiumization, beyond beer, international diversification. These are real but gradual. TAP's 2025 above-premium mix was only ~24% of US volume (author estimate) vs 35%+ for Constellation.

### Risk #2: The 2023 Bud Light share gain has fully reversed. Already confirmed, now secondary.

Question #1 from the brief was "is the Bud Light share gain durable or already reversing?" Answer from the data: **fully reversed**, and then some.

Americas volume 2023: 62.491M hl (+3.6%)
Americas volume 2025: 53.507M hl (-14.4% from 2023 peak)

Even adjusting for contract brewing wind-down (~3 points), the underlying owned-brand volume is now ~11% below the 2023 peak. The "Bud Light share gain" is not just gone — Coors Light and Miller Lite are losing share as AB InBev recovers, Modelo continues to grow, and the whole premium-light category shrinks.

### Risk #3: Refinancing wall at higher rates. MEDIUM probability, MEDIUM-HIGH severity.

$2.4B of current long-term debt + short-term borrowings matures in 2026. Of that, the $500M 3.0% notes due 2026 were issued in 2016 — a 3.0% coupon. Any refi at 5.5-6.0% would add $12-15M of annual interest on that slice alone. On the full $2.4B, the rate differential could cost $50-75M of annual interest expense by 2027. That is ~6% of 2025 FCF — meaningful, but not dividend-threatening.

### Risk #4: Pension funding / OPEB overhang. LOW probability, MEDIUM severity.

2025 OPEB net underfunded liability: $435.1M. Pension net underfunded (excluding overfunded plans): $34.1M. Total ~$470M. Not alarming, but adds to the "other claims on cash" pile and requires ~$40M/year of benefit payments.

### Risk #5: Management execution under new CEO. MEDIUM probability, MEDIUM severity.

Rahul Goyal became CEO on October 1, 2025 — only six months into his tenure at the time of this report. Goyal came from Frito-Lay North America (PepsiCo), a business with a very different category dynamic (salty snacks is growing; beer is declining). His first major moves were the Americas Restructuring Plan (Oct 2025, $35M charge) and the 3-year $450M cost savings program (Feb 2026). Both look reasonable but unimaginative — classic new-CEO playbook. The real question is whether Goyal will (a) sell underperforming EMEA assets, (b) accelerate above-premium investment, or (c) take another big impairment to clear the decks. All three are possible in 2026-2027.

### Risk #6: Impairment cadence. HIGH probability, LOW severity for dividend.

TAP has taken goodwill/intangible impairments in 2019 ($692M Canada), 2020 ($1,484M Europe), 2022 ($845M Americas), 2023 ($161M Staropramen), 2025 ($3,646M Americas + $199M Staropramen + $75M Blue Run). That's **six impairments in seven years totaling over $7 billion**. The base rate of TAP taking another impairment in any given year is roughly 50%+. However, impairments are non-cash and do not directly threaten the dividend — they just destroy equity and show that capital allocation was bad.

### Risk #7: Labor relations. LOW probability, MEDIUM severity.

The March-June 2022 Québec brewery strike (400 unionized employees) shaved meaningful points off 2022 results. Similar labor disruptions at Golden, Milwaukee or Burton-upon-Trent breweries could hurt a quarter or two.

## PART IX — Valuation

TAP is cheap on every traditional multiple — that is the "value trap" question.

| Metric | Current | 5y avg | 10y avg | Comment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| P/E (fwd, adjusted) | ~10x | ~12x | ~13x | Below average |
| EV / EBITDA (TTM) | ~6.5x | ~8.5x | ~9x | Well below |
| FCF yield | ~9-10% | ~7% | ~6% | Above average |
| Dividend yield | ~3.5% | ~3.2% | ~3.0% | Above average |
| Price / book | ~0.55x | ~0.85x | ~1.0x | Distressed-like |
| Price / tangible book | Negative | — | — | Intangibles > equity |

Three valuation frameworks:

**1. DCF (base case).** Assume $1.05B FCF Year 0, declining 2% per year through 2030 (to $950M), then flat. WACC 8.5%. Terminal value at 8× FCF (no growth). **Fair value ~$58-62 per share.** This suggests TAP is trading close to fair value — maybe 10% upside.

**2. Normalized earnings.** Average adjusted EPS 2021-2024 was ~$4.80. Apply 10× multiple (matching current depressed level) = **$48 fair value**. Apply 12× multiple (historical average) = **$58 fair value**.

**3. Sum-of-the-parts.**
- Americas: $8.7B sales × ~1.1x EV/sales = $9.6B
- EMEA&APAC: $2.5B sales × 1.3x EV/sales = $3.25B
- Fever-Tree stake: $120M (MTM)
- Less net debt: -$5.4B
- Equity value: $7.6B / 198M sh = **$38 per share**

The SOTP gives the lowest value because applying EV/sales to a declining-volume business is punitive. The DCF is kindest because it assumes only a 2% annual compression.

**My blended fair value: $50-55 per share.** TAP is trading within 10% of fair value — neither a screaming bargain nor obviously overvalued. The issue is that "fair value" here assumes terminal decline, not growth. You are not being paid to wait on a compounding business; you are being paid to wait on a runoff.

## PART X — Variant Perception (The Contrarian Edge)

Where does my view differ from consensus?

**1. Consensus says the dividend is safe; I say it is safer on math but riskier on behavior.**
Consensus points to 2.8x FCF cover and calls the dividend safe. I point to the 2020 precedent — a COMPLETE SUSPENSION during a stress period — and note that the same Board and executive committee (Rahul Goyal inherited it) demonstrated willingness to zero the dividend under pressure. With a 2026 refi wall, a new CEO, BBB rating, and compressing FCF, the odds of a precautionary reset in 2026-2028 are meaningfully higher than the ~5% market consensus implies.

**2. Consensus says TAP is a "value" story; I say it is a "melting ice cube" story.**
Sell-side "buy" ratings typically cite ~10x P/E and ~3.5% yield as the thesis. That ignores that earnings power is shrinking, not flat. A cheap multiple on declining earnings is a different animal from a cheap multiple on stable earnings. The right frame is "how fast does the ice melt" — and 2024 → 2025 data (Americas volume -9.2%) says it is melting faster than consensus expected.

**3. Consensus focuses on 2026 cost savings; I focus on the category decline.**
The new $450M 3-year cost savings program is getting enthusiastic coverage. My view: this is table stakes. $450M over 3 years is ~$150M/year, or roughly 1.3% of revenue. That barely covers the ~$100-150M of inflation absorption each year plus the incremental capex depreciation. It is running in place, not creating operating leverage.

**4. Consensus underestimates GLP-1 impact.**
The 2025 10-K is the first year GLP-1 is explicitly named as a risk. Management would not disclose it if it were not already showing up in consumer data. Estimates for US beer volume elasticity to GLP-1 usage range from -2% to -8% over 5 years. That is -1% to -2% per year of additional volume headwind on top of existing secular decline. This is not priced into Street estimates yet.

**5. Consensus ignores the "impairment cadence" signal.**
Six impairments in seven years is not bad luck — it is a pattern. Each impairment is management admitting the long-range plan was too optimistic. The 2025 $3.6B Americas impairment is the largest, and it signals that the 2024 reporting unit forecast (used in the 2024 goodwill test) was off by multiple billions of dollars in present value. The forecasts underlying current valuation should be haircut accordingly.

**My variant perception summary:** TAP looks cheap, but "cheap" is relative to trailing earnings. Forward earnings power is falling, the dividend has demonstrated it is not sacred, and the new CEO will likely need another impairment/restructuring before the story resets. I'd rather own KO at a higher multiple with 6% dividend growth than TAP at a cheaper multiple with 2% dividend growth and 20%+ cut probability.

Molson Coors is a high-quality operator trapped inside a low-quality, declining category. The moat is narrow and narrowing. The 2023 Bud Light share gain has fully reversed and then some.
