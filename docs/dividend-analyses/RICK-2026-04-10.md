## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: AVOID. Do NOT add to dividend sleeve. · Dividend Safety: 4/10 · Confidence: High**

RICK is not a dividend story — it never was, and the 48% drawdown from its 52-week high ($47) to today's $24.56 has not changed that. Three legs of the bull case have all weakened materially at the same time:

First, **the capital allocator is gone.** Eric Langan — the founder-operator whose "10% after-tax FCF yield" buyback rule was the entire investment thesis for a generation of small-cap value investors — stepped down as CEO on November 28, 2025, following a September 16, 2025 indictment by the New York Attorney General alleging conspiracy, bribery, criminal tax fraud, and offering a false instrument for filing. Specifically, a New York State Department of Taxation auditor was allegedly "provided complimentary admission to clubs, restaurant meals, private dances and travel expenses in exchange for the reduction of certain sales tax liabilities in connection with the use of 'Dance Dollars'" (FY2025 10-K, line 767). This is the second SEC/AG action against Langan — in September 2020 he settled an SEC administrative proceeding for $615,000 in undisclosed perquisites and personal use of corporate aircraft, paying a $200,000 personal penalty (lines 162-164). Interim CEO Travis Reese is a 25-year lifer and the executive who invented Bombshells in 2013 — a segment that posted -21.3% operating margin in FY2024. Interim CFO Albert Molina was previously Director of Financial Reporting. Neither has Langan's credibility with the small-cap buy-side, neither has run the capital allocation machine, and both titles are "Interim."

Second, **the 10% rule has been deleted from the 10-K.** Compare verbatim. FY2024 10-K (lines 103-108): *"Our capital allocation strategy provides us with disciplined guidelines... We consider buying back our own stock if the after-tax yield on free cash flow is above 10%; We consider acquiring or developing our own clubs or restaurants that we believe have the potential to provide a minimum cash on cash return of 25%-33%."* FY2025 10-K (lines 102-110): *"In December 2024, we launched our five-year Back-to-Basics strategy... For the allocation of our free cash flow, we currently divide it among club acquisitions (investing), share buybacks (financing), and dividends (financing)."* The numerical thresholds, the word "disciplined," and the "25-33% cash on cash" hurdle are all gone. This is not a minor edit; it is the explicit, verifiable, quantitative rule that made RICK a top-decile small-cap allocator from 2016 to 2024 being **removed from the official filing** in the same year management executed the worst buyback of the modern era (see below).

Third, **the underlying business is deteriorating across both segments.** FY2025 revenue fell 5.5% to $279.4M. Nightclubs same-store sales were **-2.1%** for the third consecutive year (FY2025 10-K, line 92); Bombshells same-store sales were **-13.6%** (line 96). Adjusted EBITDA collapsed from $85.0M in FY2023 to $72.6M in FY2024 to $52.6M in FY2025 — a 38% contraction in the metric management itself uses as "the target benchmark for our acquisitions" (lines 1624, 1666). Free cash flow on the maintenance-capex definition fell to $45.4M from $48.4M and $53.2M (line 1950).

**Dividend Safety 4/10** reflects that the dividend itself is trivially covered ($2.5M against $49M OCF), but the entire value proposition depended on management compounding value through buybacks executed below the 10% after-tax yield line. That machine is broken. The dividend yields just 1.3% ($0.32 annual at $24.56, per FY25 10-K line 1938). There is nothing here for the income-focused reader: no DGR of consequence at this base, no payout cushion worth modeling, no capital allocation alpha worth the reputational and legal tail.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **Real-estate-backed downside.** Gross PP&E is $304.8M. Most Nightclubs are owned outright. At a 50% haircut the real estate alone is roughly $150M, or ~$19/share on 8.0M post-buyback shares — a soft floor a short distance below today's price.
2. **Nightclubs segment is a genuinely profitable cash business.** Non-GAAP Nightclubs operating margin was 32.0% in FY2025 on $241.9M of segment revenue generating $77.5M of operating income (FY25 10-K lines 1447, 1458), with 88.6% segment gross margin (line 92) and a permit/zoning moat. Stripped of Bombshells and corporate overhead, Nightclubs alone throws off more cash than the current enterprise value implies.
3. **Mathematical FCF yield of 23%.** On the post-Nov25 share count, $45M of FCF against a $196M market cap is an extreme statistical cheapness signal. The entire market cap could be returned to shareholders in less than five years at 100% of FCF direction.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

RCI Hospitality Holdings is a Texas-headquartered specialty hospitality rollup that aggregates 50+ operating entities into two core segments — **Nightclubs** and **Bombshells** — plus a residual "Other" bucket (ED Publications media, Drink Robust energy drink, a food hall). The Nightclub segment comprises adult entertainment cabarets under two dozen proprietary brands (Rick's Cabaret, Tootsie's, XTC Cabaret, Jaguars, Club Onyx, Scarlett's, Silver City, Temptations, Baby Dolls, Chicas Locas, Diamond Cabaret, PT's, Hoops, Foxy's, and others — FY2025 10-K line 112). Most nightclubs own the underlying real estate, reported as gross PP&E of $304.8M at FY25 end (FMP balance). Nightclubs generated 88.6% segment gross margin and 32.0% non-GAAP operating margin in FY2025 — exceptional unit economics reflecting (i) high-margin alcohol sales, (ii) independent-contractor dancer model (no payroll for entertainment), (iii) permit moat (SOB permits are non-discretionary but grandfathered and zoning-restricted), and (iv) cash-intensive operations with pricing power from upper-tier clientele.

Bombshells is a military-themed sports bar/restaurant concept launched March 2013 in Dallas as an explicit diversification away from the reputational and regulatory constraints of Nightclubs (concept origin attributed to Travis Reese per FY25 10-K line 167). The concept peaked at ~15-16 units with an attempted franchise arm. Bombshells same-store sales were **-13.6% in FY2025** after **-18.4% in FY2024** (FY25 10-K lines 96, 1353), and the segment's non-GAAP operating margin compressed from 13.1% in FY2023 to 5.7% in FY2024 to 1.8% in FY2025. By FY2025 end management had closed or sold five Bombshells units and stated they do not plan to add any more after the one currently under construction (line 110). **This is effectively the end of Bombshells as a growth platform.**

**The "Other" segment** is a rounding error: Drink Robust sales of $129,000 and Media revenue of $991,000 in FY2025 (line 1383). Not material to the thesis.

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — TEN-YEAR STRATEGIC ARC

### Phase I — Framework Introduction (FY2016–FY2019)

FY2016 was the first full year of the explicit capital allocation framework: buybacks above 10% after-tax FCF yield, acquisitions at 25-33% cash-on-cash, disposal of underperformers, paydown of expensive debt (FY2021 10-K lines 240-254). The FY16 base was $134.9M revenue, 15.3% GAAP operating margin, $1.12 diluted EPS. Bombshells (launched March 2013) grew to 8 units by FY2019, all Texas, with ~75% segment gross margin and 27.9% YoY revenue growth in its early years (FY19 10-K lines 191-192). Free cash flow compounded from minimal levels to $16.5M by FY2019 (FMP).

### Phase II — COVID Nadir & Snap-Back (FY2020-FY2021)

FY2020 was the deepest trough: revenue -27% to $132.3M, GAAP operating income just $2.7M (2.1% margin), net loss of -$6.1M, OCF of only $15.6M. The company survived without equity issuance thanks to the asset-light club model and owned real estate as collateral. FY2021 snapped back to $195.3M revenue (+48%) and $42.0M OCF.

### Phase III — Post-COVID M&A Peak (FY2022-FY2023)

This is the phase where the story was made. FY2022 acquired **fifteen clubs** for $132.6M aggregate ($55.3M cash + $49.0M debt + $29.9M stock — FY23 10-K line 1790). Revenue grew 37% to $267.6M. Non-GAAP operating margin expanded to 28.5%. Adj EBITDA hit $84.5M. FCF was $58.9M. FY2023 added **six more clubs** for $72.3M (line 1789), bringing two-year rollup total to 21 clubs for ~$205M of consideration. The stock printed its high near $95 in 2023. Critical detail: the FY22-23 acquisitions were financed partly with equity issued at **$60-64/share** (500,000 shares at $29.9M in FY22, 200,000 shares at $12.8M in FY23). Those shares are still outstanding. The math that worked at $64 is brutal at $24.

### Phase IV — Consolidation and Regime Change (FY2024-FY2025)

FY2024 revealed the cracks: revenue +0.6% to $295.6M, Bombshells SSS -18.4%, consolidated non-GAAP operating margin from 24.9% to 20.2%, **$38.5M of impairment charges** ($8.9M goodwill on 4 clubs, $11.8M SOB licenses on 7 clubs, $6.5M ROU assets on 5 Bombshells, $10.6M PP&E, $693K tradename — FY25 10-K line 1406). GAAP diluted EPS collapsed to $0.33. "Back-to-Basics" was announced December 2024.

FY2025 was the regime change year: revenue -5.5% to $279.4M, Nightclubs SSS -2.1%, Bombshells SSS -13.6%, another $5.3M impairment. Adj EBITDA fell 28% to $52.6M. Non-GAAP diluted EPS fell to $2.12 from $4.72 — a **55% decline**. The company still acquired three clubs for $21M (line 1828), but the acquisition pace was a fraction of the FY22-23 peak and the "25-33% cash-on-cash" threshold language was already gone from the 10-K. In November 2025 Langan and Chhay stepped down following the September 2025 NY indictment.

## PART VI · THE "10% FCF YIELD RULE" — VERIFICATION AND OBITUARY

The "10% FCF yield rule" is real. It was written, verbatim, into every 10-K from at least FY2016 through FY2024. Here is the full framework, quoted from the FY2024 10-K Item 1 (filed December 2024 for FY ending September 2024):

> *"Our capital allocation strategy provides us with disciplined guidelines on how we should use our free cash flows; provided however, that we may deviate from this strategy if other strategic rationale warrants. We calculate free cash flow as net cash flows from operating activities minus maintenance capital expenditures. Using the after-tax yield of buying our own stock as baseline, management believes that we are able to make better investment decisions.*
>
> *Based on our current capital allocation strategy:*
> - *We consider buying back our own stock if the after-tax yield on free cash flow is above 10%;*
> - *We consider acquiring or developing our own clubs or restaurants that we believe have the potential to provide a minimum cash on cash return of 25%-33%, absent an otherwise strategic rationale;*
> - *We consider disposing of underperforming units to free up capital for more productive use;*
> - *We consider paying down our most expensive debt if it makes sense on a tax-adjusted basis, or there is an otherwise strategic rationale."*

(FY2024 10-K lines 103-108; identical language in FY2023 10-K lines 105-110 and FY2021 10-K lines 240-254.)

**The FY2025 10-K omits all of this language.** In its place (FY2025 10-K, Item 1, lines 102-110):

> *"Our overall objective is to create value for our shareholders by developing and operating profitable businesses in the hospitality and related space... In December 2024, we launched our five-year Back-to-Basics strategy where we focus on improving performance of existing clubs and Bombshells units to fuel our capital allocation priorities. For the allocation of our free cash flow, we currently divide it among club acquisitions (investing), share buybacks (financing), and dividends (financing). Our financial targets by fiscal 2029 are to achieve: Total revenues of $400 million; Free cash flow of $75 million; Shares outstanding of 7.5 million."*

**This is a watershed change.** What was specific and measurable became vague and aspirational. The only thing left that looks like a numerical target is the FY2029 guidance, which is an aggregate target without any unit-level return discipline.

### Has the 10% rule been broken? Was it ever binding?

## PART VII · NIGHTCLUB SECULAR RISKS — THE REAL LONG-TERM QUESTION

The nightclubs segment is 97% of RICK's non-GAAP operating income (Part II). If the nightclub segment has a secular decline problem, RICK has a secular decline problem.

### GLP-1 weight loss drugs and alcohol consumption

GLP-1 receptor agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound) entered broad US use in ~2023. Retail-level studies now show measurable reductions in alcohol consumption among GLP-1 users — effect sizes in the 10-20% range for heavy drinkers. Sell-side surveys suggest aggregate alcohol consumption falls ~15-20% among users, with beer and wine hit hardest.

RICK's revenue mix is 43.7% alcoholic beverages consolidated; Nightclubs segment is 43% alcohol and Bombshells 52% alcohol (FY25 10-K lines 92, 96). A 15-20% reduction in alcohol consumption among a GLP-1-using subset (forecast to reach 10-15% of US adults) translates mechanically to a **6-9% revenue headwind over 3-5 years**.

But the GLP-1 argument is more sinister than the alcohol math alone. **Nightclub revenue is not driven by the average drinker — it is driven by the top-decile customer who spends multiples of the mean per visit.** That cohort is disproportionately male, 35-55, upper-income, and already taking or being targeted for GLP-1 drugs for metabolic reasons. If the top-spending decile reduces visit frequency by 20%, the impact on Nightclubs revenue could be an order of magnitude larger than the population-weighted alcohol math implies.

**The FY2025 10-K does NOT mention GLP-1 drugs, weight-loss pharmaceuticals, or secular changes in alcohol consumption** as a risk factor. Either blindness or deliberate silence. The Nightclubs same-store trajectory — three consecutive years of -2% while the broader restaurant industry has been flat-to-positive — is consistent with a GLP-1 impact. Management attributes it to "the general local economy" (standard FY19 framing), but that explanation has worn thin in FY2025 when US consumer spending was solid.

### Other secular and regulatory risks

- **ESG exclusions cap the multiple.** Pension funds, ESG-screened index funds, and major mutual fund complexes have progressively excluded SOB operators for over a decade. This limits the institutional buyer base and caps the multiple structurally.
- **Payment processor de-platform risk** is known but has not materialized (FY25 10-K line 731).
- **NY is the critical risk-concentrated state** — the NY State Department of Taxation audit that led to the September 2025 indictment targets "Dance Dollars" sales tax. Whatever the settlement outcome, NY operations are now under intensified scrutiny. Rick's Cabaret NY, Vivid Cabaret NY, and Hoops Cabaret NY are all named (line 767).
- **Texas "patron tax"** on SOB operators has been periodically increased and flagged in MD&A as a cost driver.

### Real estate value as downside support — erode on closer look

At FY25, gross PP&E is $304.8M and intangibles (largely SOB licenses) are $172M. Most nightclubs own the underlying real estate. In a worst-case wind-down at a 50% haircut, the real estate is worth roughly $150M or ~$19/share on 8.0M post-buyback shares. Adding the SOB license portfolio (which has genuine scarcity value) brings a rough liquidation floor to roughly $18-24 per share.

**But that is before debt.** See Part IX breakup valuation: after netting $266M of debt, the equity residual in a strict liquidation scenario is negative. **The "real-estate backstop" argument is weaker than I expected entering this analysis.** The leverage has absorbed the real estate cushion.
