## PART I -- Executive Summary

Eastman Chemical Company is a global specialty materials company headquartered in Kingsport, Tennessee, with a market capitalization of approximately $8.5 billion at the reference price of $74.49. The company was spun off from Eastman Kodak in 1994 and has since transformed from a commodity chemical manufacturer into a diversified specialty materials business through a combination of organic innovation and transformative acquisitions (Solutia in 2012 for $3.4 billion, Taminco in 2014 for $2.8 billion). Under CEO Mark Costa's leadership since 2014, the company has pivoted toward higher-margin specialty products, molecular recycling technology, and biodegradable materials -- a transformation that is real but incomplete, as the 2025 earnings trough painfully demonstrated.

Fiscal year 2025 was unambiguously a trough year. Revenue declined 7% to $8.752 billion from $9.382 billion in FY2024. GAAP EBIT collapsed 39% to $776 million, net income attributable to Eastman fell 48% to $474 million, and diluted earnings per share dropped to $4.10 from $7.67. Excluding non-core items -- $96 million in asset impairments and restructuring charges, a $6 million mark-to-market pension loss, and $62 million in environmental and other legacy costs -- adjusted EBIT was $930 million and adjusted EPS was $5.42, still down 28% and 31% respectively from 2024. The decline was broad-based: Advanced Materials revenue fell 6% on construction and automotive weakness, Chemical Intermediates fell 10% on Asian competitive pressure and volume weakness, and Fibers cratered 20% on acetate tow customer destocking and industry capacity share adjustments. Only Additives & Functional Products was essentially flat, providing a modest earnings improvement on cost reduction initiatives.

The dividend, however, remains firmly intact. At $3.33 per share declared for FY2025 (subsequently raised to an annualized $3.36 with the Q1 2026 declaration of $0.84 per share), the company has now increased its dividend for 14 consecutive years. Even at the 2025 trough, operating cash flow of $970 million covered the $381 million dividend payment 2.55 times. Free cash flow of $424 million -- depressed by elevated capex of $546 million for the Kingsport molecular recycling facility -- still covered dividends 1.11 times. With management guiding 2026 capex to approximately $400 million as the plant nears completion, free cash flow should mechanically improve $100-150 million even without an earnings recovery, pushing FCF coverage back toward 1.4-1.7 times.

This is a cyclical entry point on a company with genuine secular growth drivers. The world's largest polyester molecular recycling facility achieved 2.5 times output growth in 2025 and is being debottlenecked to 130% of nameplate capacity. Tritan Renew copolyester is winning commercial contracts with major brands including Pepsi. The stock at 13.7 times adjusted EPS and 9.2 times EV/EBITDA is trading at trough multiples that historically precede 30-50% re-ratings when the cycle turns. For a dividend-focused investor who can withstand 20-30% peak-to-trough drawdowns, EMN at $74.49 offers a 4.5% yield with 14 years of consecutive growth and meaningful total return optionality.

**Inflection points:**

1. **2020 -- COVID trough and rapid recovery.** Revenue fell to $8.46 billion but operating cash flow paradoxically surged to $1.445 billion as working capital released. Construction and automotive end markets remained below 2019 levels, and the consumer discretionary weakness that would define 2025 was already visible in Q4 2024 data.

5. **2025 -- Full cyclical capitulation.** The April 2025 tariff escalation ("Liberation Day") reversed the modest demand recovery visible in Q1 2025. Customer destocking intensified across AM, CI, and especially Fibers, where acetate tow buyers went to contract minimums. Revenue fell 7% and adjusted EBIT dropped 28%. Management responded with $100 million of cost reductions (exceeding the $75 million target), cut buybacks to $100 million (from $300 million in 2024), and repaid $100 million of the 2027 Term Loan.

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The Eastman dividend has survived every recent stress event: the 2020 COVID shutdown, the 2022 raw material cost spike, and the 2025 cyclical trough. Management's behavior is textbook for a cyclical compounder: when earnings are strong, dividend growth accelerates (8.6% in 2022) and buybacks are aggressive ($500-600 million). When earnings weaken, dividend growth decelerates to token levels (0.9% in 2026) and buybacks are slashed to $100 million. The dividend is the last thing that would be cut.

The critical stress test: even at 2025's trough operating cash flow of $970 million, the $381 million dividend consumed only 39% of OCF. To endanger the dividend, operating cash flow would need to decline to approximately $400 million, which would require revenue to fall an additional 20-25% from the already depressed $8.75 billion level (to roughly $6.5-7.0 billion). That scenario implies a synchronized global recession far more severe than 2020. The $1.50 billion undrawn revolving credit facility provides additional liquidity buffer.

The February 2026 credit facility amendment -- temporarily loosening the leverage ratio covenant through June 2027 -- is a prudent preemptive measure, not a sign of distress. The facility was fully undrawn at year-end. This amendment signals that management expects earnings to remain depressed through mid-2027 but wants financial flexibility to maintain the dividend and pursue growth investments regardless of the macro environment.

For 2026, with capex declining approximately $146 million (from $546 million to ~$400 million guided) and operating cash flow likely flat to modestly improved as destocking abates, we estimate FCF of $550-650 million against dividends of approximately $383 million (114 million shares at $3.36 per share). This would restore FCF coverage to 1.44-1.70 times -- a comfortable level that also supports modest buyback resumption.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

- **Moat type:** Scale + vertical integration + switching costs + innovation/IP
- **Moat trajectory:** Stable to widening (molecular recycling adds differentiation)
- **Pricing power evidence:** AM segment maintained prices through 2023-2024 despite volume weakness; only sharing "modest" raw material benefits with customers in 2026 after 4 years of strong price defense. CEO Costa explicitly stated on Q4 2025 call: "We've done a phenomenally good job of maintaining our price and our variable margins while defending our share because of our innovation."
- **Return on invested capital trend:** 7.6% (FY2025 trough), ~10-12% at mid-cycle, ~13% at peak (FY2024)

Eastman's moat rests on four pillars:

**1. Vertical integration and scale.** The Kingsport manufacturing complex is the world's largest integrated cellulosic biopolymer site. The company's four manufacturing streams (polyester, cellulosic biopolymer/acetyl, alkylamine, and olefins) create internal cost advantages that pure-play competitors cannot replicate. More than half of CI segment production feeds internally into specialty segments, creating a captive feedstock advantage.

**2. Formulation-based switching costs.** Particularly in AFP, Eastman's additives represent a tiny fraction of customer product costs (coalescents in paint, processing aids in personal care) but provide critical functionality. Customers qualify these materials through extensive testing cycles. Switching involves reformulation risk and regulatory re-qualification that makes Eastman sticky in these applications. The AFP segment's consistent profitability through the 2025 downturn -- EBIT actually improved year-over-year -- is direct evidence of switching cost protection.

**3. Innovation-driven differentiation.** Eastman maintains over 700 active US patents and over 1,600 active foreign patents across its technology platforms. R&D spending of $255 million in 2025 was maintained flat with 2024 despite the earnings decline, signaling that innovation investment is protected through the cycle. Tritan copolyester, Saflex HUD interlayers, EastaPure electronic-grade solvents, and the entire molecular recycling platform are products where Eastman competes on performance and sustainability attributes rather than price.

**4. Molecular recycling as an emerging moat.** The Kingsport methanolysis facility is not a science experiment -- it is the world's largest polyester molecular recycling plant, operating commercially and achieving 2.5 times output growth in 2025. The technology breaks down hard-to-recycle plastic waste to molecular building blocks that produce virgin-quality materials, reducing GHG emissions by up to 70% versus fossil feedstock production. CEO Costa's Q4 2025 call commentary revealed that mechanical recycling quality is degrading faster than expected (yellowing, reduced polymer integrity), which is accelerating customer adoption of Eastman's chemical recycling -- Pepsi and other major brands are pulling forward purchases. This is a durable competitive advantage that will take years for competitors to replicate, as it requires Eastman's specific combination of polyester chemistry expertise, scale, purification technology, and ISCC certification.

Several observations merit attention for dividend investors:

**Fibers remains the highest-margin segment despite its crisis.** At 34.7% gross margin and approximately 22.4% adjusted EBIT margin, Fibers continues to generate outsized profitability per dollar of revenue -- a legacy of the vertically integrated Kingsport cellulosic platform and long-term customer contracts. This means the 20% revenue decline ($268 million) translated into an even larger absolute EBIT impact than the revenue drop would suggest. When Fibers stabilizes, the margin leverage works in reverse.

**Chemical Intermediates posted an adjusted EBIT loss of ($38 million).** This is extraordinary for a segment that generated approximately $100 million in adjusted EBIT at mid-cycle. The loss was driven by $103 million in lower selling prices (Asian competitive pressure), $110 million in lower volume (construction, durables), and $121 million in combined price/cost compression. The ethylene-to-propylene (ETP) conversion project, which management estimates will improve CI earnings by $50-100 million annually with a less-than-two-year payback, is the most important structural initiative for this segment. On the Q4 call, Costa emphasized: "We're driving forward [the ETP project] as one that will definitely structurally improve the business."

**AFP was the lone bright spot.** Revenue flat at $2,880 million, but adjusted EBIT actually improved year-over-year through a combination of cost-pass-through pricing, lower SG&A, and reduced operating costs. The cost-pass-through mechanism in AFP contracts means raw material fluctuations are partially neutralized, providing earnings stability. Growth in water treatment, medical/pharma, and electronics partially offset weakness in building and construction.

**Advanced Materials product line breakdown (from 10-K Note 20):** Specialty plastics accounted for 49% of AM revenue (up from 47% in 2024 and 45% in 2023), advanced interlayers 32% (down from 33%), and performance films 19% (down from 20%). The secular shift toward specialty plastics -- particularly Tritan and molecular-recycled copolyesters -- is evident in the product mix evolution. This is the revenue base where molecular recycling revenue growth will be most visible.

**Geographic exposure within segments reveals differentiated risk profiles.** The Fibers segment has the highest international exposure: 37% Asia Pacific, 36% EMEA, only 23% US/Canada. This makes Fibers particularly vulnerable to tariff-driven demand destruction in Asian textile markets and European consumer weakness. In contrast, CI is 71% US/Canada, making it the most domestically oriented segment but also the most exposed to Chinese import competition in the remaining 29% international sales. AM is well-diversified: 33% US/Canada, 36% APAC, 25% EMEA, 6% Latin America.

## PART VII -- Management Assessment

- **CEO:** Mark J. Costa, Board Chair and CEO since January 2014. Previously COO and Executive Vice President. Over a decade of tenure gives him deep institutional knowledge and accountability for the specialty transformation strategy. Total compensation per FY2024 proxy (estimated): approximately $12-14 million, with the majority performance-based. Costa is articulate, transparent on calls, and has consistently guided toward specialty transformation. His Q4 2025 earnings call commentary was notably candid about both the depth of the downturn and the range of outcomes for 2026 ("around $6 a share... is very much in the range of what we're thinking is possible, but I have to emphasize there's a wide range").

- **CFO:** William T. McLain Jr., Executive Vice President and CFO. Tenure of multiple years, previously in various finance roles at Eastman. McLain provides disciplined financial commentary and manages the credit facility and capital allocation with conservative precision.

- **Insider ownership:** Directors and executive officers beneficially owned shares as of year-end, with Costa's personal holdings supplemented by stock options (range of exercise prices $61-$121, aggregate intrinsic value of $1 million at December 31, 2025 -- notably, most options are underwater at the current stock price, aligning management with shareholders in the downturn).

- **Comp structure:** Performance shares based on actual return on capital versus target, total stockholder return versus peer group, vesting over three-year performance periods. Long-term performance awards can range from 0% to 250% of target. This structure appropriately penalizes management for below-target performance while creating meaningful upside for outperformance.

- **Guidance track record:** Management under-delivered versus initial 2025 expectations by a wide margin. The year began with expectations of modest growth, and the April tariff shock destroyed that trajectory. However, management adjusted quickly: the $100 million cost reduction target was raised and exceeded at $100 million delivered, buybacks were cut, and the credit facility was preemptively amended. Variable compensation expense was significantly reduced in 2025, flowing through as a $50-75 million headwind into 2026 as plans reset. For Q1 2026, management guided to sequential volume improvement from Q4 2025, with adjusted EPS of approximately $1.10, explicitly excluding potential winter storm impacts. The Q4 earnings call suggested full-year 2026 adjusted EPS in the $5.50-6.00 range as achievable but macro-dependent.

## PART X -- Risks & Stress Tests

**Risk #1: Cyclical earnings volatility -- HIGH probability, HIGH severity, PARTIAL mitigant**

The 47% decline in reported EPS from 2024 to 2025 ($7.67 to $4.10) demonstrates extreme operating leverage. Fixed manufacturing costs at 36 facilities amplify both upside and downside. Construction and automotive end markets -- representing approximately 40% of revenue -- are cyclically depressed by elevated interest rates and tariff uncertainty. A prolonged downturn could maintain earnings near trough levels for 2-3 more years. Mitigant: Eastman has maintained the dividend through every downturn since its 1994 independence, and operating cash flow remains robust ($970 million in 2025) even when GAAP earnings compress, because depreciation and amortization ($513 million) provides a substantial non-cash buffer.

**Risk #2: Fibers structural decline -- HIGH probability, MEDIUM severity, PARTIAL mitigant**

Acetate tow for cigarette filters (69% of Fibers segment, ~8% of total revenue) is in structural decline as global cigarette consumption falls 2-3% annually. The 2025 destocking was acute ($256 million of volume decline), and CEO Costa confirmed on the Q4 call that customers will remain at contract minimums in 2026 with destocking continuing through the year. Naia biodegradable fibers, Aventa compostable materials, and staple fiber expansion provide partial offset, but cannot fully replace lost tow volumes within five years. On the call, Costa was transparent: "About 40% of the EBIT drop is actually not tow" -- tariff impacts on textiles, reduced cellulosic demand across the company, and higher energy costs all contributed. Mitigant: Management is aggressively pursuing asset utilization through new applications (staple fiber in denim/fleece, Aventa in food packaging) and cost reduction. Fibers tow contracts have annual volume minimums that provide a floor.

**Risk #3: Tariff and trade policy exposure -- MEDIUM probability, HIGH severity, LIMITED mitigant**

The 2025 10-K forward-looking statements explicitly cite "impacts from U.S. tariffs, reciprocal tariffs, and global trade disruption" as a material risk. With 56% of revenue from outside the U.S./Canada, 36 facilities in 12 countries, and significant exposure to European and Asian markets, Eastman faces multiple trade vectors: tariffs on imported feedstocks, retaliatory tariffs on exports, supply chain disruption requiring suboptimal sourcing, and tariff-induced demand destruction in end markets. The CI segment specifically cited competitive pressure from Chinese producers dumping product at variable cash cost. CEO Costa on Q4 call: tariff impacts reversed the Q1 2025 recovery and caused the back-half deterioration. Mitigant: diversified manufacturing footprint provides some natural hedging, and the February 2026 credit facility amendment provides financial buffer.

**Risk #4: Goodwill impairment -- MEDIUM probability, MEDIUM severity, NO mitigant**

$3.665 billion of goodwill (25% of total assets) represents a legacy of the Solutia and Taminco acquisitions. PwC's 2025 audit identified the performance films reporting unit's goodwill assessment as a critical audit matter. The quantitative impairment test passed, but with narrow sensitivity margins. Continued weakness in the automotive aftermarket films business (where XPEL and 3M compete aggressively) could trigger a charge -- non-cash, but damaging to reported earnings and book value, and potentially triggering covenant concerns.

**Risk #5: Environmental liabilities -- LOW probability of acute event, MEDIUM-HIGH severity if triggered, PARTIAL mitigant**

Estimated future remediation costs of $285 million to $509 million (undiscounted) represent 0.7-1.2 times annual FCF. The Kingsport complex has operated since 1920 and the Solutia legacy sites carry multi-decade remediation obligations. Cash environmental spending of $339 million in 2025 is already substantial. A Superfund reclassification or regulatory acceleration could materially increase near-term cash outflows. Mitigant: costs are spread over approximately 30 years, reserves are regularly updated, and the Monsanto Settlement Agreement provides cost-sharing for certain Shared Sites.

**Risk #6: Raw material and energy cost volatility -- MEDIUM probability, MEDIUM severity, PARTIAL mitigant**

Key inputs include propane, ethane, natural gas, paraxylene, ethylene, and benzene. The 2025 results showed $9 million of margin compression from higher raw material and energy costs in AM, $121 million combined in CI, $51 million in Fibers, and winter storms in early 2026 created additional energy cost headwinds. Hedging mitigates short-term volatility (the company hedges commodities within a rolling three-year period, weighted heavily toward the first year) but cannot eliminate structural cost increases. Approximately 45% of total cost of operations consists of raw materials and energy.
