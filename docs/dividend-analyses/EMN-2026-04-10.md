The FMP DCF of $86.82 and analyst consensus target of $76 both suggest the stock is moderately undervalued. Our view: EMN is a cyclical BUY below $75 for patient dividend collectors who can stomach 20-30% peak-to-trough drawdowns.

## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mark Costa has run Eastman since 2014, and his decade-long transformation thesis is simple: convert a commodity chemical company into a specialty materials company by leveraging cellulosic biopolymer, copolyester, olefin, polyester, and alkylamine technology platforms. The evidence is mixed. Revenue has gone essentially sideways over five years ($8.46B in 2020, $8.75B in 2025), but the mix has shifted decisively toward higher-margin specialty products. Gross margin in 2024 reached 24.4% versus sub-20% in the commodity era. The problem is that 2025 was a down year across virtually every end market, and the gross margin compressed back to 21.1% -- exposing the inconvenient truth that "specialty" does not mean "non-cyclical."

**The bull case in three numbers:**
1. **$5.42 adjusted EPS vs $3.36 DPS = 1.61x coverage.** Even in a bad year, the dividend is comfortably covered by core earnings. At mid-cycle EPS of $7.00-7.50, coverage expands to 2.0-2.2x -- elite territory for an industrial.
2. **$970M operating cash flow vs $381M dividends = 2.55x.** Cash generation remains robust even as GAAP earnings compress because of the capital-intensive nature of the business (D&A of $513M provides significant non-cash buffer).
3. **$424M free cash flow on $546M capex, but 2026 capex guided to ~$400M.** As the Kingsport methanolysis plant ramp-up normalizes, capex intensity drops ~27%, boosting FCF toward $570-600M.

**The bear case in three numbers:**
1. **Revenue -7%, EPS -47% (reported), adjusted EBIT -29%.** This is a deeply cyclical business. The 2025 results demonstrate that earnings leverage cuts both ways. A prolonged downturn in construction, automotive, or consumer discretionary would maintain pressure.
2. **$4.8B total debt, ~3.5x net debt/EBITDA.** Leverage is elevated relative to the earnings trough. Management refinanced the credit facility in February 2026 with a temporary loosening of the leverage covenant through Q2 2027 -- a prudent but revealing concession.
3. **$3.7B goodwill, 25% of total assets.** The 2012 Solutia and 2014 Taminco acquisitions embedded significant goodwill, and the performance films reporting unit within Advanced Materials required a quantitative impairment test in 2025 (it passed, but narrowly -- $812M of goodwill is allocated to performance films).

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **Cyclical trough valuations on a company with secular growth drivers.** Molecular recycling (circular economy), Tritan copolyesters in food-contact and medical, Naia biodegradable fibers -- these are not commodity plays. The market is pricing EMN as if the trough is permanent.
2. **14 consecutive years of dividend growth with a 4.5% yield.** The dividend was raised from $3.26 to $3.33 per share in 2025 (+2.1%) and again to $3.36 (implied by the Q1 2026 declaration of $0.84/share). Management has raised the dividend every year since the post-Kodak spinoff era, including through COVID, the 2022 raw material spike, and the 2025 earnings trough.
3. **Share count discipline is real.** Shares outstanding declined from 134.9M (2021 weighted average) to 114.7M (2025 weighted average) -- a 15% reduction in four years. The $2.5B buyback authorization (2021) still has $1.3B remaining. In 2025, management slowed buybacks to $100M (vs $300M in 2024) to conserve cash -- prudent trough-year behavior.

**Top 3 reasons not to own:**
1. **Cyclicality is extreme and can persist.** From 2022 peak revenue of $10.54B to 2025's $8.75B is a 17% decline. EBIT dropped from $1.16B to $776M (-33%). If you cannot hold through a multi-year downturn, EMN will shake you out.
2. **The Fibers segment ($1.05B revenue, -20% YoY) is structurally threatened.** Acetate tow -- the main product, used in cigarette filters -- is in secular volume decline. Customer destocking and industry overcapacity drove the 2025 collapse. Fibers has historically been the high-margin cash cow, and its degradation undermines the overall earnings base.
3. **Tariff and trade war exposure is significant.** Approximately 55% of 2025 sales were generated outside the U.S. and Canada. The 10-K explicitly calls out tariff uncertainty as a headwind in the Advanced Materials segment. Eastman's manufacturing footprint spans 36 facilities in 12 countries -- trade disruption is a real risk.

## PART VI-B -- SEGMENT PROFITABILITY DEEP DIVE

Understanding EMN requires looking beyond headline revenue to segment-level profitability dynamics:

**Advanced Materials ($2.88B revenue, ~33% of total):**
This is the crown jewel segment, home to Tritan copolyesters, Saflex interlayers, and performance films. Adjusted EBIT decreased ~$115M in 2025 due to $116M lower sales volume (construction weakness affecting interlayers, automotive weakness hitting films) and $9M margin compression. The performance films reporting unit is under particular stress: market share in automotive aftermarket films is being contested by XPEL and 3M, and $812M of goodwill sits on this sub-segment.

However, the AM segment also contains Eastman's most promising growth platforms: Tritan Renew (molecular-recycled copolyester), Saflex Evoca (EV-specific interlayers), and the broader automotive HUD interlayer franchise. When construction and automotive recover, this segment has the highest earnings leverage.

**Additives & Functional Products ($2.88B revenue, ~33% of total):**
AFP was the resilient segment in 2025 -- revenue essentially flat, with EBIT actually improving on cost reduction initiatives. This segment sells small-volume, high-value additives that represent a tiny fraction of customer product costs but provide critical functionality (coatings coalescents, water treatment chemicals, personal care ingredients). The formulation-based customer relationships create significant switching costs.

AFP is the least cyclical of Eastman's four segments and provides earnings stability through downturns. Its exposure to water treatment, medical/pharma, and electronics provides counter-cyclical ballast against construction/automotive weakness in AM and CI.

**Chemical Intermediates ($1.93B revenue, ~22% of total):**
CI is the most commodity-exposed segment, selling olefins, acetyls, and plasticizers into construction, durables, and energy markets. Revenue fell 10% in 2025 with EBIT declining sharply on competitive pressure in Asia (particularly from Chinese producers) and volume weakness. The Texas City operations divestiture in late 2023 removed some CI revenue but improved the margin profile.

CI is where Eastman's commodity heritage is most visible. It provides integration advantages (captive feedstock for AM and AFP) but generates the lowest margins and is most vulnerable to global oversupply and price competition. Long-term, management has been gradually pruning CI through divestitures and shifting investment toward specialty segments.

**Fibers ($1.05B revenue, ~12% of total):**
The Fibers segment is the controversial one. Acetate tow for cigarette filters is in structural decline globally, and the 2025 results were catastrophic: -20% revenue, -38% EBIT decline, driven by customer inventory destocking and industry capacity share adjustments. Acetate tow accounted for approximately 65% of Fibers segment revenue, with the balance from textiles (Naia filament and staple fiber) and cellulosic innovation products (Aventa compostable materials).

The destocking dynamic is important to understand. Cigarette manufacturers periodically build acetate tow inventories during periods of price uncertainty, then destock sharply when they feel oversupplied. This creates lumpy demand patterns that exaggerate the underlying secular decline. The 2025 destocking was particularly severe because it coincided with industry capacity share adjustments (i.e., competitors gaining share from Eastman). While some recovery is likely in 2026 as inventories normalize, the long-term trajectory for acetate tow is negative -- global cigarette consumption is declining 2-3% annually, and this cannot be fully offset by innovation products within the segment.

## PART VII -- GROWTH ENGINES AND INNOVATION

**1. Molecular Recycling (Circular Economy) -- The Signature Bet**

Eastman's molecular recycling technology breaks down hard-to-recycle plastic waste into molecular building blocks that are then used to produce virgin-quality materials. The Kingsport methanolysis facility -- the world's largest polyester molecular recycling plant -- began operations in 2024 and achieved approximately 2.5x output growth in 2025 (EMN 2025 10-K ITEM 1).

This is not greenwashing. The facility produces commercial quantities of Tritan Renew copolyester and other "Renew" branded products that command premium pricing. The technology can reduce GHG emissions by up to 70% compared to fossil-feedstock production (EMN 10-K). The capital investment phase is largely complete (2026 capex guided to ~$400M vs peak of $828M in 2023), meaning the economics should begin to inflect as volumes ramp without commensurate capital spending.

**Our view:** Molecular recycling is a legitimate competitive moat that will take 3-5 years to fully commercialize but has the potential to add $200-400M of incremental annual revenue at premium margins by 2028-2030. The market is assigning approximately zero value to this optionality at the current stock price.

**2. Tritan Copolyester -- Proven Growth Platform**

Tritan is Eastman's flagship BPA-free copolyester used in food containers, medical devices, consumer electronics, and packaging. It is the product that embodies the "commodity to specialty" transformation. Tritan sells at a premium to commodity polyesters and competes on performance characteristics (clarity, durability, chemical resistance, BPA-free certification) rather than price. The Renew extension (molecular-recycled content) adds a sustainability premium.

**3. Saflex Advanced Interlayers -- Automotive Secular Growth**

Saflex PVB interlayers are used in automotive safety glass, acoustic windshields, and heads-up displays (HUD). The automotive trend toward larger windshields, acoustic comfort, and HUD integration is a secular tailwind. Saflex Evoca, designed specifically for electric vehicles, provides lightweight, acoustic, and solar properties that are difficult to replicate.

**4. Naia/Aventa -- Biodegradable Materials**

Naia (for textiles/apparel) and Aventa (for food packaging, straws, cutlery) are cellulosic biopolymers that are approximately 60% bio-derived, biodegradable, and compostable. These address the single-use plastic regulatory wave hitting the EU and major Asian markets. Revenue contribution is still small but growing.

## PART VIII -- RISK FACTORS

**1. Cyclical earnings volatility.** The 47% decline in reported EPS from 2024 to 2025 ($7.67 to $4.10) demonstrates the operating leverage inherent in a capital-intensive chemical company. Fixed manufacturing costs amplify both upside and downside. Investors must underwrite a normalized earnings range of $5.50-8.00 and accept that specific years will fall outside this range.

**2. Fibers structural decline.** The Fibers segment generated $1.05B of revenue in 2025, down 20% from 2024 and likely to face continued pressure from secular decline in global cigarette consumption. Acetate tow customer destocking in 2025 was acute, but the underlying trend is negative. The segment accounted for 12% of revenue but historically contributed a disproportionate share of profitability due to its high margins. Naia and Aventa may partially offset the decline but are unlikely to fully replace lost acetate tow volumes within five years.

**3. Tariff and trade policy exposure.** The 2025 10-K forward-looking statements explicitly cite tariffs, reciprocal tariffs, and global trade disruption as risk factors. With 55% of revenue from outside the U.S./Canada and 36 facilities in 12 countries, Eastman is directly exposed to trade friction. The Chemical Intermediates segment specifically cited competitive pressure in Asia as a driver of lower selling prices.

**4. Goodwill impairment risk.** The performance films reporting unit carries $812M of goodwill and required quantitative testing in 2025. While it passed, the sensitivity analysis showed a narrow margin. Continued weakness in the automotive aftermarket films business could trigger a charge -- non-cash, but damaging to reported earnings and book value.

**5. Environmental liabilities.** Estimated future environmental remediation costs range from $285M to $509M. Eastman's Kingsport manufacturing complex is a legacy site with decades of chemical production history. While the company has established appropriate reserves, unexpected remediation requirements could arise.

**6. Raw material and energy cost volatility.** Key inputs include propane, ethane, natural gas, paraxylene, ethylene, and benzene. The 2025 results showed $9M of margin compression from higher raw material and energy costs in the AM segment alone, with similar headwinds in CI ($121M combined from lower prices and higher costs) and Fibers ($51M). Hedging mitigates short-term volatility but cannot eliminate structural cost increases.

## PART VIII-A -- TARIFF AND TRADE EXPOSURE ANALYSIS

The 2025 10-K forward-looking statements section is unusually explicit about trade risk, citing "impacts from U.S. tariffs, reciprocal tariffs, and global trade disruption" as a material risk factor. This deserves dedicated analysis because Eastman's global manufacturing footprint makes it particularly exposed.

**Manufacturing geography:** 36 facilities in 12 countries, including the massive Kingsport, Tennessee complex (headquarters and largest facility), plus significant operations in Europe, Asia, and Latin America. Key international manufacturing sites include facilities in Germany, the Netherlands, Spain, Finland, Malaysia, and China.

**Revenue geography (FY2025):**
- United States: $3.66B (42%)
- All Foreign: $4.14B (47%)
- Asia Pacific: $950M (11%)

**Trade risk vectors:**
1. **U.S. tariffs on imported raw materials.** Eastman imports certain feedstocks (paraxylene, ethylene glycol, specialty chemicals) that could be subject to tariffs, increasing input costs. The Chemical Intermediates segment explicitly cited competitive pressure from Asian producers as a margin headwind in 2025.
2. **Reciprocal tariffs on Eastman exports.** Products manufactured in the U.S. and exported to China, EU, and other markets face retaliatory tariffs that reduce competitiveness and volume. The AM segment noted that tariff uncertainty specifically impacted specialty plastics demand.
3. **Supply chain disruption.** Eastman's integrated manufacturing model requires cross-border movement of intermediates between facilities. Trade barriers could force suboptimal sourcing decisions and increase logistics costs.
4. **Customer demand destruction.** Tariff-induced inflation in end markets (automotive, construction, consumer goods) reduces demand for Eastman's products independently of any direct tariff on chemicals.

**Management's mitigation:** Eastman's diversified geographic manufacturing base provides some natural hedging -- products can be sourced from facilities in multiple countries to minimize tariff exposure. The company also uses derivative instruments to hedge currency and commodity price risks. However, a sustained trade war would structurally impair the CI segment's economics and could force production rationalization.

**The February 2026 credit facility amendment** is partly a response to this risk. By temporarily loosening the leverage covenant through Q2 2027, management is building financial buffer against a scenario where trade disruption compresses earnings below current expectations.

## PART VIII-B -- PENSION AND ENVIRONMENTAL LIABILITY ANALYSIS

Two off-balance-sheet-adjacent liabilities deserve attention for dividend investors because they represent contingent claims on cash flow:

**Pension obligations:** Eastman maintains defined benefit pension plans with weighted-average discount rates of 5.26% (U.S.) and 4.81% (non-U.S.) and expected returns on plan assets of 7.50% (U.S.) and 5.13% (non-U.S.). In 2025, the company recorded a mark-to-market pension loss of $6M (vs a $54M gain in 2024), reflecting the normalization of actuarial assumptions. Cash pension contributions were $60M in 2025. The pension obligation is manageable but represents a persistent cash drain that competes with dividends for cash flow allocation. A sharp decline in discount rates (i.e., lower interest rates) would increase the unfunded obligation and potentially require accelerated contributions.

**Environmental liabilities:** Estimated future environmental remediation costs range from $285M to $509M (undiscounted). The Kingsport manufacturing complex, in continuous operation since the 1920s, has accumulated legacy contamination typical of century-old chemical plants. Environmental capital expenditures were approximately $80M in 2025 and $70M in 2024. While the annual cash cost is predictable and incorporated into operating budgets, the upper end of the remediation range ($509M) represents a material contingent liability -- roughly equal to one year of FCF.

Neither of these liabilities threatens the dividend in the near term, but they are the kind of slow-burning cash obligations that income investors should monitor annually. A regulatory reclassification of any Kingsport site as a Superfund priority could accelerate environmental spending materially.

Eastman Chemical is a high-quality cyclical compounder going through an ugly earnings trough. The 2025 results -- revenue -7%, reported EPS -47%, Fibers collapsing on destocking -- are genuinely bad. But "genuinely bad" is exactly when you want to buy cyclicals, not sell them.

The 15.7% share count reduction since 2021 compounds the per-share recovery when the cycle turns. Management's decision to cut buybacks rather than the dividend in 2025 demonstrates the correct priority hierarchy.

The molecular recycling/circular economy strategy is the most underappreciated asset on the balance sheet. The risks are real: cyclical downside can persist, Fibers faces structural headwinds, goodwill is large, and tariff exposure is significant. These are the reasons the stock yields 4.5% instead of 2.5%.
