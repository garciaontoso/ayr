## PART I -- Executive Summary

NNN REIT is a single-tenant, triple-net lease REIT that has increased its annual dividend for 36 consecutive years -- the third-longest active streak among all publicly traded REITs, behind Federal Realty Investment Trust (57 years) and Essex Property Trust. Formed in 1984 and headquartered in Orlando, Florida with just 85 employees, NNN owns 3,692 freestanding properties across all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico. The business model is deliberately simple: acquire well-located properties, lease them to creditworthy tenants under 10-to-20-year triple-net leases where the tenant pays all operating expenses, and collect rent. NNN does not manage properties, does not build speculative developments, and does not venture outside the United States. This simplicity is not a limitation; it is the competitive advantage.

At a reference price of approximately $43.77, NNN yields roughly 5.5% on an annualized $2.40 per share dividend (based on the $0.60 quarterly dividend declared January 2026). That yield is 90-130 basis points wider than Realty Income (O, approximately 4.2%) and Agree Realty (ADC, approximately 4.0%), despite NNN carrying a longer dividend streak, comparable occupancy, and a more conservative capital structure than both. The yield spread to Realty Income has historically averaged approximately 80 basis points; the current 130-basis-point spread implies either a re-rating opportunity or a permanent discount that history does not support. For fiscal year 2025, NNN reported core FFO of $3.41 per share and AFFO of $3.44 per share, each up 2.7% year-over-year, while completing a record $931 million of acquisitions at a 7.4% weighted average initial cash cap rate. The AFFO payout ratio stands at 69%, which is comfortable and provides meaningful room before the dividend would be at risk.

The company navigated two simultaneous tenant disruptions in 2025 -- a furniture retailer (Badcock) bankruptcy and a restaurant operator (Frisch's) lease termination followed by a new tenant dispute -- and emerged with occupancy recovering to 98.3% by year-end after dipping to 97.5% in Q3 2025. The total impact of these two tenant events amounted to less than 1% of annual FFO per share, a testament to the real estate quality and management's rapid resolution capabilities. The CFO transition from long-tenured Kevin Habicht to Vincent Chao was executed smoothly, and the 2026 AFFO guidance of $3.52-$3.58 per share implies 3.2% growth at the midpoint -- an acceleration from 2025's 2.7% growth rate.

The primary risk for NNN over the next 12-24 months is the interest rate environment. The company has $350 million of 3.60% notes maturing in December 2026 and $400 million of 3.50% notes due in 2027, both of which will be refinanced at rates approximately 150-200 basis points higher. This mechanical headwind will add an estimated $10-15 million in annual interest expense and is the single largest constraint on FFO growth. However, the balance sheet's 10.8-year weighted average debt maturity (the longest in the net lease sector), $1.2 billion of available liquidity, and 100% unsecured debt structure provide ample flexibility to manage this refinancing cycle. The business model itself -- 85% necessity-based and service-oriented tenants, domestic-only, e-commerce-resistant -- is structurally well-positioned for economic uncertainty, including the tariff-driven environment that has pressured more cyclical REITs.

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

NNN REIT, Inc. (formerly National Retail Properties) is a fully integrated REIT that acquires, owns, invests in, and develops single-tenant, freestanding properties leased under long-term, triple-net leases. The triple-net lease structure means the tenant is responsible for all property-level operating expenses, including real estate taxes, insurance, maintenance, repairs, and capital expenditures. NNN's role is purely that of a capital allocator and underwriter -- the company sources properties through long-standing tenant relationships, underwrites the credit and real estate quality, and collects contractual rent payments. There is no property management function. There is no construction management team. There is no international operations office. The entire company operates with 85 employees, roughly 40% of whom have been with the company for 10 or more years.

- **Sector / Industry:** Real Estate -- REIT (Retail)
- **Business segments:** Single segment -- net lease real estate (100% of revenue)
- **Geographic exposure:** 100% domestic (all 50 states, DC, and Puerto Rico); top 5 states by ABR: Texas (18.4%), Florida (8.7%), Illinois (5.1%), Georgia (4.5%), Ohio (4.2%)

**Revenue trajectory:** Revenue has compounded at approximately 7.0% annually from FY2020 ($661 million) to FY2025 ($926 million), driven almost entirely by property acquisitions and contractual rent escalators. There is no organic volume growth in the traditional sense -- NNN grows by buying more properties. Revenue per property has increased from $210K to $256K over the same period, reflecting both contractual escalators (the portfolio-wide average is approximately 1.5% annually) and the company's deliberate shift toward higher-value properties in recent years.

**Margin evolution:** G&A as a percentage of revenue has been remarkably stable at 5.0-5.1% over the past five years, reflecting the 85-employee operating model and absence of property management costs. Operating margin (operating income divided by revenue) was 61.5% in FY2025, down from 66.5% in FY2024 and 67.0% in FY2023. The decline is driven by the $29 million spike in impairment losses (4.3 times the FY2024 level of $6.6 million) and elevated real estate expenses from temporary vacancies. Excluding impairments, the operating margin was approximately 64.6% -- stable and healthy. Net margins have been more variable due to the rising interest expense burden: net margin compressed from 45.6% in FY2024 to 42.1% in FY2025, with interest expense increasing $20 million year-over-year.

**Inflection points:**

1. **2020 (COVID stress test):** Revenue declined only 1.5%, occupancy held at 98.5%, and the quarterly dividend rate was maintained throughout. Operating cash flow declined to $450 million from $542 million in 2019, but the dividend was covered. NNN issued $700 million of unsecured notes at attractive rates during the credit market dislocation, demonstrating institutional confidence in its credit quality.

2. **2022-2024 (rate-hiking cycle):** Interest expense rose from $140 million (FY2022) to $204 million (FY2025), a 45% increase driven by refinancing maturing low-coupon debt at higher rates. Despite this, NNN maintained its dividend growth streak and expanded the property portfolio from 3,317 (end of FY2022) to 3,692 (end of FY2025), adding 375 properties over three years.

3. **Late 2024 -- tenant disruptions:** Two tenant events in Q4 2024 -- the Badcock furniture chain filing bankruptcy (35 properties) and the Frisch's restaurant operator requiring eviction (64 properties) -- created temporary vacancy pressure. Occupancy dipped to 97.5% in Q3 2025 before recovering to 98.3% by Q4 2025. Management's rapid resolution of these vacancies (releasing or selling 75% of affected properties within four quarters) demonstrated the quality of the underlying real estate and the operational capability of the asset management team.

4. **2025 (record acquisitions, CFO transition, CIO appointment):** NNN invested a record $931 million in acquisitions at a 7.4% cap rate while simultaneously resolving the tenant vacancies, executing a $500 million bond offering, transitioning the CFO role, and appointing a new Chief Investment Officer. The fact that AFFO per share still grew 2.7% during this operationally complex year is a strong signal about the resilience of the business model.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:** The dividend is safe by multiple measures. The AFFO payout ratio of 69% provides 31 cents of cushion for every dollar of AFFO generated. Even in the stress scenario of a 25% decline in operating cash flow (which has never occurred -- the worst was approximately 17% during COVID in 2020), the dividend would still be covered. The operating cash flow payout ratio of 66% means NNN generated $224 million of free cash flow after dividends in FY2025 -- capital that was available for acquisitions, debt repayment, or further dividend growth.

The critical question is not whether the dividend can be maintained (it can, easily) but whether it can continue growing at 2-3% per year through the refinancing cycle. The answer is yes, but the margin is tightening. The AFFO payout ratio has crept upward from approximately 66% in FY2023 to 69% in FY2025, and the 2026 guidance of $3.55 AFFO midpoint implies a payout ratio of approximately 68% (assuming a $2.42-$2.46 dividend for FY2026). The company has roughly $0.15-$0.20 per share of annual interest expense headwind baked in from the 2026-2028 refinancing cycle, which limits dividend growth to 2-3% per year unless acquisition volume or cap rate spread provides offsetting accretion.

On the Q4 2025 earnings call, CFO Vin Chao stated that the 2026 AFFO guidance midpoint of $3.55 represents "3.2% year-over-year growth, accelerating from 2.7% growth in 2025." CEO Steve Horn reinforced the long-term target of "mid-single-digit consistent FFO growth" over a multiyear horizon. If NNN can deliver 3-4% AFFO growth, dividend growth of 2-3% is sustainable indefinitely at a 68-72% payout ratio.

## PART V -- Business Quality and Moat

- **Moat type:** Scale advantages, relationship-based deal sourcing, and long lease durations creating switching cost barriers
- **Moat trajectory:** Stable to slightly widening
- **Pricing power evidence:** Renewal rates at 104-108% of prior rents through FY2025 (per Q3 and Q4 2025 earnings calls); contractual rent escalators averaging 1.5% annually across the portfolio
- **Return on invested capital trend:** Difficult to calculate for a net lease REIT in the traditional sense; the relevant metric is the acquisition cap rate spread over cost of capital, which was approximately 100-150 basis points in FY2025

NNN's competitive advantage derives from three interlocking sources. First, the tenant relationship network. CEO Steve Horn stated on the Q3 2025 call that "NNN historically sources most of its acquisitions through long-standing relationships" and that in Q2 2025, "8 of the 11 closings this quarter were with existing relationship partners." These relationships -- built over 17+ years by CIO Josh Lewis and the broader acquisitions team -- give NNN a first-and-last-call advantage on sale-leaseback transactions with medium-sized, unrated operators that larger competitors like Realty Income cannot efficiently pursue.

Second, NNN operates in a deliberately unglamorous corner of the net lease market: $10 million to $20 million sale-leaseback transactions with auto service chains, convenience store operators, and QSR restaurants. As Horn noted on the Q3 2025 call: "The $15 million to $20 million deals, we're not seeing that much competition outside of our ordinary competitors. We did 5 deals this past quarter under $5 million." This small-deal focus insulates NNN from the intense competition for $50-$200 million portfolios where private capital funds and larger REITs compress cap rates.

Third, the 85-employee operating model generates enormous operating leverage. NNN produces approximately $10.9 million of revenue per employee -- among the highest in the REIT sector. The triple-net lease structure means there is no property management staff, no construction team, no tenant improvement budget. G&A has been stable at 5.0-5.1% of revenue for five consecutive years. This lean cost structure means that incremental acquisitions flow almost entirely to the bottom line.

**What the moat does not include:** NNN has no brand-driven pricing power in the consumer sense. Its tenants have brand power; NNN does not. The company also has limited ability to raise rents above contractual escalators -- it cannot reprice leases mid-term the way a multifamily or industrial REIT can. The 1.5% annual escalator is a floor and a ceiling, and in a high-inflation environment, NNN's real rental income erodes. The triple-net structure provides inflation protection on the cost side (tenants absorb property tax and insurance increases) but not on the revenue side.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

Capital allocation at NNN follows a consistent, self-funding model that management has articulated clearly across all eight quarters of earnings calls analyzed for this report. The priorities are:

1. **Dividends:** $443 million paid in FY2025, representing 66.4% of operating cash flow. This is the non-negotiable first claim on cash. The 36-year streak is the company's most important institutional asset, and management has demonstrated through COVID and tenant disruptions that it will not be compromised.

2. **Acquisitions:** $931 million invested in FY2025 at a 7.4% weighted average initial cash cap rate, with a weighted average lease term of 18+ years. The 2026 guidance embeds $600 million of acquisitions at the midpoint, funded through approximately $210 million of retained free cash flow, $130 million of dispositions, and leverage-neutral incremental debt financing. All FY2025 acquisitions were structured as sale-leasebacks with existing relationship tenants -- no portfolio acquisitions, no development-stage bets.

3. **Dispositions (capital recycling):** $190 million of dispositions in FY2025 at a 6.4% cap rate on income-producing assets, including 67 vacant properties. The 100-basis-point spread between disposition cap rates (6.4%) and acquisition cap rates (7.4%) represents accretive capital recycling. NNN is selling lower-yielding, mature assets and reinvesting at higher yields. This is intelligent portfolio management.

4. **Debt management:** NNN issued $500 million of 4.60% notes due 2031 in July 2025, redeemed $400 million of 4.00% notes at maturity in November 2025, and entered a $300 million delayed-draw term loan in December 2025 with forward-starting swaps fixing SOFR at 3.22%. The term loan gives NNN a new tool in its capital structure toolkit -- shorter-duration, lower-cost floating-rate debt that management will use opportunistically when the debt duration mismatch (10.8 years on debt vs 10.2 years on leases) provides flexibility.

5. **Equity issuance (ATM):** NNN issued 1.93 million shares through its ATM program in FY2025 at an average net price of $42.07, raising $81 million. In FY2024, it issued 4.65 million shares at $45.49 for $212 million. ATM issuance is a necessary but dilutive funding source. At current stock prices ($43-$44), ATM issuance is barely accretive relative to the 7.4% acquisition cap rate once the 5.5% dividend yield on new shares is accounted for. Management has signaled reluctance to issue equity at depressed prices: on the Q3 2025 call, Horn stated that "if the equity is where it's at today, dispositions could be an alternative form of equity to help fund deals."

**Capital allocation grade: A-** -- Disciplined, self-funding model with appropriate prioritization of dividends, accretive acquisitions, and balance sheet management. The only deduction is the modest ATM dilution at prices that are marginally accretive, and the inability to materially accelerate growth without a higher stock price.

## PART X -- Risks and Stress Tests

**Risk #1: Refinancing headwind ($1.15 billion in 2026-2028 at below-market coupons)**
The $350 million of 3.60% notes due December 2026 and $400 million of 3.50% notes due 2027 will be refinanced at estimated rates of 5.20-5.25% (per CFO Chao's Q4 2025 call commentary: "if you're just talking about where could we price a 10-year today, it'd probably be in the 5.25-ish maybe 5.20%"). This adds approximately $10-15 million of annual interest expense, reducing FFO per share by approximately $0.05-$0.08. Probability: certain. Severity: moderate. Mitigant: staggered maturity schedule, $1.2 billion liquidity, potential for rate cuts reducing refinancing costs.

**Risk #2: Tenant concentration in discretionary/cyclical services**
Dave & Buster's (3.6% ABR) and Camping World (3.5% ABR) are consumer-cyclical names with leveraged balance sheets. In a recession, these tenants would face pressure. Additionally, theaters (3.7% ABR) remain structurally challenged by streaming trends. Probability: moderate (recession scenario). Severity: low to moderate (combined 10.8% ABR; maximum loss scenario assuming 50% rent impairment on these tenants would be approximately $50 million, or 5.4% of ABR). Mitigant: low in-place rents, strong real estate locations, NNN's ability to re-lease or sell properties (as demonstrated with Badcock and Frisch's vacancies).

**Risk #3: Impairment acceleration**
Real estate impairments jumped from $6.6 million (FY2024) to $28.6 million (FY2025), a 4.3x increase. At $29 million per year, impairments consume roughly 4% of operating cash flow. If this level persists or increases, it signals deteriorating asset quality in parts of the portfolio. Probability: moderate. Severity: low (non-cash charge; real impact is through reduced disposition proceeds). Mitigant: triple-net structure means NNN does not bear property-level maintenance costs; impairments are typically on vacant properties being disposed.

**Risk #4: Tariff and macro uncertainty**
NNN's 10-K explicitly cites tariffs as a risk factor. However, the practical exposure is low: 85% of ABR comes from service-based and necessity tenants that cannot be imported from abroad. The indirect risk is that tariff-driven inflation increases tenant operating costs and compresses consumer spending, which would pressure the discretionary tenants more than necessity tenants. CEO Horn confirmed on the Q1 2025 call that auto service and car wash tenants reported "the last 2 months, they've seen an uptick in their sales" -- tariff-driven higher new-car prices appear to be benefiting the auto repair and maintenance ecosystem.

## Rate Sensitivity and Macro Positioning

NNN is among the most interest-rate-sensitive REITs in the public market, and this is the single most important variable for the stock price over the next 12-24 months.

**Why NNN is rate-sensitive:** The 10.2-year weighted average lease term means NNN's income stream behaves like a fixed-income instrument. When risk-free rates rise, the present value of that income stream declines. The spread investing model (acquiring assets at 7-8% cap rates funded with 4-5% debt) compresses as the cost of debt rises. And the 5.5% dividend yield competes directly with high-yield corporate bonds and investment-grade preferreds -- when the 10-year Treasury was at 1.5% in 2021, NNN's yield was uniquely compelling; at 4.5%, it faces more competition from fixed-income alternatives.

**Variable rate exposure is minimal:** As of December 31, 2025, only $348 million (7.2% of total debt) was variable rate. A 100-basis-point increase in SOFR would add approximately $3.5 million in annual interest expense -- negligible. The $200 million of forward-starting interest rate swaps fixing SOFR at 3.22% through January 2029 further limit exposure. Pro forma for full drawdown of the term loan, floating rate debt represented just 1% of total debt per Chao's Q4 2025 commentary.

**NNN's beta of 0.83 confirms the bond-like behavior:** the stock moves more with Treasury yields than with the S&P 500. In a rate-cutting cycle, NNN is a leveraged play on falling yields. In a rate-rising environment, it underperforms equities while offering yield support.

**Tariff and trade policy positioning:** NNN is among the best-positioned REITs for a tariff-driven economic environment. The tenant base is overwhelmingly domestic service businesses -- auto repair, convenience stores, car washes, QSR restaurants. As CFO Chao noted on the Q1 2025 call: "Between service tenants and nondiscretionary tenants, that's about 85% of our ABR." The indirect benefit is that tariff-driven higher new car prices extend the useful life of existing vehicles, increasing demand for auto repair and auto parts services. CEO Horn confirmed on the Q1 call that auto service and car wash tenants reported sales upticks in the preceding months. NNN's exposure to import-dependent tenants is negligible.

**Inflation dynamics under triple-net leases:** The triple-net structure provides asymmetric inflation protection. On the cost side, tenants absorb all property-level inflation (taxes, insurance, maintenance). NNN's primary cost inflation exposure is G&A (5.1% of revenue, guided to 5.5% in 2026) and debt costs (mostly fixed). On the revenue side, contractual escalators average 1.5% annually across the portfolio -- below recent CPI levels, meaning real rental income erodes in a high-inflation environment. This is the structural trade-off of the net lease model: you get cost insulation at the expense of revenue lag.

## COVID Resilience and Historical Stress Test

The COVID-19 pandemic was the most severe real-world stress test for net lease REITs in decades. NNN passed it.

During FY2020: revenue declined only 1.5% ($672 million to $661 million) despite widespread tenant closures; occupancy held at 98.5% (vs 99% pre-COVID); net earnings fell 23.5% to $229 million from impairments ($21 million) and rent deferrals; operating cash flow declined to $450 million from $542 million in 2019; and the quarterly dividend rate was maintained at $0.52 per share throughout.

NNN collected over 95% of base rent during Q2 and Q3 2020 even as theaters and full-service restaurants temporarily closed. The tenant mix proved critical: convenience stores (essential, stayed open), automotive service (essential, stayed open), and QSR restaurants (drive-through operational) carried the portfolio. NNN also issued $700 million of unsecured notes at attractive rates during the peak of credit-market dislocation in March 2020 (the 2.50% notes due 2030 and 3.10% notes due 2050), demonstrating that the capital markets viewed NNN's credit as investment-grade even under maximum stress.

The dividend streak survived intact. No cut, no freeze, no "rebasing." Compare this to WPC (dividend cut in 2023/2024 to exit office), VEREIT/Spirit (dividend cuts prior to merging with O), and numerous smaller net lease REITs that froze dividends during COVID. NNN's willingness to maintain the streak through the pandemic is precisely the institutional signal that differentiates it from peers and justifies the premium valuation that comes with 36 consecutive years of increases.

Post-COVID, NNN has deliberately reduced exposure to the tenant categories that caused the most stress. Theaters declined from approximately 4.5% of ABR in 2020 to 3.7% in 2025. Full-service restaurants declined from approximately 8.5% to 6.4%. Meanwhile, automotive service (the most resilient category during lockdowns) increased from approximately 14% to 18.6%. This portfolio rotation demonstrates that management learns from stress events and actively repositions the portfolio to reduce future vulnerability.
