## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE below $42. HOLD at $43.94. Dividend Safety: 8.5/10. Confidence: High.**

NNN REIT is the most boring company in your portfolio, and that is exactly the point. Formerly National Retail Properties, this Orlando-based single-tenant net lease REIT has increased its dividend for 36 consecutive years -- the third-longest streak among all publicly traded REITs, trailing only Realty Income (O, 30+ years but reset its series after restructuring) and Federal Realty (FRT, 57 years). Only FRT and Essex Property Trust have longer active streaks in the entire REIT universe. That 36-year streak spans five recessions, the Global Financial Crisis, a pandemic, and a 525-basis-point rate-hiking cycle. The dividend was never cut, never frozen, never "restructured." It went up, every single year.

The stock is priced like the market has forgotten this. At $43.94, NNN yields approximately 5.5% ($2.40 annualized based on the $0.600/share quarterly dividend declared January 2026, per 2025 10-K). That yield is 90-130 basis points wider than Realty Income (~4.2%) and Agree Realty (~4.0%), despite NNN having a longer dividend streak, comparable occupancy, lower leverage relative to enterprise value, and a more disciplined acquisition strategy. The spread exists because NNN is smaller ($8.3B market cap vs O's $50B+), has no investment-grade tenant disclosure requirements, and lacks the "blue chip REIT" marketing halo. That is the opportunity.

The business in 2025 was solid but not spectacular: revenue grew 6.6% to $926M, operating cash flow reached $667M (+5.0%), and NNN invested $931M in acquisitions at a 7.4% weighted average cap rate (2025 10-K Item 7). Occupancy held at 98.3%. The weighted average remaining lease term was 10.2 years. Interest expense rose $20M to $204M as the company refinanced maturing low-coupon debt at higher rates -- the single largest near-term headwind, and the one the market is correctly pricing. Net earnings per share dipped to $2.07 from $2.16, entirely attributable to higher interest expense and $29M of real estate impairments (vs $7M in 2024).

The key question for dividend investors is not whether NNN can sustain the dividend -- it can, comfortably -- but whether it can continue growing it at 2-3% per year while simultaneously refinancing $1.15B of notes due in 2026-2028 at rates 100-200bps above the maturing coupons. My answer is yes, but barely. The AFFO payout ratio is stretching toward 70-72% (from ~65% three years ago), and management will need to thread the needle between acquisition volume, ATM dilution, and debt cost for the next 18 months. The margin for error is thin but nonzero.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**

1. **36-year dividend growth streak with 5.5% current yield is mispriced vs peers.** NNN's yield spread over O has averaged ~80bps historically; it is currently ~130bps. Either NNN re-rates tighter (10-15% upside) or the market is assigning a permanent discount that history says is unjustified. The tenant base (convenience stores, auto service, QSR) proved more recession-resistant during COVID than O's office/industrial exposure.

2. **Acquisition cap rates of 7.4% vs 4.2% cost of debt = positive spread investing.** NNN is acquiring assets at a 320bp positive spread to its weighted average debt cost. Every $100M of net acquisitions funded at the margin adds roughly $3.2M of incremental NOI. The pipeline was $931M in 2025, almost double the $565M in 2024. This is the growth engine.

3. **Balance sheet is investment-grade clean with no secured debt.** 100% of NNN's $4.82B debt is unsecured. Weighted average interest rate is 4.2%, weighted average maturity is 10.8 years (2025 10-K Item 7A). The $1.2B credit facility (only $348M drawn) plus $300M term loan provide ample liquidity. Debt-to-gross-assets at 42%. No covenants are in stress.

**Top 3 risks to the dividend (specific):**

1. **$1.15B of debt maturities in 2026-2028 at 3.55-4.39% coupons will be refinanced at 4.5-5.5%.** The 2026 Notes ($350M at 3.73%) and 2027 Notes ($400M at 3.55%) will add approximately $10-15M of incremental annual interest expense, reducing FFO per share by $0.05-0.08. This is real, mechanical, and not priced into consensus estimates.

2. **Tenant concentration in discretionary/cyclical services.** Top 5 tenants represent 17.8% of ABR: 7-Eleven (4.3%), Mister Car Wash (3.8%), Dave & Buster's (3.6%), Camping World (3.5%), Kent Distributors (2.6%) (2025 10-K Item 1A). Dave & Buster's and Camping World are consumer-cyclical names with leveraged balance sheets. A recession would pressure these tenants.

3. **Impairments are accelerating.** Real estate impairments jumped from $6M (2024) to $29M (2025), a 4.3x increase. Management acknowledges this is likely to continue. At $29M/year, impairments consume roughly 4% of operating cash flow -- manageable but worth monitoring as a canary for tenant distress.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- Q1 2026 earnings (April 30, 2026) -- consensus $0.52 EPS, revenue $238M
- Federal Reserve rate path: each 25bp cut reduces variable-rate interest expense by ~$0.9M/year on current $348M drawn on credit facility
- 2026 Note maturity ($350M at 3.73%) -- refinancing terms will signal cost of capital trajectory
- Potential dividend increase announcement (typically Q4 or early Q1) -- 37th consecutive year

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

NNN REIT, Inc. is a fully integrated REIT formed in 1984, headquartered in Orlando, Florida with 85 employees. The company acquires, owns, and develops single-tenant, freestanding properties leased primarily under long-term, triple-net leases. Under a triple-net lease, the tenant is responsible for all property-level operating expenses including taxes, insurance, maintenance, repairs, and capital expenditures. NNN's role is effectively that of a passive landlord collecting rent checks -- the simplest possible real estate business model.

**The acquisition cap rate narrative is critical.** NNN acquired properties at a 7.4% cap rate in 2025 while disposing of properties at a 6.4% cap rate. This 100bp spread between acquisition and disposition cap rates represents accretive capital recycling -- NNN is selling lower-yielding, higher-quality assets and reinvesting at higher yields. This is intelligent portfolio management.

More importantly, NNN's 7.4% acquisition cap rate in 2025 compares favorably to Realty Income's typical 6.0-6.5% and Agree Realty's 6.5-7.0%. NNN is buying in a less competitive corner of the market (smaller, unrated tenants, service-oriented businesses) and being compensated for it. The question is whether the higher yield adequately compensates for the higher credit risk. Given NNN's 98%+ occupancy through multiple cycles, the answer is a qualified yes.

**Construction pipeline:** NNN has committed to fund construction on 19 properties with a total commitment of $136M, of which $67M had been funded as of December 31, 2025, leaving $70M remaining. This is a small but growing part of the strategy that offers higher yields than open-market acquisitions.

**ATM equity issuance:** NNN issued 1.93M shares through its ATM program in 2025 at an average net price of $42.07, raising $81M. In 2024, it issued 4.65M shares at $45.49 for $212M. Equity issuance at these levels is modestly dilutive but necessary to fund acquisitions without over-leveraging. At $42-43 per share, ATM issuance is barely accretive relative to the 7.4% acquisition cap rate once you account for the 5.5% dividend yield on the new shares. This is the tightest part of the capital allocation equation.

NNN is the most boring option -- and for a dividend investor, boring is a feature. The company does not chase headlines, does not expand internationally, and does not pivot into new asset classes. It buys gas stations, car washes, and Taco Bells in strip-mall America, collects rent, and mails you a check. There is deep institutional value in that consistency.

The yield discount to WPC is warranted (WPC's dividend record is now impaired). The yield premium over O and ADC partly reflects NNN's smaller size and lower tenant quality, but the premium is too wide for the actual risk differential. In a normal rate environment, NNN should trade at a 50-80bp spread to O, not 130bp.

**The Realty Income comparison deserves elaboration.** O is the 800-pound gorilla in net lease, with a market cap 6x NNN's and monthly dividend payments that attract a massive retail investor base. O has diversified into industrial, gaming (Bellagio, Encore Boston Harbor), and European assets -- a strategy that broadens the opportunity set but introduces currency risk, political risk, and complexity. NNN has refused to follow this path. There is no international exposure, no gaming, no industrial. The portfolio is 100% domestic, overwhelmingly retail/service, and operationally simple.

This simplicity means NNN's management team of 85 employees generates more revenue per employee ($10.9M) than any net lease REIT I am aware of. O has approximately 400+ employees generating roughly $4.8B in revenue -- about $12M per employee, but with vastly greater operational complexity. NNN's lean model is a competitive advantage that is underappreciated by the market.

**Agree Realty (ADC) is the growth competitor.** ADC trades at a lower yield (~4.0%) because it targets investment-grade tenants (67% IG per recent disclosures) and has delivered superior FFO/share growth. ADC is the right REIT for investors who want dividend growth over current income. NNN is the right REIT for investors who want current income with moderate growth. These are different strategies for different portfolio allocations, and both have a place.

**W.P. Carey (WPC) is the cautionary tale.** WPC cut its dividend in late 2023/early 2024 by approximately 20% to exit its office portfolio. The stock collapsed and has not recovered to pre-cut levels. WPC's yield at ~5.8% is now wider than NNN's, but the broken dividend streak means WPC carries a permanent credibility discount. NNN's willingness to maintain its streak through COVID, through the rate-hiking cycle, and through tenant bankruptcies is precisely the institutional signal that WPC no longer provides. The premium NNN earns for its 36-year streak is not irrational -- it is the market correctly pricing institutional credibility.

## PART X -- RATE SENSITIVITY AND MACRO RISKS

NNN is among the most interest-rate-sensitive REITs in the universe, and this is the single most important variable for the stock price over the next 12-24 months.

**Why NNN is rate-sensitive:**
1. **Long-duration assets with contractual rents.** The 10.2-year WALT means NNN's income stream behaves like a fixed-income instrument. When risk-free rates rise, the present value of that income stream declines.
2. **Spread investing model.** NNN's economic model is acquiring assets at 7-8% cap rates funded with 4-5% debt. As rates rise, the cost of debt rises, compressing the acquisition spread. At some point, acquisitions become uneconomic.
3. **Yield comparison.** At 5.5%, NNN competes directly with high-yield corporate bonds and investment-grade preferreds. When the 10-year Treasury was at 1.5% in 2021, NNN's 5.5% yield was compelling; at 4.5%, it is less so.

**Variable rate exposure:** As of December 31, 2025, only $348M (7.2% of total debt) was variable rate. A 100bp increase in SOFR would add approximately $3.5M in annual interest expense -- negligible. NNN has effectively hedged its near-term rate exposure through fixed-rate notes and forward-starting swaps ($200M notional at 3.22% fixed through January 2029).

**The bull case for rates:** The Fed is widely expected to continue cutting in 2026. Each 25bp cut reduces NNN's variable-rate interest expense marginally but, more importantly, compresses the risk-free rate against which NNN's 5.5% yield is compared. If the 10-year Treasury moves from 4.0% to 3.5%, NNN's yield spread widens from 150bp to 200bp, making the stock meaningfully more attractive to income-seeking capital. A 150bp spread to the 10-year at 3.5% implies a 5.0% yield and a stock price of approximately $48 -- roughly 9% upside from here.

**The bear case for rates:** If inflation re-accelerates and the Fed pauses or reverses cuts, NNN would face a triple headwind: higher refinancing costs on maturing debt, compressed acquisition spreads, and relative yield unattractiveness. In this scenario, the stock could test the 52-week low of $38.44.

**Beta:** 0.83 per FMP profile, confirming below-market sensitivity to broad equity moves but not zero. NNN moves more with Treasury yields than with the S&P 500.

**Tariff and trade policy risk:** NNN's 2025 10-K explicitly cites tariffs as a risk factor, noting that financial and economic conditions including tariffs may adversely impact NNN and its tenants (2025 10-K Item 1A). However, the practical exposure is low. NNN's tenants are overwhelmingly domestic service businesses -- you cannot import a car wash or a convenience store visit from China. The indirect risk is that tariff-driven inflation increases tenant operating costs (construction materials, equipment replacement) and compresses consumer spending, which would pressure the discretionary tenants (Dave & Buster's, Camping World) more than the necessity tenants (7-Eleven, Mister Car Wash). NNN is better positioned for a tariff-induced economic slowdown than REITs with industrial, logistics, or import-dependent retail tenants.

**Inflation risk:** NNN's leases typically provide for limited increases in rent through fixed escalators or CPI-linked adjustments (2025 10-K Item 7). In a high-inflation environment, these escalators may lag actual inflation, eroding real rental income. However, the triple-net structure means NNN's cost base is largely insulated -- tenants absorb property tax increases, insurance cost inflation, and maintenance inflation. NNN's primary inflation exposure is through its own G&A (which is small at 5.1% of revenue) and through its debt costs (which are mostly fixed).

## PART XI -- COVID RESILIENCE AND STRESS-TEST TRACK RECORD

The COVID-19 pandemic was the most severe real-world stress test for net lease REITs in decades, and NNN passed it -- not perfectly, but decisively.

During 2020:
- Revenue declined only 1.5% ($672M to $661M) despite widespread tenant closures
- Occupancy held at 98.5% (vs 99% pre-COVID)
- Net earnings fell 23.5% to $229M, primarily from impairments ($21M) and rent deferrals
- Operating cash flow declined to $450M from $542M in 2019
- The quarterly dividend rate was maintained at $0.52/share through the entire pandemic
- NNN issued $700M of unsecured notes at attractive rates during the peak of credit-market dislocation, demonstrating institutional confidence in the credit

The dividend streak survived intact. No cut, no freeze, no "rebasing." NNN emerged from COVID with the same strategy, the same leadership, and the same commitment to the streak. This matters enormously for dividend growth investors who rely on the signal value of an unbroken record.

Compare this to WPC, which cut its dividend in late 2023/2024 during the office portfolio exit, or to VEREIT (now merged into O), which cut its dividend in 2014 due to accounting issues. NNN has never had such an episode. That institutional memory is worth something.

The tenant mix proved critical during COVID. Convenience stores (essential, stayed open), automotive service (essential, stayed open), and QSR restaurants (drive-through, stayed open) carried the portfolio through lockdowns. Theaters and entertainment (then ~12% of ABR, now ~11%) were the primary source of stress. NNN's deliberate reduction in theater/entertainment exposure from ~12% to ~11% post-COVID reflects lessons learned.
