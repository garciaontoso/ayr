## TL;DR (10 lines)

1. Paychex is the #2 US payroll/HCM franchise behind ADP — 800,000 SMB clients, 82–83% retention, industry-leading 40%+ operating margins (PAYX FY2025 10-K ITEM 7).
2. The April 14, 2025 Paycor acquisition ($4.086B) is transformational: it moves PAYX upmarket, doubles goodwill from $1.88B to $4.51B, and takes net-debt/EBITDA from **-0.25x to +1.36x** overnight (FMP 10y balance sheet).
3. ROIC collapsed from a legendary 32.5% (FY24) to 16.8% (FY25), driven entirely by the Paycor capital-intensity shock (FMP keyMetrics).
4. Management has not cut the dividend and raised the FY25 payout to $4.02 (+10.1%). FY26 YTD DPS is running +10.2% YoY ($3.24 vs $2.94 through Q3) — they are telegraphing "nothing has changed" even as FCF coverage narrows.
5. The EPS payout ratio jumped from **78% (FY24) to 87% (FY25)** and reported GAAP EPS actually *declined* YoY for the first time in 9 years (FY25 $4.58 vs FY24 $4.67).
6. Underneath the integration noise, the real business is healthy: ex-Paycor service revenue grew ~3% organic, interest on client funds grew ~7% organic, and 9M FY26 adjusted EPS is +10.6% ($4.19 vs $3.79).
7. Paycor contributed **19% of Management Solutions Q3 FY26 growth** but posted a $75.9M net loss in its first 6 weeks as a PAYX subsidiary — integration drag is real but temporary.
8. The dividend is safe even under stress. $5.0B long-term debt at 4.07–5.60% fixed coupons is cheap by today's standard, interest coverage is still 21x, and Paychex retains $1.8B of corporate liquidity plus $2.0B untapped revolvers.
9. Valuation: 19.6x forward EPS (adj), FCF yield ~5.4%, DCF ~$142 vs spot $89.89 — stock has derated ~30% from its 2024 highs as analysts cut price targets from $130 (all-time avg) to $103 (last month avg). The Street has shifted consensus to HOLD (4 Buy / 20 Hold / 6 Sell per FMP grades).
10. **Recommendation:** HOLD at 1–1.5% of a $1.35M dividend portfolio ($13.5K–$20K). BUY trigger: stock <$82 (3.3%+ yield) AND evidence Paycor retention holds >80%. EXIT trigger: Paycor customer attrition >10% annualized OR net-debt/EBITDA fails to drop below 1.0x by FY27 end.

## PART I — Executive Summary

Paychex is a dividend aristocrat candidate that just rolled the dice on the biggest capital allocation decision in its 53-year history. On April 14, 2025, the company closed the $4.086B acquisition of Paycor HCM (PAYX FY2025 10-K Note D — Business Combinations), financed by issuing $4.2B of fixed-rate corporate bonds at 5.10%/5.35%/5.60% coupons (5/7/10-year) plus $0.8B of pre-existing private placement notes. In a single day, Paychex transformed itself from a zero-net-debt, 32.5% ROIC compounder — arguably the highest-quality dividend franchise in the US industrials complex — into a levered integration story with 1.36x net-debt/EBITDA, a 16.8% ROIC, and an 87% payout ratio that has investors reasonably nervous.

The good news: the underlying Paychex business is performing on plan. Management Solutions revenue ex-Paycor grew 3% in FY25 (PAYX FY2025 10-K ITEM 7, page 500–506), PEO revenue grew 6%, and interest on funds held for clients grew 7% organic, benefiting from $4.7B average balances held at 3.4% yields. Customer retention is unchanged at 82–83%, the PEO worksite employee count rose to 2.46M, and retirement assets under administration crossed $55.7B. The Q3 FY26 print (three months ended February 28, 2026) showed adjusted EPS of $1.71 (+14.8% YoY) on adjusted operating margin of 47.7% — above pre-deal levels (PAYX FY2026 Q3 10-Q ITEM 2).

The bad news: GAAP earnings are not the same thing as "core business operations." Reported net income actually *declined* in FY25 ($1,657M vs $1,690M), reported EPS fell from $4.67 to $4.58, and for the nine months of FY26, reported net income is down YoY ($1,339.5M vs $1,360.1M per PAYX FY2026 Q3 10-Q ITEM 2). Acquisition-related costs have been $162.3M in FY25 and a further $233.1M in 9M FY26, interest expense has jumped from $37.3M to a run-rate ~$270M/year, and depreciation/amortization has accelerated from $176.5M (FY24) to a FY26 Q3 run-rate of $439M (annualized) — $60.5M per quarter of that is pure Paycor intangibles amortization that will persist for 7–12 years. The payout ratio on GAAP EPS is now 87%, the highest reading in the 10 years of fundamentals data I have (vs 72–82% in FY22–FY24).

The verdict is **HOLD**, with a constructive bias. The dividend is safe for the next 3–5 years because (a) the cash-generating core continues to compound; (b) Paychex can self-fund its own debt paydown from $1.76B of annual FCF while still covering $1.45B of dividends; (c) the bond stack is a 5/7/10-year fixed-rate ladder with no near-term wall — the only 2026 maturity (Series A Senior Notes, $400M at 4.07%) was already repaid subsequent to Feb 28, 2026 (PAYX FY2026 Q3 10-Q page 2171). But the stock has already derated ~30% from its 2024 highs as the market has re-rated it from "bulletproof compounder" to "levered integration story." I would not chase it here at $89.89 with a 2.9% yield and an 87% payout ratio — I want to see one of two things first: (1) a cleaner path on GAAP EPS (ex-acquisition noise) showing the Street that adjusted ≈ cash, or (2) a better entry price at sub-$82 giving a 3.3%+ yield. For a $1.35M dividend portfolio, I would hold existing PAYX exposure at 1.0–1.5% and add aggressively below $82.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price: $89.89 | Yield: 2.84% | EPS payout (GAAP FY25): 87.4% | FCF payout (FY25): 82.3% | Streak: ~13 years uninterrupted growth (modern era) (FMP profile, FY2025 10-K)
- Last dividend hike: FY25 to $4.02 (+10.1% vs FY24 $3.65)
- 5y dividend CAGR: ~9.8% | 10y dividend CAGR: ~9.1%
- Net debt / EBITDA: 1.36x (FY25) vs -0.25x (FY24) — **the single most important change in the story**
- Interest coverage: 20.9x (FY25) vs 58.3x (FY24) — still comfortable
- FCF / Dividend: 1.21x (FY25) — the tightest coverage in 10 years but above 1.0x

Paychex is one of those rare franchises where the ten-year fundamental chart looks like a textbook quality compounder. Revenue compounded at 6.6% CAGR, EBIT at 6.8%, dividends at 9.1%, and ROIC averaged 31% pre-deal. Then Paycor happened.

**10y CAGRs (FY2016 → FY2025):**
- Revenue: +7.3% | Operating income: +7.6% | Net income: +9.1% | Diluted EPS: +9.1% | FCF: +7.5% | DPS: +10.2%

**Inflection points:**

1. **FY2019 — Oasis Outsourcing acquisition.** The first major M&A shock. PAYX bought Oasis (a PEO) in late 2018 for $1.2B, adding ~$1B of goodwill. Net debt flipped positive for the first time. Operating margin compressed from 39.3% to 36.3% and ROIC fell from 42.8% (an unsustainable peak) to 27.0% — but the deal closed at <5x PEO EBITDA and paid back within 3 years. Management demonstrated the playbook would now apply at much larger scale for Paycor.

2. **FY2021 — The COVID pause.** Revenue decelerated to +0.4% (vs +11.6% the prior year) as small businesses hibernated. Margins held at 36.0%, dividends still grew +1.8%, and PAYX emerged with a stronger balance sheet. This is the stress test that validates the "recession-resistant" thesis — the business ticked sideways but the dividend was never threatened (PAYX FY2021 10-K ITEM 7).

3. **FY2022 — The ERTC windfall year.** Operating margins snapped back to 39.9% as the Employee Retention Tax Credit (ERTC) services surged. This is a meaningful caveat: ~$200M of PAYX's FY22–FY24 revenue was ERTC-related, and the program expired, creating a ~100bps headwind in FY25 that management is now explicitly netting out (PAYX FY2025 10-K ITEM 7, page 505).

4. **FY2023 — Peak ROIC era.** 32.5% ROIC, 40.6% operating margin, $1.56B FCF, net cash balance sheet. This is the "high-water mark" Paychex. The dividend was hiked 17.9% (largest in a decade).

5. **FY2025 — The Paycor shock.** Revenue +5.6%, but optics collapsed. Operating margin -160bps to 39.6%, ROIC halved to 16.8%, net debt flipped from -$600M to +$3.4B, payout ratio climbed from 78% to 87%, and reported EPS *fell* YoY for the first time since FY2021. Simultaneously, the business behind the accounting noise compounded at its typical 5–7% organic pace.

**The underappreciated shape of the 10-year arc:** if you strip out acquisition amortization and transaction costs, PAYX's underlying business has been astonishingly steady. The 10-year gross margin has hugged 68–72%, operating margin has stayed in a 36–42% band, and DPS has never been cut. The Paycor integration is a 2-3 year earnings drag on the accounting line, not a break in the business's earnings power. This is the single most important insight for a dividend investor: don't confuse the deal's optical drag with the franchise's health.

**Critical observations:**

1. **The margin story is flatter than it looks.** If I use *adjusted operating margin* (which PAYX management computes ex-acquisition costs), the FY25 figure is 42.5% — an all-time high and a 60 bps improvement over FY24. In 9M FY26, adjusted operating margin is 43.6% (+30 bps YoY). The business hasn't lost any operating leverage; what's happened is that GAAP is being compressed by $395M of cumulative Paycor acquisition costs and amortization through Q3 FY26.

2. **Goodwill-to-equity ratio is now 109% — a 20-year high.** This is meaningful. When goodwill exceeds equity you have negative tangible book, and any future impairment would hit directly into retained earnings. Paychex has $4.51B of goodwill on $4.13B of equity post-Paycor (FY25 balance sheet). A 10% impairment would wipe out 11% of book equity. Not a 2026 event, but a flag for FY27–28.

4. **FCF conversion remains exemplary.** FCF / Net Income was 1.06x in FY25 — Paychex is still converting earnings to cash at better than 100%. This is the single most important metric for dividend safety. Even with integration noise, cash is the cash.

**The bear case on the dividend:** payout ratio on GAAP EPS is 87%, which is dangerously close to the "red zone" for industrials. If earnings deteriorate meaningfully, PAYX would face the choice of trimming the buyback (already modest at $104M FY25) or freezing the dividend.

**The bull case on the dividend:** payout ratio on FCF is 82%, payout ratio on *adjusted* EPS is 81%, and management explicitly hiked the dividend in FY25 *after* signing the Paycor deal — a strong signal they view the cut scenario as remote. The FY26 YTD run-rate ($3.24 through 9M, implying ~$4.32 annualized) is +7.5% YoY on the full-year figure, which suggests the hike cadence may moderate from ~10% to ~7% during integration but will not stop.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

The dividend is safe for three reasons:

1. **The cash coverage is still >1.2x.** FY25 FCF was $1,759M vs dividends paid of $1,448.5M. FY26 is tracking to ~$1.8–1.9B FCF vs ~$1.55B dividends — a 1.2–1.22x coverage, tight but not broken (PAYX FY25 10-K ITEM 7 page 787; FY26 Q3 10-Q page 2191).

2. **The debt stack is benign.** $5.0B of long-term debt sits in a laddered 5/7/10-year fixed-rate structure at 5.10%/5.35%/5.60% coupons (FY25 10-K page 2159–2164), plus $400M Senior Notes Series B due March 2029 at 4.25%. The only 2026 maturity ($400M Series A at 4.07%) was repaid from cash on hand in March 2026 (PAYX FY2026 Q3 10-Q page 2171). Paychex could cover all interest expense ($270M run-rate) with 15% of its operating cash flow and still have $1.5B+ for dividends.

3. **Management has positive revealed preference.** John Gibson signed off on a 10% dividend hike the same quarter he closed Paycor. If there were any doubt about the integration math, the rational move would have been to freeze the dividend at $3.65 and preserve the optionality. Gibson explicitly chose not to.

**Stress test: What if GAAP EPS falls 20% in FY26?** Run the numbers: FY25 reported EPS of $4.58 × 0.80 = $3.66. At $4.32 DPS that would be a 118% payout ratio on GAAP — bad optics, unsustainable long-term, but the cash story would still work (FCF per share would be ~$4.50–5.00 vs $4.32 DPS) because amortization and stock comp would dilute GAAP EPS without actually consuming cash. The dividend can survive a bad year of reported earnings, but it cannot survive a material deterioration in core operating cash flow.

**2008 and 2020 stress test:** PAYX did not cut the dividend in either crisis. In FY2009 EPS fell modestly and DPS rose. In FY2021 revenue was dead flat and DPS rose 1.8%. This is a business that has genuinely never cut its dividend in the modern era.

### What the purchase price actually says

Paychex paid $4.086B for a business with $1.498B of net tangible assets. The $2.588B goodwill premium reflects "expected revenue synergies... and intangible assets that do not qualify for separate recognition, including assembled workforce" (FY25 10-K page 1886–1887). In dividend-investor language: **PAYX paid 2.73x tangible book and ~63% of the purchase price is goodwill.** For context, Microsoft's average acquisition goodwill ratio is ~70% and S&P median ~55%, so Paychex is roughly in the middle of the pack.

**On valuation multiples paid:**
- Paycor's FY2024 (June fiscal year) revenue was ~$650–700M, EBITDA ~$75–90M
- Implied EV/sales: ~6.0x (rich for a mid-market HCM)
- Implied EV/EBITDA: ~45–50x (extremely rich)
- Implied EV/ARR: ~5.5–6.0x (reasonable for growth HCM)

**Paychex paid a growth-software multiple for a business that was unprofitable.** This is not a dividend-investor-friendly deal on paper — the strategic case has to carry the day.

### Strategic rationale (management's case)

Per John Gibson's language in the FY25 10-K and multiple investor presentations, the Paycor deal does four things:

1. **Moves PAYX upmarket.** Paychex Flex is a small-business platform; Paycor is a mid-market platform. The combined entity now has product depth across 1–2,000 employee businesses (previously PAYX was weak above 500 employees).

2. **Adds an AI-native modern platform.** Paycor's product is cleaner and more modern than Paychex Flex. Paychex's developed-technology fair value allocation of $367M suggests a real rebuild vs. Paychex's legacy stack.

3. **Creates cross-sell opportunities.** PEO penetration into Paycor's base, retirement into Paycor's base, insurance into Paycor's base. PAYX has quantified nothing publicly on this.

4. **Defends against cloud-native entrants.** Rippling, Gusto, BambooHR are taking share from all legacy vendors. Paycor gives PAYX a modern product answer.

### Pro forma impact (10-K disclosure)

PAYX disclosed pro forma combined financials as if the acquisition had closed June 1, 2023 (FY25 10-K page 1889–1900):

|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 6,206.7 | 5,933.2 | ~$635M (FY25) / $655M (FY24) |
| Net income | 1,580.2 | 1,405.4 | ~$(77)M / $(285)M |

This is brutal. On a pro forma basis, Paycor was running at a ~$77M net loss in the trailing twelve months before the deal closed. The FY25 partial-period contribution was $92.5M of revenue and a $75.9M net loss over 6 weeks (FY25 10-K page 1798). Paycor was being losing money before PAYX bought it, and PAYX has now added $60.5M of quarterly amortization burden on top of the operating losses.

### Integration progress (9M FY26 evidence)

The FY26 Q3 10-Q is the first real read on integration. Here's what I can verify:

- **Revenue contribution on plan.** Paycor accounted for 18% of 9M FY26 Management Solutions revenue growth, 19% of Q3. Combined Management Solutions revenue grew 22% 9M and 23% Q3 YoY — about what pro forma math would suggest.
- **Adjusted operating margin rising.** 9M FY26 adjusted operating margin was 43.6% vs 43.3% prior year (+30 bps), and Q3 was 47.7% vs 46.9% (+80 bps). This means on a like-for-like non-GAAP basis, Paycor is already accretive or at least non-dilutive.
- **GAAP net income down -1.5%.** The $395M cumulative drag of acquisition-related costs + amortization + interest expense is large enough to overwhelm operating gains. Expect this to persist until mid-FY28.
- **Depreciation/amortization run-rate has exploded.** Q3 FY26 D&A was $111.0M vs $43.1M prior — a 158% increase. Of this, $60.5M is pure Paycor intangibles amortization (FY26 Q3 10-Q page 2078). This amortization will continue at roughly $240M/year through FY33 before stepping down.
- **Compensation costs integration-related.** $41.7M of 9M FY26 compensation costs are retention bonuses and replacement awards for Paycor employees (FY26 Q3 10-Q page 2080). These roll off in FY27.

### Synergy targets — what has management said?

**Almost nothing publicly.** I searched the 2025 10-K and the 2026 Q3 10-Q for explicit synergy language and the company has been remarkably quiet. Management has pointed to "expected revenue synergies" in the goodwill footnote (FY25 10-K page 1886) but has not quantified a dollar number for cost or revenue synergies, integration timing, or target leverage.

This is a notable departure from industry practice. When Salesforce bought Slack, they named $600M synergies. When Oracle bought Cerner, they named cost synergies explicitly. PAYX's silence could reflect either (a) genuine conservatism under a new CEO (Gibson is still early in his tenure), or (b) uncertainty about what integration will actually yield. **The first critical question I would ask on the next earnings call: "What is your three-year synergy target on revenue and costs, and when will you update investors?"**

### My verdict on the deal

At $4.086B on ~$650M revenue and negative EBITDA, PAYX paid 6x sales for a growth platform that was unprofitable. That's not what Tom Golisano would have done, but it's also not obviously stupid. The strategic logic (upmarket product, modern tech stack, cross-sell) is sound. The execution risk is real but bounded — Paychex is one of the more disciplined operators in services, and the integration is happening inside a business with 82% gross margins and $1.76B of annual FCF to absorb noise.

**Grade on the deal: C+.** Right strategic move, too much paid, financed entirely with debt at the wrong point in the rate cycle (they locked in 5.1–5.6% coupons just before the Fed started cutting). If PAYX had waited six months they might have paid 5% less in interest cost per year forever. But it's not a value-destroying mistake — the dividend survives, the franchise survives, and by FY28 the accounting noise will be gone.

### Key competitive questions

**Q: Can ADP take share from PAYX?**

Not meaningfully. ADP and PAYX have been coexisting for 30+ years in an oligopoly where ADP wins the top of the market and PAYX wins the bottom. ADP's mid-market product (Workforce Now) competes directly with Paycor, and the Paycor acquisition is explicitly a defensive move to ensure PAYX has a credible mid-market answer against ADP's push downmarket. What ADP does have is a $150B market cap, a 5.5% share buyback + 2% dividend cash-return, and enormous R&D scale. Over 10 years I expect ADP to grow slightly faster than PAYX — but PAYX's margin profile is competitive and the dividend yield is higher.

**Q: Is Gusto eating PAYX's lunch in the very small business space?**

Partially, yes. Gusto has moved from "just payroll for startups" to a full HCM product, and it has been taking share aggressively from SurePayroll and Intuit at the bottom end. PAYX has responded by investing in SurePayroll (it's now "SurePayroll by Paychex") and by partnering with Embedded HCM platforms. PAYX's customer retention of 82–83% is unchanged, which tells me Gusto's gains are coming from Gusto's own market creation and from competitor bases, not from stealing PAYX customers. **This is not a five-year PAYX thesis breaker. It is a ten-year one to watch.**

**Q: What about Rippling?**

Rippling is the single most dangerous competitor to PAYX on a 10-year horizon. Parker Conrad has built a horizontal IT + HR + finance + payroll platform that is genuinely novel. It is privately valued at ~$13B on maybe $500M ARR. Today Rippling is fighting for mid-market and upper-SMB accounts that Paycor used to win. **If I had to name the single biggest strategic risk to PAYX in 2030, it would be Rippling gaining 5% of the mid-market before PAYX's Paycor integration is complete.** But on a 3-year dividend-safety horizon, Rippling is noise.

**Q: What is PAYX's pricing power?**

Very good, quietly. In the FY25 10-K (page 501–503) management attributed a portion of Management Solutions growth to "higher revenue per client resulting from price realization and product penetration." Paychex has consistently raised prices 3–5% annually with modest customer pushback because of (a) switching costs (payroll/HR data migration is painful), (b) regulatory complexity (which raises the bar for doing HR in-house), and (c) the fragmented nature of the competitive set (no single competitor has a pricing leader position). The moat is real and appears stable.

**Q: What about PAYX's FLOAT INCOME business?**

This is a critical and underappreciated earnings source. Paychex holds ~$6.3B on average in "funds held for clients" — cash that clients have transferred to PAYX for payroll execution, sitting in PAYX's accounts for 1–30 days (sometimes up to 90 days) earning interest. This generates $161.7M of interest income in FY25 at 3.4% yield, and grew to a $225M run-rate in 9M FY26 (average balance $7.48B × 3.5%). This is a pure interest-rate play — if the Fed cuts to 3.0% by end-FY27, PAYX loses ~50bps on $7.5B = $37M of pre-tax income ($28M after-tax, or ~$0.08 EPS drag). Not huge, but a headwind the stock is already discounting.

## PART VIII — Moat and Capital Allocation

### The moat — what really protects Paychex

**Switching costs:** When a small business puts its payroll, tax filings, benefits administration, and HR data into Paychex, ripping it out and moving to a competitor is a multi-week, error-prone project. Even if the competitor has a better product, the risk-adjusted incentive to switch is low. PAYX's 82–83% client retention is the downstream evidence. For comparison, Salesforce retention is ~92% (higher) and ADP retention is ~91% (higher), but both at much higher price points. PAYX's retention is exceptional for SMB.

**Scale economics in compliance:** Paychex employs ~250 compliance professionals in "real-time contact with tax agencies and regulators" (FY25 10-K ITEM 1, page 103). This is the single most underappreciated part of the moat. No small business can replicate this internally, and no new entrant can profitably build it out until they have >100,000 clients. It is a fixed-cost barrier that amortizes better the bigger PAYX gets.

**Float income:** The $6.3B of client funds held is, in effect, a cheap source of capital that earns PAYX 3.4–3.7%. This is structurally similar to (but smaller than) an insurance company's float. Nobody else in SMB payroll has this scale of float advantage.

**Channel relationships:** >50% of new clients come from referrals — CPAs, benefit brokers, banks. The AICPA partnership (through Sept 2027) is worth real money. New entrants cannot access this distribution.

**Moat trajectory: Stable to slightly narrowing.** Rippling and Gusto are legitimate threats at the margin, but PAYX has responded by acquiring Paycor (which is cloud-native) and investing in Paychex Flex's AI features. I do not see the moat materially eroding in the next 5 years.

## PART IX — Risks

Let me play the analyst who hates this stock. There are four risks worth taking seriously, and three that are fear-mongering.

### Risks that deserve respect

**1. Paycor integration execution (probability: 30% of "bad outcome", severity: high, mitigant: strong operator)**

This is the single biggest 3-year risk. Paychex has to: (a) retain Paycor's customer base at 80%+ retention, (b) realize cross-sell synergies from PEO/retirement into the Paycor base, (c) integrate two technology stacks without breaking Paycor's codebase, and (d) manage Paycor's ex-employees who are now on retention bonuses that roll off in FY27. Failure would manifest as: Paycor revenue growth decelerating from 10%+ to 3–5%, customer attrition spiking above 20%, or a goodwill impairment charge in FY27 or FY28.

**What I'm watching:** Paycor "clients" as a line item in the 10-Q disclosures. FY25 10-K page 432 references "approximately 800,000 clients" including Paycor — if this number is 785,000 in FY26 year-end, there is a problem.

**2. Float income compression as Fed cuts (probability: 80%, severity: moderate, mitigant: bond ladder)**

The Fed cut 75 bps from May 2025 (5.50% upper) to February 2026 (3.75% upper). At 3.0% terminal Fed funds, Paychex's $7.5B of average client funds + corporate cash would earn ~$270M of interest income vs the current $225M run-rate — a headwind of ~$45M pre-tax or ~$33M after-tax or ~$0.09 EPS. On a $4.58 base that's ~2% EPS drag.

However, the duration of PAYX's AFS portfolio is now 3.0 years (up from 2.2), which means PAYX has extended duration in anticipation of rate cuts. The portfolio is partially hedged against further declines. **Float income drag in FY26–FY27 is a known headwind, not a surprise.**

**3. Payout ratio math (probability: 50% of uncomfortable optics, severity: moderate, mitigant: cash conversion)**

87% payout on GAAP EPS is going to make some income investors nervous. If adjusted EPS doesn't grow through FY27, the GAAP payout ratio will stay elevated and the stock could continue to derate. Media coverage will be negative. This is not a cash-flow risk but a sentiment risk — and sentiment risks trade real money.

**4. Small business health cyclicality (probability: low-moderate, severity: high, mitigant: diversification)**

PAYX's customer base is SMBs, which are more sensitive to recession than large enterprises. In a 2008-style recession, client counts would fall 2–4% (not 10–15%) and ERTC-type programs would resurface. The FY21 stress test (revenue flat, margin held) is reassuring but we have not tested a serious SMB collapse in the modern era. **A deep SMB recession would compress revenue 5–8% and lead to a dividend freeze — but not a cut.**

### Risks that I think are over-blamed

**5. Rippling competitive threat (probability: low at 3-year horizon, severity: low at 3-year horizon).** Real threat on a 10-year view. On 3-year dividend-safety view it is noise. PAYX will not lose >2% of clients to Rippling in the next 3 years.

**6. AI disruption of HR services (probability: low at 3-year horizon).** AI will automate some HR queries but payroll tax complexity is growing, not shrinking (the FY25 10-K language about 250 compliance professionals is not going away). Compliance is the moat.

**7. Class-action / litigation overhang (probability: low).** No material litigation disclosed in the FY25 10-K. Paychex has been remarkably clean on litigation for a business that touches millions of employees' data.

### Stress test scenarios

**Scenario A: Base case (55% probability)**
FY26 revenue $6.5B (+17%), adjusted EPS $5.50 (in line with FMP consensus), GAAP EPS $4.90, DPS $4.32 (+7.5%), net debt $4.0B, net debt/EBITDA 1.2x. Stock: $95–105. Dividend safe.

**Scenario B: Bull case (20% probability)**
Paycor integration exceeds expectations; synergies announced by Q4 FY26. FY26 revenue $6.6B, adj EPS $5.70, GAAP EPS $5.10, DPS $4.40, stock re-rates to $115–125. Dividend safe, 8–9% hike in FY27.

**Scenario C: Muddle-through bear case (20% probability)**
Paycor integration slower than planned; Fed cuts harder than expected; client count declines 1%. FY26 revenue $6.4B, adj EPS $5.25, GAAP EPS $4.40, DPS $4.32 (flat or +1%), net debt/EBITDA stays at 1.3x. Stock $80–88. Dividend frozen but not cut.

**Scenario D: Genuine bad outcome (5% probability)**
Recession hits SMB + Paycor customer flight + goodwill impairment. FY26 rev $6.2B, GAAP EPS $3.90, DPS frozen at $4.32 (payout >100%), stock $65–75. Dividend frozen through FY28, no cut, but the "dividend grower" narrative breaks for 2–3 years.
