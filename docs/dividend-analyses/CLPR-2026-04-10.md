## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: AVOID. Do not initiate. Existing holders should reassess. -- Dividend Safety: 2/10 -- Confidence: High**

Clipper Realty is a textbook value trap masquerading as a high-yield micro-cap REIT. The ~7-8% dividend yield on paper looks like compensation for illiquidity risk in a concentrated NYC portfolio. In reality, it is compensation for a combination of structural risks that no rational income investor should accept at anything close to the current payout level: negative book equity, $1.28B of property-level mortgage debt against ~$1.23B of total assets, two major commercial properties in active litigation with their lenders, a $33.8M impairment charge taken in Q1 2025 on a property subsequently sold at a loss, and operating cash flows that have declined from $31.9M (FY2024) to a $16.5M run rate (9-month 2025 annualized at $22.0M). The company paid $18.5M in dividends and distributions in FY2025 against $22.6M of operating cash flow -- an AFFO payout ratio that is stretched dangerously thin once you account for the capital needs of an aging, rent-stabilized Brooklyn portfolio and an active $160M+ development project at Dean Street.

The single most important fact about CLPR that most yield-seekers miss: **the continuing investors (the Bistricer family and associated entities) own 62.1% of the company on a fully diluted basis through Class B LLC units**. The ~16.1M public shares represent only 37.9% of the economic interest. When Clipper declares a quarterly distribution of ~$4.6M, only ~$1.5M goes to common stockholders -- the other $3.1M flows to the Class B holders. David Bistricer and family are not paying themselves out of excess cash flow; they are paying themselves because the REIT distribution requirement forces it, and the Class B structure ensures the family takes the majority of every dollar distributed.

The loss of the City of New York as the anchor tenant at both 250 Livingston Street (342,496 sq ft, vacated August 2025, ~$15.4M/yr rent) and 141 Livingston Street (206,084 sq ft, lease expired December 2025, ~$10.3M/yr rent) represents a ~$25.7M annual revenue cliff -- roughly 17% of 2025 total revenue. 250 Livingston is in active default on its $125M CMBS mortgage with the loan transferred to special servicing. 141 Livingston faces a foreclosure lawsuit from Wells Fargo as trustee, with the court denying the lender's receiver motion but the case ongoing. The company is simultaneously refinancing, litigating, and hoping for a lease renewal that its own lender is blocking. This is not a stable income play.

**Top 3 reasons someone might own this (being generous):**
1. **Deep discount to replacement cost.** At ~$3.50/share and ~42.5M fully diluted units, the implied equity value is ~$149M. Add $1.28B of debt and you get ~$1.43B enterprise value for a portfolio with $1.15B of net real estate carrying value. NYC multifamily replacement cost per square foot is significantly higher than CLPR's implied value per square foot -- Flatbush Gardens alone (1.75M sq ft, 2,494 units) would cost $500M+ to build today.
2. **Flatbush Gardens Article 11 Agreement.** The 40-year regulatory agreement with NYC HPD provides property tax exemption and enhanced Section 8 payments in exchange for maintaining affordability. This stabilizes cash flow at the company's single largest property and removes most regulatory risk from the rent-stabilized portfolio for four decades.
3. **Dean Street completion optionality.** The 160,000 sq ft, 9-story new-construction residential building in Brooklyn is nearing completion and, once stabilized, should add $8-12M of incremental NOI on a ~$160M cost basis. At stabilization, this asset alone could be worth $200-250M based on Brooklyn cap rates.

**Top 3 reasons this dividend should be rated 2/10:**
1. **Negative stockholders' equity of -$30.7M.** This is not a transitional balance sheet. Total equity including non-controlling interests is -$80.8M as of December 31, 2025. The company has negative net worth. Every dollar of retained earnings generated has been more than consumed by accumulated losses and distributions.
2. **Two commercial properties in lender disputes simultaneously.** 250 Livingston is in default with cash management account activated. 141 Livingston faces a foreclosure lawsuit. Together these properties carry $225M of mortgage debt. If either loan is accelerated and foreclosed, CLPR loses a property and the associated cash flow -- and may face guarantor liability.
3. **Dividend is not covered by GAAP earnings -- ever.** CLPR has never reported positive net income in any fiscal year since its 2017 IPO. The company has posted cumulative GAAP net losses exceeding $120M. The dividend is funded entirely through the gap between operating cash flow and net income (i.e., depreciation add-backs and working capital timing), plus additional borrowing. When the music stops on refinancing access, the dividend stops.

**Next 12 months catalysts (high-probability):**
- **141 Livingston lease resolution.** Either the five-year extension with NYC gets finalized (positive) or the litigation with the CMBS lender escalates to forced sale/receiver (very negative). The court has already denied receiver motions twice, but the lender is appealing.
- **250 Livingston disposition or modification.** The company has signaled it will not continue supporting the operating and debt service shortfall. A loan modification, discounted payoff, or deed-in-lieu is likely in 2026. Any resolution crystallizes a loss but removes a drag.
- **Dean Street stabilization.** The $160M development project should begin leasing in 2026. Stabilized NOI adds to cash flow but is pledged to the $147M of Dean Street debt.
- **Tribeca House refinancing.** The $360M Deutsche Bank mortgage at 4.506% matures March 2028. Pre-positioning for this refinancing should begin in 2026. At current interest rates, the refinancing cost will be materially higher.
- **Dividend cut or suspension.** The probability of a dividend reduction in the next 12-18 months is, in my assessment, above 50%. The commercial lease cliff plus rising interest costs on variable-rate Dean Street debt squeeze cash available for distribution.

These two refinancing events alone represent $689M of debt -- over half the total -- that will reprice higher. At a 6% refinancing rate, the incremental annual interest expense would be approximately $16-20M, which would consume virtually all of the company's current free cash flow after distributions.

## PART VI -- PROPERTY-LEVEL DEEP DIVE

### Flatbush Gardens (Brooklyn) -- The Anchor

The crown jewel and the millstone, simultaneously. Flatbush Gardens is a 59-building, 2,494-unit, 1.75M sq ft residential complex in the Flatbush neighborhood of Brooklyn. It is the largest property in the portfolio by far, generating an estimated 35-40% of total revenue.

The June 2023 Article 11 Agreement with NYC HPD is a 40-year commitment that effectively converts Flatbush Gardens into a quasi-affordable housing complex. The company committed to maintain rents at existing area median income (AMI) levels, lease 249 units to formerly homeless families, pay prevailing wages, and execute a 3-year capital improvement plan estimated at $27M ($18.9M spent through September 2025). In exchange, the property receives property tax exemptions and enhanced Section 8 payments.

**My assessment:** This was a defensive move. Flatbush Gardens was already overwhelmingly rent-stabilized under the Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019, which eliminated most avenues for deregulating stabilized units. The Article 11 Agreement traded theoretical optionality (that the rent stabilization laws might someday be relaxed) for concrete cash flow stability (tax exemptions and Section 8 payments). For a company with CLPR's leverage, this was the right call. But it also permanently caps the upside on the portfolio's single largest asset.

### Tribeca House (Manhattan) -- The Cash Cow

Two towers (21-story and 12-story) in Tribeca with 483,000 sq ft of residential and 77,000 sq ft of retail. This is the portfolio's premier asset by location and rent levels. The Equinox gym lease was extended to August 2040 in September 2025 with increased rent and a $3M renovation credit (Q3 10-Q Note 5). Market-rate rents in Tribeca are among the highest in Manhattan.

The $360M Deutsche Bank mortgage at 4.506% matures March 2028. This is the single most important refinancing event on the horizon. At current cap rates, Tribeca House is likely worth $500-600M, providing significant equity cushion for refinancing. But the rate reset will be material -- at 6% on $360M, annual interest increases by ~$5.4M.

### 250 Livingston Street (Brooklyn) -- The Crisis

This 370,000 sq ft office building in downtown Brooklyn was the company's most reliable commercial asset until NYC gave termination notice in February 2024. The city vacated its 342,496 sq ft on August 23, 2025. The terminated lease provided approximately $15.4M per annum in rent plus reimbursements.

The $125M CMBS mortgage with Citi Real Estate Funding at 3.63% is now in serious distress. The property was transferred to special servicing. In March 2025, the lender declared an event of default for failure to deposit revenue into the cash management account. In October 2025, the company failed to make its required escrow deposit and received notices of nonpayment. In November 2025, the company formally requested transfer to special servicing for loan modification, stating it would not continue to support the operating and debt service shortfall. A consent and cooperation agreement for sale is being negotiated.

**My assessment:** 250 Livingston is effectively a dead asset walking. The company will likely lose it through a discounted sale, deed-in-lieu, or foreclosure. The question is whether the loss is contained at the property level (the $125M mortgage is non-recourse to the parent, subject to limited guarantees) or whether it triggers cross-default provisions or guarantor liability. The company's engagement of IronHound Management (whose principal is CLPR board director Roberto Verrone) to advise on the loan modification raises governance questions.

### 141 Livingston Street (Brooklyn) -- The Lawsuit

This 216,000 sq ft, 15-story office building in downtown Brooklyn was leased entirely to NYC agencies at $10.3M per annum. The lease expired December 27, 2025. The company and NYC agreed to a five-year extension in April 2025, but the CMBS loan special servicer attached conditions the company rejected. The lender (Wells Fargo as trustee) filed a foreclosure lawsuit in March 2025. The court denied the lender's receiver motion twice (May and September 2025), finding the lender's likelihood of prevailing appeared remote, but the case continues with appeals.

**My assessment:** The legal situation is actually somewhat favorable for CLPR -- the court's language suggesting the lender's claims appear remote is encouraging. But the property is caught in a three-way standoff between CLPR, NYC, and the CMBS special servicer. If the lease extension gets finalized at ~$10M/yr, the property remains viable. If not, another $100M of property-level debt becomes distressed.

### Dean Street (Brooklyn) -- The Bet

A 160,000 sq ft, 9-story new-construction residential building with 9,000 sq ft of retail. This development project has been under construction since 2021 and is approaching completion. Total capitalized cost is approximately $160M+, financed by $115M senior + $32M mezzanine loans from MF1 Capital (refinanced May 2025 at SOFR+2.65% with a SOFR floor of 2.25%, effective rate ~6.8% at September 2025).

The financing history of Dean Street tells a story of serial refinancing under pressure. The original acquisition loan with Bank Leumi ($30M, December 2021) was refinanced with Valley National Bank ($80M, February 2023), then expanded with a $20M supplemental draw (September 2023), then refinanced again with a $115M senior + $8M mezzanine construction loan from Valley (August 2023), and then refinanced yet again with MF1 Capital for $115M senior + $32M mezzanine (May 2025, with $18.25M of additional availability under the mezzanine). The company subsequently drew an additional $5.25M from the mezzanine. Each refinancing has been at successively higher leverage, with the initial maturity now set at May 2027 -- barely 24 months out -- though three one-year extensions are available if performance conditions are met.

The interest rate cap with US Bank (capping SOFR at 6%) provides some protection, but the all-in rate at September 2025 was already ~6.8%. During construction, the company capitalized $6.8M of interest in the first nine months of 2025 and an additional $1.9M of non-cash capitalized interest (Q3 10-Q cash flow statement). Once the property is placed in service and interest stops being capitalized, the full ~$10M annual interest charge hits the income statement.

Upon stabilization, Dean Street should generate $8-12M of NOI based on 169,000 sq ft at Brooklyn market rents of ~$55-65/sq ft for new construction. At a 4.5-5.0% stabilized cap rate, the property could be worth $160-267M, providing meaningful value creation. However, the $147M of debt at variable rates means interest expense alone is ~$10M/yr -- the property will be essentially breakeven at stabilization until rates decline or the loans are refinanced. The initial 2-year lease-up period (2026-2027) will be negative carry: the $4.25M shortfall reserve deposited with MF1 Capital is designed to bridge this gap, but it covers only approximately 5 months of interest at current rates.

### 1010 Pacific Street (Brooklyn) -- Quiet Performer

This 119,000 sq ft, 9-story residential building was developed by CLPR and placed in service within the past few years. It was originally financed with an $80M mortgage from Valley National Bank and a $20M supplemental draw. In October 2025, the company refinanced with a new $84.5M loan from Citi Real Estate Funding and Morgan Stanley Bank at 5.73% fixed, maturing October 2030. The refinancing generated ~$2.1M of net proceeds after repaying Valley's $80.4M balance and closing costs.

This is actually one of the cleaner transactions in CLPR's recent history -- a fixed-rate, 5-year mortgage on a stabilized residential property at a manageable rate. The $4.5M step-up in principal ($80M to $84.5M) is modest, and the 5.73% rate, while not cheap, is locked in through 2030.

### 10 West 65th Street (Manhattan) -- The Write-Off

This 76,000 sq ft, 6-story residential building on the Upper West Side was sold on May 30, 2025 for gross proceeds of $45.5M. The company recorded a $33.8M impairment charge in Q1 2025 and an additional $857K loss on disposal. The $31.2M Flagstar Bank mortgage was repaid in full at closing, generating approximately $13M in net proceeds.

The impairment is significant for two reasons: (1) it reveals that CLPR's carried book values for properties may overstate fair value, raising questions about whether other properties (particularly the two distressed commercial assets) are also overvalued on the balance sheet, and (2) the $33.8M charge represents nearly the entire market capitalization of the public equity -- a single property write-down larger than the company's equity market value is an extraordinary statement about the mismatch between accounting values and economic reality.

## PART VII -- DIVIDEND HISTORY AND SUSTAINABILITY

CLPR's dividend history can be divided into three phases:

**Phase 1 -- Pre-COVID growth (2017-2019):** The company paid quarterly distributions that grew modestly after the 2017 IPO.

**Phase 2 -- COVID suspension (2020-2021):** Distributions were suspended in Q2 2020 and did not resume until Q4 2021 at a reduced level. FY2021 total dividends and distributions were only $6.1M vs $17.6M in 2019.

**Phase 3 -- Post-COVID restoration (2022-2025):** Distributions were restored to approximately $4.4-4.6M per quarter (~$17-18.5M annually), where they have remained since.

**Quarterly distribution timeline (total, $000):**

| Quarter | Total Dist. | Per Common Share (est.) |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 2025 | $4,614 | ~$0.095 |
| Q2 2025 | $4,614 | ~$0.095 |
| Q3 2025 | $4,614 | ~$0.095 |
| Q4 2025 | $4,614 | ~$0.095 |
| Annual 2025 | ~$18,455 | ~$0.38 |

The distribution has been flat for eight consecutive quarters. There has been no growth. For a company that has never generated positive GAAP net income, the maintenance of a flat distribution while equity erodes should be viewed as distribution of capital, not distribution of earnings. The REIT status requires 90% of taxable income be distributed; CLPR's REIT taxable income is likely minimal given depreciation shields and interest expense, meaning the actual REIT-required minimum is probably well below what is being distributed.

**FFO and AFFO -- the numbers the company wants you to focus on:**

The quarterly 10-Q provides FFO and AFFO reconciliations. These are the metrics REIT management teams promote because they add back depreciation and non-cash charges that compress GAAP net income. Here is the 9-month comparison:

Two things stand out. First, FFO is deeply negative in 2025 because of the impairment, but AFFO conveniently adds it back. Second, recurring CapEx of only $100K for nine months across an eight-property, 3.5M+ sq ft portfolio is laughably understated. The company is capitalizing virtually all of its property-level spending rather than treating it as maintenance CapEx. If you applied a $3/sq ft maintenance CapEx assumption (conservative for NYC), annual maintenance CapEx would be ~$10.5M, which would reduce AFFO from ~$29.3M annualized to ~$18.8M -- essentially equal to the annual distribution. The AFFO payout ratio, properly adjusted, is approximately 98%.

**Adjusted EBITDA** for 9M 2025 was $56.6M (Q3 10-Q reconciliation), annualizing to ~$75.5M. Against net debt of $1.247B, that yields a net debt / Adjusted EBITDA of approximately 16.5x. ## PART IX -- RENT STABILIZATION AND REGULATORY RISK

This section matters because it is the structural constraint that defines CLPR's residential portfolio.

New York's Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (HSTPA) fundamentally altered the economics of rent-stabilized apartments by:
- Eliminating vacancy decontrol (stabilized units remain stabilized permanently)
- Capping Individual Apartment Improvements (IAI) to $15,000 over 15 years
- Eliminating the 20% vacancy bonus
- Restricting Major Capital Improvements (MCI) rent increases

For CLPR, whose Flatbush Gardens complex contains ~2,494 predominantly stabilized units, this means organic rent growth at that property is capped at the Rent Guidelines Board's annual adjustment (typically 2-4% for one-year leases in recent years). The Article 11 Agreement further constrains rents at AMI levels.

The "Good Cause Eviction" law adds another layer, limiting evictions for tenants who refuse rent increases deemed "unreasonable." Combined with HSTPA, this creates a regulatory environment where CLPR's largest asset -- which comprises roughly 50% of the portfolio's square footage -- has structurally limited pricing power.

NYC Local Law 97 (LL97) adds compliance costs: buildings over 25,000 sq ft must meet carbon emissions caps starting in 2024. CLPR has been investing in roof replacements, LED lighting, and pipe insulation (2025 10-K ITEM 1). The cost of compliance across 59 buildings at Flatbush Gardens alone could be substantial over the next decade.

**The Kuzmich/Crowe rent overcharge litigation** is an additional drag. Tenants at Tribeca House (50 Murray Street / 53 Park Place) have won multiple court rulings establishing that they were overcharged under rent stabilization laws during the period the buildings received 421-g tax abatements. The company has already paid $2.3M to Kuzmich plaintiffs (2022) and $400K to Crowe plaintiffs (2023), plus an additional $175K in overcharges and $94K in attorney fees in 2024-2025. While these amounts are manageable individually, the precedent is set: CLPR's Tribeca House units that received 421-g benefits are subject to rent stabilization, constraining the company's ability to raise rents to market levels on those units.

The broader implication is that CLPR's portfolio straddles two regulatory regimes -- market-rate and rent-stabilized -- and the company has not always correctly identified which regime applies. The series of court losses in Kuzmich/Crowe/Horn suggests compliance gaps that could generate additional liabilities if other tenants bring similar claims.

The public company costs (audit, legal, SEC filings, board fees, LTIP grants) consume several million dollars annually. A going-private transaction at a price that reflects the current distress -- but not the long-term replacement value of the assets -- would be extraordinarily advantageous for the controlling family and punitive for minority holders who bought at higher prices.

There is no poison pill or shareholder rights plan that would prevent this. The charter's explicit permission for officers to compete with the company, combined with 62% voting control, means the Bistricer family could pursue a going-private transaction with minimal opposition. ## PART XII -- CONCLUSION AND DIVIDEND VERDICT

**Dividend Safety Rating: 2/10**

The only reasons this is not 1/10 are: (a) the REIT distribution requirement creates structural pressure to maintain some payout, and (b) the Bistricer family's 62% economic interest means they are personally incentivized to keep distributions flowing. But incentive is not the same as capacity.

CLPR represents the worst combination for a dividend investor: a high yield supported not by earnings or durable cash flow but by leverage, depreciation shields, and a controlling insider who benefits disproportionately from every dollar distributed. The company has negative book equity, two major properties in lender disputes, a $25.7M revenue cliff from commercial lease losses, and a wall of debt maturities starting in 2027 that will reprice at significantly higher rates.

The bull case -- deep discount to replacement cost, NYC multifamily scarcity value, Dean Street optionality -- is real but requires patience measured in years, tolerance for potential further equity dilution or a going-private transaction, and willingness to accept a likely dividend cut in the interim.

For a dividend-focused portfolio, CLPR is disqualified by every screening criterion that matters: negative earnings coverage, negative equity, extreme leverage, concentrated geographic and tenant risk, insider control, and litigation overhang. The yield is the warning, not the opportunity.

**Rating: AVOID for income. SPECULATIVE HOLD only for deep-value investors willing to underwrite a 2-3 year restructuring thesis with 40% probability of permanent capital impairment.**
