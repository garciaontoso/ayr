## TL;DR (10 lines)

1. **REXR is a pure-play Southern California infill industrial REIT at an inflection point** — from the best-performing REIT of 2015-2023 (DPS 10y CAGR ~12.5%, Core FFO up 8x in 10y) to a 2025 print marked by zero acquisitions, $89M of development impairments, a Q2 quarter of negative re-leasing spreads, and two departing co-CEOs.
2. **The headline that matters most is buried in MD&A**: 2025 cash re-leasing spreads were just **10.7% vs 58.8% in 2022** — a 48-point collapse in three years. GAAP spreads fell from **80.9% (2022)** to **23.4% (2025)**. The mark-to-market engine, which was the entire growth thesis, has lost 80% of its torque.
3. **Q2 2025 new leases produced negative spreads of (17.6)% GAAP and (22.9)% cash** (2025 10-K leasing tables). This is not a normalization — it is a pocket of genuine rent roll-down. Market rents are down 22% from the mid-2023 peak per third-party data cited by management.
4. **Co-CEOs Howard Schwimmer and Michael Frankel depart March 31, 2026** — the founders — replaced by Laura Clark (current COO). The company recognized **$60.2M of accelerated non-cash equity compensation in Q4 2025** related to their separation (2025 10-K Other Expenses). This is the most consequential governance event in REXR's history as a public company.
5. **Dividend is safe, growth is over**: 2026 quarterly at $0.435 ($1.74 annualized) — a 4.8% raise, but the first decline in the DPS growth rate since IPO after 10y of low-double-digit raises. Core FFO/sh ~$2.40 → payout ratio ~72% on Core FFO, ~62% on AFFO-adjusted. No coverage distress, but also no visible runway for 10%+ raises anymore.
6. **Balance sheet is a fortress**: Baa2/BBB+ investment grade, $1.245B of revolver capacity, zero drawn, $165.8M cash, 93%+ fixed-rate debt at 3.6-5.0% weighted-average cost, 5.4x net debt/EBITDAre, only $67.6M due within 12 months. The company can absorb two bad years.
7. **$250M buyback in 2025 at $39.51 avg**, and $486.6M authorized into 2028 — management is saying the stock is cheaper than the properties. They are probably right if you believe SoCal infill is structurally short-supplied; they are probably wrong if AB-98 and SB-415 re-tenanting risks bite harder than expected.
8. **The SoCal infill moat is real but less unique than REXR bulls argue** — market rents are still ~40% above 2019 levels even after the 22% drawdown. Land scarcity thesis intact. But the narrative that spreads would stay 50%+ forever was always a cycle-high extrapolation, not a structural feature.
9. **Fair value: $40-$46 on a 15-17x 2026E Core FFO of ~$2.55**. DCF says $18.74 (FMP), consensus PT $42.80, high $45. The $34 print is pricing the co-CEO departure, the spread collapse, and 2% AFFO growth for 3 years. If 2026 leasing spreads stabilize at 10-15% cash and Laura Clark doesn't blow anything up, this is the best entry point since 2018.
10. **Verdict: BUY at $34.23 · Accumulate to 1.0% portfolio weight ($13,500 on $1.35M) · Add below $32 · Trim above $48 · Dividend Safety 8/10 · Confidence Medium-High.** This is a rare case where a quality compounder is trading at book value with a 5% yield because the market is correctly pricing real near-term problems but probably mispricing the long-term terminal value of 51M sqft of irreplaceable SoCal infill.

## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: BUY at $34.23 · Add below $32 · Trim above $48 · Dividend Safety: 8/10 · Confidence: Medium-High**

Rexford Industrial is the cleanest case in the industrial REIT universe of what happens when a growth-REIT compounder runs headfirst into the wall of its own cycle. From the 2013 IPO to the end of 2023, Rexford was the textbook definition of a concentrated thematic winner: pure-play Southern California infill industrial real estate, the most supply-constrained industrial market in the United States, levered to e-commerce, port logistics, and a structural shortfall in buildable land. Revenue grew from $126M in 2016 to $797M in 2023 (a **6.3x expansion in seven years**, or ~30% CAGR), Core FFO per share compounded in the low double digits, the property count went from 213 in 2019 to 373 in 2023 to 419 by year-end 2025, and the dividend ramped from $0.575 in 2016 to $1.8175 in 2025 — a **10-year DPS CAGR of 12.5%** that is essentially unmatched outside of the Prologis/Terreno club at scale (REXR 2025 10-K cash flow statement; FMP ratios array).

Then 2024-2025 happened. Three things collapsed simultaneously.

**First, the mark-to-market engine stalled.** This is the single most important variable in any industrial REIT thesis, because the entire point of owning below-market assets in a rising-rent market is that you capture the spread at the moment of renewal. In 2022, Rexford's full-year net effective leasing spreads ran at **80.9% GAAP / 58.8% cash** (2022 10-K Highlights). In 2023 they were still **77.5% / 58.7%** (2023 10-K). In 2024, spreads dropped sharply to **38.9% GAAP / 28.6% cash** — and even that number was flattered by a single 1.1M sqft extension; stripping that out, spreads were **55.3% / 38.7%** (2024 10-K Highlights). In 2025, spreads collapsed further to **23.4% GAAP / 10.7% cash**, with Q2 2025 *new leases* producing negative spreads of **(17.6)% GAAP / (22.9)% cash** (2025 10-K Leasing Activity table). That is not normalization; that is the tail end of a rent cycle rolling over in real time.

**Second, the growth engine turned off.** Rexford acquired **$970M of properties in 2019**, another large tranche in 2020, 114 properties across 2021-2022, and 48 additional Blackstone-sourced properties in 2023-2024. In 2025 they acquired **zero properties** (2025 10-K Highlights). The company also recorded an **$89.1M impairment charge in Q4 2025** on seven development properties whose strategy was changed from long-term ownership to disposition (2025 10-K Impairment of Real Estate), and it cancelled six more near-term developments totaling approximately 850,000 square feet (2025 10-K Factors That May Influence Future Results). Combined, the development pipeline shrank by roughly 2 million square feet of future supply — and the company explicitly cited CA Assembly Bill 98 and CA Senate Bill 415, effective January 1, 2026, as restrictive new rules on industrial/warehouse development.

**Third, the governance changed.** In November 2025, Rexford announced that **co-CEOs Howard Schwimmer and Michael Frankel — the founders — would depart effective March 31, 2026**, replaced by **Laura Clark, the current Chief Operating Officer** (2025 10-K Management Update). They will remain on the board until their terms expire at the 2026 Annual Meeting. The company recognized **$60.2M of accelerated non-cash share-based compensation in Q4 2025** in connection with their transition and separation (2025 10-K Other Expenses, Note 14). This is the single biggest governance event in REXR's history as a public company. It can be interpreted as either (a) founders stepping away at the cycle top with their equity cashed out cleanly; or (b) strategic repositioning to a professionalized, less entrepreneurial operating model under a proven internal executive. Both readings are at least partially true.

**Variant perception:** The market is pricing REXR as if 2025's problems are permanent. At $34.23 (roughly the lows for the post-2018 era), REXR trades at ~1.06x book value, a 5.1% forward yield, and roughly ~14x my estimate of 2026 Core FFO per share (~$2.40-2.50). That multiple is lower than every other Class-A industrial REIT — Prologis (PLD) trades at ~18x, EastGroup (EGP) at ~19x, First Industrial (FR) at ~17x, Terreno (TRNO) at ~24x (FMP peer extract for 2026-04-09). The discount is not justified by either the balance sheet (BBB+ investment grade, 5.4x leverage, $1.245B revolver dry) or the portfolio quality (51.2M sqft, 96.5% same-property occupancy, single-tenant concentration <2.4%). It is justified purely by three things: (i) leadership transition execution risk, (ii) the fear that the leasing spread collapse extends further, and (iii) legitimate concerns about AB-98/SB-415 development restrictions adding a second-derivative supply constraint (which ironically is bullish for in-place rents over the long term).

**Top 3 reasons to buy at $34.23:**
1. **You are buying 51.2 million square feet of irreplaceable Southern California infill industrial at essentially book value (~1.06x P/B).** The 419 properties sit in Los Angeles (57.3% of portfolio), San Bernardino / Inland Empire West (18.7%), Orange County (10.9%), San Diego (6.8%), and Ventura (6.3%). These are not inner-ring distribution centers you can replicate in Texas or the Inland Empire East — they are the last mile for 20 million people and the largest port complex in North America (LA/Long Beach). Replacement cost of this portfolio is materially above book. If industrial rents re-accelerate even modestly in 2027-2028, the optionality value is enormous.
2. **The dividend is covered, the balance sheet is underlevered, and management is buying back stock at $39.51 average.** Net debt / EBITDAre is 5.2x (below the 6.0x peer average for industrial REITs); debt service coverage is 3.68x; 93%+ of debt is fixed at blended ~3.6-5.0%; only $67.6M of $3.3B in debt matures within 12 months. The Baa2/BBB+ ratings are not at risk. The 2025 buyback ($250M) plus a fresh $500M authorization (plus $13.4M already executed under the new program post year-end) is a credible signal that the board believes the stock is trading below private-market value. Buybacks at 1.06x P/B accrete NAV per share almost immediately.
3. **The mark-to-market story still has runway, just not the runway bulls thought.** Even with market rents down 22% from mid-2023 peak, management explicitly states that market rents **"remain approximately 40% above pre-pandemic levels"** (2025 10-K Factors That May Influence Future Results). Rexford's in-place rents still reflect leases signed 3-7 years ago at pre-2022 market levels. Renewal spreads of 26-29% were still being achieved in Q1-Q4 2025 on **renewal leases** (2025 10-K Renewal Leases table) — it is only *new* leases (where the spread captures both mark-to-market and lease-up gap) that are soft. Over a full cycle of 2026-2029 lease rolls, I estimate ~15-20% cumulative cash NOI growth from embedded rent uplift alone, even at zero market rent growth.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own at $34.23:**
1. **The co-CEO departure creates real execution risk that no outside investor can fully evaluate.** Schwimmer and Frankel were not figureheads — they were deal originators with 30+ years of relationships across every broker, owner, lender, and municipality in the five SoCal counties. Laura Clark, a CFO/COO archetype promoted from within, may be the right operator for a mature, dispositions-focused platform. But "programmatic dispositions into higher-return repositioning projects, share repurchases, and selective acquisitions aligned with rigorous underwriting standards" (2025 10-K Acquisitions and Value-Add) is strategic language for a much smaller, much less aggressive growth story. The market should probably discount REXR's forward multiple by 100-200 bps vs. peers until Clark has 8 quarters of execution under her belt.
2. **Leasing spread visibility is poor and Q2 2025 was a real shock.** Market rents fell 14% in LA County and 6% in Inland Empire West year-over-year; vacancy increased in every one of REXR's five submarkets (2025 10-K General Market Conditions). The Q2 2025 negative-spread new lease print, even if disproportionately impacted by one 106,251 sqft lease, signals that some tenants are renegotiating below prior contract rates. In 2026, 15.1% of the portfolio is up for renewal; in 2027, another 14.0%. If 2026 cash spreads average 5-10% instead of the 15-20% I'm modeling, Core FFO/sh growth is probably zero for two years, not 2-3%.
3. **AB-98 and SB-415 are real, not theater.** California Assembly Bill 98 (enacted 2024, effective January 1, 2026) imposes heightened industrial development standards — setback requirements, truck routing, emissions limits, and notably larger buffers from schools, residences, and parks. SB-415 adds further restrictions on warehouse entitlements. These laws primarily affect new ground-up development, which is why REXR explicitly cancelled six projects. While this is long-term bullish for existing landlords (it ensures no new competition gets built), it also means that Rexford's repositioning/development platform — which was **$228.8M of nonrecurring capex in 2025 alone** (2025 10-K Capital Expenditures) — now faces meaningfully higher friction and longer permitting timelines, which compresses the yield on development spend.

**Next 12-18 months catalysts:**
- **Q1 2026 earnings (~April 2026)**: first quarter under Laura Clark, watch for 2026 Core FFO guidance. Above $2.55 per share = growth intact, $2.30-2.55 = base case, below $2.30 = re-rate lower.
- **2026 renewal spread run rate**: Q1-Q2 2026 leasing data will tell us whether the Q2 2025 negative print was a one-off outlier or the start of a deeper rent cycle.
- **Disposition program**: Rexford signalled heightened emphasis on "programmatic dispositions". Watch for $300-500M of sales over the next 18 months; at above-1.06x P/B, each dollar sold and redeployed into buybacks is NAV-accretive.
- **Stock buyback pace**: $486.6M remaining on the new authorization — if REXR repurchases $200-300M in 2026 at sub-$40, that is 2-3% of the share count retired at book value, which mechanically adds ~5 cents of Core FFO/sh.
- **SoCal market data**: vacancy trajectory in LA County and Inland Empire West, plus any sign of rent stabilization, will be the leading indicator for the stock.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Rexford Industrial Realty, Inc. (NYSE: REXR) is a self-administered and self-managed REIT focused exclusively on owning, operating, and acquiring industrial properties in Southern California infill markets (2025 10-K ITEM 1 Company Overview). The company was incorporated in Maryland on January 18, 2013 and IPO'd the same year. As of December 31, 2025, the consolidated portfolio consisted of **419 properties totaling approximately 51.2 million rentable square feet** (2025 10-K), diversified across 1,558 leases with no single tenant accounting for more than **2.4% of total annualized base rent**. Rexford employs 256 people across five regional offices in Southern California.

The core thesis rests on three pillars the 2025 10-K states in plain language:
- Southern California infill industrial is "the largest, most fragmented industrial market in the nation" (ITEM 1 Competitive Strengths);
- these markets "demonstrate favorable long-term tenant demand fundamentals in the face of an ongoing long-term scarcity and diminishment of supply over time" (ITEM 1);
- Rexford's smaller-footprint portfolio (**average ~26,000 sqft per space**) is positioned to serve last-mile distribution demand that is "less susceptible to changes in global trade flows as compared to large warehouses located within non-infill submarkets" (2025 10-K General Market Conditions).

The co-CEO model had been in place since IPO and was, for better or worse, a defining feature of the company's entrepreneurial deal-sourcing culture.

## PART III · 10-YEAR ARC (2016-2025)

The REXR story in ten years is a compounder that hit every inflection it could hit, and is now navigating the one it cannot out-deal its way around.

**Key milestones:**
- **2013** — IPO at $14/share, 59 properties, 4.1M sqft.
- **2016** — 100+ properties, $126M revenue, $25M net income. First investment grade considerations.
- **2019** — 213 properties, 26.6M sqft. Acquired $970M of properties in 2019 alone. Company issued $100M of senior notes at 3.88-4.03%. Core FFO approached $135M. Credit ratings emerging investment grade.
- **2020** — COVID pandemic. REXR was exceptional: stayed at ~98% portfolio occupancy, collected rent at ~97%, and even accelerated ecommerce-driven tenant demand (2020 10-K Market Fundamentals). Leasing activity "continued at pre-COVID-19 levels" per management commentary.
- **2021** — **$230M Core FFO, 486 leases executed, leasing spreads 42.7% GAAP / 28.8% cash**. Stock rallied to all-time highs above $80 in late 2021.
- **2022** — **The peak year of the cycle**. 442 leases, **leasing spreads 80.9% GAAP / 58.8% cash** — the highest ever reported. Core FFO +45.3% YoY to $334.7M. Same-property average occupancy 98.7%. Revenue +39.6% to $631M. Net debt / EBITDAre 4.95x.
- **2023** — **The last full year of cycle-peak numbers**. 459 leases, **leasing spreads 77.5% GAAP / 58.7% cash**, still extraordinary. 373 properties, 45.9M sqft. Core FFO $475M (+42%). Then in mid-2023 the SoCal industrial market turned: vacancy started to tick up, asking rents started to fall. Management described this as "post-COVID normalization" and "low market vacancy and pervasive supply and demand imbalance" but warned of "signs of a post-pandemic normalizing of tenant demand" (2023 10-K Market Fundamentals).
- **2024** — **The first cracks**. 436 leases, **spreads 38.9% / 28.6%** (headline) or 55.3% / 38.7% excluding the one 1.1M sqft outlier. Blackstone portfolio acquisition of 48 properties across three transactions. Total portfolio occupancy fell to 91.3%. Core FFO $544.6M (+14.6%). Net debt / EBITDAre 5.36x. Same-property average occupancy 96.9%.
- **2025** — **The year of re-rating**. **Zero acquisitions**. **Leasing spreads 23.4% / 10.7%.** **Q2 2025 negative new lease spreads.** **$89.1M impairment** on 7 development projects. **6 projects cancelled** (~850K sqft). **$250M stock buyback** at $39.51 average. **$60.2M accelerated CEO transition compensation.** **Co-CEO announcement of departure**. Core FFO $591M (+8.5%) — the growth rate cut in half from 2024. Same-property NOI growth decelerated to **+1.4% rental revenue / +1.8% rental income** (from ~10% in peak years).

## PART V · DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE

**Forward yield: 5.08% ($1.74 annualized / $34.23).**
**Dividend Safety Score (my proprietary 0-10 scale): 8 / 10.**
**Cut probability: <5% over 3 years, <10% over 6 years, conditional on balance sheet stability.**

### Historical growth pattern

REXR has raised its dividend every year since the IPO with growth rates that averaged **~14% from 2016-2024 and now run at ~4-5%**. The 2025 DPS of $1.8175 represents cumulative 216% growth from the 2016 base of $0.575 — a 12.5% 10-year CAGR that is rivaled only by Prologis (11%) and Terreno (14%) in the industrial REIT space and by almost nothing outside of software and financials.

The 2026 first-quarter declaration of $0.435 (annualized $1.74) represents either a 4% increase from the 2025 run-rate Q4 level or flat vs Q4 — either way it is a **clean break** from the double-digit growth pattern. Management has not explicitly guided on future DPS growth, but the 2025 narrative signals prudence: declining same-property NOI growth, cancelled developments, elevated impairments, and CEO transition are not the usual context for announcing 15% dividend hikes.

**My base case for DPS 2026-2028: $1.74 → $1.82 → $1.92 (4.5%, 5.5% growth).** If the leasing environment normalizes in 2027 with cash spreads returning to 15-25%, growth could re-accelerate to 6-8% by 2028. I do not model a return to 10%+ growth unless market rents turn materially positive again.

### Coverage analysis

On forward basis at $1.74 annualized:
- **Core FFO coverage**: $2.45 estimated / $1.74 = **1.41x** — comfortable, but tighter than historical 1.50x.
- **AFFO coverage**: $2.20 estimated / $1.74 = **1.26x** — adequate, at the lower end of the industrial REIT comfort zone (peers typically 1.30-1.50x).
- **Cash flow coverage**: 2025 CFO $542M / total dividends paid ~$380M = **1.43x** — healthy.

The **payout ratio on Core FFO rose to 76% in 2025** and is the single most important signal of the cycle. In 2021-2023, payout ratios were 66-70%. The 76% in 2025 reflects two things: (i) the modest Core FFO per share growth (6-7% vs. 15%+ historical), and (ii) the continuation of double-digit dividend raises into 2024. Management appears to have recognized that 10-15% DPS raises on 2-4% Core FFO/sh growth is not sustainable, hence the 2026 pause.

### What would cause a cut?

Three scenarios, in ascending order of severity:
1. **Leasing spreads go materially negative for 2-3 quarters** (cash spreads < 0% on renewals), driving same-store NOI growth below -3%. Probability: ~10%.
2. **SoCal industrial market enters a deeper downturn** — vacancy above 8%, market rents down another 15%, triggering tenant bankruptcies and impairment cycles. Probability: ~5%.
3. **Credit rating downgrade to sub-investment grade** forcing a defensive dividend cut to preserve ratings. Probability: <1% given current 5.2x leverage and 3.68x DSCR.

None of these is a base case. The closest historical analogue to 2025's situation is **STAG Industrial post-2009** or **DCT Industrial 2012-2013**, both of which held their dividends through growth deceleration without cutting. REXR's balance sheet is meaningfully stronger than either of those comparables at the time.

### Tax treatment

As a REIT, REXR distributions are treated as ordinary income for U.S. taxpayers (no qualified rate). For the 2025 tax year, the bulk of distributions were classified as ordinary dividends with a small portion as return of capital (2025 10-K Dividends section, dividend composition table). For the user's fiscal residency (China tax resident, US-China treaty 10% WHT on U.S.-source REIT distributions), REIT distributions generally do NOT qualify for the 10% reduced rate and are subject to the **30% U.S. withholding rate on REIT ordinary dividends** unless the investor qualifies for a specific exemption. This is a material drag: a 5.08% gross yield becomes **3.56% net** in the hands of the user after U.S. WHT. If purchased in a tax-advantaged U.S. account (not applicable here), the full 5.08% is retained. I am noting this because the user's actual cash yield from REXR is meaningfully lower than a U.S. domestic investor would receive.

## PART VI · SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA INDUSTRIAL MARKET DEEP-DIVE

### The supply side: why land scarcity is real but less unique than bulls claim

Southern California infill industrial is one of the four genuinely supply-constrained major industrial markets in North America (the others being Northern New Jersey, Miami, and the Greater Seattle area). The constraint has four layers:

1. **Geographic**: LA County is effectively landlocked — Pacific to the west, mountains to the east and north, Mexico to the south. Once you exclude residential, commercial, and open space, the industrial land pool is fixed. The Inland Empire West (San Bernardino, Riverside north of I-10) is the only submarket with meaningful developable land, and it is being consumed rapidly.
2. **Regulatory**: California is the most land-use-restrictive state in the US. AB-98 and SB-415 (effective Jan 1, 2026) impose new setback, truck routing, and emissions requirements specifically targeting warehouse development. Cities like Fontana, Moreno Valley, and Ontario have declared moratoria on new warehouse projects at various times. Orange County municipalities have largely opposed new warehouse capacity for a decade.
3. **Conversion**: Existing industrial land is being converted to higher-value uses — mixed-use residential, commercial, data centers. Management explicitly states that "more industrial property is converted to non-industrial uses than can be delivered" (2025 10-K Factors That May Influence). Net supply is structurally negative in the LA County infill submarkets.
4. **Construction economics**: Land at $2.5-4.0M per acre in LA County infill locations + construction costs of $300-450/sqft = stabilized yields of 4-6% at current rents, which are below what private capital requires and well below public REIT cost of capital at current prices.

All four of these constraints are genuine. My variant perception is that the bull case overstated them as a *moat* vs. other major markets, when in reality they are the *table stakes* for owning Class-A industrial in any top-5 US metro. The real question is not "is SoCal infill supply-constrained" (yes) but "is it *more* supply-constrained than Prologis's global platform, EastGroup's Sun Belt portfolio, or First Industrial's Chicago/NJ/CA mix" (probably not dramatically so).

### The demand side: normalization is happening and is mostly priced

From 2020 through 2022, Southern California industrial demand benefited from a once-in-a-generation surge driven by (i) pandemic-accelerated e-commerce, (ii) reshoring and inventory stockpiling as a reaction to supply chain disruptions, (iii) the Port of LA/Long Beach congestion which pushed tenants to secure capacity "at any price," and (iv) near-zero interest rates that made anything with a cap rate look cheap. Management's own disclosure, citing third-party market data, quantifies the peak precisely:

> "Based on the same third-party market data, overall market rents remain approximately 40% above pre-pandemic levels. Market rent growth within our infill Southern California markets has decreased by approximately 22% from the peak levels reached in mid-2023. This decline follows an average increase of approximately 80% during the pandemic years of 2020 through 2022." — 2025 10-K ITEM 7 Market and Portfolio Fundamentals

Parsing this disclosure (which is unusually explicit for a public REIT): market rents rose 80% in 2020-2022, then fell 22% from mid-2023 to late 2025. The net is approximately +40% from pre-pandemic baseline. Applied to REXR's in-place portfolio, this means:
- Leases signed in **2019-2020** (still ~5-6 years into 5-7 year terms, most rolling 2024-2026) carry rents that are ~35-45% below current market — the rich mark-to-market opportunity.
- Leases signed in **2021-2022** carry rents that are 5-15% above current market — potential rent roll-down on renewal (see Q2 2025 negative spread print).
- Leases signed in **2023-2024** carry rents close to or slightly above current market — neutral to mildly negative spreads.

This is **exactly what the Q2 2025 leasing data shows**. Q1 2025 renewal spreads were +29.4% (2019/2020 vintage rolling), Q2 new lease spreads were (17.6)% (2022/2023 vintage rolling short), Q3 renewal spreads were +26.5% (back to older vintage), Q4 renewal spreads were +26.0%. The average obscures the vintage mix. The **full-year 2025 weighted-average renewal spread of +28.3% GAAP / +14.7% cash** tells me that the mark-to-market engine is **still working on renewals** — just not on new leases, which capture the new-market reality more accurately.

### The market outlook: base case and scenarios

**Base case (probability 55%):** Market rents stabilize in Q2-Q3 2026 at current levels (+40% vs pre-pandemic, -22% from peak), vacancy in LA County peaks at 5.5-6.0%, and REXR's cash leasing spreads normalize to **12-18% blended** in 2026-2027 as the remaining 2019-2021 vintage leases roll. Core FFO/sh grows at 3-4% in 2026 and 4-6% in 2027-2028.

**Bull case (probability 20%):** Market rents bottom in H1 2026 and begin rising modestly as new supply drops off a cliff due to AB-98/SB-415 and cancelled developer pipelines. Rexford's cash spreads re-accelerate to **20-30% blended** by 2027. Core FFO/sh grows 6-8% in 2027-2028. Stock re-rates to 18x at $2.70 2028E Core FFO = **$49**.

**Bear case (probability 25%):** LA County vacancy rises to 7-8% on a combination of continued rent pressure and specific logistics tenant weakness (small 3PLs, import/export-sensitive tenants hit by tariff policy). Cash spreads turn negative for 2-3 quarters in 2026. Development impairments continue. Core FFO/sh goes flat to slightly negative in 2026. Stock trades to **$26-29** before stabilizing.

## PART VII · MOAT + CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### The moat: concentrated, not unique

REXR's moat is best described as **high concentration in a genuinely scarce asset class, executed by a specialized team**. It is not a wide moat in the Prologis or Realty Income sense (Prologis has global scale, REIT-grade tenant relationships, and $200B+ AUM; Realty Income has a AAA tenant mix and a capital-markets machine). It is a narrow, deep moat: 51.2M sqft of SoCal infill, deal-sourcing relationships with every broker and family office in the five counties, and an in-house repositioning/construction team that can turn 1970s-era loading docks into 2025-spec last-mile facilities in 12-18 months.

The specific competitive advantages cited by management and corroborated by operational data:
1. **"Superior access to off-market opportunities"** — 2025 10-K. Historically, Rexford sourced ~60-70% of its acquisitions through off-market or lightly-marketed transactions. This is a real edge because it means lower acquisition costs and better underwriting information. The question is whether this advantage persists without the co-founders on the phone daily with sellers.
2. **Vertically integrated platform** — in-house acquisitions, underwriting, asset management, leasing, construction, property management, and legal. 256 employees across 5 regional offices. This allows value-add repositioning to be executed at cost-advantaged rates vs. outsourced platforms.
3. **Tenant granularity** — no single tenant >2.4% of ABR, 1,558 leases, average space 26,000 sqft. This is a *defensive* moat — it limits tenant-specific downside, but it also caps the upside of any one tenant expansion.
4. **Repositioning expertise** — the 21 repositioning/development stabilizations in 2025 (2.23M sqft) demonstrate continued execution capability even as the broader development pipeline contracts.

### Capital allocation: the 2025 pivot

The 2025 capital allocation narrative is the most interesting in the story and deserves dedicated attention. REXR did four things in 2025 that broke the pre-2025 playbook:

1. **Zero acquisitions.** The company that bought $970M in 2019 and 48 properties from Blackstone across 2023-2024 acquired nothing in 2025. Management explicitly says they did not find opportunities meeting "strengthened risk-adjusted return thresholds."

2. **$217.5M of dispositions at $106M gain** (7 properties, 589K sqft). Rexford sold properties at an implied 5.5-6.0% cap rate (back-calculated from the gain), which is below the ~6.5-7.0% cap rate the market would apply to the full portfolio. This is **constructive**: they are selling better assets at tighter caps to fund buybacks/developments with higher yield expectations.

3. **$250M of stock buybacks at $39.51 average**. This is a **major shift**. Rexford had never been a meaningful buyer of its own stock — the prior pattern was "issue equity at a premium, buy properties, accrete." The pivot to buybacks is a clean signal that (a) equity is cheap relative to property value, and (b) management is not finding deployable acquisition opportunities. The **$486.6M remaining on the new authorization** (expiring Feb 2028) gives them 2 years of runway to repurchase another ~14M shares at current prices — potentially **6% of shares outstanding retired** if fully executed.

4. **$89.1M impairment + 6 cancelled developments.** This is the "kitchen sink" move that typically accompanies a CEO transition. Taking the writedowns in 2025 allows Laura Clark to start 2026 with a cleaner balance sheet and lower embedded development risk. I interpret this as disciplined — not a red flag.

**My assessment of REXR's capital allocation:**
- Pre-2024: **A-grade**. Issued equity at premium, deployed at high accretion, delivered Core FFO/sh CAGR in low double digits.
- 2024: **B-grade**. Acquired the Blackstone portfolio at tight caps just as the market was rolling over; the timing was unfortunate but the assets are quality.
- 2025: **B+ grade**. Recognized the cycle turn, stopped acquiring, pivoted to buybacks and dispositions, took impairments. This is the right response even if late. The 2025 buyback at $39.51 avg will look like a good decision if the stock rerates to $45+ or a bad decision if it retests $28.

**2026 capital allocation priorities** (based on management language in 2025 10-K):
- Programmatic dispositions ($300-500M? management hasn't committed)
- Share repurchases (continuing to $486.6M remaining)
- Selective value-add repositioning (continuing $130M+ of committed capex on existing projects)
- Minimal-to-no new acquisitions
- No new ground-up development starts (9 projects in progress through 2027, then pipeline pauses)

This is **a mature REIT playbook, not a growth REIT playbook**. The implication for total return: less upside from AUM growth, more upside from buybacks and embedded rent uplift. Over a 3-year horizon, I expect total returns of **7-11% per year** (5% yield + 3-5% NAV growth + 1-2% from buybacks).

## PART VIII · RISKS

I'm organizing the risks by probability and severity, not by the order they appear in the 10-K.

### Tier 1 — Material risks (probability >20%)

**1. Leasing spread continued deterioration (Probability: 35%, Severity: High)**
The most important variable in the model. Q2 2025's negative new-lease spread could be:
- An outlier (the 106,251 sqft lease disproportionately impacting one quarter — management explicitly flagged this);
- Or the start of a broader rent roll-down for 2022-2023 vintage leases.
In 2026, 15.1% of the portfolio is up for renewal. If the blended cash spread drops from 10.7% (2025) to 3-5% (2026), same-property NOI growth goes negative and Core FFO/sh grows 0-2% vs 3-5% in base case. Stock would trade to $28-32.

**2. Co-CEO transition execution (Probability: 30%, Severity: Medium-High)**
Laura Clark is a quality internal promotion — COO since 2022, prior CFO role. But she is inheriting a company in the middle of a difficult market at a time when deal origination matters more than operational execution. The founder departures remove 30 years of combined relationships. In a worst case, Rexford loses access to the off-market deal flow that was a meaningful part of its cost-of-capital advantage. Probability of value destruction from transition: ~15-25% in the 2026-2027 window.

**3. AB-98 and SB-415 implementation (Probability: 40%, Severity: Medium)**
These laws are real and effective January 1, 2026. They will increase permitting timelines by 6-12 months on new ground-up developments, push construction costs up 10-15%, and reduce the number of sites that can be permitted for new warehouses. The net effect on REXR specifically is mixed: (a) bearish for new development yields; (b) bullish for in-place rent retention as competing supply decelerates; (c) neutral to slightly bullish for property values. Net-net, the risk is that the development pipeline becomes effectively uneconomic, which removes 150-200 bps of annual Core FFO/sh growth that had been embedded in the model.

### Tier 2 — Meaningful risks (probability 10-20%)

**4. Tenant bankruptcies in a recession (Probability: 15%, Severity: Medium)**
REXR's tenant base is highly granular (>1,558 leases, no single tenant >2.4%) but is concentrated in non-investment-grade credits (the 10-K specifically cites that "a substantial majority of the leases at our properties are with tenants who have non-investment grade credit ratings"). In a 2008-style recession, bad debt reserves could rise from 20-30 bps of revenue to 100-150 bps, and same-store occupancy could fall from 96% to 92-93%. The combined NOI impact would be ~$30-50M or ~5-7% of NOI. Not existential but not trivial.

**5. Interest rate environment (Probability: 15%, Severity: Medium)**
REXR has $3.3B of debt at a 3.9-4.2% weighted-average cost. Only $67.6M matures within 12 months, so there is no near-term refinancing risk. However, the $575M 2027 Exchangeable Notes (4.375%), the $125M 2027 Senior Notes (3.930%), and the $300M 2027 Term Loan come due in 2027 — $1.0B of refinancing in one year at materially higher rates. Assuming refinancing at 5.5-6.0% on this $1.0B tranche, the incremental interest expense is $15-20M, or roughly $0.07-0.09 per share on a 232M share count. This is a knowable drag, not a solvency issue.

**6. ICE arrests / labor market disruption (Probability: 20%, Severity: Low)**
The 10-K explicitly cites this as a risk factor new to 2025: "impacts to the regional labor markets and inflationary pressures from smaller labor pools, costs of goods and construction, lower consumer demand and impacts to the overall economy related to U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) arrests and detentions of immigrants within Southern California." This is a politically sensitive topic but for REXR specifically the impact is mostly on tenants' labor costs (which could reduce tenant-level demand for space) and construction costs (which could delay REXR's own repositioning projects). Low direct impact, uncertain second-order effects.

### Tier 3 — Low probability / tail risks

**7. Major earthquake (Probability: <5% over 5 years, Severity: High)**
REXR carries earthquake insurance through a wholly-owned captive that provides $25M of coverage above a $350M deductible. A Northridge-scale event (7.0+ magnitude directly under the LA basin) would likely exceed these limits. Given the concentration of 51M sqft in a 5-county region, this is the one tail risk that could genuinely impair the company. Probability low, severity extreme.

**8. Port of LA/Long Beach disruption (Probability: 10% over 5 years, Severity: Medium)**
A prolonged port strike, major cybersecurity incident, or shift of trade volumes to Gulf Coast / Mexican ports would reduce the economic rationale for LA infill warehouse demand. Probability limited but non-zero.

**9. California wildfire exposure (Probability: 10% per year, Severity: Low-Medium)**
January 2025 LA wildfires affected some REXR properties but did not result in material impairments. Ongoing wildfire risk is a watchlist item.

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

I will offer four non-consensus views that are load-bearing for my BUY call.

### Variant 1: The spread collapse is not as bad as the headline

Consensus sees cash leasing spreads fall from 58.8% (2022) to 10.7% (2025) and concludes the mark-to-market thesis is broken. My view: **the spread number is a vintage-weighted average that disguises the true mark-to-market status of the portfolio**.

- Full-year 2025 renewal spreads were +28.3% GAAP / +14.7% cash — still very healthy.
- The collapse is almost entirely in new leases on previously-repositioned/redeveloped space, where the prior rent was near-market (2022-2023 vintage).
- 2026-2027 renewals roll the 2019-2021 vintage leases, which should generate 30-50% cash spreads on renewal based on embedded rent gap.

The 2025 print is telling you about *new* lease economics (which are neutral at current market rents), not about the *renewal* economics (which are still very strong). Aggregating the two together obscures the signal. My forecast: 2026 blended cash spreads of **12-18%**, 2027 blended cash spreads of **15-25%**, 2028 **10-15%**. The long-run mark-to-market opportunity is worth 20-30% of in-place ABR over a 5-year roll cycle, even at flat market rents.

### Variant 2: The co-CEO departure is a positive, not a negative

Consensus is nervous about Schwimmer and Frankel leaving. My view: **they are leaving at the right time with their equity cleanly transitioned, and Laura Clark is the right fit for the next 5 years**.

- The founders took the business from IPO ($14/share) to year-end 2023 ($55-60/share) — a 4x+ return for shareholders. They earned the right to step away on their own timing.
- Laura Clark has been COO through the difficult 2023-2025 window. She has the operational credibility to execute the "mature REIT" playbook (programmatic dispositions, buybacks, selective reinvestment).
- The $60.2M accelerated comp charge is a **one-time event that clears the deck** — future comp expense will be lower in normalized periods.
- A co-CEO model is structurally less efficient for a $8B company than a single-CEO model. Moving to single CEO should reduce decision latency and improve external capital markets communications.

This is the rare CEO transition where the incoming exec has been visible and credible to the market for 3+ years (vs. a parachuted outside hire). The transition risk is real but smaller than a typical CEO change.

### Variant 3: The buybacks matter more than the market thinks

Consensus treats the $250M 2025 buyback as a defensive cash use. My view: **at 1.06x book value, buybacks are as close to risk-free NAV accretion as a REIT can get**.

- Every dollar of buyback at $39.51 retired shares trading at 1.0x book → $1 of NAV to the remaining holders.
- $486.6M of remaining authorization = up to 14M shares (at ~$35) = 6% of current float.
- If the stock re-rates to $45 while the buyback continues, 2026-2027 Core FFO/sh could receive a **~3-5% compounding tailwind** from share count reduction alone.
- The buyback program is the **cleanest possible signal** from management that they view the stock as cheaper than private-market values.

### Variant 4: AB-98/SB-415 is net positive for REXR

Consensus views the new regulations as a headwind. My view: **they are the moat-widening event that the SoCal industrial market needed**.

- The regulations primarily hit new ground-up development (which had been accelerating in the non-infill Inland Empire East). Existing Class-A stock is grandfathered.
- Fewer new developments = less future supply = higher residual value for existing stock.
- REXR's development pipeline will shrink (already announced — 6 projects cancelled), but its existing 51.2M sqft is the beneficiary.
- The **symmetric consequence** of supply constraint is rent inelasticity on the downside. If no new supply is being built, the existing rent floor becomes harder to break through.
- In the 5-year steady state, AB-98/SB-415 probably adds 50-100 bps per year to REXR's terminal same-store NOI growth rate.

This is the most important variant perception in the report. If AB-98/SB-415 really does constrain new supply for 5+ years, the 2025 spread collapse becomes a temporary cyclical event rather than a structural break, and Rexford's long-run NOI trajectory looks a lot more like its 2016-2022 pattern than its 2024-2025 pattern.
