## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE at EUR 66.70. BUY below EUR 60. · Dividend Safety: 8/10 · Confidence: Medium-High**

Henkel is in the midst of the most significant strategic transformation in its 148-year history, and the market is paying approximately zero for it. CEO Carsten Knobel's 2022 decision to merge the Beauty Care and Laundry & Home Care divisions into a single Consumer Brands segment — eliminating an entire management layer, consolidating supply chains, and rationalizing a bloated portfolio of 700+ brands down to the strongest — is working. Operating margin expanded from 9.3% (2023, trough) to 14.6% (2025), a 530bps improvement in two years. EBITDA grew from EUR 2.8B to EUR 3.7B. The Adhesive Technologies division — the crown jewel, #1 globally with 25%+ market share — continues to compound at mid-single digits with structurally high margins. And the stock trades at 13.6x trailing EPS and 8.2x EV/EBITDA, with a FMP DCF of EUR 141.67 that implies 112% upside.

The dividend tell the story even more clearly. Henkel has paid dividends without interruption for 30+ years and has been growing them consistently. The last declared dividend was EUR 2.04 per preferred share, representing a 3.1% yield at current prices. The 2025 payout ratio on EPS of EUR 4.91 is approximately 42% — conservative, with substantial room for increases. FCF of EUR 1,824M in 2025 provides robust 2.2x coverage of the estimated ~EUR 845M dividend outlay.

The risk is well-known: Henkel's Consumer Brands division has historically been a mediocre performer, and the Adhesive Technologies division's margins are exposed to industrial cycle downturns. But at EUR 66.70, you are paying trough multiples for a company that has already inflected on the restructuring. The margin trajectory is up, not down.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**
1. **Adhesive Technologies is a world-class industrial franchise.** #1 global market share in adhesives, sealants, and functional coatings. Revenue EUR ~11B (approximately 53% of group sales). Serves automotive, electronics, packaging, and construction end-markets with technically differentiated products. Switching costs are high — adhesives are specified into manufacturing processes and changing suppliers requires re-qualification. This is a genuine Buffett-style moat.
2. **Consumer Brands restructuring is delivering measurable results.** The merger of Beauty Care and Laundry & Home Care (2022) eliminated approximately EUR 500M of costs, rationalized the portfolio from 700+ to ~400 brands, and is driving margin expansion. Operating margin in Consumer Brands has improved from approximately 8-10% (2022) to an estimated 12-14% (2025). The turnaround is real, not aspirational.
3. **Valuation is compressed to industrial-cycle-trough levels.** 13.6x P/E, 8.2x EV/EBITDA, 3.1% yield, 42% payout ratio. Henkel has traded at 18-22x P/E in normal environments. If margins continue expanding and the multiple re-rates to 16-18x, the stock goes to EUR 80-90 — 20-35% upside plus the dividend.

**Top 3 reasons not to own:**
1. **Consumer Brands may never be best-in-class.** Persil and Schwarzkopf are strong regional brands but lack the global scale of P&G's Tide or L'Oreal's mass portfolio. The restructuring has improved margins, but the division may remain a 12-15% operating margin business — adequate but not exceptional.
2. **Adhesive Technologies has cyclical exposure.** Auto production (~25% of Adhesive revenue), electronics (~20%), and construction (~15%) are all cyclical. A global manufacturing downturn — particularly in Europe and China — would compress Adhesive margins from their current 17-18% level.
3. **KGaA governance structure limits shareholder influence.** Henkel's preferred shares (HEN3) have no voting rights. The Henkel family controls the company through ordinary shares with a >50% voting stake. This is a permanent governance discount that limits activist pressure and may allow management to pursue strategies that don't maximize shareholder value.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- 2025 dividend announcement (Q1 2026) — expect EUR 2.10-2.20
- H1 2026 results (August 2026) — margin trajectory confirmation
- Potential bolt-on M&A in Adhesive Technologies (management has flagged interest)
- Consumer Brands portfolio rationalization progress update

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 — Kasper Rorsted Era (2008-2016).** Rorsted was the efficiency CEO. He restructured Henkel from a slow-moving German conglomerate into a leaner operator, expanding margins from ~12% to ~16%, divesting non-core assets, and setting ambitious financial targets ("Henkel 2020+" strategy). Rorsted left for adidas in 2016 with Henkel at approximately EUR 110/share and a 16% operating margin.

**Phase 2 — Hans Van Bylen Interregnum (2016-2020).** Van Bylen maintained Rorsted's discipline but lacked strategic ambition. Revenue growth was anemic (0-2% organic), the Beauty Care division stagnated against aggressive L'Oreal and Unilever competition, and the market began to question whether Henkel's multi-category model was sustainable. The stock drifted from EUR 110 to EUR 80.

**Phase 3 — Knobel Restructuring (2020-present).** Carsten Knobel became CEO in January 2020, just before COVID. His first three years were consumed by firefighting: COVID disruption (2020), raw material inflation (2021-2022), and organizational restructuring. The transformative decision came in January 2022: merge Beauty Care and Laundry & Home Care into a single Consumer Brands division. This was not a cosmetic change — it eliminated an entire P&L reporting line, consolidated procurement, unified supply chains, and forced the rationalization of 700+ consumer brands down to the strongest ~400. The results became visible from 2024: operating margins re-expanded sharply. By 2025, the combined Consumer Brands division is operating at estimated 12-14% margins versus the 8-10% Beauty Care was producing standalone. Adhesive Technologies has continued to compound reliably throughout.

**Phase 4 — Portfolio Optimization (2024-2025).** Revenue declined from EUR 22.4B to EUR 20.5B — not because the business is shrinking, but because Henkel deliberately exited approximately EUR 1-1.5B of low-margin tail brands. This is precisely the right strategic move: shrink revenue to improve returns, exactly as KO did with refranchising.

## PART VI · ADHESIVE TECHNOLOGIES — THE CROWN JEWEL

Adhesive Technologies is the reason to own Henkel. This is not a consumer staple business — it is a specialty industrial platform with structural competitive advantages.

**Market position:** #1 globally with approximately 25%+ market share, roughly 2x the #2 player (H.B. Fuller). The global adhesives market is approximately EUR 45-50B and grows 3-5% annually, driven by lightweighting in automotive (structural adhesives replacing welds), electronics miniaturization (thermal management adhesives), e-commerce packaging (hot-melt and pressure-sensitive adhesives), and sustainable packaging (water-based adhesives replacing solvent-based).

**Economic characteristics:**
- Revenue: approximately EUR 11B (53% of group)
- Operating margin: approximately 17-18% (estimated, segment reporting merged in FMP data)
- Customer retention: 95%+ — adhesives are specified into manufacturing processes; changing suppliers requires re-qualification testing that can take 6-18 months
- Pricing power: cost-plus model with raw material pass-through clauses. Henkel typically passes input cost changes within 1-2 quarters.
- R&D intensity: ~3% of Adhesive revenue. The division holds thousands of patents in specialty formulations.

**Why it deserves a premium multiple:** If Adhesive Technologies were a standalone company (EUR 11B revenue, 17-18% operating margins, #1 global market share, 95%+ retention, 3-5% organic growth), it would trade at 16-20x EBITDA — comparable to specialty chemical peers like RPM International, H.B. Fuller, or Sika. At those multiples, Adhesive alone would be worth EUR 30-37B. Henkel's entire enterprise value today is approximately EUR 30B. The market is assigning approximately zero value to Consumer Brands.

## PART VII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Moat pillars:**
1. **Technical switching costs (Adhesives).** Products are specified into customer manufacturing processes. Re-qualification takes 6-18 months and costs customers millions in testing and potential production disruption.
2. **Scale advantages (Adhesives).** Global manufacturing footprint with 40+ production sites enables just-in-time delivery to automotive and electronics OEMs, who require single-source supply reliability.
3. **Brand loyalty (Consumer).** Persil is the #1 premium detergent brand in Europe. Schwarzkopf is the #1 mass-market hair care brand in Europe. These are durable brand positions, though not as dominant globally as P&G or Unilever.
4. **Geographic diversification.** Approximately 45% of revenue from Europe, 25% North America, 20% Asia Pacific, 10% rest of world. No single market dominates.

**Capital allocation scorecard:**
- **Dividends: B+.** 30+ year track record, but held flat for five years during restructuring. Pragmatic but not inspiring for a dividend growth investor.
- **Buybacks: B.** Inconsistent — EUR 856M in 2025 but zero in several recent years. Share count reduction is modest (434M to 414M over 2019-2025).
- **M&A: B+.** No value-destructive deals. The 2008 National Starch acquisition was transformative for Adhesives. Recent activity has been bolt-on.
- **Restructuring investment: A.** The Consumer Brands merger is delivering measurable results. Management deserves credit for making a difficult structural decision and executing it.

## PART XI · ESG AND GOVERNANCE

Henkel consistently ranks in the top 5% of its industry on major ESG ratings (Sustainalytics, MSCI ESG). The company has ambitious 2030 climate targets, uses ~50% recycled plastic in its consumer products, and has achieved carbon neutrality in production operations in multiple geographies.

Governance: KGaA structure — the Henkel family controls >50% of voting rights through ordinary shares. Preferred shareholders (HEN3) have no vote but receive a EUR 0.02 higher dividend than ordinary shares. This is a permanent governance discount of approximately 5-10% that investors must accept.
