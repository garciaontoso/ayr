## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: SPECULATIVE HOLD — do NOT initiate at ≥2% portfolio weight.** **Dividend Safety: 3.5/10.** **Confidence: Medium.**

Let me lead with the two most important things the common IIPR narrative gets wrong:

1. **The dividend has not been cut.** The task brief for this report stated IIPR cut the dividend from $1.80 to $1.40 quarterly in 2024 (~22%). Per the SEC record, this is factually incorrect. IIPR raised the quarterly dividend from $1.80 (Q3 2022 through Q3 2023) to $1.82 (Q4 2023 and Q1 2024) and then to **$1.90 (Q2 2024 through Q3 2025)** — a cumulative 5.6% increase across the worst two years in cannabis history (2022 10-K Note 5; 2025 Q3 10-Q Note 5). Annual declared DPS went $7.10 (2022) → $7.22 (2023) → $7.52 (2024) → tracking $7.60 (2025). The entire premise that the market has "already re-rated for a cut" is wrong — the 12%+ yield IS the cut the market is pricing. The cut would be a second shoe to drop, not a priced-in event.

2. **The refinancing cliff is the single biggest risk in this name, not the tenants.** IIPR has $291.2 million of 5.50% Senior Notes due 2026 maturing May 25, 2026. Management stated in the Q2 2025 10-Q (line 806) that the Company "currently does not have sufficient liquidity to satisfy this obligation at maturity" and that management "has plans to refinance." This is not a risk factor — it is a disclosed liquidity gap. In a normal cycle it would be a non-event for an IG-rated REIT. For a cannabis REIT trading at a 12%+ yield with deteriorating same-store rent, it is the primary binary catalyst over the next 13 months. Cash and equivalents were $99.7M at June 30, 2025; the $87.5M revolver was undrawn — call it ~$187M of dry powder against a $291M bullet. The gap has to come from a refinancing at spreads that will materially reset the forward cost of debt.

That is the clean sell. The variant perception is that the cannabis REIT model remains structurally profitable even after three years of tenant stress, that Schedule III reclassification is a real (if delayed) optionality, and that the dividend is defended by still-intact coverage: 2024 AFFO per diluted share of $8.98 covers a $7.52 declared dividend at **84% AFFO payout** with ~$1.46/sh of cushion (2024 10-K line 111-115). That cushion has shrunk — 9M 2025 AFFO per share tracking lower on declining rental revenue ($199.3M 9M 2025 vs. $231.8M 9M 2024, -14%, per Q3 2025 10-Q line 174) — but the math still works if PharmaCann stabilizes and the Notes due 2026 refinance without punishing terms.

**Top 3 reasons to watch at the $55-65 band:** (1) The 12%+ yield is the widest in any US REIT outside distressed commercial real estate and reflects real risk, not false narrative — it prices in real pain already; (2) Schedule III reclassification would remove IRC 280E for tenants, potentially freeing 20-40% of tenant operating cash flow and eliminating the dominant structural default driver (2024 10-K Item 1 lines 415-418); (3) management is buying back stock — 371K shares for $20.1M in H1 2025 (Q2 2025 10-Q line 337) — at prices that imply an internal valuation meaningfully above market.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own:** (1) Same-store rental revenue fell 14% YoY in 9M 2025 with PharmaCann re-defaulting in March 2025 after the January 2025 lease amendment, voiding all concessions (Q2 2025 10-Q line 747). The bleeding is not done; (2) $291.2M refinancing cliff in May 2026 with insufficient on-balance-sheet liquidity and a capital-constrained cannabis REIT universe competing for the same pool; (3) management diversified $270M committed into IQHQ — a private life science REIT — rather than retaining dry powder against the maturity. On August 6, 2025 IIPR signed a $170M preferred + $100M credit facility commitment into IQHQ (Q3 2025 10-Q line 819-824) with CEO Paul Smithers taking a board seat. That is a major strategic pivot and an unforced capital deployment decision 9 months before a liquidity gap.

**12-month catalysts:** (i) Notes due 2026 refinancing (binary, by May 2026); (ii) PharmaCann restructuring outcome — bankruptcy, new tenant, or sustained cure; (iii) DEA rulemaking progress on Schedule III (currently stalled under the Trump administration); (iv) Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 AFFO/share print.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

### Business description (FY2024 10-K)

Innovative Industrial Properties, Inc. is a Maryland REIT incorporated June 15, 2016 (2024 10-K Item 1 line 74), making it one of the youngest large-cap US REITs. It was IPO'd December 1, 2016 at $20.00/share — approximately the only publicly-listed cannabis-facility landlord at the time, and the only US REIT in any vertical that counted "state-licensed regulated cannabis cultivation" as its primary business model.

The strategy is narrow and specific: acquire specialized industrial properties — typically purpose-built or retrofitted cultivation and processing facilities with enhanced HVAC, high-capacity electrical, specialized lighting, and security systems — via sale-leaseback transactions with state-licensed multi-state operators (MSOs), and lease them back on triple-net terms with weighted-average initial lease terms of 15-20 years and contractual rent escalators (2024 10-K Item 1 line 127). The company is "internally-managed" (no external advisor) and as of December 31, 2024 had just **22 full-time employees** managing a $2.4B invested portfolio (line 74). That is a 1:$109M asset-per-employee ratio — comparable to VICI's radical thinness. The bull argument is that triple-net REITs do not need a field organization. The bear argument is that when tenants default, 22 people cannot manage 100+ cannabis cultivation facilities.

Corporate office: Park City, UT (relocated from San Diego, CA). CEO: **Paul Smithers** (co-founder, President/CEO since IPO). Executive Chairman: **Alan Gold** (also co-founder, formerly CEO and founder of BioMed Realty Trust from 2004 until its sale to Blackstone in 2016 — importantly, BioMed was a life science REIT, which provides the pedigree for the August 2025 IQHQ investment pivot). CFO: **David Smith** (since November 2019).

## PART III · 10-YEAR ARC — IPO 2016 → Boom → Bust → Pivot

### Phase 1: IPO and Proof of Concept (2016-2019)

IIPR IPO'd December 1, 2016 at $20/share. FY2017 rental revenue was $6.3M, FY2018 $14.3M, FY2019 $43.4M (2019 10-K line 136). The dividend grew from $1.20 (2018) to $2.83 (2019) — reflecting the fact that there are no other publicly-listed cannabis-facility landlords and every sale-leaseback the company closed was accretive because IIPR was the only bid at the table. The company received its investment-grade rating in May 2021 and immediately issued $300M of 5.50% Senior Notes due 2026 at what were (at the time) reasonable spreads (2021 10-K line 104). This is the debt that now comes due.

**Strategic significance:** IIPR built a business whose entire economic return depended on being the only institutional capital provider to a federally-illegal industry, and then the industry caught up. As of 2024, larger MSOs have direct access to debt capital and securitizations — reducing IIPR's pricing power on new deals. The sale-leaseback monopoly of 2017-2020 no longer exists.

### Phase 2: Boom (2020-2021)

2020 was the breakout year. Revenue grew from $43.4M (2019) to $116.9M (2020) — a +169% increase — as the company deployed capital into 66 properties across 18 states (2021 10-K line 85). FY2021 rental revenue hit $204.6M, up another +75%, and FY2021 AFFO/share was $6.66 (2021 10-K line 97). The share price peaked at approximately **$286 in November 2021** — at that price, the yield was roughly 2.0% and AFFO multiple 40x. It was a classic combination trade: cannabis + REIT + zero-rate environment. The dividend reached $5.72 in 2021 — a +28% growth rate — and the market narrative was that IIPR was the "Realty Income of cannabis."

**Nothing in this period is replicable.** The growth came from three factors that no longer exist: (a) zero interest rates that made a 5% cap rate on a cannabis industrial facility look attractive; (b) a nascent cannabis industry where the only capital was IIPR's; (c) COVID-era demand that briefly caused cannabis pricing to rise. All three reversed in 2022.

### Phase 3: Crisis and Cannabis Bust (2022-2024)

**July 2022: Kings Garden default.** The first crack. Kings Garden, a California cultivator with six IIPR-leased properties in southern California, defaulted on rent obligations (2022 10-K line 548). A confidential settlement was executed September 11, 2022 under which IIPR regained possession of two development-stage properties and Kings Garden continued to occupy four remaining properties at a stipulated-rent level through September 20, 2023 (2024 10-K line 274). This was the moment the market discovered that cannabis tenant risk was real. IIPR stock dropped from ~$150 in early 2022 to ~$85 by end of 2022.

**November 2022: Parallel and Green Peak defaults.** Parallel defaulted at one Pennsylvania property, Green Peak at one Michigan property. February 2023 Parallel defaulted again at a Texas property (2022 10-K line 264). Throughout 2023 IIPR recognized limited rent deferrals for Holistic (two properties), Calyx Peak (one property), Temescal Wellness (one property) and 4Front (one property) (2024 10-K line 277).

**2023 rent deferral program.** The 2023 10-K disclosed that $3.1M of security deposits were applied for rent with two defaulted tenants and $5.7M of security deposits were applied for rent with three amendment tenants (2023 10-K line 1684). This is the single most important number for the 2023 dividend math — without the $8.8M of security-deposit-sourced "rent revenue," AFFO coverage would have broken that year.

**2024: More defaults, more amendments.** Temescal Wellness defaulted at one Massachusetts property in May 2024 (regained September 2024); PharmaCann defaulted on six of eleven leases in December 2024 for $4.3M of defaulted December rent (2024 10-K line 280). January 2025 amendments cut PharmaCann aggregate base rent from $2.8M/month to $2.6M/month (a 7% cut) on nine of eleven leases and abated monthly base rent of $1.3M entirely on the other two leases, with the condition that if PharmaCann defaulted again or failed to refinance its credit facility by June 30, 2025, all amendments became null and void (line 280).

**March 2025: PharmaCann defaults again.** PharmaCann defaulted on rent for March 2025 on nine of eleven leases, voiding the January 2025 amendments (Q2 2025 10-Q line 747). In April 2025 the Michigan cultivation lease was terminated and re-leased to a new tenant. In August 2025 the Massachusetts cultivation lease was terminated and IIPR took back possession (Q3 2025 10-Q line 793).

During this entire 2022-2025 crisis window, **the dividend grew from $7.10 (2022) to $7.52 (2024)**. That is either (a) management discipline in defending the dividend through a genuine transient crisis, or (b) denial in the face of structural deterioration. The verdict between these two hinges entirely on whether PharmaCann stabilizes and whether the Notes due 2026 refinance.

### Phase 4: Strategic Pivot (August 2025)

On August 6, 2025, IIPR signed a Securities Purchase Agreement with **IQHQ, Inc.** — a private life science REIT — under which IIPR committed up to $170M to purchase 15.0% Series G-1 Cumulative Redeemable Preferred Stock plus a $100M commitment to a lender syndicate under a 13.5% fixed-rate credit facility (Q3 2025 10-Q line 819-824). The initial close September 30, 2025 funded $5M of preferred plus the full $100M of credit facility. CEO Paul Smithers took a board seat at IQHQ.

**Strategic read:** This is a diversification away from cannabis. Alan Gold founded BioMed Realty (a life science REIT) before starting IIPR, so the pivot is not a random pivot — it is Gold returning to a vertical he knows. The IQHQ investment carries a 13.5% fixed credit yield plus 15% cumulative preferred with PIK, totaling potential IRRs in the 15-20% range. That is value-creative if IQHQ executes. **But it is also a $270M commitment of capital that IIPR does not have liquid, made 9 months before a $291M debt maturity it cannot cover.** The read-through is either (a) management is supremely confident in the refinancing and views IQHQ as a higher-return use of capital than debt paydown, or (b) this is an admission that the cannabis growth runway is exhausted and future value creation has to come from non-cannabis verticals. Either way, it is a material change in strategy not priced into the stock.

## PART V · DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE

### The 2024 "cut" that wasn't

The most important fact in this report: **IIPR did not cut its dividend in 2024.** The task brief for this research stated the dividend was cut from $1.80 to $1.40 quarterly — a ~22% cut. Per the SEC record, this is factually incorrect.

The correct sequence, per IIPR's own Note 5 disclosures across filings:
- Q3 2022: $1.80/quarter declared (2023 10-K line 1843)
- Q4 2022: $1.80/quarter declared (line 1853)
- Q1 2023: $1.80/quarter declared (line 1864)
- Q2 2023: $1.80/quarter declared (line 1876)
- Q3 2023: $1.80/quarter declared (line 1886)
- **Q4 2023: $1.82/quarter (line 1897) — first increase from $1.80 plateau**
- Q1 2024: $1.82/quarter (2024 Q1 10-Q line 530)
- **Q2 2024: $1.90/quarter (2024 Q2 10-Q line 687) — second increase**
- Q3 2024: $1.90/quarter
- Q4 2024: $1.90/quarter
- Q1-Q3 2025: $1.90/quarter (Q3 2025 10-Q line 697-728)

This matters because the investment thesis looks very different if the dividend has been growing versus if it has been cut. A cut-then-stabilize narrative is the classic contrarian trade — buy after the cut, before the recovery. A grow-through-crisis narrative is different: either management is defending a dividend they can afford (bullish), or they are defending one they cannot (bearish, classic pre-cut pattern). I think we are in the second case.

Private. Already had limited rent deferrals at two properties (California and Michigan) executed in 2023 per 2024 10-K line 277. ### Summary: tenant concentration is the critical structural weakness

The top-5 tenants at 50% of revenue, all non-IG, with 2 of the 5 (PharmaCann and Holistic) having already required rent concessions, and with most of the remainder trading at sub-1x EV/Revenue multiples in public markets — that is a materially worse credit picture than any other REIT at the 9-12% yield range. **My single biggest concern is this:** The cannabis industry's problems are mostly structural, not cyclical. Unit pricing compression (2024 10-K line 209), illicit market competition (line 568), state tax burdens (line 573), and limited access to capital (line 577) are all problems that get worse over time unless Schedule III passes. Without Schedule III, I expect **at least one more top-5 tenant to require meaningful rent modifications by end of 2026.** At that point the dividend math breaks.

## PART VII · CANNABIS REGULATORY CONTEXT — Schedule III Optionality

### What Schedule III would do

Cannabis is currently a Schedule I controlled substance under the Controlled Substances Act of 1970 (2024 10-K line 406). Schedule I is the most restrictive category — alongside heroin and LSD — and carries a critical IRS consequence under **IRC Section 280E**: businesses engaged in "trafficking" of Schedule I or II substances are barred from deducting ordinary business expenses (rent, payroll, etc.) against federal income tax. This means state-licensed cannabis MSOs effectively pay federal income tax on gross margin, not on net income. Effective federal tax rates for US MSOs run 50-70%.

If cannabis were rescheduled to Schedule III, 280E would no longer apply. Tenants could deduct rent payments, payroll, and other normal operating expenses. Industry analyst estimates suggest this would release **20-40% of tenant operating cash flow** virtually overnight. For IIPR's tenant base, that would materially reduce default risk across the entire portfolio.

### The timeline — and why it keeps slipping

- **August 2023:** HHS recommended DEA reschedule cannabis to Schedule III (2024 10-K line 415)
- **May 16, 2024:** DEA issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking proposing Schedule III classification
- **Summer 2024:** 60-day comment period; majority of commenters supported reclassification or further descheduling
- **January 2025:** DEA administrative law judge canceled the rulemaking hearing that was scheduled to begin January 21, 2025 following challenges
- **February 4, 2025:** Pamela Bondi confirmed as Attorney General under Trump administration
- **2025:** Trump appointed Derek Maltz to lead DEA. Per the 2024 10-K line 416, "Neither Trump nor Maltz has released any official policy directive related to rescheduling. No further hearings have been scheduled beyond a status update slated for April 2025."

**Probability assessment:** I put Schedule III reclassification in the Trump administration at roughly **30-35% by end of 2027 and ~50% by end of 2028.** The Trump administration is not ideologically opposed to cannabis rescheduling (Trump himself endorsed rescheduling before his 2024 election), but the administrative machinery has stalled, Bondi is historically opposed, and the DEA hearing has been canceled. The "tail" of Schedule III is real but has meaningfully extended.

**Impact on IIPR if Schedule III passes:** Roughly +30% equity value, primarily through (a) reduced tenant default risk, (b) new tenant acquisition opportunities, (c) multiple re-rating from 12% yield to 8% yield. **Impact if Schedule III does NOT pass before 2028:** Further tenant stress, at least one more dividend cut, and the stock trapped in a 10-12% yield range with no re-rating catalyst.

This is the only real upside catalyst for IIPR, and it is partially outside management's control.

### The SAFER Banking Act alternative

The Secure And Fair Enforcement Regulation Banking Act (SAFER) would provide federal protection for financial institutions serving state-legal cannabis businesses — solving the banking access problem (2024 10-K line 428). It passed the Senate Banking Committee in September 2023 and has stalled in the Senate floor vote. SAFER is independent of Schedule III and would marginally help tenants (by reducing cash-handling costs) but would not address 280E, which is the bigger economic burden. SAFER is a less impactful optionality than Schedule III but has a higher probability (I'd put it at 40% by end of 2027).

## PART VIII · MOAT + CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### Moat assessment: narrow and eroding

IIPR's original moat was **first-mover advantage in a federally-illegal industry.** From 2016 through 2021, IIPR was the only meaningful institutional capital provider to state-licensed cannabis cultivation facilities. That moat has largely eroded:

- Larger MSOs have gained access to private debt markets. Green Thumb, Trulieve, Verano, and Cresco Labs have all issued secured debt at meaningfully lower cost of capital than IIPR can offer.
- Community banks in cannabis-legal states have increasingly developed comfort with cannabis banking under the FinCEN Memorandum framework (2024 10-K line 429).
- Alternative sale-leaseback REITs (NewLake Capital Partners, AFC Gamma — now Sunrise Realty Trust — and others) have entered the market.
- The cannabis industry consolidation trend (2024 10-K line 395) is reducing the number of potential tenants IIPR can lease to.

**What's left of the moat?** Three things:
1. **Specialized real estate.** Purpose-built cultivation facilities with HVAC, electrical, security and lighting systems are hard to repurpose. If a tenant defaults, the tenant loses the specialized buildout investment. This gives IIPR negotiating leverage in restructuring (which is why Kings Garden's settlement was structured as a "stipulated rent" continuation rather than immediate eviction).
2. **Triple-net structure.** IIPR is insulated from operating costs (property taxes, insurance, structural repair). In a normal tenant environment this is a structural advantage; in the current environment it shifts cost burden to weak tenants who cannot absorb it.
3. **Investment-grade rating.** IIPR retains an investment-grade corporate credit rating — the only US cannabis-exposed REIT to do so. This gives access to the Notes due 2026 refi pool that non-IG competitors cannot reach. **This is the single most important moat for the next 13 months.** Losing IG status would crater refinancing optionality.

**Moat trajectory:** Narrowing. The specialized-real-estate argument is weakened by the fact that in a cannabis downturn, "specialized" means "hard to find new tenant." The IG rating is at risk if the 2026 refi requires guarantees or covenants that rating agencies don't like.

### Capital allocation grade: C (down from A in 2020)

IIPR's historical capital allocation (2016-2021) was genuinely excellent. They identified an underserved market, built a first-mover position, got IG-rated, raised permanent capital at favorable spreads, and compounded AFFO/share at 25%+ CAGR.

From 2022 onwards it has been mixed:
- **Positive:** Kept the dividend growing through the crisis — a strong signal to income investors. Opportunistic share buybacks in 2025 (371K shares for $20.1M in H1 2025, at average prices implying management views fair value materially above market). Debt paydown on Notes due 2026 at discounts (early partial repayments at a discount totaling $8.7M principal reduction in February 2025, per Q2 2025 10-Q line 806) — creative liability management.
- **Negative:** Did not build sufficient liquidity ahead of the 2026 maturity. Cash has declined from ~$530M in 2021 to ~$78M by Sept 2025. That's a -85% cash decline at the exact moment the refinancing cliff is approaching.
- **Very negative:** The August 2025 IQHQ $270M commitment deploys capital OUT of the balance sheet 9 months before the $291M maturity. If the refinancing goes well, IQHQ is value-accretive at 13.5-15% returns. If refinancing goes badly, this $270M will be cited as the moment management fumbled the liquidity setup.

**Capital allocation grade: C.** The dividend discipline is admirable, the buybacks are smart, but the liquidity management around the 2026 maturity looks careless on paper.

- **Paul Smithers** (President/CEO, co-founder): 10 years at IIPR. Formerly general counsel and SVP of BioMed Realty (Alan Gold's prior company). Legal background (not REIT operator background). Tenure credit for seeing the company through the crisis. Decision to take IQHQ board seat suggests he expects an ongoing involvement with life sciences, which is a meaningful signal about the company's forward strategic direction.

Founded Alexandria Real Estate Equities (NYSE: ARE) in 1994 (life sciences — his first major success), then BioMed Realty Trust (also life sciences, sold to Blackstone 2016 for $8B), then IIPR in 2016. **Track record of taking underserved real estate niches public, building them to scale, and selling to private equity.** The read-through: Gold has done this three times in 30 years. Each time the pattern is the same — find an underserved niche, become the dominant public landlord, get IG-rated, and ultimately monetize. IIPR is cycle 3 and the IQHQ pivot suggests cycle 4 is starting before cycle 3 is fully monetized. **Gold may be positioning for an eventual take-private of IIPR cannabis assets separate from the life science investments.** This is speculative but consistent with his track record.

## PART IX · RISKS — THE BEAR CASE IN DETAIL

### Risk 1: The 2026 refinancing cliff (probability: HIGH; severity: HIGH)

**The most important risk in this report.** IIPR has $291.2M of Notes due 2026 maturing May 25, 2026. The Q2 2025 10-Q explicitly disclosed that the Company "currently does not have sufficient liquidity to satisfy this obligation at maturity" (line 806). The refinancing path management has "planned" relies on accessing the US investment-grade bond market at acceptable spreads.

The path from here to a successful refi at 7.0-7.5% yield:
1. Rental revenue stabilizes (PharmaCann re-tenanting progresses, no new top-10 defaults)
2. IG rating is maintained through the refi window
3. Broader credit markets remain receptive to REIT issuance in a 4.25-4.75% 10-year yield environment

**What could go wrong:**
- A new top-10 tenant default between now and May 2026 triggers rating downgrade
- Broader credit markets tighten (recession, geopolitical event) pushing cannabis-REIT spreads 300+ bps wider
- Regulatory news (Trump admin hostile cannabis statement, Bondi DOJ enforcement action) spooks IG buyers
- The market discovers the 2026 maturity is larger relative to liquidity than reported and demands a liquidity premium

In a bear refinancing scenario — 9-10% coupon on a new $300M issuance — annual interest expense rises by $10-12M, which directly compresses AFFO/share by ~$0.35-0.42. On top of a shrinking AFFO base, this alone would push IIPR's payout ratio over 100% and force a cut.

**Mitigant:** IIPR can draw the $87.5M revolver and tap the ATM program for equity issuance. The ATM is sized at up to $500M per the May 2024 equity distribution agreement (2024 10-K line 120). But equity issuance at $55/share dilutes existing holders painfully — at current market cap, a $200M ATM raise would be ~10% dilution.

### Risk 2: Tenant concentration and domino default (probability: MEDIUM; severity: HIGH)

Top 5 tenants at 50% of revenue, all non-IG, all already showing stress. A second PharmaCann — i.e., a top-5 tenant entering default without a clean re-tenanting path — is a 30-40% probability event over 18 months. Ascend and Curaleaf are the candidates I watch most closely.

### Risk 3: Schedule III permanently stalls (probability: MEDIUM; severity: MEDIUM)

If Schedule III doesn't pass in the Trump administration's first term, the cannabis tenant recovery has no catalyst. The 280E burden persists, tenant cash flows stay compressed, and IIPR's portfolio slowly degrades. This is a slow-burn scenario, not a sudden crisis.

### Risk 4: IG rating downgrade (probability: MEDIUM; severity: HIGH)

IIPR's IG rating is the single most valuable corporate asset right now. Moody's and S&P have both signaled reviews in 2024-2025 but have not acted. A downgrade to BB+ would (a) increase Notes due 2026 coupon to 6.0-6.5% per the indenture's step-up provision (Q2 2025 10-Q line 771), (b) materially widen the refi spread, (c) kick the company out of investment-grade indices, and (d) trigger covenant attention on the revolver. A single notch downgrade alone costs IIPR $4-6M/year of incremental interest expense.

### Risk 5: IQHQ investment impairment (probability: LOW-MEDIUM; severity: MEDIUM)

IIPR has committed $270M to IQHQ. The $100M credit facility is now fully funded (Q3 2025 10-Q line 824). IQHQ is a private life science REIT with its own challenges — the life sciences real estate sector has been weak since 2022 as biotech funding dried up. If IQHQ gets into trouble, IIPR's $100M credit facility becomes a distressed asset and the $170M preferred commitment may not be recoverable. **This is a bolt-on risk that didn't exist six months ago.**

### Risk 6: Secondary litigation (probability: MEDIUM; severity: LOW)

The 2024 10-K Item 3 discloses pending securities class action lawsuits alleging the Company made false or misleading statements (line 620). These have been outstanding since 2022 and are in the normal course of litigation. Not a near-term binary risk but creates ongoing legal expense and management distraction.

## PART XI · VARIANT PERCEPTION — Why I Think the Consensus Is Wrong

The consensus bull case (as seen in Seeking Alpha, r/dividends, and a handful of retail newsletters) for IIPR runs as follows:

> "12% yield is absurdly high for an IG-rated REIT. Management has defended the dividend through the worst cannabis downturn ever. The 84% AFFO payout still has cushion. Schedule III is coming — probably 2026, definitely by 2028. The stock is trading at 7x AFFO versus 15x historical. Book value support at $67. This is the definition of a contrarian buy."

**I disagree with this case on four specific points:**

### 1. "12% yield is absurdly high for an IG-rated REIT"

**Rebuttal:** It is not absurdly high. It is pricing in a real and calculable probability of cut + refi trouble. At 40% cut probability × 25% cut size + 50% refi spread-widening probability × 250 bps of refi cost = ~6% of expected dividend compression + 2% of expected AFFO compression = roughly 8% of expected yield erosion. Add a 4% cost-of-equity for the sector and the 12% yield is actually **priced reasonably fairly for the risk**, not absurdly. The consensus is mistaking a justified risk premium for market inefficiency.

### 2. "Management has defended the dividend through the worst cannabis downturn"

**Rebuttal:** This is true as a historical statement but it is a bearish signal, not a bullish one. Management has defended the dividend because the equity story is built on it. The moment IIPR cuts, the stock loses its retail investor base. The board is in a behavioral trap — it is psychologically easier to keep paying a 96%-payout-ratio dividend than to cut it. When the cut eventually comes, it will be larger than necessary because the delay compounds the pressure. **Dividend defense through crisis is often a late-cycle signal, not an early-cycle one.** MPW, AT&T, and numerous others have followed this exact pattern.

### 3. "Trading at 7x AFFO vs. 15x historical is a value setup"

**Rebuttal:** The 15x historical was earned during a zero-interest-rate regime with cannabis tailwinds, investment-grade financing at 5.50%, and explosive revenue growth. None of those conditions exist now. **The correct comparable is not IIPR's own history — it is other distressed REITs trading at 6-8x AFFO.** At that comp set, IIPR is fairly valued, not deeply undervalued. Calling it "7x vs. 15x historical" is anchoring bias.

### 4. "Schedule III is coming"

**Rebuttal:** Schedule III was "coming" in 2024. It was "coming" in early 2025. The DEA rulemaking was canceled. The Trump administration has been silent. Every month without action is another month of tenant cash flow compression. The option has a positive expected value but negative theta — time is working against IIPR's tenants. **If Schedule III passes in 2027, the recovery is 12-18 months delayed from tenant economics recovering, which puts meaningful AFFO recovery into late 2028.** The time value of money on that option has decayed meaningfully.

### My variant view

**The correct trade is not a long position — it is either a pair trade or a wait.** If you want cannabis-rescheduling optionality without the refinancing and tenant risk, you are better off owning Green Thumb Industries equity directly (it is IIPR's strongest tenant, has its own balance sheet, and would benefit more from Schedule III than IIPR as a landlord would). If you want distressed REIT yield, there are cleaner setups in the commercial sector. IIPR is the intersection of three risks — tenant credit, refinancing, and regulatory — and the 12% yield compensates for roughly two of the three. The consensus is underweighting the refinancing cliff because it reads like a technical issue that "management will handle."

**My call: the next 13 months will be defined by the Notes due 2026 refinancing. Everything else is noise. If the refi closes clean (≤7.5% coupon, no equity raise required), IIPR re-rates to $75+. If the refi closes ugly (>8% coupon or requires a dilutive ATM raise), IIPR drops to $45. The current $55-65 range reflects uncertainty on this binary event.**

### Thesis in one paragraph

IIPR is a first-mover cannabis-facility REIT that earned a generational position in 2016-2021, then hit structural cannabis-industry headwinds in 2022-2025 while carrying an approaching 2026 debt maturity that exceeds its current liquidity. Management has admirably defended the dividend through three years of tenant stress — it has actually grown from $7.10 (2022) to $7.52 (2024) to $7.60 (2025) rather than being cut — but the AFFO payout ratio is now approaching 96% and the cushion has evaporated. The single binary catalyst is the May 2026 refinancing of the $291M Notes due 2026. A clean refinancing re-rates the stock to $75; a dirty refinancing or forced equity raise drops it to $45. The 12%+ yield reasonably prices this risk. Schedule III reclassification is real optionality but has extended past the Trump first term. I would not initiate a position above $55 and would wait for the refinancing to clear before sizing up. This is a name to watch, not to own, for a senior dividend investor.

## AI-ONLY LAYER — Insights from reading all 28 IIPR filings end-to-end

### Tone evolution (2022-2025)

I read all 28 SEC filings in the IIPR archive in chronological order. The management tone evolution is striking and, I think, more informative than any single quarterly number:

- **2019 10-K:** Euphoric. Growth is the only topic. Phrases like "tremendous growth" and "significant opportunities" dominate.
- **2020 10-K:** Confident. COVID is mentioned but framed as a "test we passed."
- **2021 10-K:** Peak confidence. "Preserving Financial Flexibility" section notes 15% debt-to-gross-assets, "no debt maturing in 2022 or 2023."
- **2022 10-K:** First cracks. The Kings Garden default is disclosed but framed as a one-off. New risk factor language emerges around "continuing unfavorable market dynamics."
- **2023 10-K:** Defensive. "Continuing unfavorable market dynamics affecting the regulated cannabis industry could adversely affect our business, liquidity and financial condition, and overall results of operations" — this risk factor was new in 2023. Rent deferral program disclosed.
- **2024 10-K:** **Clinical.** The tone has shifted from salesmanship to clinical disclosure. PharmaCann December 2024 default is described factually with specific dollar amounts. Management emphasizes the "strength of our investment-grade rated balance sheet" in explicitly defensive language.
- **Q2 2025 10-Q:** First explicit liquidity disclosure. "[T]he Company currently does not have sufficient liquidity to satisfy this obligation at maturity." This is the language a company uses when it is laying the groundwork for a specific disclosure — either the refi announcement or a more difficult conversation with investors.

**The tone is now at the clinical-disclosure stage, which precedes either (a) a successful refi announcement or (b) a restructuring.** The next 2-3 quarters will resolve which.

### Cross-filing consistency checks

Several items tracked across the 28 filings caught my attention:

1. **"Preserving Financial Flexibility" section.** This section appears in every 10-K from 2017-2024. In 2021 and 2022, it emphasizes the low 12-15% debt/gross-asset ratio and the "no near-term maturities." **In the 2024 10-K, the same section still claims low leverage (11%) but omits any discussion of how the Notes due 2026 maturity will be addressed.** This is a material omission. The conversation had to move to the Q2 2025 10-Q liquidity disclosure.

2. **Cash balance trajectory.** Cash and equivalents peaked at approximately $530M in 2021, declined to ~$200M by 2023, ~$146M by end of 2024, ~$100M by mid-2025, and ~$78M (estimated) by end of Q3 2025. **The decline is steady and continuing.** No filing discusses the cash decline explicitly. Management is not addressing a material trend in its own disclosures, which I consider a minor yellow flag.

3. **Employee count.** 22 full-time employees as of 2024. No disclosure of any staff expansion despite the complexity of managing tenant defaults and a strategic pivot. For comparison, VICI has 28 employees managing $46B of assets (similar ratio), but VICI also has a 100% rent collection track record. 22 employees managing 109 properties across 19 states WITH active tenant workouts is tight.

4. **Kings Garden recovery disclosure.** The Kings Garden settlement was initially disclosed as a "confidential settlement" in September 2022. By 2024, the disclosure had evolved to reveal Kings Garden had been continuing to occupy properties through September 2023 under a "stipulated rent" arrangement. **This pattern — initial thin disclosure, later more complete disclosure — is how cannabis tenant workouts get handled at IIPR.** Investors should assume the current PharmaCann disclosures are also incomplete.

### Topics that disappeared from filings

Several topics present in 2019-2021 filings that are no longer mentioned in 2024-2025:

- **Growth in new states.** The 2019-2021 filings emphasized "expanding as additional states enact regulated cannabis programs." In 2024-2025 this has been replaced with more defensive language about "maintaining our existing portfolio."
- **Forward acquisition pipeline.** 2021 10-K disclosed a robust pipeline; 2024 10-K acquired only 2 new properties all year.
- **Exchangeable Senior Notes.** These matured February 2024 and the topic is now closed. But the fact that IIPR once had a $143M exchangeable note and now has nothing equivalent outstanding tells you the company is increasingly reliant on the Notes due 2026 as its sole debt instrument.
