## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE at $8.64 · Dividend Safety: 6/10 · Confidence: Medium**

Blue Owl Capital is an alternative asset manager purpose-built to deliver dividend income from an industry that traditionally delivers lumpy, performance-driven returns. The company's three platforms -- Credit ($157.8B AUM), Real Assets ($80.6B AUM), and GP Strategic Capital ($69.1B AUM) -- collectively manage $307.4B as of December 31, 2025, nearly quadrupling the approximately $83B at the May 2021 IPO. Approximately 85% of management fees derive from Permanent Capital vehicles with no fund-life expiration and no forced redemption pressure. Fee-Related Earnings have grown every single quarter since going public -- now 20 consecutive quarters of management fee and FRE growth through Q4 2025.

The stock price has collapsed roughly 60% from the $21.50 level of early 2025, driven by a combination of tariff-induced market panic, credit cycle fears, and the market's difficulty parsing OWL's complex GAAP financials. At $8.64, OWL yields approximately 10.6% on the announced $0.92 annualized dividend for 2026. The yield-on-cost for our position (cost basis $14.19) is 6.5%. This is a dislocation-level yield for a growing alternative asset manager with $307B of permanent and long-dated capital.

The critical question is whether the dividend is sustainable in a downturn. FY2025 Distributable Earnings were $0.84/share on a fully diluted basis, while dividends paid were $0.855/share -- a payout ratio of approximately 107-108%. Management explicitly acknowledged this on the Q4 2025 earnings call and stated they intend to bring the payout ratio back to approximately 85% over the next few years, with the 2026 dividend set at $0.92 (up 7.6% from $0.855). The math works only if FRE per share growth accelerates to the low-to-mid teens, which management guided toward for 2027 specifically. At 6/10 dividend safety, this is a real but manageable concern: the underlying business generates $1.26B of operating cash flow and $1.20B of free cash flow on $547M of Class A dividends -- the gap is the $929M of equivalent distributions to noncontrolling interests (Class C and D holders) that flow through the partnership structure.

At $8.64, the risk-reward is compelling for a patient dividend investor. If the business continues to grow FRE at 12-15% annually (as guided), the payout ratio will compress naturally, the dividend will resume growing at double-digit rates, and the share price should re-rate toward $15-20 over 2-3 years. If a severe credit cycle materializes, direct lending losses could impair AUM and fees, but the Permanent Capital base provides a structural floor that traditional asset managers lack. The position is small (2,000 shares, ~0.67% of portfolio), appropriately sized for the risk profile.

Blue Owl Capital Inc. is a Delaware-incorporated global alternative asset manager headquartered at 399 Park Avenue, New York. The company was formed through the May 2021 three-way business combination of Owl Rock Capital Group (founded 2016, middle-market direct lending), Dyal Capital Partners (founded 2010, GP minority stakes), and Altimar Acquisition Corporation (a SPAC). The firm subsequently acquired Oak Street Real Estate Capital (net lease, December 2021), Wellfleet (CLOs, April 2022), Par Four (CLO management, August 2023), CHI/Cowen Healthcare Investments (life sciences, December 2023), Prima Capital Advisors (real estate credit, June 2024), Kuvare Asset Management (insurance asset management, July 2024), Atalaya Capital Management (alternative credit, September 2024), and IPI Partners (digital infrastructure, January 2025). This is nine acquisitions in four and a half years -- an extraordinarily aggressive pace.

The firm is led by co-founders Doug Ostrover (Co-CEO), Marc Lipschultz (Co-CEO), and Craig Packer (Co-President and COO), with Michael Rees (Co-President, GP Strategic Capital), Marc Zahr (Co-President, Real Assets), and Alan Kirshenbaum (CFO). ## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW -- STRATEGIC ARC SINCE IPO

Blue Owl's history is best understood as four distinct phases, each with different implications for dividend investors.

**Phase 1 -- Business Combination and IPO (May 2021).** Three complementary businesses combined: Owl Rock's $25B direct lending franchise generated predictable management fees and Part I Fees from public BDCs (OBDC, OBDE); Dyal's $23B GP stakes business collected recurring fee shares from 30+ alternative asset managers; and the SPAC provided the public listing vehicle plus cash. The combination created a roughly $52B AUM platform. Within seven months, Oak Street Real Estate Capital was acquired, adding a third leg (net lease real estate) and bringing AUM to approximately $70B by year-end 2021.

**Phase 2 -- Permanent Capital Buildout (2022-2023).** Management focused on organic growth of the Permanent Capital base. OCIC (Blue Owl Credit Income Corp) emerged as the fastest-growing non-traded BDC in the industry, driven by private wealth distribution through wirehouses and RIAs. ORENT (Blue Owl Real Estate Net Lease Trust) launched as a non-traded REIT. GP Strategic Capital raised successive vintage funds, with GP Stakes V becoming the largest-ever GP minority stakes fund. AUM reached approximately $128B by year-end 2022 and approximately $165B by year-end 2023. FRE margin expanded from approximately 50% to approximately 56%. The dividend grew from approximately $0.12/quarter (late 2021) to $0.14/quarter (late 2023), tracking Distributable Earnings growth.

**Phase 3 -- The Acquisition Sprint (2024-2025).** This phase fundamentally changed the scale and composition of Blue Owl. Six acquisitions in twenty-four months transformed the firm from a direct-lending-plus-GP-stakes platform into a full-spectrum alternative asset manager:

| Acquisition | Date | AUM Added (~) | Strategic Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prima Capital Advisors | June 2024 | ~$15B | Real estate credit, CRE lending |
| Kuvare Asset Management (KAM) | July 2024 | ~$18B | Insurance channel, investment grade credit |
| Atalaya Capital Management | Sept 2024 | ~$10B | Alternative credit, specialty finance |
| CHI/Cowen Healthcare | Dec 2023 | ~$2B | Life sciences equity |
| Par Four CLO Management | Aug 2023 | ~$3B | Liquid credit, CLOs |
| IPI Partners | Jan 2025 | ~$14.2B | Digital infrastructure, data centers |

The acquisitions added approximately $60B of AUM and diversified revenue from approximately 70% direct lending/GP stakes to a broader mix including alternative credit (Atalaya), insurance channel (KAM), digital infrastructure (IPI), and real estate credit (Prima). Total AUM reached $307.4B by year-end 2025. However, this growth came at a cost: debt rose from approximately $1.5B (2022) to $3.32B (2025), Class A dilution was 44% over two years (464M to 667M shares), and integration complexity increased dramatically.

**Phase 4 -- Consolidation, Organic Growth, and Price Collapse (2026-Forward).** The current phase is defined by two simultaneous dynamics: (1) management is pivoting from acquisition-driven growth to organic growth, with a stated goal of proving the platform compounds without M&A, and (2) the stock price has collapsed approximately 60% from its 2025 peak, driven by tariff fears, credit cycle concerns, and the broader re-pricing of financial services equities. The Q4 2025 earnings call struck a confident tone -- FRE per share growth of 12% in 2025, guidance for modest acceleration in 2026 and further acceleration in 2027. The dividend was raised 7.6% (from $0.855 to $0.92), and management explicitly stated the payout ratio will be brought back to approximately 85% over the next few years.

## PART VI · THE PERMANENT CAPITAL ADVANTAGE

This section is the core of the investment thesis. Blue Owl's investability as a dividend stock rests entirely on the permanence and durability of its fee base.

**What "Permanent Capital" means operationally.** In traditional private equity or hedge funds, the fund has a fixed life (typically 10 years). When the fund expires, AUM goes to zero and management fees stop. The manager must raise a successor fund or lose revenue -- creating a perpetual fundraising treadmill. Permanent Capital vehicles have no fixed expiration date. OCIC (the non-traded direct lending BDC), ORENT (the non-traded net lease REIT), OBDC (the publicly traded BDC), and similar vehicles can exist indefinitely. Investors may have quarterly or semi-annual redemption rights, but there is no forced liquidation date. Management fees accrue continuously on deployed capital.

**Quantification from the 10-K.** For the year ended December 31, 2025, approximately 85% of management fees were earned from Permanent Capital vehicles. The remainder was predominantly from long-dated capital (5+ year fund lives). During Q1 2025, this figure was even higher at 88-89%, as the IPI acquisition temporarily increased the Permanent Capital percentage (IPI's funds are digital infrastructure strategies with very long deployment periods). The permanence percentage may decline modestly as Real Assets launches more drawdown-style funds (like the seventh vintage net lease fund), but the structural floor is approximately 80%.

**Redemption risk is real but manageable.** The Q4 2025 earnings call directly addressed the BDC redemption dynamic: "Industry non-traded BDCs experienced a slowdown in capital raising and elevated redemptions during the fourth quarter. This is in line with what we have seen in prior market environments with heightened volatility and fear. We saw this during COVID, with the Silicon Valley Bank failure, and after tariffs were announced last year. We've always managed our funds with a sharp focus on leverage and liquidity, and during the fourth quarter, we met all investor requests for tenders as we have every quarter since inception." This is the critical test case -- Blue Owl has never gated redemptions in any product. The OTIC product (technology-focused BDC distributed heavily in Asia) saw the most stress, but management raised its tender threshold proactively. For context, Blackstone's BREIT faced a severe redemption crisis in 2022-2023 that became front-page news and impaired fundraising for over a year. Blue Owl has avoided a comparable event so far.

**The embedded growth wedge.** As of December 31, 2025, $28.4B of AUM was not yet paying fees, representing approximately $326M of annualized management fees once deployed. At a 58.3% FRE margin, this translates to approximately $190M of incremental FRE -- roughly 13% growth from AUM already committed and in-house. This is deferred revenue that requires only capital deployment (in credit products) or lease-up and development (in net lease and digital infrastructure products) to convert into earnings. No additional fundraising required.

## PART VII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Four pillars of the moat:**

1. **Scale in direct lending.** $115B AUM in direct lending makes Blue Owl one of the two or three largest private lenders in the world (alongside Ares and Apollo). Scale matters because larger platforms see more deal flow, can hold larger positions (reducing syndication risk for borrowers), and can provide one-stop financing across the capital structure. Average deal size reached approximately $2 billion in 2025, up 23% year-over-year. Approximately 60% of 2025 gross originations came from existing borrower relationships -- a powerful indicator of incumbency advantage. Gross deployment of $45.4B over the trailing twelve months through Q4 2025 is volume that mid-market competitors cannot replicate.

2. **GP Strategic Capital network effects.** Blue Owl owns minority stakes in 60+ alternative asset managers through its GP minority stakes strategy ($65.1B AUM). This creates a unique information network: Blue Owl sees the fundraising, deployment, and performance data of its Partner Managers. This proprietary data informs credit underwriting decisions, co-investment opportunities, and new product development. The platform generated $4B of distributions to investors in 2025 through innovative "strip sale" transactions, ranking in the top quartile on distributions-paid-in -- a critical metric for institutional investors. Blue Owl GP Stakes V was more than twice as large as the next competitor's fund.

3. **Private wealth distribution infrastructure.** OCIC, ORENT, OTIC, and the newly launched ODiT (digital infrastructure evergreen) and OWLCX (alternative credit interval fund) are distributed through a network of wirehouses, RIAs, and wealth platforms. Total equity raised across the five private wealth evergreen products was $15.4B in 2025, representing 66% of beginning-of-period FPAUM in those products. This distribution infrastructure took years to build and represents a durable competitive advantage -- new entrants cannot simply replicate these relationships.

4. **Data center / AI infrastructure positioning.** The IPI acquisition positioned Blue Owl at the nexus of the data center construction boom. The firm is financing three of the largest data center projects in the United States. Digital infrastructure Fund I was fully realized at an approximately 11.5% net IRR (announced Q4 2025). Fund III is already $7.6B in AUM with a 33.5% gross IRR. The new ODiT evergreen product raised $1.7B in its first close. This is a secular tailwind -- hyperscaler CapEx is measured in hundreds of billions annually -- and Blue Owl has positioned itself as a financing partner of choice.

**Risk 1 -- Credit cycle hits direct lending.** This is the existential risk for OWL as a dividend stock. Direct lending accounts for $115B AUM (37% of total) and generates the majority of Credit platform management fees. In a recession, middle-market borrower defaults rise, BDC NAVs decline, Part I Fees compress, and management fees on NAV-based products decline mechanically. The Q4 2025 earnings call was remarkably bullish on credit quality: "average annualized net realized loss rate has been eight basis points, encompassing realized losses and gains...well below the industry average." Borrowers showed "high single-digit revenue growth and low teens EBITDA growth year over year." Weighted average LTVs were "high thirties" across diversified lending and "low thirties" in tech lending. These metrics are pristine today, but they have never been tested by a severe recession. The tariff shock of early 2026 is the first real stress test. If defaults surge to 3-5% and NAVs decline 10-15%, management fees could decline 5-8% and Part I Fees could compress 20-30%.

**Risk 2 -- Tariff-driven recession.** The new risk since the prior report. The Q2 2025 10-Q explicitly noted: "During the second quarter of 2025, global equity and debt markets experienced significant volatility driven by the announcement of tariffs, which impacted investor expectations regarding economic growth and multi-national trade dynamics." The Q1 2025 10-Q was even more pointed: "global equity and debt markets experienced volatility driven by elevated inflation, economic slowdown and ongoing developments regarding international trade and economic policies." If tariffs lead to a sustained recession, PE deal flow declines (reducing direct lending origination volume), BDC investors redeem (potentially creating BREIT-like liquidity stress), and private wealth fundraising slows (reducing the growth engine). OWL's defense is the Permanent Capital structure: even if new fundraising slows to zero, the existing AUM base generates approximately $2.5B of management fees annually.

**Risk 3 -- Share dilution.** Class A shares outstanding grew from 464M (YE2023) to 667M (YE2025) -- a 44% increase in two years. The dilution sources are: (a) Class C-to-Class A exchanges (58.2M shares in FY2025, 108.7M in FY2024, 17.9M in FY2023 -- the pace is decelerating); (b) acquisition consideration (IPI added 43.5M Class C units, Atalaya added 20.0M, KAM consideration was primarily in Class A shares); (c) RSU settlements (4.5M shares in FY2025); and (d) vested Common Unit deliveries (13.3M shares in FY2025). The remaining Class C shares (584.6M) will eventually exchange for Class A, adding up to 87% to the current Class A count over time. However, this is not pure dilution -- each exchange reduces the noncontrolling interest distributions by the equivalent per-unit amount. Total DPS is unaffected by exchanges; the denominator of Class A shares grows but so does the numerator (Class A share of total distributions).

In the mild recession scenario, Blue Owl would likely freeze the dividend at $0.92 and draw on the revolver for 2-3 quarters. The decline in fees is proportional to NAV decline, not to AUM "walking out the door." This is the structural advantage versus traditional asset managers, where a recession can trigger fund-life expirations, accelerated realizations, and complete AUM loss.
