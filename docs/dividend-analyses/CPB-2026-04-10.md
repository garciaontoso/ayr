## PART I -- Executive Summary

The Campbell's Company (formerly Campbell Soup Company, renamed September 2024) is a 157-year-old packaged food and snacks business trading near multi-year lows at approximately $20.70 per share -- down more than 50% from its 2023 highs near $53. The stock now offers a yield north of 7.5%, a level that historically signals either deep value or an imminent dividend cut. This report, based on a line-by-line reading of seven years of SEC filings from FY2019 through Q2 FY2026, concludes that the truth lies somewhere in between.

Campbell's is in the middle of a high-stakes transformation. The company levered up $2.899 billion in March 2024 to acquire Sovos Brands (the parent of Rao's premium pasta sauces), took $305 million in cumulative trademark impairment charges on its Snacks portfolio in FY2024--FY2025, changed its corporate name, replaced its CEO, and is now absorbing tariff-driven gross margin compression of 230+ basis points in Q2 FY2026 alone. The $1.56 annualized dividend ($0.39/quarter) is technically fundable from free cash flow -- FY2025 FCF of $705 million covers the approximately $465 million annual dividend cost at 66% -- but there is virtually no cushion after servicing $345 million in annual interest expense, $420 million in guided capex, $286 million for the pending La Regina vertical integration deal, and $185 million in restructuring cash costs. The "free" in free cash flow is not very free.

The thesis reduces to a single question: can Rao's growth and $375 million in annualized cost savings (targeted by FY2028 end, of which $180 million has been realized through Q2 FY2026) offset the structural decline in soup volumes and Snacks segment deterioration -- all while tariffs eat 230+ basis points of gross margin? If yes, adjusted EPS recovers to $3.00+ and the stock re-rates to $36--49. If no, the dividend eventually gets cut and the stock finds $15--18. We assign approximately 65--70% probability to the former and 30--35% to the latter, making this a HOLD at $20.70 for existing shareholders, with speculative accumulation only below $18.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield / Payout: $20.70 / 7.5% / 75% (EPS) / 66% (FCF)
- Last dividend hike: H2 FY2025, from $0.37 to $0.39/quarter (+5.4%)
- 5y dividend CAGR: approximately 1.3% ($1.40 in FY2019 to $1.56 annualized in FY2026)
- Net debt / EBITDA: approximately 4.5x ($7.08B net debt / $1.575B FY2025 EBITDA)
- Free cash flow coverage: 1.52x dividend ($705M / $465M)
- Dividend streak: approximately 46 years of consecutive payments
- Shares outstanding: 298 million (essentially flat since FY2022)

**Inflection points:**

1. **FY2019 -- Post-Snyder's-Lance debt mountain.** Campbell's had just completed the $6.1 billion Snyder's-Lance acquisition (March 2018) and the $688 million Pacific Foods deal (December 2017). Total debt peaked near $9.5 billion. The company simultaneously began divesting non-core assets (Bolthouse Farms $500M, Kelsen $300M, Arnott's $2.2B) to delever. Interest expense hit $356 million, consuming a third of EBIT. This was survival mode.

2. **FY2020--FY2021 -- COVID pantry loading and debt paydown.** The pandemic drove extraordinary soup demand. Revenue peaked at $8,691M in FY2020 (a 53-week year). Gross margin hit 34.5%, the highest in the modern era. Campbell's used the windfall to aggressively pay down debt, reducing total borrowings from $9.5B to approximately $5.0B by August 2021 -- a remarkable $4.5 billion deleveraging in three years funded by asset sales and strong cash generation.

3. **FY2022--FY2023 -- Inflation hits, pricing power tested.** As pandemic demand normalized, Campbell's faced severe cost inflation across the supply chain. Gross margin compressed from 33.2% (FY2021) to 30.7% (FY2022) before partially recovering to 31.2% (FY2023) as pricing actions stuck. EPS recovered from $2.51 to $2.85 as the company demonstrated reasonable pricing discipline.

4. **FY2024 -- The Sovos Brands bet.** The $2.899B acquisition of Sovos Brands (closed March 12, 2024) re-leveraged the balance sheet dramatically. Total debt jumped from approximately $5.1B to $7.2B. Interest expense rose 33% from $188M to $249M. Integration costs of $128M ($109M after tax) further depressed earnings. EPS fell to $1.89 despite revenue rising 3% on the Sovos contribution.

5. **FY2025 -- Name change, impairments, tariffs arrive.** Campbell's became "The Campbell's Company" and reported FY2025 revenue of $10,253M, boosted by Sovos (8 points) and the 53rd week (2 points). But the headline masked $176M in Snacks trademark impairments (Snyder's of Hanover $150M, Late July $11M, Allied brands $15M), $125M in cost savings initiative charges, and the beginning of tariff headwinds ($0.02/share in FY2025). Net income of $602M and EPS of $2.01 included $0.97/share in items impacting comparability; adjusted EPS was approximately $2.98.

6. **H1 FY2026 -- Full tariff impact.** Q2 FY2026 (ended February 1, 2026) showed the damage: net sales declined 5% to $2,564M, gross margin collapsed to 28.0% from 30.5% (230 basis points of tariff-driven margin compression), and EPS fell 17% to $0.48 from $0.58. Snacks volume/mix declined 6%. Management warned of "more significant cost pressures primarily driven by tariff impacts" continuing throughout FY2026.

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The dividend appears fundable but not comfortable. At $465 million annualized (298M shares x $1.56), it consumes 66% of FY2025 FCF ($705M) and 75--77% of FY2025 GAAP earnings. However, "free cash flow" is misleading here because it ignores several non-discretionary cash demands:

1. **Interest expense:** $345M in FY2025, running at approximately $340M annualized in FY2026 ($163M for H1 per 10-Q x 2). This is already subtracted from operating cash flow, so it does not double-count with the FCF payout ratio, but it constrains flexibility.

2. **Mandatory capex:** Management guided $420M for FY2026 (10-K), plus $215M in cost-savings-related capital spending (of which $187M has been spent through Q2 FY2026). The guided $370M capex figure in Q2 FY2026 10-Q reflects updated estimates.

3. **La Regina acquisition:** $146M cash at closing (expected by June 8, 2026) plus $140M on the first anniversary (payable in cash or stock at Campbell's discretion). If paid in cash, this is $286M over 12--13 months.

4. **Restructuring cash costs:** Of the $245M total pre-tax costs identified, approximately $185M are expected to be cash expenditures (per Q2 FY2026 10-Q Note 8).

When layered together, true discretionary cash flow after the dividend is near zero in FY2026--FY2027. The revolving credit facility ($1.85B, extended to April 2030, unused as of February 2026) provides a liquidity backstop, but a company that must rely on revolving credit to fund its dividend is, by definition, living on borrowed time.

The Dorrance family's historical ownership stake provides a soft floor for the dividend, as the family reportedly depends on dividend income. But even family shareholders may prefer a temporary reduction over permanent balance sheet deterioration if FY2026 FCF disappoints.

**Recession stress test:** In FY2021, when soup demand was elevated, Campbell's generated $1,035M in OCF on $8,476M revenue. A moderate recession that reduced revenue 10% from current levels (to ~$9.2B) while compressing gross margins to 29% would yield approximately $850--900M in OCF and $500--550M in FCF after maintenance capex. The dividend would still be covered at 1.1--1.2x, but with zero room for debt service flexibility. A severe recession (15% revenue decline + 27% gross margin) would make the dividend mathematically impossible without drawing on the revolver.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

- **Moat type:** Brand (primary), scale (secondary), distribution (Snacks DSD network)
- **Moat trajectory:** Narrowing. Private label is gaining share across soup, sauces, and snacks. The sole widening element is Rao's premium positioning.
- **Pricing power evidence:** Mixed. FY2023 saw positive net price realization; FY2024 was neutral; FY2025 was slightly negative (-1 point in Meals & Beverages). In Q2 FY2026, net price realization was "neutral" despite 230bps of tariff cost increases -- meaning Campbell's is absorbing tariffs rather than passing them through, a sign of constrained pricing power in a volume-challenged environment.

If Rao's continues gaining share, the moat is stabilizing. If Rao's growth decelerates (premium fatigue in a recessionary consumer environment), the moat is decisively narrowing.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

1. **Dividends:** $459M in FY2025, $445M in FY2024, $447M in FY2023. Approximately 65% of FCF. This is management's stated first priority. The board declared $0.39/quarter through Q3 FY2026 (payable May 4, 2026).

2. **Buybacks:** $62M in FY2025 (1.303M shares at ~$47.58 average), $67M in FY2024, $142M in FY2023. Slashed to $26M in H1 FY2026. As of February 2026, $172M remains under the September 2024 anti-dilutive program and $301M under the September 2021 strategic program. Buybacks are effectively suspended, which is rational given the leverage, but it means the 298M share count will not shrink.

3. **Capex:** $426M in FY2025, $517M in FY2024, $370M in FY2023. Guided $420M for FY2026 (10-K), later updated to approximately $370M (Q2 FY2026 10-Q). Major projects include manufacturing network optimization, wastewater facility upgrades (Napoleon, Ohio: $35M), and SAP implementation for Sovos Brands ($215M total budget, $187M spent through Q2 FY2026).

4. **M&A:** Sovos Brands acquired for $2.899B in March 2024 (funded by $2B delayed draw term loan + cash). La Regina 49% stake pending at $286M ($146M at closing + $140M at one year). Pop Secret sold for $70M (August 2024), noosa sold for $188M (February 2025), Emerald nuts sold for $41M (May 2023).

5. **Debt paydown:** The deleveraging cycle from FY2019 to FY2021 was impressive ($9.5B to $5.0B). However, the Sovos acquisition reversed the trajectory entirely. Total debt climbed from approximately $5.1B (FY2023) to $7.2B (FY2025) to approximately $7.4B (Q2 FY2026). The $400M March 2026 note maturity was expected to be refinanced using available resources. Long-term debt rose from $6,095M to $6,647M between August 2025 and February 2026, partly due to the $550M note issuance at 4.55% (December 2025).

**Capital allocation grade: C.** The Sovos Brands acquisition was strategically sensible but poorly timed -- taking on $2.9B of debt at the exact moment when the legacy portfolio entered volume decline, tariffs were rising, and consumer trade-down was accelerating. The prior deleveraging discipline was commendable; the decision to re-lever was aggressive. Selling noosa at a $15M after-tax loss 11 months after acquiring it through Sovos suggests insufficient pre-acquisition diligence on non-core assets.

### PART VIII-A -- The Sovos Brands Acquisition: The $2.9 Billion Bet

On March 12, 2024, Campbell's closed the acquisition of Sovos Brands, Inc. for total purchase consideration of $2.899 billion (FY2025 10-K, Note 3). The deal was funded by a $2 billion delayed draw term loan credit agreement plus cash on hand (FY2024 10-K). This was the largest acquisition in Campbell's history since the $6.1 billion Snyder's-Lance deal in 2018 -- and it carries the same transformative risk.

**What was acquired:** Sovos Brands owned three primary brands: Rao's (premium pasta sauces, dry pasta, frozen entrees, frozen pizza, and soups), Michael Angelo's (frozen entrees and pasta sauces), and noosa (yogurt). The crown jewel was unambiguously Rao's, which the market valued at the vast majority of the $2.9B purchase price. Campbell's assigned a $1.47 billion indefinite-lived trademark value to Rao's alone -- the single most valuable brand in the combined portfolio, exceeding any individual Campbell's, Pepperidge Farm, or Snyder's trademark.

**Strategic rationale:** Campbell's was seeking a premium growth platform to offset the structural decline in its soup business. Rao's had grown from a niche restaurant brand to a mass-market premium sauce with significant distribution momentum. By acquiring Sovos, Campbell's gained a brand with demonstrated pricing power (premium price point of $8--10 per jar vs $3--4 for mainstream sauces), strong consumer loyalty, and category share gains in a flat-to-declining pasta sauce market. The rationale was sound; the execution risk was in the timing and leverage.

**What has gone right:**
- Rao's has continued gaining share. The Q2 FY2026 10-Q MD&A notes "gains in Rao's" as the sole positive contributor within Meals & Beverages while every other major brand declined. The H1 FY2026 six-month discussion confirms this pattern year-to-date.
- The Rao's trademark value has not been impaired (unlike multiple Snacks trademarks), suggesting cash flow expectations remain on track.
- La Regina vertical integration ($286M for 49% stake) addresses the single biggest supply chain risk -- Rao's dependence on a third-party manufacturer at a single Italian facility. Once closed, Campbell's will consolidate La Regina's results and control its own supply chain for its most valuable brand.
- Sovos integration is progressing: the SAP enterprise-resource planning system implementation for Sovos Brands is underway ($187M of $215M capital budget spent through Q2 FY2026), and integration cost synergies have been folded into the broader $375M savings program.

**What has gone wrong:**
- **Timing was terrible.** Campbell's re-levered from $5.1B to $7.2B in total debt at the precise moment when tariffs were escalating (increasing costs for Italian imports -- the very supply chain Rao's depends on), consumer trade-down was accelerating (pressuring premium price points), and the legacy Soup and Snacks businesses were entering volume decline. Interest expense jumped from $188M (FY2023) to $345M (FY2025) -- an incremental $157M annual cash cost.
- **Noosa was a waste.** The noosa yogurt business, included in the Sovos deal, was sold for $188M in February 2025 -- just 11 months after acquisition. The after-tax loss was $15M (FY2025 10-K). This suggests either insufficient pre-acquisition diligence on the Sovos portfolio or an inability to negotiate the yogurt business out of the transaction.
- **Integration costs were substantial.** FY2024 incurred $128M ($109M after tax, $0.36/share) in acquisition-related costs. These are real economic costs that shareholders bore.
- **Dilutive to margins.** The FY2025 10-K noted a negative margin impact from "the dilutive impact of the acquisition" on gross profit. Sovos Brands' margin profile, while healthy for Rao's, included lower-margin Michael Angelo's frozen products that diluted the consolidated gross margin.

**The La Regina deal (announced December 8, 2025):**
Campbell's is acquiring 49% of La Regina di San Marzano di Antonio Romano S.p.A. and La Regina Atlantica, LLC -- the entities that manufacture all of Rao's tomato-based pasta sauces. The aggregate consideration is $286 million: $146M in cash at closing, plus $140M payable at Campbell's discretion in cash or unregistered shares on the first anniversary. Campbell's also holds a call option to acquire additional equity (2%+ increments, from the first anniversary through the tenth anniversary, at an implied total equity value of approximately $584M plus up to 20% control premium). La Regina equity holders hold a put option exercisable from three years after the first anniversary.

This is strategically smart: the FY2025 10-K risk factors explicitly warn that Rao's sauce production depends on a single facility in Italy and a single facility in the US. Vertical integration removes single-source risk. But it requires $146M more cash at an already strained moment, with the outside closing date of June 8, 2026. If the deal does not close, the supply chain concentration risk persists indefinitely.

### PART VIII-B -- Snacks Segment: The Diversification Thesis Under Siege

The Snacks segment was supposed to be Campbell's escape from soup dependence. The $6.1 billion Snyder's-Lance acquisition (March 2018) and the legacy Pepperidge Farm/Goldfish franchise created a $4.6 billion snacking platform. The thesis was straightforward: snacking is secular growth, soup is secular decline, and the combined portfolio would stabilize revenue and margins.

Seven years later, the thesis is under severe strain.

**Revenue trajectory (FMP + 10-K data):**

The Snyder's of Hanover impairment ($150M) is particularly significant -- this was one of the cornerstone brands of the $6.1B Snyder's-Lance deal. Its trademark carrying value now has less than 10% excess fair-value coverage (per FY2025 annual impairment testing, as disclosed in the Q2 FY2026 10-Q Note 6).

**The DSD route optimization initiative** (up to $115M cost through 2029, $43M incurred through Q2 FY2026) is a further signal that the distribution model needs restructuring. Campbell's is buying routes from independent contractor distributors, combining them, and reselling combined routes -- essentially admitting the fragmented DSD network inherited from Snyder's-Lance is sub-scale in many markets.

**The lone bright spot: Goldfish.** Through FY2024, Goldfish was consistently cited as a growth driver. But the FY2025 10-K listed "declines in... Goldfish crackers" among the factors driving Snacks revenue lower. This may be temporary (new flavor cycle, promotional timing), but if Goldfish -- the single strongest sub-brand in the Snacks portfolio -- has plateaued, the segment has no organic growth engine remaining.

## PART X -- Risks & Stress Tests

**Risk 1: Dividend cut (probability: 25--35% over 18 months)**
The 7.5% yield implies the market is pricing 20--30% probability of a cut. We estimate actual probability at 25--35%. Triggers: FY2026 FCF below $500M; a credit downgrade; or further intangible impairments that erode equity. The interest coverage covenant floor of 3.25x (revolving credit facility) provides a hard constraint -- if trailing coverage approaches this level, the dividend becomes the obvious source of cash preservation. Q2 FY2026 trailing EBIT-to-interest is already near 3.2x on a quarterly annualized basis.

**Risk 2: Rao's single-source concentration**
The FY2025 10-K and Q2 FY2026 10-Q risk factors explicitly state: the substantial majority of Rao's tomato-based sauce products are produced by a third-party manufacturer at a single facility in Italy and a single facility in the US. Until the La Regina deal closes (outside date June 8, 2026), this represents an unhedged catastrophic risk for the company's most valuable brand ($1.47B trademark). Fire, labor disruption, geopolitical shock, or regulatory issue at the Italian facility would remove Rao's sauces from shelves.

**Risk 3: Private label structural shift**
The 10-K risk factors warn repeatedly about private label competition. This is not cyclical -- retailer investment in private label quality, branding, and shelf space is a one-way door. In soup, sauces, and snacks, private label share is growing at the expense of mid-tier brands (which includes much of Campbell's portfolio outside Rao's and Goldfish). Campbell's cannot compete on price because it needs branded margins to service $7.2B of debt.

**Risk 4: Tariff escalation and commodity costs**
Campbell's is among the most tariff-exposed packaged food companies due to: (a) tinplate steel for cans (Campbell's is one of the largest can users in US food); (b) Italian imports for Rao's (sauces manufactured using Italian tomatoes at La Regina's facility); (c) Canadian operations and cross-border sourcing. The Q2 FY2026 10-Q quantifies the damage: 230 basis points of gross margin headwind, $0.06/share net EPS impact in Q2 alone, $0.10/share for H1. At annualized rates of $0.20/share, tariffs consume approximately 7--10% of adjusted earnings.

**Risk 5: Intangible impairment cascade**
$2.587 billion of indefinite-lived trademarks had 10% or less excess fair-value coverage at FY2025 annual testing (10-K and Q2 10-Q Note 6). These include Rao's ($1.47B), Snyder's of Hanover (already impaired $150M in FY2025), Pace, Pacific Foods, Late July, and Allied brands. A further decline in discount rates, volumes, or margins could trigger hundreds of millions in additional impairment charges. While non-cash, impairments destroy reported earnings, reduce equity, and can trigger debt covenant concerns if equity cushion narrows further.

**Risk 6: Middle East conflict**
The Q2 FY2026 10-Q specifically references the February 28, 2026 military conflict involving the US, Israel, and Iran, warning it "could lead to significant disruption of energy supplies and increases in global energy prices." Campbell's is directly exposed through natural gas (production), diesel fuel (Snacks DSD distribution network), and petroleum-derived packaging inputs.

**Risk 7: CEO and CFO transition**
The CEO changed (Clouse to Beekhuizen) and the CFO changed (Anderson to Cunfer) within approximately 18 months. Both the CEO and CFO are relatively new to their roles at a moment requiring exceptional operational execution.

## PART XI -- Thesis & Verdict

**Thesis (one paragraph):**
Campbell's is a leveraged turnaround bet disguised as a blue-chip dividend stock. The Sovos Brands acquisition gives the company a genuine growth engine in Rao's and the $375M cost savings program has a credible track record ($180M realized through Q2 FY2026, prior Snyder's-Lance program delivered $950M). But the balance sheet is stretched at 4.5x net debt/EBITDA, gross margins are under severe tariff pressure (28% in Q2 FY2026 vs 33%+ pre-pandemic), the Snacks segment is deteriorating with $305M in cumulative impairments, and the dividend consumes virtually all discretionary cash. The approximately 46-year dividend streak survives only because management is prioritizing it over debt reduction and share buybacks. If tariffs moderate and cost savings accelerate, adjusted EPS recovers to $3.00+ and the stock re-rates 75--100% from here. If tariffs persist and volumes continue declining, a dividend cut within 18 months becomes highly probable. The risk/reward at $20.70 is balanced but does not offer sufficient margin of safety for aggressive accumulation.

**Verdict:** HOLD at $20.70 if already owned. Speculative BUY below $18 with eyes open to 25--35% cut probability. Do not add at current levels if portfolio cannot absorb a 25--50% dividend reduction.

**Position sizing:** Maximum 1.5--2% of a dividend portfolio. The yield is attractive but the downside risk from either a dividend cut or continued earnings deterioration is material. This is not a core holding -- it is a turnaround option with a coupon.

**Monitoring checklist:**
- [ ] Q3 FY2026 earnings (expected June 2026): gross margin trajectory -- does 28% Q2 improve or worsen?
- [ ] La Regina acquisition closing (outside date June 8, 2026): removes Rao's single-source risk
- [ ] FY2026 annual dividend declaration (typically September): the binary event for the streak
- [ ] Cost savings program update: track toward $375M by FY2028 end ($180M realized through Q2)
- [ ] Interest coverage ratio: must stay above 3.25x covenant threshold
- [ ] Tariff negotiation outcomes: tinplate steel, Italian imports, Canadian inputs
- [ ] Snacks segment volume trends: any stabilization in chips, pretzels, fresh bakery?
- [ ] Rao's market share data: continued gains or signs of premium fatigue?

**Exit triggers:**
- Dividend cut or suspension
- Interest coverage falling below 3.5x on a trailing four-quarter basis
- Further intangible impairments exceeding $200M (cumulative beyond FY2025 levels)
- Rao's revenue declining for two consecutive quarters
- Credit rating downgrade to below investment grade

## AI-ONLY LAYER

### Tone evolution (FY2023--Q2 FY2026)

Reading the MD&A sections sequentially across all filings reveals a clear shift from confidence to defensive caution:

**FY2023 10-K (September 2023):** Confident tone. Management highlighted "continued progress on our strategic agenda" and "gains in Goldfish crackers, Lance sandwich crackers and Cape Cod potato chips." The cost inflation narrative was presented as manageable via "supply chain productivity improvements" and "favorable net price realization." The Sovos Brands acquisition announcement was framed as transformative.

**FY2024 10-K (September 2024):** Transitional. The tone shifted to acknowledge "a dynamic operating environment marked by shifting global trade policies." Integration costs were disclosed matter-of-factly ($128M). The name change was positioned as forward-looking. But the $129M in impairments (Pop Secret, Allied brands) introduced a defensive undertone. The phrase "consumer behavior shifts" appeared for the first time as a risk factor.

**FY2025 10-K (September 2025):** Increasingly cautious. The word "tariff" appears 47 times in the FY2025 10-K, versus zero times in the FY2023 10-K. The $176M Snacks impairment disclosure was buried in Other Expenses but the magnitude was impossible to ignore. The cost savings target raise from $250M to $375M was framed positively but read more as an admission that more savings were needed to offset deteriorating top-line trends. The forward-looking language shifted from "growth" to "mitigation."

**Q2 FY2026 10-Q (March 2026):** Overtly defensive. The MD&A opens with a warning paragraph about "commodity cost volatility, supply chain pressures, tariffs and shifting global trade policies, evolving consumer purchasing and spending patterns and other economic uncertainties." The Middle East conflict disclosure was new and alarming. Gross profit discussion quantified tariff damage precisely (230bps, $0.06/share), which reads as management preparing investors for a difficult full year. The phrase "surgical pricing actions where necessary" (from the FY2025 10-K) became more tentative in the 10-Q context.

### Guidance track record

Campbell's does not provide formal quantitative EPS or revenue guidance in SEC filings, making tracking difficult. However, the cost savings program serves as a proxy:

- September 2024: Announced $250M annualized savings target by FY2028 end
- September 2025: Raised to $375M (a 50% increase, suggesting over-delivery vs initial plan)
- August 2025 (FY2025 year-end): $145M program-to-date savings realized
- February 2026 (Q2 FY2026): $180M program-to-date savings realized (added $35M in one quarter)

The prior Snyder's-Lance / multi-year cost program delivered $950M total vs no specific public target (but management described it as substantially complete in FY2024). This suggests management is credible on cost savings execution. Revenue and earnings trends, however, have consistently disappointed relative to the bullish acquisition thesis.

### Cross-filing consistency check

One notable inconsistency: the FY2025 10-K (September 2025) stated capital expenditures are "expected to total approximately $420 million in 2026." The Q2 FY2026 10-Q (March 2026) revised this to "approximately $370 million in 2026." The $50M reduction was not explicitly explained but likely reflects either project deferrals due to cash flow pressure or completed cost-savings-related spending ($187M of the $215M capital budget had been spent by Q2 FY2026). This is not a contradiction per se, but the quiet downward revision of capex guidance during a period of cash strain warrants attention -- it could indicate early cash conservation measures.

Another notable item: the CFO transition from Carrie L. Anderson (signed the FY2025 10-K in September 2025) to Todd E. Cunfer (signed the Q2 FY2026 10-Q in March 2026) was not prominently disclosed in the Q2 10-Q itself. An 8-K would have been filed, but the lack of discussion in the MD&A of a CFO change during a period of financial stress is unusual.

### Topics they stopped discussing

1. **Share buybacks as a strategic priority.** In FY2023, the $500M September 2021 program and the anti-dilutive program were discussed with forward-looking language about returning capital to shareholders. By FY2025 and Q2 FY2026, buybacks are barely mentioned -- $26M in H1 FY2026 vs $56M in H1 FY2025. The strategic buyback program ($301M remaining) appears shelved.

2. **Organic growth narrative.** The FY2023 10-K emphasized "organic net sales growth" as a strategic metric. By FY2025, organic growth is essentially absent from the discussion -- the entire revenue narrative is about Sovos Brands' contribution, the 53rd week benefit, and divestiture impacts. Underlying organic trends are negative.

3. **International expansion.** After divesting Arnott's, Kelsen, and international operations in FY2019--FY2020, Campbell's became an almost purely domestic company. There has been zero discussion of re-entering international markets. The Latin America reallocation from Snacks to Meals & Beverages (beginning FY2026) is an internal segment reshuffling, not expansion.

4. **Goldfish as a growth driver.** FY2024 highlighted Goldfish crackers as a bright spot in Snacks. By FY2025, Goldfish is cited as part of the decline: "declines in ... Goldfish crackers" appeared in the FY2025 Snacks discussion for the first time. By Q2 FY2026, Goldfish is not mentioned as a positive contributor in any segment discussion.
