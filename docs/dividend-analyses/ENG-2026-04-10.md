## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD at EUR 17.09. WAIT for EUR 13-14 to accumulate. DO NOT SELL below EUR 12. Dividend Safety: 5/10. Confidence: Medium.**

Enagas is Spain's sole natural gas transmission system operator -- a regulated monopoly operating ~12,000 km of high-pressure pipelines and three of Europe's most strategically important LNG regasification terminals (Barcelona, Cartagena, Huelva). At EUR 17.09, the stock trades near its 52-week high (EUR 17.37), having rallied 35% from the EUR 12.64 low over the past twelve months. The central question: is this a genuine turnaround story driven by the 2025 recovery in reported earnings and the hydrogen transition narrative, or a classic bear-market rally in a structurally declining business?

The answer is more nuanced than the original 2025 report suggested. Three facts dominate the updated analysis:

First, **FY2025 Earnings After Tax of EUR 339 million exceeded the company's own guidance** (guidance range EUR 280-290M [approx]) and represented a sharp recovery from the EUR 299M net loss in FY2024. Revenue grew 6.1% to EUR 960M, the first material top-line growth in five years. EBITDA of EUR 469M is below FY2021's EUR 636M peak but above the depressed FY2024 level. Critically, the FY2024 loss was driven by a EUR 697M non-operating charge primarily related to the Tallgrass Energy impairment -- this was a one-time, specific asset write-down, not a recurring operational deterioration. Stripping that impairment, underlying earnings remained in the EUR 280-320M range across FY2023-FY2025.

Second, **the dividend policy has now stabilized at EUR 1.00 per share** for the 2023-2026 period, down from the EUR 1.74 peak but explicitly protected by the Strategic Plan 2024-2030. Management has guided this as a floor, not a ceiling. At EUR 17.09, the 5.85% yield reflects both the income opportunity and the market's recognition that the previous EUR 1.74 payout was never sustainable under the revised regulatory framework. The payout ratio of 77% on FY2025 EPS is high but manageable for a regulated utility; the key vulnerability is the free cash flow gap (EUR 94M FCF vs EUR 263M dividend), which management is bridging through asset sales (notably the Tallgrass partial monetization [unverified timing]) and continued deleveraging.

Third, **the 2027-2032 regulatory period is the single most important variable** and will be decided by the CNMC (Comision Nacional de los Mercados y la Competencia) during 2026. The prior regulatory period (2021-2026) set the allowed rate of return on regulated asset base (RAB) at 5.4%; market expectations for the next period range from 5.0% to 5.7% depending on European sovereign bond dynamics. Each 25bps reduction in the allowed return translates to approximately EUR 15-20M of EBITDA impact [approx]. A return stable at 5.4% or higher would validate the current dividend; a cut below 5.0% would force a second dividend reduction.

**Should you sell an existing EUR 17 position?** No. The 5.85% yield is attractive, the balance sheet is healthier than five years ago (net debt down from EUR 5.26B in FY2020 to EUR 2.10B in FY2025 -- a 60% reduction), and the hydrogen transition represents genuine long-dated optionality. But adding new capital here is not compelling -- there is no margin of safety above EUR 15, and the next 18 months include multiple binary catalysts (CNMC decision, Tallgrass monetization path, H2Med Final Investment Decision).

**Top 3 reasons to OWN:**
1. **Regulated monopoly with visible cash flows**. Spain's gas transmission grid is a natural monopoly; the regulated framework provides 10-15 years of earnings visibility regardless of individual year volatility. The current RAB (approximately EUR 6-7B [approx]) generates ~EUR 340-380M of annual regulated EBITDA.
2. **LNG regasification is a European strategic asset**. After the 2022 Russia-Ukraine shock, Spain's six LNG terminals (Enagas operates three directly, plus stakes in others) became critical to European energy security. Utilization rates remain 3-4x higher than pre-crisis norms and European regasification tariffs have been revalued upward.
3. **Hydrogen optionality is structural, not cosmetic**. H2Med received renewal as a European "Project of Common Interest" in early 2026, unlocking potential Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) funding. If the project proceeds, Enagas becomes the operator of Europe's first cross-border hydrogen backbone -- a decade-plus value creation path currently priced at near-zero by the market.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to load up here:**
1. **Free cash flow does not cover the dividend**. FY2025 FCF of EUR 94M against a EUR 263M dividend outlay represents 35% FCF coverage. Management is funding the shortfall via balance sheet optimization (asset sales + deleveraging slowdown) -- this cannot persist indefinitely without another dividend adjustment.
2. **Regulatory period 2027-2032 is an unresolved binary**. The CNMC process is opaque, politically sensitive, and subject to European Commission involvement. A hawkish outcome (allowed return below 5%) would force renewed earnings and dividend pressure.
3. **International investments remain a value-destruction risk**. Tallgrass (~30% stake) has already been written down EUR 500M+ cumulatively. The remaining carry value is substantial but exposed to US midstream valuation cycles. GNL Quintero (Chile) and Trans Adriatic Pipeline (TAP) provide smaller but similar exposures. Management's international M&A track record is poor.

**12-month catalysts:** CNMC regulatory period 2027-2032 proposal (expected H2 2026). H2Med Final Investment Decision and EU CEF funding allocation. Potential Tallgrass Energy partial or full monetization. Interim dividend declaration continuity. Q1 2026 results April 29, 2026.

## PART III -- HISTORICAL REVIEW: FROM DIVIDEND DARLING TO TRANSITION STORY

Enagas's story divides into three distinct phases that explain both the dividend history and the current valuation:

**Phase 1 -- Privatization to Regulatory Golden Age (2002-2014):** After the 2002 IPO, Enagas built Spain's modern gas transmission network during a period of rapidly growing gas demand. Spain's total gas consumption peaked at approximately 440 TWh in 2008 before declining. Enagas benefited from a regulatory framework that allowed ~7-8% returns on RAB, aggressive capex to build the grid, and a dividend policy that grew from EUR 0.30/share (2002) to EUR 1.40+/share (2014). The balance sheet was modestly leveraged (net debt/EBITDA ~3x), and the stock routinely traded at 15-18x earnings.

**Phase 2 -- Regulatory Reset and International Diversification (2014-2021):** Starting in 2014, the Spanish government began compressing allowed utility returns in response to political pressure on energy prices. The 2014 regulatory reform reduced allowed returns from ~7.5% to ~5.9%. Enagas responded with two strategies: (a) international diversification to access higher-return markets, and (b) increased leverage to maintain EPS and dividend growth. Between 2014-2020, Enagas invested approximately EUR 3-4B in international assets: Tallgrass (US midstream), TAP (Caucasus-Europe pipeline), GNL Quintero (Chile), Transportadora de Gas del Peru. The dividend grew from EUR 1.40/share (2014) to EUR 1.68/share (2020). Net debt peaked at EUR 5.26B (FY2021).

**Phase 3 -- Transition Crisis and Recovery (2022-2025):** Three shocks hit simultaneously. First, the Russia-Ukraine invasion scrambled European gas markets, creating unprecedented LNG demand and elevated volatility. Second, the accelerating EU decarbonization agenda raised structural questions about long-term gas infrastructure value. Third, the 2021-2026 regulatory period (approved in 2019) further compressed allowed returns to ~5.4%. Enagas was forced to cut the dividend from EUR 1.74 (FY2022) to EUR 1.00 (FY2023), took the Tallgrass impairment in FY2024 (EUR 697M charge), and is now executing a simultaneous deleveraging program and hydrogen pivot under the Strategic Plan 2024-2030.

## PART VII -- THE REGULATORY FRAMEWORK: 2021-2026 AND THE CRUCIAL 2027-2032 PERIOD

Enagas's core earnings are determined by the CNMC (Comision Nacional de los Mercados y la Competencia), Spain's utility regulator. Understanding the regulatory framework is essential to any Enagas investment thesis.

**Table 9 -- Spanish Gas TSO Regulatory Periods**

| Period | Allowed Return on RAB | RAB Growth Allowance | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2002-2008 | ~7.5% [unverified] | High | Golden age, aggressive grid buildout |
| 2008-2014 | ~7.5% [unverified] | Moderate | Continued build |
| 2014-2020 | 5.9% | Low | First major compression |
| 2021-2026 | 5.4% | Low-Neg | Current period, incorporates decarb |
| 2027-2032 | 5.0%-5.7% est | ? | TBD by CNMC -- crucial |

Source: CNMC published frameworks and regulatory press reports. Specific methodologies for the 2027-2032 period remain under consultation.

**The 2027-2032 Process:** The CNMC is expected to publish draft methodology for the next regulatory period in mid-2026, with final approval by the Spanish Council of Ministers in late 2026 or early 2027. Key variables being debated:

1. **Allowed real return on RAB:** The previous period used a methodology based on Spanish 10-year sovereign bond yields + equity risk premium. With current 10-year yields at ~3.5% and equity risk premiums of ~150-200bps, a mechanical application would produce ~5.0-5.5% real return. Utility investors are lobbying for ~5.7% to account for hydrogen transition risk; consumer advocates argue for ~4.5-4.8%.

2. **Asset base treatment during hydrogen transition:** One of the most complex issues is how to treat gas infrastructure that is repurposed for hydrogen. The regulator must decide whether: (a) the existing RAB continues to earn the allowed return during conversion, (b) conversion capex earns additional return, (c) stranded assets receive a separate recovery mechanism, or (d) some combination.

3. **Tariff structure during demand decline:** Spanish gas demand is forecast to decline 2-4% per year through 2035 as buildings and industry electrify. The tariff structure must allow Enagas to recover costs on a shrinking volume base. Options: capacity-based tariffs (favorable to Enagas), or commodity-based (penalizing).

**Base Case CNMC Outcome (55% probability):** Allowed return of 5.2-5.4% (modest compression from current 5.4%), continued RAB treatment during hydrogen conversion, hybrid tariff structure. EBITDA impact: roughly flat to -EUR 20M annually from current baseline.

**Hawkish Outcome (25% probability):** Allowed return of 4.8-5.0%, stringent tariff design, no explicit hydrogen RAB protection. EBITDA impact: -EUR 40-70M annually, triggering dividend cut risk.

**Dovish Outcome (20% probability):** Allowed return of 5.5-5.7%, explicit hydrogen capex uplift, favorable tariff design. EBITDA impact: +EUR 30-50M annually, supporting dividend stability and potential growth.

The expected value is approximately neutral to slightly negative, which aligns with the current market pricing at EUR 17 -- the market is not pricing in significant regulatory upside or downside.

## PART VIII -- MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Moat Assessment: Narrow but durable.**

Enagas's moat rests on two pillars: (1) natural monopoly status as Spain's gas TSO -- no economic scenario exists where Spain builds a second transmission grid, and (2) regulated return framework provides baseline earnings visibility independent of commodity prices or demand swings.

The moat has real limitations. The "monopoly" has no pricing power above the regulated rate, so if the regulated return is set too low, there is no recourse. The assets are depreciating faster than demand growth can replace them, meaning the RAB itself is in secular decline. And the hydrogen transition introduces execution risk that dilutes the clean monopoly attributes.

**Capital allocation track record -- honest grade: C-.**

The Enagas management team excels at operating regulated Spanish gas infrastructure and has a poor record in international M&A. Specifically:

- **Tallgrass Energy (~30% stake acquired 2019-2020):** Cumulative write-down of EUR 500M+. Grade: F. The acquisition was poorly timed (late-cycle US midstream), strategically questionable (limited synergy with Spanish regulated operations), and the decision to use equity-method accounting deferred loss recognition until FY2024.
- **Trans Adriatic Pipeline (16% stake, acquired 2013-2018):** Broadly in line with original underwriting. Grade: B. The project completed on schedule in 2020, generates stable associate dividends, and has strategic value as a southern gas corridor.
- **GNL Quintero (Chile) and TgP (Peru):** Mixed. Currency and political risks have compressed returns below original underwriting. Grade: C.
- **Deleveraging program (2021-2025):** EUR 2.4B debt reduction executed on schedule. Grade: A.
- **Dividend policy reset (2023):** Painful but proactive, preserved balance sheet. Grade: B.
- **Hydrogen pivot (ongoing):** Too early to grade. Conceptually sound given pipeline-compatible infrastructure; execution remains unproven. Grade: B- provisional.

## PART IX -- HYDROGEN: REAL OPTION OR REGULATORY CAPTURE?

The hydrogen transition is the single variable with the largest potential impact on Enagas's multi-decade value, and also the most uncertain.

**The Bull Case for Hydrogen:**

1. **Physical infrastructure advantage:** Enagas owns 12,000 km of high-pressure pipelines. Converting these to hydrogen service, while capital-intensive, is economically superior to building green-field hydrogen infrastructure. Independent estimates suggest pipeline conversion costs of EUR 0.5-1.5M per km vs EUR 3-5M per km for new pipelines [unverified industry estimates].

2. **H2Med as European anchor:** The 455 km Barcelona-to-Marseille undersea hydrogen pipeline (connecting the Spanish hydrogen backbone to France and onward to Germany) received renewed designation as a European "Project of Common Interest" in early 2026. This unlocks Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) funding of up to 50% of eligible costs, potentially EUR 1.0-1.5B in EU grants [approx]. Enagas is the lead developer alongside Teréga (France) and GRTgaz.

3. **First-mover in Iberian hydrogen hub ambitions:** Spain has uniquely favorable solar and wind resources (capacity factors 25-40% higher than Northern Europe), positioning it as a potential green hydrogen exporter to EU industrial centers. The Spanish Hydrogen Backbone Network, led by Enagas, would handle production from Andalusia, Extremadura, Aragon, and Castilla y Leon solar/wind parks.

4. **Regulatory conversion path:** The EU Hydrogen and Decarbonized Gas Package (adopted 2024) creates explicit pathways for gas TSOs to recover hydrogen conversion costs through regulated returns. This treats hydrogen infrastructure analogously to gas infrastructure for regulatory purposes.

**The Bear Case for Hydrogen:**

1. **Green hydrogen economics remain uncompetitive.** At current electricity prices and electrolyzer costs, green hydrogen production costs EUR 4-8/kg. Natural gas-derived grey hydrogen costs EUR 1-2/kg; industrial customers (refineries, ammonia, steel) will not pay the green premium without subsidies. The EU is proposing mandatory green hydrogen quotas for some industries, but political willingness to absorb the cost has been shaky.

2. **Pipeline conversion technical challenges.** Hydrogen causes embrittlement in carbon steel at scale, requires different compression (rotating equipment 30-40% more expensive), demands tighter safety systems, and has 1/3 the volumetric energy density of methane. The "just repurpose the pipes" narrative dramatically understates the engineering complexity. Real-world experience with hydrogen pipelines at scale (vs small industrial segments) is limited.

3. **Political and macroeconomic fragility.** The European Green Deal was conceived during a period of low interest rates, fiscal expansion, and geopolitical stability. The 2022-2025 period has featured higher rates, inflation crises, energy security shocks, and rising competitiveness concerns vs the US (Inflation Reduction Act) and China. Political support for expensive hydrogen transition policies has wavered in Germany (coalition collapse), France (far-right gains), Italy (Meloni government), and Netherlands.

4. **Timeline uncertainty.** Even in the most optimistic scenario, H2Med operational by 2030 means meaningful hydrogen revenue to Enagas starts 2032-2035. The present value of cash flows beginning in 2032 is small at any reasonable discount rate. Hydrogen is "real option" value, not "base case" earnings value.

**My assessment:** Hydrogen is a real, but heavily discounted, option. Conservative valuation: EUR 1-3 per share of option value on top of the regulated base. Aggressive valuation: EUR 5-8 per share of option value. The current market pricing (EUR 17) appears to embed approximately EUR 2-3 of option value, meaning the hydrogen upside is partially but not fully priced.

## APPENDIX C -- DEEP DIVE ON SPANISH GAS DEMAND AND ENAGAS OPERATIONAL METRICS

**Spanish Gas Demand Trajectory (historical and forecast):**

Spain's total natural gas demand has gone through three distinct phases:

**Phase 1 (2000-2008):** Rapid growth from 150 TWh (2000) to peak 440 TWh (2008), driven by: (a) CCGT power generation displacing fuel oil, (b) industrial customers switching from coal, (c) residential gas network expansion. Enagas grew its pipeline network from 7,000 km to 11,000 km during this period.

**Phase 2 (2008-2021):** Structural decline from 440 TWh (2008) to approximately 300 TWh (2021), with significant volatility. Key drivers: (a) Spain's economic crisis 2008-2013 reduced industrial demand, (b) renewable power generation (wind + solar) displaced CCGT, (c) coal-to-gas switching partially offset renewable displacement, (d) residential demand flat.

**Phase 3 (2022-present):** Ukraine crisis temporary spike, then decline. 2022 demand reached 330 TWh (+10% spike driven by industrial resilience against Russian gas disruption), 2023 fell to 310 TWh, 2024 around 305 TWh. FY2025 preliminary data suggests demand between 315-325 TWh [approx].

**Forecast (2025-2035):** The Spanish National Energy and Climate Plan (PNIEC 2021-2030) targets gas demand of 240-260 TWh by 2030 (implicit -3% annual decline). Industry analysts project an acceleration to -3-4% annually from 2028 onward as building electrification scales. Key variables: (a) pace of heat pump installations in residential, (b) industrial decarbonization (steel, cement, chemicals), (c) renewable hydrogen substituting gas, (d) CCGT displacement by renewables + storage.
