## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: AVOID for dividend portfolios. SPECULATIVE HOLD for value/turnaround investors only. Dividend Safety: 4/10. Confidence: Medium.**

Wendy's is a broken dividend story trading at a broken price. At $6.93, the stock has declined approximately 65% from its 2023 highs near $20 and sits at levels not seen since 2020. The annualized $0.56 dividend ($0.14/quarter) yields approximately 8.1% -- a distress signal, not an income opportunity. The dividend has already been cut once in 2025, from $0.25/quarter to $0.14/quarter, a 44% reduction that eliminated the stock from serious consideration by dividend growth investors. The question is no longer "will Wendy's grow the dividend" but "will the reduced dividend survive another year of traffic declines."

The central tension is between the franchise model's cash-flow resilience and the operating deterioration that is pressuring that cash flow. In FY2025, approximately 94% of the 7,397-restaurant system was franchised, generating $838.5M in franchise-related revenue (royalties of $504.5M, fees of $98.2M, and rental income of $235.8M). This fee-based revenue provides a structural floor under cash generation -- even in FY2025's weak environment, operating cash flow was $344.5M and free cash flow (after $101.9M capex) was $242.6M. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 2328-2334.) Against an annualized dividend of approximately $106.6M at the $0.14/quarter rate (per management's own estimate in the 10-K, line 1515), the FCF payout ratio is approximately 44%. That looks comfortable in isolation.

But the math ignores two critical facts. First, the Company repurchased $200.8M of shares in FY2025 -- nearly double the dividend cost -- while simultaneously cutting the dividend. This capital allocation choice is incoherent from an income investor's perspective. Second, the operating trajectory is deteriorating: US same-restaurant sales fell 5.6% in FY2025, Company-operated restaurant margin compressed 180 basis points to 13.6%, and income before taxes declined 16.6% to $227.2M. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 711-713.) The $4.15B of total debt against $117.4M of equity (35:1 debt-to-equity) with interest coverage of 2.7x provides no margin for error if Project Fresh fails to stabilize traffic.

The company was founded in 1969 by Dave Thomas in Columbus, Ohio and is the second-largest US QSR burger chain by traffic and dollar share and the third-largest globally by unit count. WEN trades on the Nasdaq Global Select Market. Shares outstanding: 190.3M as of February 16, 2026.

The same-restaurant sales trajectory tells the critical story: after the COVID-era boom of 2021-2022, growth decelerated sharply (+3.7% in FY2023, +1.4% in FY2024) before collapsing to -5.6% in FY2025. On a two-year stacked basis, global comps were -3.2% -- meaning the 2024 growth was fully reversed and then some. US franchised AUVs declined from $2,086K to $1,984K, putting direct pressure on franchisee unit-level economics and, by extension, the royalty stream that supports Wendy's entire capital structure. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 1024-1029.)

Company-operated restaurant margin contracted to 13.6% in FY2025 -- the lowest in the seven-year period. The margin squeeze came from all directions simultaneously: food and paper costs rose 80bps to 31.8% of sales, restaurant labor increased 20bps to 32.4%, and occupancy/advertising/other operating costs surged 80bps to 22.2%. The combined 180bps cost of sales increase as a percentage of revenue is particularly concerning because it occurred during a period of traffic decline -- costs were rising while the revenue base was shrinking. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 929-957, 1172.)

**Inflection Points:**

1. **2019 -- The Pre-Pandemic Baseline.** Revenue $1.71B, net income ~$171M, DPS $0.48, ~7,095 restaurants. The franchise model was mature, the refranchising wave complete, and same-restaurant sales growing at +2.9%.

2. **2020 -- COVID Resilience.** Wendy's proved the QSR drive-thru model's durability. Revenue grew slightly to $1.73B as drive-thru sales offset dine-in closures. Breakfast launched in March 2020 -- terrible timing but ultimately incremental. Net income fell to ~$108M due to restructuring charges and impairments ($17M reorganization + $7M impairment per the FY2020 10-K).

3. **2021 -- The Post-COVID Boom.** US same-restaurant sales surged 9.2% (including a +11.9% Company-operated gain) as consumer spending rebounded. Revenue jumped to $1.90B. Operating income spiked to $367M (19.3% margin) -- the highest in the period. Net income reached $200M. Share count declined to 224M as buybacks accelerated.

4. **2022 -- The Dividend Hike and Confidence Peak.** In Q4 2022, management more than doubled the quarterly dividend from $0.12 to $0.25. Total revenue hit $2.10B on advertising fund reclassification and same-restaurant sales growth (+10.0% global). But operating margin contracted to 16.9% as commodity and labor inflation eroded gains. Share repurchases continued aggressively.

5. **2023-2024 -- Deceleration.** Same-restaurant sales growth decelerated: +4.3% global in 2023, +1.5% in 2024. Revenue grew to $2.18B and $2.25B. Net income was essentially flat at $204M and $194M. The company maintained the $1.00/year dividend while continuing buybacks, funding the combined distribution partly through debt.

6. **2025 -- The Dividend Cut and Traffic Cliff.** US same-restaurant sales plunged -5.6%. Revenue fell to $2.18B, net income to $165M, operating margin to 15.8% (the 10-K reports operating profit of $343.5M on total revenue of $2,176.9M). The dividend was cut 44% in Q2 (from $0.25 to $0.14/quarter). Kirk Tanner launched Project Fresh. Meanwhile, $200M of buybacks continued -- a puzzling capital allocation choice that prioritized share count reduction over income investors.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

The reduced $0.56 annual dividend appears mathematically sustainable at current operating levels. Management themselves stated in the FY2025 10-K (line 1515) that "If the Company pays regular quarterly cash dividends for the remainder of 2026 at the same rate as declared in the first quarter of 2026, the Company's total cash requirement for dividends for all of 2026 would be approximately $106.6 million based on the number of shares of its common stock outstanding at February 16, 2026." Against FY2025 OCF of $344.5M and FCF of $242.6M, this leaves ample room.

However, four considerations complicate the picture:

First, the FY2025 cash flow statement (line 2373) shows actual dividends paid of $129.6M in FY2025 (reflecting the blended $0.25/$0.14 rate). In FY2024, dividends were $204.4M; in FY2023, $209.3M. The cut reduced the cash outflow by approximately $80-100M annually -- meaningful relief but not transformative.

Second, the Company simultaneously spent $200.8M on share buybacks in FY2025 (line 2370), nearly double the dividend cost. Total shareholder distributions (dividends + buybacks) of $330.4M exceeded FCF of $242.6M by $87.8M. This is not sustainable and was funded partly by drawing down the cash balance (from $503.6M to $357.7M end-of-year). (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 2397-2408.)

Third, the securitized debt structure creates hard constraints. The $2.76B of securitized notes are backed by franchise cash flows, and covenant violations could trigger rapid amortization, forcing cash flow away from dividends. The recent Series 2025-1 issuance ($450M at 5.422%, anticipated repayment 2032) shows the Company can still access the securitization market, but at higher rates than the older Series 2021-1 (2.370-2.775%). (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 3213-3248.)

Fourth, the buyback authorization has only $35M remaining (expiring February 2027), which means FY2026 buyback activity should decline dramatically. This is actually positive for dividend sustainability -- it frees up ~$165M of annual cash flow that was being consumed by buybacks. If buybacks decline, the dividend at $0.14/quarter appears well-covered even in a stress scenario.

**What would it take for another cut?** If US same-restaurant sales fall another 5%+ in FY2026, net income could decline to $120-140M and FCF to $200-220M. At that level, the $107M dividend remains covered, but interest coverage would thin toward 2.3x and the Board would face pressure to conserve cash. A second cut becomes plausible if FY2026 US comps are -5% or worse AND securitization covenants tighten.

## PART V -- BUSINESS QUALITY AND MOAT

Wendy's competitive moat is narrow and under pressure.

- **Moat type:** Brand (moderate) + Scale (limited vs. MCD)
- **Moat trajectory:** Narrowing
- **Pricing power evidence:** Limited. The FY2025 10-K notes "Company-operated same-restaurant sales decreased due to a decrease in traffic, partially offset by higher average check" (line 1091) -- meaning Wendy's has some pricing power (average check increased) but not enough to offset traffic losses. In a QSR value war, the #2 player cannot match the #1 player's promotional depth.

**Market position:** Wendy's self-identifies as #2 US QSR burger chain by traffic/dollar share and #3 globally by unit count. The competitive gap is structural:

| Chain | US Units [approx] | US AUV ($K) | Global Scale |
|---|---|---|---|
| McDonald's | ~13,400 | ~3,700 [unverified] | ~40,000 |
| Burger King | ~7,000 [unverified] | ~1,500 [unverified] | ~18,000 [unverified] |
| Wendy's | 5,969 | 2,001 | 7,397 |

Source: WEN US AUV from FY2025 10-K, line 1029. Competitor figures are industry estimates.

**Franchise Economics and the Royalty Dependency**

The franchise model's strength is also its vulnerability. Wendy's earns a 4.0% royalty on franchisee sales (per the Current Franchise Agreement, line 139), plus a standard $50,000 technical assistance fee per new restaurant. In FY2025, franchise royalty revenue of $504.5M implies a royalty base of approximately $12.6B in franchisee sales -- closely matching the reported US franchised systemwide sales of $11.0B plus a portion of international sales. When franchisee AUVs decline (from $2,086K to $1,984K in the US), the royalty stream declines proportionally without any offset from cost management -- royalties are pure revenue to Wendy's.

The franchise support and other costs line of $81.0M (up $13.3M from FY2024) is a warning sign. The 10-K attributes the increase to "an increase in the provision for doubtful accounts and an increase in costs incurred to provide information technology services and other services to franchisees." (WEN FY2025 10-K, line 1185.) Doubtful accounts rising means franchisees are falling behind on royalty and rent payments -- a leading indicator of system stress. If Wendy's is forced to provide financial relief to struggling franchisees (reduced royalties, rent deferrals, capex support), the franchise model's cash flow advantage narrows.

The advertising contribution structure also creates an asymmetric risk. US restaurants contribute 3.5% of sales to the national advertising fund plus 0.5% for local advertising. (WEN FY2025 10-K, line 154.) When systemwide sales decline, advertising dollars decline proportionally -- exactly when the brand most needs marketing support to stabilize traffic. The FY2025 advertising funds revenue fell $36.0M to $422.1M, directly reducing marketing firepower during a downturn. The company's decision to reduce incremental breakfast advertising (line 1211) may have been a consequence of this lower advertising budget rather than a strategic choice.

Wendy's US systemwide AUV of $2,001K positions it between McDonald's premium volumes and Burger King's lower volumes. The "fresh, never frozen beef" positioning generates a quality premium over BK but cannot compete with McDonald's scale economics. The FY2025 decline from $2,098K to $2,001K (a -4.6% decline) indicates the brand premium is eroding. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 1028-1029.)

**Digital transformation:** Digital sales reached 20.8% of global systemwide sales in FY2025 (from 17.6% in FY2024). Cloud computing amortization of $18.6M in FY2025 (line 1291) represents meaningful investment. Digital is a genuine competitive asset, but all QSR peers are investing comparably.

**Breakfast:** Launched March 2020. The FY2025 10-K notes decreased incremental breakfast advertising funding (line 1211), suggesting diminishing returns on the breakfast investment. Breakfast has not achieved transformative growth.

**International:** The genuine bright spot. International systemwide sales grew 8.1% constant currency. International AUVs of $1,483K (line 1032) are below US levels but growing. International franchise royalty revenue reached $75.5M in FY2025, up from $71.7M in FY2024 and $67.5M in FY2023. The model is asset-light and growing. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 1466-1503.)

**ROIC trend:** Per the FMP data, ROIC is approximately 10.5% -- reasonable for a franchise model but declining. Asset turnover of 0.44x reflects the heavy intangibles/goodwill base ($774M goodwill + $1,171M other intangibles + $937M properties = substantial asset base). (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 1892-1905.)

## PART X -- RISKS AND STRESS TESTS

**Risk 1: Continued US traffic decline.**

- **Description:** US same-restaurant sales fell -5.6% in FY2025, primarily traffic-driven (higher average check partially offset). On a two-year stacked basis, global comps are -3.2%. The decline is the worst in Wendy's recent history.
- **Probability:** High (60%+) that US comps remain negative in FY2026 given macro headwinds and QSR value war intensity.
- **Severity:** High. Each 1% decline in US same-restaurant sales reduces franchise royalty revenue by approximately $5M and Company-operated sales by approximately $9M.
- **Mitigant:** Project Fresh's brand revitalization and operational excellence pillars, plus planned closure of 5-6% of underperforming US restaurants.

**Risk 2: Securitization covenant violation.**

- **Description:** The whole-business securitization pledges franchise cash flows. If performance metrics fall below covenant thresholds, the structure could enter rapid amortization, redirecting cash from dividends to debt repayment.
- **Probability:** Low-to-moderate (15-25%) within 3 years. The recent Series 2025-1 issuance suggests current compliance.
- **Severity:** Extreme. Rapid amortization would likely force dividend suspension.
- **Mitigant:** The $300M undrawn revolver provides buffer; management is focused on deleveraging as a Project Fresh pillar.

**Risk 3: Value war intensification.**

- **Description:** McDonald's aggressive value positioning (the $5 Meal Deal and successors) directly pressures Wendy's traffic. McDonald's can absorb value pricing through scale; Wendy's franchisees have less margin buffer.
- **Probability:** High (70%+) -- value wars are the new normal in QSR.
- **Severity:** Moderate-to-high. Sustained value war erodes Wendy's quality positioning and forces margin-destroying promotional activity.
- **Mitigant:** Wendy's "fresh, never frozen" differentiation provides some insulation, and the 2025 breakfast advertising reduction suggests a shift away from share-buying toward profitability.

**Risk 4: Franchisee financial health deterioration.**

- **Description:** The provision for doubtful accounts increased in FY2025 (line 1185). Franchisee AUVs declined from $2,086K to $1,984K (-4.9%). Impairment of long-lived assets rose to $12.1M from $9.7M, reflecting "deterioration in operating performance of certain Company-operated restaurants." (WEN FY2025 10-K, line 1345.)
- **Probability:** Moderate (30-40%) that franchisee stress leads to elevated closures.
- **Severity:** Moderate. Each closure reduces royalty/rent/ad fund revenue and may require lease buyouts.
- **Mitigant:** The planned 5-6% US closure initiative under Project Fresh is designed to proactively address underperformers.

**Risk 5: CEO execution failure.**

- **Description:** Tanner has been CEO for ~2 years with declining results. If Project Fresh does not stabilize US comps by H2 2026, another CEO change is plausible given Trian's 16% ownership and activist history.
- **Probability:** Moderate (25-35%) if FY2026 comps remain negative.
- **Severity:** High. CEO turnover during a restructuring creates strategic uncertainty.

**Risk 6: Tariff and commodity inflation.**

- **Description:** The FY2025 10-K cites commodity costs, labor inflation, and trade policy risks. Food and paper rose to 31.8% of sales from 31.0% in FY2024. Restaurant labor reached 32.4%. (WEN FY2025 10-K, lines 930-957.) New tariffs could increase import costs on certain ingredients.
- **Probability:** Moderate. Difficult to quantify without knowing exact tariff scope.
- **Severity:** Moderate. Each 100bps increase in cost of sales reduces Company-operated margins proportionally.

## PART XI -- THESIS AND VERDICT

Wendy's is not a dividend investment at current prices. The 44% dividend cut in 2025, combined with a -5.6% US same-restaurant sales decline, 35:1 debt-to-equity leverage, and interest coverage trending toward 2.5x, eliminates WEN from consideration by dividend growth investors.
