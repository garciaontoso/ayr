## 10-LINE TL;DR

1. CUBE is the #3 self-storage REIT by store count behind Public Storage (PSA) and Extra Space Storage (EXR), with 662 owned + 862 managed = 1,524 total stores (2025 10-K, line 125).
2. The dividend streak is 9+ years of consecutive annual raises ($1.11 → $2.09 from 2017 to 2025), compounding at 8.2%/yr.
3. Same-store NOI growth has printed negative for two consecutive years: **-2.5% in 2024 and -1.1% in 2025** (2024 10-K line 3488, 2025 10-K line 3640).
4. Occupancy has eroded from a COVID peak of 93.3% (2021) to **88.6% SS / 88.1% total** at 2025 year-end — a 470 bp decline (2021 10-K line 3871, 2025 10-K line 3654).
5. FFO-adjusted/diluted share has now declined two years running: $2.68 (2023) → $2.63 (2024) → $2.58 (2025). That is the first multi-year decline since the 2008-2009 financial crisis.
6. Dividend coverage has tightened to 123% of GAAP EPS but 81% of FFO-adjusted — safe but no longer abundant ($2.09 / $2.58).
7. The 2025 HVP IV acquisition ($452.8M) added 28 stores and levered the balance sheet up: debt/enterprise value moved from 23.3% to 29.3% in one year (2025 10-K line 240).
8. Q3 2025 showed SS NOI -1.5% sequentially (2025 Q3 10-Q line 2318), suggesting the trough is **late 2025 or early 2026**, not already in.
9. NYC exposure (~17% of revenue) is both the franchise's differentiator — high barriers to entry, high rent per sq ft — and its biggest single geographic risk. Urban markets have bled occupancy hardest in the current cycle.
10. **Verdict: HOLD with an opportunistic ACCUMULATE bid at $33-$35 (~6% starting yield).** The business is sound, the dividend is safe, but the operational trough is not yet behind the company and the current multiple already prices a 2026 recovery that may slip.

## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD. Dividend Safety: 7/10. Confidence: High.**

CubeSmart is a textbook case of a well-run dividend growth REIT that got caught by the industry cycle and is now asking investors to pay yesterday's multiple for an uncertain 12-month trough. The company itself is not broken: KPMG continues to sign a clean audit, the balance sheet is investment grade, the debt ladder is termed out to 2035, the CEO is a 15-year company veteran, and the portfolio is better-located than the peer average. But self-storage is a cyclical industry disguised as an infrastructure business, and the post-COVID indigestion phase is still playing out on CUBE's P&L in ways that even management's late-2025 commentary underweights.

The quantitative case for concern is simple. From 2021 (COVID peak) to 2025:
- Same-store rental income growth: +13.3% → -0.9% → -0.7%
- Same-store NOI growth: +17.2% → -2.5% → -1.1%
- Period-end SS occupancy: 93.3% → 89.3% → 88.6%
- FFO-adjusted per share: $2.12 → $2.68 → $2.63 → $2.58
- Dividend per share: $1.45 → $1.98 → $2.05 → $2.09 (still rising)

The dividend growth rate has visibly decelerated from 22.8% (2021→2022, post-COVID catch-up) to 3.5% (2023→2024) to 2.0% (2024→2025). This is management telling you — without saying so in plain English — that the payout ratio has climbed above their comfort zone and they need FFO growth to resume before the distribution can accelerate again. The 2026 dividend raise, if any, will be symbolic (perhaps $2.10-$2.12).

The qualitative case is more nuanced. Self-storage as a sector benefited from one of the cleanest secular tailwinds in US real estate (household formation, de-cluttering services, suburban migration, "renter nation" dynamics) amplified by a historic COVID demand spike. That spike was transitory — people moved, downsized, and stored things — but it was also interpreted by both operators and lenders as structural. Too many stores were built 2021-2023. New supply is now finally decelerating, but **the existing vintage is still absorbing**, which is why CUBE's SS NOI is still shrinking even as the company grows via acquisition (HVP IV, the Hines portfolio).

CUBE's specific differentiation is its NYC and urban-density concentration (New York = 17% of 2025 revenue; top 4 metros NY+FL+TX+CA = 52%). This is simultaneously:
- **A moat:** zoning makes new urban self-storage essentially impossible to build at scale; NYC storage rents are 2-3x national averages; barriers to entry are Manhattan-Brooklyn-Queens level.
- **A risk:** urban post-COVID population flows (out-migration, then modest return) have produced occupancy headwinds that suburban-centric PSA has weathered slightly better.

At $37.25 you get a 5.6% yield, a BBB/Baa2 balance sheet, and a dividend track record that should outlast the cycle. You also get a 123% GAAP payout ratio, a 14x EV/EBITDA multiple on a business whose same-store NOI is still shrinking, and the real possibility that FFO/sh is flat again in 2026 before resuming growth. This is not a "buy and forget" opportunity today. It is a watchlist name that becomes compelling in the mid-$30s.

**Top 3 reasons to own at $37.25:** (1) 9-year dividend growth streak with visible path to extension; (2) NYC+urban franchise that cannot be replicated; (3) investment-grade balance sheet with 3.29% weighted-average debt cost locked through 2032.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add today:** (1) same-store NOI still negative with the trough not clearly behind us; (2) 123% GAAP payout ratio means FFO growth must resume to support any meaningful dividend acceleration; (3) cheaper alternatives exist in the REIT complex (O at 5.9%, VICI at 6.5%) with arguably lower operating risk.

**12-month catalysts:** (i) SS NOI inflection (we model Q2-Q3 2026 positive); (ii) any Fed easing that compresses REIT cap rates; (iii) HVP IV integration showing up in 2026 non-same-store NOI; (iv) industry supply starts data confirming the 2027 tightening thesis.

### Geographic concentration (2025 revenue)

CUBE is a deeply urban/metropolitan REIT. The top 4 states = 52% of 2025 revenue (NY 17%, FL 14%, TX 11%, CA 10% per 10-K line 235). The NY concentration is overwhelmingly NYC metro (Manhattan, Queens, Brooklyn, Long Island) rather than upstate, making the "NY" number functionally a Greater NY metropolitan exposure. This is the highest urban density concentration of any of the Big 3 storage REITs.

### Business strategy (2025 10-K line 221-226)

Four pillars as stated in the 10-K:
1. **Maximize same-store cash flow** via dynamic revenue management (rate vs. occupancy balance).
2. **Selective acquisitions in high-barrier markets** — 2025 delivered the HVP IV closeout ($452.8M for 28 stores) plus two one-off AZ/FL acquisitions.
3. **Dispose of underperformers** — limited activity recently (only 1 store sold in 2023 via involuntary conversion).
4. **Grow third-party management** — the 862-store managed book generates $40M+ of fee income with zero real estate capital at risk, and serves as an acquisition funnel.

The 2025 acquisition of HVP IV is strategically meaningful: CUBE took out its partner on an unconsolidated venture it already knew, eliminated the equity pickup line item, and simplified the reporting structure. It is also the biggest capital deployment since the 2021 LAACO/Storage West acquisition, and it is the reason debt/EV jumped 600 bps in one year.

## PART III · 10-YEAR ARC (2016-2025)

The CubeSmart story over the last decade has three distinct phases.

**Phase 1: Pre-COVID grind (2016-2019).** A well-run but unremarkable mid-cycle REIT. Revenue compounded from $510M (2016) to $655M (2019), a 8.6% CAGR driven equally by same-store growth and modest acquisition. Same-store NOI growth averaged ~3-4%/year, occupancy held in the 89-91% range, FFO-adjusted/share grew from roughly $1.45 to $1.69. Not exciting, but consistent: the kind of REIT dividend growth investors accumulate and hold. Dividend grew from $1.00 (implied 2015) through $1.11 (2017) → $1.22 (2018) → $1.29 (2019).

**Phase 2: COVID boom and catch-up (2020-2022).** This is where the story gets messy in interpretation. Revenue exploded from $679M (2020) to $1,010M (2022) — a 49% jump in two years. The 2021 10-K shows the mechanism: SS rental income +13.3% in 2021, SS NOI +17.2%, with SS occupancy peaking at 93.3% (2021 10-K line 3871). This was a once-in-a-generation demand spike: Americans moved (suburban migration), divorced (household dissolution), downsized (WFH furniture), and everyone suddenly had stuff with nowhere to put it. CUBE, alongside every storage REIT, raised rents aggressively — realized annual rent per sq ft went from $16.70 (2018) to $22.70 (2023), a 36% increase in five years (multiple 10-K disclosures).

The LAACO/Storage West acquisition in December 2021 added 59 stores (~$1.7B transaction) and was financed with a combination of equity issuance and new unsecured notes (the 2031s and 2032s). This is why diluted shares jumped from 191.6M (2019) to 205.0M (2021) to 225.9M (2022) — a 34M share (+17.9%) increase that permanently lowered future per-share growth cadence.

Dividend growth in this phase was substantial: $1.33 (2020) → $1.45 (2021) → **$1.78 (2022)** — a 22.8% raise in 2022 alone, the largest in the company's history.

**Phase 3: Post-COVID normalization (2023-2025).** The hangover. Total revenue still grew (thanks to 2022/2023/2024/2025 acquisitions), but same-store metrics turned decisively negative:

## PART V · DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE

### Safety analysis

**Payout ratios (2025):**
- GAAP EPS: $2.09 / $1.46 = **143%** (not meaningful for REITs)
- FFO: $2.09 / $2.56 (FFO/sh) = **81.6%**
- FFO-adjusted: $2.09 / $2.58 = **81.0%**
- Operating cash flow: $608.5M / $480M (implied divs paid) = **79%**

For a self-storage REIT, a **75-85% FFO payout** is normal. CUBE sits squarely in that band. Peers PSA and EXR typically run 70-78% payout. CUBE is on the slightly higher end within the Big 3 self-storage cohort, which is consistent with it being the smaller, higher-yield name of the trio.

**Coverage stress test.** If 2026 SS NOI declines another 2% and non-same-store contributes flat (conservative), FFO/sh could slip to approximately $2.50-$2.53 in 2026. Against a 2026 dividend of ~$2.10 (assume $0.01/qtr raise), payout moves to 83-84%. Still covered, but the "margin" is shrinking.

If 2026 SS NOI turns positive (+1-2%) and HVP IV contributes incremental ~$30M in NOI, FFO/sh recovers to $2.60-$2.65 and payout drops back below 80%.

The dividend is safe in either scenario. It is **not** safe in a scenario where FFO/sh drops to $2.40 or below — which would require SS NOI of -4% or worse and/or interest expense running another $20M hotter. We put that probability at ~10% for 2026.

### Growth trajectory

**Our 2026 dividend call:** $2.11-$2.13 (1-2% raise). The board will want to extend the streak and protect the "dividend growth REIT" branding, but they will not want to push payout above ~83%. The visible deceleration from 22.8% (2022) to 11.2% (2023) to 3.5% (2024) to 2.0% (2025) is a management signal, not noise.

**Medium-term (2026-2028) dividend CAGR:** 3-5% is the base case. 6-8% requires SS NOI to reaccelerate decisively, which depends on:
1. Occupancy recovery (88.6% → 90%+)
2. Rate stabilization (89.7% SS avg occ in 2025 vs 90.4% in 2024)
3. Pricing discipline — no more aggressive rate cuts to backfill vacancies

**Long-term (5-10 year) dividend CAGR:** 5-7%. This is consistent with self-storage as a mature industry where the 2014-2019 historical growth rates (SS NOI growing 4-6%/year) re-assert after the post-COVID indigestion clears.

### Versus the "Storage Aristocrat" benchmark

PSA: 10+ years of consecutive increases (longer history, slower growth — PSA's multi-year $8.00 dividend freeze 2017-2022 is the caveat).

EXR: 14 consecutive years of increases through 2024. The Life Storage merger (July 2023) has created integration complexity but EXR has continued raising.

CUBE: 9+ consecutive years, #3 by streak length. The 2.0% 2025 raise is the smallest since the 2016 cycle low.

**Our verdict: CUBE's dividend is safe, the streak will extend in 2026, but investors should expect low-single-digit growth for the next 18-24 months.**

## PART VI · SELF-STORAGE SECTOR DEEP-DIVE

### The supply/demand frame

Self-storage is traditionally characterized as a "recession-resistant" niche within real estate. The pitch goes: people need storage during the 4 Ds (divorce, death, downsizing, dislocation), and storage demand is actually somewhat counter-cyclical. This is mostly true on the demand side over 3-5 year windows.

What the pitch understates is **supply cyclicality**. Self-storage construction:
- Has a short lead time (12-18 months from permit to CoO)
- Is financed locally (regional bank construction loans)
- Has low technological barriers (it's a box with hallways)
- Has limited zoning restrictions in most of suburban America

This makes supply cycles intense. Historically, self-storage deliveries oscillate between ~800 new facilities/year (trough) and ~2,500/year (peak). The 2018-2020 period saw deliveries peak around 800 stores/year (actually moderate). **2021-2023 was the real supply cycle:** the COVID rent spike pulled forward a wave of development that delivered 2023-2025.

Industry-wide, "completions as % of existing stock" peaked in 2023 at ~3.0-3.5% in the top 50 MSAs (CUBE's core markets), well above the ~1.5% long-term sustainable rate. This is the mathematical reason why **every** storage REIT — PSA, EXR, CUBE, NSA — is reporting negative to flat SS NOI in 2024-2025 even though the underlying demand is fine. Supply grew faster than demand could absorb.

### Same-store NOI trajectory — the Big 3

Rough comparison (publicly disclosed, not in this source but well-known):

| Year | CUBE SS NOI | PSA SS NOI | EXR SS NOI (pre-merger) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 2021 | +17.2% | ~+15% | ~+16% |
| 2022 | +16.7% | ~+13% | ~+14% |
| 2023 | +4.2% | ~+2% | ~+4% |
| 2024 | **-2.5%** | ~-1.5% | ~-1.5% (first merged year) |
| 2025 | **-1.1%** | ~-1% | ~-0.5% |

CUBE has had a **mildly worse same-store NOI trajectory** than PSA and EXR in 2024, but the 2024→2025 improvement rate has been comparable. Three possible readings:
1. CUBE's urban/NYC focus amplifies the supply cycle (urban markets had more overbuilding).
2. CUBE's higher proportion of climate-controlled stores carries higher fixed operating costs that exacerbate the denominator in down revenue years.
3. CUBE was slightly more aggressive on 2021-2022 rate increases, leading to more catch-up rate pressure in 2024-2025.

### Construction slowdown — timing the tightening

The best leading indicator for self-storage SS NOI is "new supply as % of existing stock" in the operator's core markets. That measure has been falling since late 2023 as construction loan underwriting tightened, rates rose, and 2021-vintage developers saw their lease-up targets miss. Industry data suggests:
- 2023 completions: ~1,050 stores
- 2024 completions: ~850 stores
- 2025 completions (est.): ~600-650 stores
- 2026 completions (est.): ~450-550 stores

That's a 50%+ peak-to-trough reduction in new stores over three years. Against a 1.5-2% underlying demand growth rate, this implies meaningful tightening in 2026-2027, with SS NOI positive again by mid-2026 and accelerating in 2027.

**Our answer to the user's question #2: "Same-store NOI trough: 2024 or 2025?"**

The reported SS NOI trough is **2024 (-2.5%)**, but the **absolute bottom in operating metrics** (occupancy at 88.6% SS, realized rent flat) is **2025**. 2026 Q1 should show continued modest deterioration (we model -1% to flat SS NOI), with sequential improvement from Q2 onward. The clean inflection — the first quarter with positive SS NOI y/y — is most likely Q2 or Q3 2026. So to answer precisely: **trough SS NOI growth rate was 2024; trough absolute operating metrics is 2025**; recovery is 2026H2.

## PART VII · MOAT + CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### What is the moat?

Self-storage moats are real but modest. They come in three flavors:

**1. Brand & marketing aggregation.** CUBE runs a centralized call center, digital marketing platform, and rental software stack that an independent operator cannot replicate. This is worth 200-400 bps of occupancy over a lifetime and roughly 100-200 bps of NOI margin. This is why CUBE's 862-store third-party management book exists — independent owners pay CUBE to run their stores because CUBE can run them better.

**2. Density advantage in chosen markets.** CUBE is not the biggest operator in most markets, but where it owns (especially NYC metro), it clusters enough stores that its local cost structure, supply curve, and brand awareness all work. In Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens specifically, CUBE is arguably the #1 or #2 operator by sq ft.

**3. Zoning protection.** In NYC and a handful of other urban markets, self-storage is functionally a "no build" zoning regime. New stores cannot be permitted in most residential or mixed-use districts. This means **existing NYC self-storage is a quasi-irreplaceable asset** — you cannot buy a ground-up development and replicate it.

### What is NOT a moat

- **Locations outside top metros.** In suburban Texas, Arizona, or Florida, storage is commoditized. CUBE competes with PSA, EXR, NSA, and dozens of regional operators, plus every local developer with a construction loan. In these markets, CUBE is a price-taker, and new supply routinely undermines pricing.
- **Scale.** At 662 owned stores, CUBE is sub-scale relative to PSA (3,300) and EXR (3,700). Scale benefits in storage are real but diminishing — the marginal gain from store 662 to store 1,000 is smaller than from 100 to 500.
- **Technology.** CUBE's revenue management system is good but not differentiated. PSA and EXR have similar capabilities.

### Capital allocation track record

**Acquisitions.** The five biggest deals of the last decade:
- **HVP IV (Feb 2025):** $452.8M for remaining 80% of 28 stores CUBE already managed. Price ~$16M/store. Reasonable — CUBE already knew the assets, and the consolidation simplifies reporting.
- **Hines Portfolio (Dec 2024):** $157.3M for 85% JV interest in 14 TX stores. $11M/store, TX market price.
- **LAACO / Storage West (Dec 2021):** ~$1.7B for 59 stores, heavy on CA/AZ/NV. Deal closed at the top of the cycle; in hindsight, CUBE paid COVID-peak pricing, and those stores have had a rough 2024-2025. Not a mistake per se, but not a bargain either.
- **HVPSE (earlier JV, partial sales in 2022-2023).**
- **Various small tuck-ins** (Connecticut, Oregon, Pennsylvania, NJ, FL in 2023-2025).

**Grade: B.** CUBE is a disciplined buyer that does not chase. But the 2021 LAACO deal was expensive, and the company has historically issued equity near price peaks (2021 at-the-market equity program raised stock at $50+ when the 2022-2023 pullback happened). The 2025 decision to pause the ATM and instead draw the revolver to fund HVP IV is the right call — issuing equity at $38-$40 after a 30% drawdown is the worst possible time to dilute.

**Buybacks.** CUBE is historically not a buyer of its own stock. 2025 marked the first material buyback in years: 0.9 million shares at $35.84 average (~$32M total). In February 2026 the board authorized 10 million additional shares for buyback — an explicit signal that management views the current price as attractive. This is **a meaningful positive signal** and worth weight in the thesis.

**Dispositions.** Minimal. Only one store sold in 2023 (involuntary conversion by Illinois DOT). This is a portfolio that has been grown, not pruned. In a post-COVID normalization, some pruning would be welcome (sell weak sub-markets, redeploy into strong ones), but CUBE has not moved that lever.

**Dividends.** 9 consecutive years of increases through 2025, with the cadence now decelerating. Well-managed relative to FFO, but not aggressive.

## PART VIII · RISKS

### 1. Same-store NOI doesn't inflect in 2026 (probability: 25%)

If SS NOI stays negative through 2026, CUBE's FFO/share will be flat to down for a third consecutive year. The board would be forced to either: freeze the dividend (breaking the streak) or tolerate a payout ratio north of 85%. Both would re-rate the multiple lower.

**Triggers for this scenario:** (a) unexpected supply delivery acceleration; (b) recession reducing household moving activity; (c) NYC-specific softening tied to commercial real estate spillover.

### 2. Interest rate reset (probability: 40% in some form)

CUBE has $3.0B of unsecured notes with a **3.29% weighted-average effective rate** (2025 10-K line 3736). The notes mature in a staggered ladder: $300M in 2026 (3.18%), $550M in 2028 (2.33%), $350M in 2029 (4.46%), $350M in 2030 (3.04%), $450M in 2031 (2.10%), $500M in 2032 (2.59%), $450M in 2035 (5.30%).

The 2026 notes at 3.18% will refinance at ~5.0-5.5% (the 2035s already issued at 5.125%). That is an additional $5.5M/year of interest expense — not catastrophic, but a headwind. Each year through 2032, roughly $300-550M of notes refi at higher rates. Over 5 years, the weighted average cost of debt likely rises from 3.29% to 4.25-4.50%. Annual drag on FFO: $25-35M, or approximately $0.11-0.15/share spread over 5 years.

**This is a real, quantifiable headwind.** It reduces long-term FFO/share growth by roughly 100 bps/year through 2030.

### 3. NYC concentration shock (probability: 10-15%)

New York = 17% of 2025 revenue. NYC self-storage has benefited from structural zoning protection but is exposed to:
- Population out-migration (2020-2022 net negative; 2023-2025 modest recovery)
- Office/CRE distress that spills into local employment
- Insurance costs that have spiked 15-25%/year in NY
- Property tax pressure from budget-strapped city governments

A NYC-specific recession or a major insurance repricing event could disproportionately hit CUBE. This is not the base case, but it is a tail risk unique to CUBE among the Big 3 storage REITs.

### 4. REIT status / tax risk (probability: 5%)

Standard REIT risk. CUBE must distribute 90% of taxable income. Given the post-COVID depreciation stack and recent acquisitions, taxable income is lower than GAAP net income, so the 90% distribution is comfortably met. 2025 distributions characterized as 88.3% ordinary income + 11.7% non-dividend distribution (return of capital) per 10-K line 3472. No excise tax incurred 2023-2025.

### 5. Competition from PSA/EXR (probability: 50%+ as ongoing condition)

PSA is structurally cheaper to fund (A/A2 rated) and has a slower depreciation schedule. In multi-bidder acquisition auctions, PSA can usually outbid CUBE. Over time, this limits CUBE's ability to grow via large portfolio acquisitions in hot markets. EXR similarly has more scale efficiency.

### 6. Regulatory / lien sale risk (probability: 5%)

Self-storage operators rely on state lien sale laws to dispose of abandoned units. These laws are state-specific and occasionally modified (NY passed a modest tenant protection update in 2023). A significant federal consumer protection move on lien sales would raise operating costs meaningfully. This is a slow-moving, low-probability risk but watch it.

### 7. Management turnover (probability: 5%)

Chris Marr has been CEO since 2014 (11 years) and with CUBE since 2006. Named executive officers are covered by severance plans but not non-competes. A succession transition is not imminent but is an eventual reality.

### What we think the market is missing

**Variant #1: The trough is later than consensus believes.** We think Q2-Q3 2026 is the earliest SS NOI inflection, not Q4 2025 or Q1 2026 as some sellside models imply. The 2024 SS NOI of -2.5% → 2025 of -1.1% is a nice sequential improvement, but 110 bps of annual improvement from this level still puts Q1 2026 at roughly -0.3% to flat. Only by Q2 or Q3 does the y/y comparison turn decisively positive, and only with help from non-same-store (HVP IV) contributing.

**Variant #2: The dividend growth will disappoint.** Consensus assumes 4-5% dividend growth in 2026. We think 1.5-2.5% is more likely, and a formal freeze (no raise) is possible. The payout ratio pressure is real, and management's 2025 2.0% raise was a message to the market that they are preserving coverage, not chasing yield-investor demand.

**Variant #3: The buyback authorization is the real tell.** In February 2026 the board authorized an incremental **10 million share** buyback on top of the remaining 2.1M from prior authorization. At $37.25, the full 12.1M authorization = $450M of potential purchases, or roughly 5.3% of shares outstanding. **This is management signaling that current prices are below intrinsic value and that internal FCF has nowhere better to go than buying the stock.** Most REITs do not do buybacks — the capital is usually deployed into new properties. When a REIT pivots to buybacks, it is either because (a) acquisition pricing is still too rich, or (b) management believes the stock is materially cheap. CUBE is saying both.

**Variant #4: NYC is actually de-risking.** The 2020-2022 "NYC is dead" narrative collapsed in 2023-2025 as population trends reversed and office return-to-work normalized. CUBE's NYC concentration was a drag in 2020-2022 (when urban was bad) and has been neutral-to-positive since 2023. Looking forward, if NYC continues its slow recovery, CUBE's 17% NY exposure becomes an alpha source, not a drag.

**Variant #5: The "Big 3" frame is wrong.** The industry structure post-2023 is actually a "Big 2 + 1" — PSA and EXR are in a different weight class (3,000+ stores, $30B+ market caps) than CUBE (662 stores, $8.5B). CUBE is functionally a "premium mid-cap" rather than a "big cap discount" name, and it should be valued accordingly. A mid-cap quality premium of 15-17x P/FFO is appropriate; the current 14.4x is a mild discount to that.
