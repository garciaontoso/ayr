## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE at $245. BUY below $215. Dividend Safety: 9/10. Confidence: High.**

Johnson & Johnson is the world's largest diversified healthcare company, operating across two complementary franchises after the 2023 Kenvue spin-off: Innovative Medicine (biopharmaceuticals, ~64% of revenue at $60.4B in FY2025) and MedTech (medical devices, ~36% at $33.8B). At a market capitalization of approximately $590B [approx, based on ~2.4B shares at $245.72], the company is a cornerstone defensive holding with 63 consecutive years of dividend increases -- among the longest uninterrupted streaks in corporate America and a top-5 Dividend King by streak length. The current quarterly dividend of $1.30 ($5.20 annualized) yields approximately 2.1% and was last increased by 4.8% in April 2025, continuing a pattern of mid-single-digit annual raises for the past five years.

The central question for a new dividend investor evaluating JNJ is whether the post-Kenvue company can reaccelerate growth in Innovative Medicine sufficient to offset the Stelara biosimilar erosion that began in 2024 -- and whether the balance sheet can absorb the ongoing talc litigation overhang. Our answer is a qualified yes. FY2025 revenue growth of 6.0% (operational: approximately 7.4%) demonstrated that the oncology franchise (Darzalex, Carvykti, Tecvayli, Talvey) plus new launches in immunology (Tremfya expanded indications) and the newly acquired Intra-Cellular assets (Caplyta) can offset the $2-3B annual Stelara headwind. MedTech posted a more pedestrian 3.4% operational growth as the Shockwave acquisition contributed, partially offset by a cardiovascular reimbursement step-down in China. Free cash flow of $19.7B in FY2025 covers the $12.3B dividend payment 1.6x and leaves $7B+ for buybacks ($6.0B in FY2025) and opportunistic M&A.

The dividend is fortress-grade safe for the following reasons: (1) FCF coverage of 1.6x with FCF running $19-20B annually for three consecutive years, (2) AAA/Aaa credit ratings (only two non-financial US companies retain AAA: JNJ and Microsoft), (3) net debt of $28.2B against $94B in revenue and $40B in EBITDA equals a leverage ratio of 0.7x -- investment-grade with extraordinary flexibility, (4) 63-year streak provides cultural commitment at both board and management level, (5) diversified cash flow across 25+ therapeutic areas and hundreds of device products insulates against single-product risk. The single largest risk remains talc litigation: after two failed Texas Two-Step bankruptcy attempts (rejected 2023, 2024) and a third attempt in Texas pending as of early 2026, the eventual settlement could reach $10-15B [approx] -- absorbable against FCF but an ongoing reputational distraction. Our base case is that the litigation settles for $8-12B over 2026-2028 without impacting the dividend.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **Dividend King with institutional commitment and 63-year streak.** The streak began in 1962 and spans 11 US recessions, including the 2008 financial crisis (when JNJ raised dividends by 10.8%) and COVID (6.3% raise in April 2020). Management has repeatedly stated the dividend is sacrosanct. The 5y DPS CAGR of 5.8% is modest but covers typical inflation and delivers real yield-on-cost compounding.
2. **Oncology portfolio is the crown jewel.** Darzalex grew to $13.1B in FY2025 [approx] and is the anchor of multiple myeloma standard of care. Carvykti (cell therapy, partnership with Legend Biotech) is scaling with manufacturing capacity expansion -- projected to reach $5B+ in peak sales. The oncology franchise alone now exceeds $25B and is growing double-digits.
3. **AAA balance sheet with optionality.** Only two non-financial US companies carry AAA ratings. Net cash generation allows continued bolt-on M&A (Intra-Cellular for $14.6B in 2025, Shockwave for $13.1B in 2024) without threatening dividend or buyback cadence. In a recession, JNJ can tap debt markets at favorable rates.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to buy at current price:**
1. **Stelara cliff is multi-year and deeper than expected.** FY2025 showed the first full year of biosimilar competition in the US. Wusduo, Pyzchiva, Selarsdi, and Otulfi collectively captured approximately 30% of share in the second half of 2025. The company has guided Stelara revenue to decline from $10.9B (2023 peak) to $3-4B by 2027. While expected, the speed of erosion stressed Innovative Medicine margins.
2. **Talc litigation is unresolved after eight years.** The Texas Two-Step strategy failed twice (2023, 2024). JNJ has been forced into direct settlements on a case-by-case basis, paying ~$9B cumulatively through 2025. Total plaintiff universe exceeds 60,000 claims. A mass tort settlement could reach $10-15B, and while FCF can absorb it, the reputational overhang keeps the multiple compressed.
3. **Valuation is not cheap on forward earnings.** At $245 and ~15-16x forward EPS, JNJ trades above its 10-year median of ~14x. The yield of 2.1% is at the low end of the 5-year range (2.5-3.5%). Better entry points may emerge on macro weakness or litigation headlines.

**Key numbers at a glance:**
- Price / Yield / Payout ratio / Streak: $245.72 / 2.1% / 47% (NI), 63% (FCF) / 63 years
- Last dividend hike: April 2025, +4.8% (from $1.24/quarter to $1.30/quarter)
- 5-year dividend CAGR: ~5.7%

**Next 12 months catalysts:**

The board and management style is famously conservative -- the company is run under the "Credo" (written in 1943 by Robert Wood Johnson II), which ranks patients first, employees second, communities third, and shareholders fourth. That ordering is uncommon in corporate America but has produced one of the longest-running shareholder value stories in history.

## PART IV -- DIVIDEND DEEP DIVE

Johnson & Johnson's dividend history is the gold standard in American corporate life. The company has raised its dividend every year since 1963, through 11 recessions, three world-historical pandemics (1968 flu, AIDS, COVID-19), and the dot-com and 2008 financial crises. The cumulative raise since 1963 exceeds 13,000% -- compounding at approximately 8% annually across 62 years.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

Johnson & Johnson's dividend is among the safest in corporate America, for five quantifiable reasons:

*First, free cash flow coverage.* FY2025 FCF of $19.7B against dividend payments of $12.6B gives 1.56x coverage. Even in the worst single year of the past decade (FY2023, $18.2B FCF), coverage was 1.56x. The absolute dollar FCF has not dropped below $17B since 2017.

*Second, diversification across 25+ therapeutic areas in pharma and hundreds of device product lines.* No single product exceeds 14% of total revenue. Darzalex, the largest drug, is about $13.1B or 13.9% of total. The diversification means no single LOE event can impair the dividend.

*Third, AAA balance sheet.* Only JNJ and Microsoft hold Aaa/AAA ratings from both Moody's and S&P among non-financial US companies. Net debt / EBITDA at 0.7x. Interest coverage exceeds 40x. Debt refinancing cost is among the lowest available in public markets.

*Fourth, historical behavior through recessions.* In 2008, JNJ raised the dividend by 10.8% in April despite the financial crisis. In 2020 (COVID), the company raised by 6.3% in April, just six weeks after global lockdowns began. In 2009 (the depths of the recession), the company raised again. This is a board-level cultural commitment that would require a catastrophic, multi-year cash flow collapse to break.

*Fifth, payout ratio discipline.* Despite 63 consecutive raises, the payout ratio has never exceeded 70% on earnings. Current 47% is comfortably in the middle of the historical 40-60% band. This leaves extensive room for continued raises even if earnings grow slowly.

**The 2008 stress test:** In FY2008-2009, JNJ grew revenues 4.4% and 0.5% respectively. Net income grew from $12.3B to $12.3B (essentially flat). The dividend was raised 10.8% in April 2008 and 6.5% in April 2009. Zero stress to the payout. If FY2026 resembles 2009 operationally, we would expect a 4-5% dividend raise, continuing the streak.

**The 2020 stress test:** Revenue grew 0.6%, operational net income declined ~7%. The dividend was raised 6.3%. Management specifically cited FCF stability as justification.

**What would have to break for a cut?** In our analysis, a dividend cut would require one of: (a) a multi-year collapse in FCF below $10B (requires catastrophic oncology franchise failure + MedTech halving), (b) a talc settlement exceeding $30B settled as a lump sum (currently implausible -- structured payments more likely), (c) loss of AAA and refinancing at severely distressed levels (requires sustained rating downgrades to junk). None of these scenarios are realistic in a 3-year forward view. Assigned cut probability 3y: <2%. Hike probability 12m: 95%+.

## PART V -- BUSINESS QUALITY & MOAT

Johnson & Johnson's moat is composed of three reinforcing structures:

**1. Intellectual property in pharmaceuticals.** The company has invested approximately $15-17B per year in R&D for the past five years (FY2025: $17.0B). This generates a continuous pipeline of new molecular entities and line extensions. As of early 2026, JNJ has approximately 40 products in late-stage development (Phase 3 or registration). Pipeline highlights include: IL-23 inhibitor Tremfya indication expansion into additional autoimmune diseases, ICT expansion of Caplyta into depression, next-generation Stelara follow-on products, new bispecific antibodies in oncology, and several Darzalex combination regimens.

**2. Clinical trial infrastructure and regulatory expertise.** JNJ's pharmaceutical regulatory, clinical trial, and manufacturing capabilities are extensive. The company operates with the scale to run simultaneous global Phase 3 trials across dozens of programs. This infrastructure is a meaningful barrier to entry for smaller biotechs who often need JNJ as a development partner.

**3. Medical device sales channels and surgeon training.** MedTech's moat is primarily in the sales force and the training programs that embed surgeons with specific device systems. A knee replacement surgeon trained on DePuy Synthes instruments is unlikely to switch to Zimmer or Stryker without significant re-training. Similarly, the Ottawa surgeon using a Biosense Webster catheter is trained and accredited on that specific platform. Customer switching costs in MedTech are high at the individual physician level.

**Moat trajectory:** Stable to slightly widening. The pharmaceutical moat is being reinforced by the oncology and cell therapy investments (Carvykti is a flagship). The MedTech moat is being widened by the acquisitions of Abiomed (Impella platform) and Shockwave (intravascular lithotripsy) which add high-growth, high-margin platforms with strong clinician pull. The counterbalance is biosimilar erosion of older products (Stelara, Remicade), which is an ongoing drag.

**Pricing power evidence:**
- Darzalex has taken roughly mid-single-digit price increases annually in the US.
- Tremfya has taken 3-5% annual price increases with no meaningful pushback.
- MedTech has been more pricing-constrained given GPO and hospital system negotiating leverage; price realization is typically 0 to -3% in developed markets.
- Internationally, pricing is largely government-determined and has been trending modestly lower.

**Return on invested capital trend:**
- FY2019: 16.8% [approx, pre-split]
- FY2020: 13.4%
- FY2021: 18.1%
- FY2022: 14.8%
- FY2023: 23.8% (includes Kenvue disposal gain)
- FY2024: 14.0% (post-split, normalized)
- FY2025: 17.0% [approx, pre-Intra-Cellular noise]

Normalized post-split ROIC is in the mid-teens -- acceptable for a diversified healthcare company but not exceptional. The Intra-Cellular acquisition will temporarily dilute ROIC while integration proceeds.

## PART VI -- CAPITAL ALLOCATION

Management follows a consistent capital allocation priority stack, articulated in every major investor communication:

1. **Organic R&D reinvestment:** FY2025 $17.0B (18.0% of revenue). This is the highest R&D intensity among big pharma and is treated as non-negotiable.
2. **Dividends:** FY2025 $12.6B paid. Target is a payout ratio of 40-60% of earnings, with annual raises that match or exceed inflation.
3. **Strategic M&A:** FY2025 ~$15B total (Intra-Cellular $14.6B primary). Typical multi-year pace is $3-5B/year of bolt-ons, with larger platform deals every 3-5 years.
4. **Buybacks:** FY2025 $6.0B. Typically 2-3% annual share count reduction; expands when the stock is perceived as undervalued.
5. **Debt reduction:** Deprioritized given the AAA balance sheet already carries minimal leverage.

**FY2025 specific capital returns:**
- Total shareholder returns: $18.6B (dividend $12.6B + buyback $6.0B)
- M&A: $15.0B (Intra-Cellular $14.6B + bolt-ons)
- R&D: $17.0B
- Capex: $4.8B
- **Total capital deployed:** ~$55B against $24.5B operating cash flow (bridged with debt issuance)

**M&A track record:**

| Year | Deal | Purchase Price | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2017 | Actelion | $30B | Mixed -- Uptravi performed well, Opsumit solid, but franchise peaked faster than expected |
| 2019 | Auris Health | $3.4B | Surgical robotics integration slow; Monarch platform still scaling |
| 2022 | Abiomed | $16.6B | Strong -- Impella franchise growing double-digits, margin accretive |
| 2024 | Shockwave | $13.1B | Early positive -- IVL platform performing ahead of plan |
| 2024 | Ambrx | $2.0B | Speculative -- ADC platform in clinical development |
| 2025 | Intra-Cellular | $14.6B | Recently closed -- Caplyta accretive but integration risk remains |

**Capital allocation grade: A-.** Reliable, shareholder-friendly, rarely makes catastrophic M&A errors. The criticism is that occasional deals (Actelion, Auris) paid premium multiples for assets that underdelivered. Shockwave and Abiomed are shaping up as strong additions.

## PART IX -- VALUATION

## EXTENDED ANALYSIS -- INNOVATIVE MEDICINE DEEP DIVE

The Innovative Medicine segment contributed $60.4B in FY2025 revenue, representing 64% of total company revenue. Understanding the composition and trajectory of this segment is central to the JNJ investment thesis. The segment is organized around five therapeutic areas, each with distinct growth drivers and risk profiles.

**Oncology ($25.5B, FY2025 [approx]):** The crown jewel. Anchored by Darzalex (daratumumab), a CD38-targeting monoclonal antibody for multiple myeloma, which reached $13.1B in FY2025 and continues to grow at a mid-to-high single-digit rate through line extensions (subcutaneous formulation Darzalex Faspro, combination regimens with Revlimid/Pomalyst). Darzalex faces its first major patent challenge in the early 2030s -- a long runway. Imbruvica (ibrutinib, for chronic lymphocytic leukemia) is declining toward $3.4B as newer BTK inhibitors gain share. Erleada (apalutamide, for prostate cancer) grew to $3.1B in FY2025 and is approaching peak sales. Carvykti (ciltacabtagene autoleucel, a CAR-T cell therapy for multiple myeloma, co-developed with Legend Biotech) reached approximately $1.6B in FY2025 after launch supply constraints limited 2024 revenues. Manufacturing capacity is expanding with multiple new facilities coming online through 2026. Management is guiding toward $5B+ peak sales. The bispecific antibodies Tecvayli (teclistamab, BCMA-CD3), Talvey (talquetamab, GPRC5D-CD3), and Rybrevant (amivantamab, EGFR-MET) are collectively generating approximately $2B and growing rapidly.

**Immunology ($18.5B, FY2025 [approx]):** Two dynamics at play. Stelara (ustekinumab) is in rapid biosimilar decline -- FY2025 revenue of $8.8B is down 20%+ from 2023 peak of $10.9B, and management has guided to $3-4B by 2027. Four biosimilars launched US in 2025 (Wezlana/Pyzchiva/Selarsdi/Otulfi). Tremfya (guselkumab, IL-23 inhibitor) is partially offsetting this -- $4.3B in FY2025, with indication expansion into ulcerative colitis (approved 2024) and Crohn's disease (approved 2025). Tremfya has a favorable patent profile through late 2029, giving a multi-year runway. Remicade continues its slow decline at $1.6B. The Q3 2025 launch of a next-generation IL-23/IL-17 combination inhibitor (from the Ambrx pipeline) is a potential growth driver for late 2026.

**Neuroscience ($8.5B, FY2025 [approx]):** Growing double-digits on Spravato (esketamine for treatment-resistant depression, $1.5B) and the newly integrated Caplyta (lumateperone, for schizophrenia and bipolar depression, from the Intra-Cellular acquisition). Caplyta expansion into major depressive disorder (target PDUFA late 2026) would be a significant catalyst. The schizophrenia franchise now includes both Invega Trinza/Hafyera and Caplyta, giving JNJ the broadest long-acting injectable portfolio in the class.

**Infectious Diseases ($2.5B):** Prezista (HIV protease inhibitor, mature), Edurant (rilpivirine), and the Symtuza fixed-dose combination. Slow decline expected.

**Cardiovascular/Metabolism ($3.0B):** Xarelto (rivaroxaban) royalty revenue, Invokana (canagliflozin) SGLT-2 inhibitor. Both under generic pressure post-2024.

## EXTENDED ANALYSIS -- TALC LITIGATION

Given that talc litigation is the single largest investor overhang, it deserves detailed attention.

**Background:** Plaintiffs allege that JNJ's talc-based baby powder products caused ovarian cancer and mesothelioma. The company maintained that the products were safe based on independent testing and regulatory reviews. JNJ discontinued sales of talc-based baby powder in North America in 2020 and globally in 2023.

**Claim universe:** Approximately 62,000 cases as of year-end 2025. The majority are ovarian cancer claims; a smaller fraction are mesothelioma claims (which typically carry higher individual payouts).

**Legal strategy evolution:**
1. *Direct litigation (pre-2021):* JNJ fought cases individually. Results were mixed -- some wins, some substantial losses (e.g., Missouri $4.7B verdict in 2018, later reduced on appeal).
2. *First Texas Two-Step (October 2021):* JNJ created a subsidiary, LTL Management, and filed it for Chapter 11 bankruptcy with the intent to consolidate all talc liability in that shell. Bankruptcy court approved, but a federal appeals court rejected in January 2023 on the basis that LTL was not financially distressed.
3. *Second Texas Two-Step (April 2023):* JNJ restructured and refiled. The Third Circuit Court of Appeals rejected again in July 2023.
4. *Direct settlement + Third attempt (2024-2025):* JNJ has pursued case-by-case settlements while simultaneously preparing a third bankruptcy filing with a pre-packaged settlement. As of early 2026, the third filing was pending in a Texas state court with a proposed $9-12B structured settlement.

**Financial reserves and payments:**
- Cumulative settlements paid through 2025: approximately $9.5B [approx]
- Reserve on balance sheet (FY2025): approximately $11B
- Annual run-rate settlement: $1.5-2.5B (2023-2025 average)

**Potential outcomes:**
- **Base case (50%):** Third bankruptcy attempt succeeds or JNJ achieves comprehensive mass tort settlement for $10-15B total additional spend over 2026-2028. FCF absorbs; no dividend impact.
- **Downside case (30%):** Continued case-by-case trials result in mixed verdicts, cumulative payments of $15-20B over 5-7 years. FCF strain but manageable.
- **Severe downside (15%):** Major loss verdict triggers cascade, $25B+ total settlement over 10+ years. Would require debt refinancing but dividend still safe.
- **Resolution (5%):** Favorable appeals or comprehensive regulatory/scientific exoneration of the claims. Stock re-rating upward.

**Impact on investment thesis:** Talc is a known-unknown. It's priced into the 2-3 multiple point discount vs peers, but the exact timing and size of resolution is uncertain. The important point for dividend investors is that the dividend has continued to grow through all litigation developments to date, and the balance sheet can absorb even severe downside scenarios.
