## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD at HK$2.85 -- Accumulate below HK$2.40 -- Trim above HK$3.50 -- Dividend Safety: 5/10 -- Confidence: Medium-Low**

Chaoju Eye Care Holdings represents one of the more interesting but least understood positions in the portfolio. It is a pure-play regional ophthalmology operator in mainland China, listed on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange via a Cayman-domiciled H-share structure, and trades at valuation multiples that imply the market believes the business is in secular decline. The reality is considerably more nuanced: the network of 17 hospitals, 2 clinics and 24 optical centers concentrated in Inner Mongolia, Hebei, Shanxi, Beijing, Jiangsu and Zhejiang generates 42% gross margins, 18% operating margins, and FY2025 net income of RMB 179M against RMB 1,325M of revenue. Cash on the balance sheet (RMB 517M) exceeds total debt (RMB 238M) by roughly RMB 279M, which is equivalent to approximately 15% of the current market capitalization. For a consumer-medical business in the Chinese healthcare complex, this is an unusually defensive financial profile.

The counter-argument, and the one the market appears to be pricing, is that the Chinese healthcare sector is in the middle of a multi-year repricing. Volume-Based Procurement (VBP) was extended from cardiovascular stents and orthopedic implants into ophthalmology consumables in 2023-2024, compressing prices for intraocular lenses (IOLs) used in cataract surgery. Medical insurance reimbursement rates have tightened as provincial fiscal positions deteriorate. The ongoing anti-corruption campaign in the healthcare sector has made doctors and administrators visibly more cautious about patient referral patterns and elective procedure volume. Private refractive surgery -- the highest-margin business for any eye care chain including the listed giant Aier Eye Hospital (300015.SZ) -- has experienced genuine price deflation as competition increased and consumer discretionary spending weakened in the post-COVID reopening period. Chaoju's revenue decline of -5.7% in FY2025, while modest in absolute terms, reflects all of these pressures simultaneously.

What makes this stock difficult to analyze is the combination of three factors that each individually reduce research visibility: (a) the business is operationally sound but the macro is deteriorating, (b) the company's size and the complexity of the Chinese medical system make Western-style institutional sell-side coverage effectively non-existent (FMP shows one analyst providing forward estimates), and (c) the dividend policy has been inconsistent -- no dividends pre-IPO, a major distribution in FY2024, a modest distribution in FY2025 -- which precludes any confident extrapolation of forward yield. The 14.46% dividend yield FMP shows for FY2024 is genuinely misleading for anyone using this figure to size a position; it reflected a one-off disgorgement of accumulated cash rather than a policy commitment.

The top-line story is clear: this is a regional market-leader with structural network effects in its core provinces but limited ability to expand nationally against entrenched competitors like Aier. The company IPO'd in July 2021 at HK$10.60 and has lost approximately 73% of its value since listing, a trajectory consistent with the broader Chinese consumer-healthcare derating. The current share price of HK$2.85 is trading at 0.82x book value and 9.6x trailing earnings, multiples that historically have marked a valuation floor even for businesses in secular decline.

**Top 3 reasons to own at HK$2.85:**

1. **The balance sheet is genuinely unleveraged**. Net cash position of ~RMB 279M against a market cap of ~HK$1.98B means roughly 15% of the current share price is backed by unencumbered cash. Total debt-to-equity is only 9.9%, current ratio 4.37x, quick ratio 4.26x, interest coverage ~20x. In a Chinese healthcare landscape where many private operators have been squeezed by refinancing pressure since 2023, Chaoju has the rare luxury of being able to ride out a multi-year revenue contraction without any refinancing risk, covenant breach risk, or forced asset sale.
2. **Operating margins remain in the high-teens despite revenue pressure**. Operating margin of 17.8% in FY2025 compares to 22.1% in FY2023 and 24.1% in FY2021 -- so margins have compressed 350-600bps from peak, but they remain comfortably above the break-even threshold and well above the 5-10% range typical for public Chinese hospitals. The gross margin of 42.2% is structurally defensible because it is driven by the consumer-pay optical and refractive businesses rather than insurance-funded basic ophthalmology. This margin structure gives the company meaningful flexibility to absorb further insurance pricing compression without crashing through break-even.
3. **The valuation is extreme on multiple axes**. Trailing P/E of 9.6x, P/B of 0.71x, EV/EBITDA of 3.8x, P/S of 1.29x, and FCF/EV yield in the mid-teens (for a debt-light, cash-heavy balance sheet the EV is close to market cap minus net cash). Every one of these multiples is below Chinese healthcare peer medians and below the stock's own historical range since IPO. If you believe ophthalmology demand in northern China is structurally intact -- aging population, rising myopia rates in children, growing demand for lifestyle eye care -- then the current price is pricing in a substantial probability of permanent impairment that the operational data does not support.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own at HK$2.85:**

1. **The dividend is not reliable income**. FY2024's RMB 0.39/share distribution was >100% of earnings and effectively a one-time return of accumulated IPO capital. FY2025's RMB 0.107 is more representative of a sustainable payout but even at 41.6% payout ratio it yields only 4.3% -- not the 14%+ the FY2024 ratio implies. Investors who size this position on the assumption of double-digit recurring yield will be disappointed. A dividend investor must treat this as a book-value-floor position that pays a modest, variable dividend, not as a yield vehicle.
2. **Volume-Based Procurement (VBP) is still rolling through ophthalmology**. The national VBP program that cut intraocular lens prices by 50-70% in 2022-2024 is being extended to additional ophthalmic consumables, and there is active regulatory discussion about including elective refractive surgery under insurance pricing controls. If refractive surgery is brought into insurance reimbursement (or if excessive private-pay pricing triggers consumer backlash), the highest-margin segment for Chaoju would face a step-change downward in unit economics. Management commentary in the FY2025 annual report acknowledges ongoing pricing pressure but does not quantify sensitivity. [unverified: primary-source quantification of refractive VBP exposure]
3. **Illiquidity and governance opacity**. Average daily trading volume is HK$1.3M (US$170K) -- a position larger than US$50K cannot be built or exited in a single session without impacting price. The Cayman/VIE structure, limited English-language disclosure, lack of earnings call transcripts, and effectively zero institutional sell-side coverage mean that due diligence is essentially restricted to periodic annual reports and the occasional HKEX announcement. For a China-resident investor, this is less of a concern; for a diversified dividend portfolio, this is a peripheral position that requires patience and a willingness to accept limited real-time visibility.

**Next 12-18 months catalysts:**

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Chaoju Eye Care Holdings Limited (HKEX: 2219) is a Cayman-Islands-incorporated H-share company that owns, through VIE and direct-subsidiary arrangements, a network of private ophthalmic hospitals, clinics, and optical retail centers concentrated in northern and eastern China. According to the FMP profile and HKEX filings, as of the most recent fiscal year-end, the group operated 17 ophthalmic hospitals, 2 clinics, and 24 optical centers, distributed across six provinces and autonomous regions: Inner Mongolia (the historical heartland of the business), Hebei, Shanxi, Beijing, Jiangsu, and Zhejiang. The company was founded in 1988 and headquartered in Beijing, with operational headquarters in the Shouke Building, Beijing. The IPO on HKEX was completed on July 7, 2021 at HK$10.60 per share, raising approximately HK$1.2-1.5B in primary proceeds to fund hospital expansion and digital/clinical infrastructure.

The group's business activity is divided, based on available disclosures, into three broad categories. Consumer ophthalmic services encompass refractive surgery (LASIK, SMILE, ICL), presbyopia correction, myopia control for children, and premium cataract surgery with premium intraocular lens (IOL) upgrades. Basic ophthalmic services include cataract surgery using standard IOLs (now subject to VBP pricing), glaucoma treatment, retinal/posterior-segment procedures, corneal disease, strabismus, pediatric ophthalmology, oculoplastic and lacrimal surgery, and emergency ocular trauma care. Optical retail centers sell eyeglasses, contact lenses, and related products as both an independent revenue stream and a downstream funnel from the clinical practice. Revenue contribution by segment is not disclosed in FMP's feed (revSegments/geoSegments returned empty), but the annual reports historically attribute approximately 55-65% of revenue to the consumer ophthalmic segment, 25-30% to basic ophthalmic services, and 10-15% to optical retail. ## PART III -- REVENUE AND EARNINGS ANALYSIS

### Revenue Trajectory

Chaoju's revenue base more than doubled from RMB 520M in FY2018 [historical HKEX prospectus data, approx] through the IPO and COVID period to a peak of RMB 1,405M in FY2024, before declining -5.7% to RMB 1,325M in FY2025. The three-year trailing revenue growth rate (FY2022-FY2025) is approximately 10.2% CAGR, the five-year rate approximately 10.8%, and the ten-year rate is in the mid-teens -- all solid by any reasonable healthcare benchmark. The decline in FY2025 is therefore the first meaningful negative revenue print since the IPO and deserves careful attribution.

| Year | Revenue (RMB M) | YoY Change | Growth Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY2025 | 1,325.2 | -5.7% | Volume/price pressure across all segments; VBP impact on cataract; refractive surgery pricing deflation |
| FY2024 | 1,405.5 | +2.6% | Modest organic growth; partial absorption of IOL price cuts from VBP Round 3 |
| FY2023 | 1,369.5 | +38.3% | Post-COVID reopening; pent-up demand for elective procedures; expanded hospital footprint |
| FY2022 | 990.0 | -0.8% | COVID restrictions in key service cities (Tianjin, Inner Mongolia) dampened elective demand |
| FY2021 | 997.8 | +25.6% | IPO year; aggressive expansion; return of elective volume |
| FY2020 | 794.3 | n/a | COVID first-wave impact mitigated by underlying network expansion |

The FY2025 decline appears driven by three factors operating simultaneously, each of which is individually manageable but collectively representative of a sector headwind. First, the volume-based procurement (VBP) program that cut standard intraocular lens prices by 50-70% began to flow through the full patient mix in FY2024-FY2025, reducing revenue per cataract procedure even as procedure volume held up. Second, the competitive environment for private refractive surgery (LASIK, SMILE) has intensified as new capacity from Aier and smaller regional competitors has pressured pricing; the FY2025 annual report acknowledges promotional activity and selective price reductions in this segment. Third, the residual post-COVID normalization of demand has continued, with some discretionary spending on premium optical products and premium IOL upgrades deferred as household consumption remained constrained. Management commentary (inferred from the annual report structure and industry norms) emphasizes that procedure volumes are holding up but average revenue per procedure is under pressure -- a textbook profile of a healthcare business in a transitional regulatory/competitive environment.

### Earnings Quality

The earnings mix tells a more nuanced story than the headline decline. Gross margin compressed only modestly from 43.5% to 42.2% -- a 130bp deterioration that is small in absolute terms and consistent with mix shift (higher proportion of insurance-reimbursed VBP cataract, lower proportion of premium self-pay refractive). Operating margin actually held flat at 17.8% vs 17.6% in FY2024, aided by SG&A discipline (SG&A fell 10.7% in FY2025 versus revenue down 5.7%, implying genuine cost control rather than scale benefits). Net margin remains in the mid-teens at 13.5%.

The +12.9% EBITDA growth in FY2025 while revenue declined 5.7% is unusual and reflects a step-change in depreciation and amortization expense -- D&A rose from RMB 88.3M in FY2024 to RMB 143.1M in FY2025. This suggests either (a) a significant FY2025 capex cycle that lifted the asset base, (b) an accounting change in useful-life estimates, or (c) impairment or accelerated amortization on a specific asset category. The higher D&A is what pushed net income down while EBITDA went up. For dividend investors, the cash-flow signal matters more than the accounting EBITDA -- and operating cash flow of RMB 354M in FY2024 (FY2025 full-year OCF not yet in the feed from FMP's lag, though the Q2 2025 operating cash flow was RMB 142.7M implying FY2025 OCF in the range of RMB 280-320M) indicates the cash-generation engine is intact even as GAAP earnings show pressure.

**The FY2024 dividend of RMB 0.387 was the outlier event.** At a 138% payout ratio, the distribution clearly drew on accumulated cash rather than FY2024 earnings alone. The most plausible interpretation is that management returned a portion of the IPO proceeds that had not been deployed in hospital expansion, recognizing that sitting on RMB 700M+ of low-yielding cash was dragging ROE. This interpretation is supported by the FY2024 cash flow statement showing RMB 292M of net cash out through financing activities, meaningfully above recurring dividend obligations. Investors who anchor their dividend yield expectations on the 14.46% FY2024 yield are misreading the payout pattern.

**The FY2025 dividend of RMB 0.107 (approximately HK$0.12) is more representative of a sustainable policy.** At a 41.6% payout ratio against earnings of RMB 178M, the dividend commitment is approximately RMB 74M annually. Against operating cash flow of roughly RMB 300M, this is well-covered and leaves ample room for reinvestment. The declared dividend of HK$0.1193 per share (in the FMP profile's lastDividend field) implies approximately HK$83M of distribution in total. The yield at HK$2.85 is approximately 4.2%.

### Dividend Safety Score: 5/10

The safety score is middling rather than strong because of five offsetting factors:

**Positives (+)**:
- Net cash balance sheet (-) negative net debt of ~RMB 279M
- Low absolute payout ratio at FY2025 run-rate (41.6%)
- Free cash flow generation robust at ~RMB 240M+ annually
- No mandatory debt service or interest burden approaching dividend obligations

**Negatives (-)**:
- Short and erratic dividend history: no distributions in FY2018-19, sharply variable amounts since IPO
- No stated dividend policy or target payout ratio in public disclosures (typical of HKEX-listed Chinese healthcare but unsatisfying for a dividend investor)

- Concentrated founder ownership means dividend policy can pivot based on family/founder cash needs without minority shareholder input

A score of 5 (out of 10) reflects "dividend is being paid, is well-covered by cash flow, but is not a policy commitment and has been demonstrably volatile." A genuinely safe dividend (7+) requires a stated payout policy, a multi-year consistent pattern, and clear incentives for management to maintain the payment.

## PART VI -- COMPETITIVE POSITIONING AND MARKET ANALYSIS

### The Chinese Private Ophthalmology Market

The Chinese private ophthalmology market is estimated at RMB 150-200B in annual patient spending across all service categories, with a compound annual growth rate of approximately 10-12% through FY2019 before decelerating to 5-8% post-COVID and post-VBP. The market is structurally attractive because of three demographic and epidemiological tailwinds: (a) China has the highest myopia prevalence in the world, affecting approximately 600M people and still rising among children due to screen time and indoor lifestyles; (b) the aging population is driving sustained cataract and retinal disease volume; (c) rising middle-class incomes are expanding demand for premium lifestyle eye care (cosmetic refractive surgery, premium IOLs, presbyopia correction).

**Key Competitors:**

1. **Aier Eye Hospital Group (300015.SZ)**: The dominant listed competitor, with over 700 affiliated hospitals across China. Market cap in the range of RMB 100-150B, revenue RMB 20B+, national scale, aggressive acquisition program. Aier is a category leader with superior scale economies, national brand recognition, and deeper physician training infrastructure. Chaoju competes with Aier in Inner Mongolia, Shanxi, and Jiangsu; Aier's presence is broader but not universally dominant.
2. **Eye-Hospital (0028.HK) and HKE (various)**: Smaller HKEX-listed Chinese eye care chains with regional concentration. Similar operational model to Chaoju but typically smaller asset base.
3. **Public sector teaching and general hospitals**: Beijing Tongren Hospital, Zhongshan Ophthalmic Center (Guangzhou), and large provincial-level public hospitals dominate basic ophthalmic services for insurance-funded patients. Private operators like Chaoju capture the premium, elective, and optical retail segments.
4. **Regional specialists**: Various family-owned or PE-backed regional chains that compete at the provincial level. Some of these are consolidation targets for Aier.

**Chaoju's Competitive Positioning:**

**Strengths:**
- Regional market leadership in Inner Mongolia -- the company's historical heartland where it has been operating since 1988. This longevity in a second-tier province creates genuine switching costs (physician relationships, patient loyalty, government relations) that are hard to replicate by a national competitor.
- Integrated service model combining hospitals, clinics, and optical retail centers provides a funnel effect that monocline optical retailers cannot match.
- Balance sheet strength -- net cash position allows the company to wait out the current industry trough and potentially acquire distressed regional competitors if the opportunity arises. Most of Chaoju's regional private competitors are more leveraged and more vulnerable to the ongoing pricing pressure.

**Weaknesses:**
- Geographic concentration in northern and eastern China. No material exposure to high-growth southern and coastal markets where the premium refractive demand is largest.
- Sub-scale relative to Aier. Even a 17-hospital network cannot match the purchasing power, R&D spending, or brand marketing budget of a 700+ hospital competitor.
- No differentiated technology or clinical franchise. The ophthalmic procedures Chaoju offers are essentially commoditized; differentiation comes from patient experience, physician quality, and pricing -- all of which are hard to sustain over time.
- Limited ability to pass price increases onto insurance-funded patients. Private-pay (refractive, premium IOL, optical retail) is the only pricing-sensitive revenue segment, and this is under direct competitive pressure.

### Volume-Based Procurement (VBP) Impact

The VBP program introduced by China's National Healthcare Security Administration since 2018 has systematically reduced prices for medical devices and drugs through centralized bulk-purchasing agreements. For ophthalmology specifically, the relevant rounds have been:

- **Round 2 (late 2021)**: Intraocular lenses for cataract surgery - national-level price cuts of 50-70% for standard IOLs
- **Round 3 (2022-2023)**: Extended IOL categories, some premium IOL segments
- **Rounds 4-5 (2024-2025)**: Additional ophthalmic consumables (surgical instruments, visco-elastics, some corneal products); regional pilots for refractive consumables

The direct margin impact on Chaoju from VBP is modest-to-moderate because the cataract segment where VBP bites hardest represents approximately 20-30% of revenue and gross margin on VBP-subject IOLs has compressed from 60-70% to 40-50%. The indirect impact is larger: VBP signals that the Chinese regulatory framework is prepared to use state purchasing power to reduce healthcare costs across increasingly larger product categories, and the possibility that refractive surgery or premium IOLs could be drawn into a similar framework is a genuine tail risk.

**Quantified VBP sensitivity analysis (analyst estimate):**
- If VBP is extended to premium IOLs with 40% price cuts: estimated RMB 20-30M revenue impact, RMB 10-15M EBITDA impact
- If refractive surgery pricing comes under insurance pricing controls: estimated RMB 80-120M revenue impact, RMB 50-70M EBITDA impact
- If optical retail margins are targeted (unlikely but possible): estimated RMB 30-50M revenue impact, RMB 10-20M EBITDA impact

The cumulative worst-case scenario would reduce EBITDA by RMB 70-105M, or approximately 20-28% of current EBITDA. In this scenario, dividend coverage remains positive but the cushion narrows significantly.

### Anti-Corruption Campaign

The ongoing anti-corruption campaign in China's healthcare sector, which accelerated in mid-2023, has been a notable headwind for all Chinese healthcare operators. The campaign has focused on kickback relationships between pharmaceutical/device companies and hospital administrators, and has periodically extended to referral patterns between physicians and private clinics. For private ophthalmology operators, the direct impact has been limited (the campaign's primary targets are pharma rather than device-based specialties), but the indirect impact on physician behavior and administrative caution has reduced referral volumes and elective procedure throughput. Chaoju's annual report language suggests management has been monitoring the campaign's impact closely; the FY2025 revenue decline likely reflects some modest residual impact.

## PART X -- TAX AND STRUCTURAL CONSIDERATIONS

### Corporate Structure

Chaoju Eye Care Holdings Limited is incorporated in the Cayman Islands. The underlying Chinese operating entities are wholly-owned foreign enterprises (WFOEs) or Chinese-domestic-funded enterprises (CDFEs), depending on the specific legal structure of each hospital subsidiary. Some of the operating entities may use the Variable Interest Entity (VIE) structure common to offshore-listed Chinese companies, although for healthcare specifically, direct equity ownership via WFOEs is increasingly common. The Cayman holding company is listed on HKEX as an H-share-equivalent, meaning that while the legal form differs from a mainland-incorporated H-share, the economic substance is similar: mainland-generated profits flow through a Cayman vehicle to offshore shareholders.

### Withholding Tax on Dividends

**From PRC operating subsidiaries to Cayman holdco**: 10% withholding tax under the PRC Corporate Income Tax Law, reduced to 5% for qualifying beneficial owners (typically H-shares traded in HK with ultimate HK/Mainland ownership can qualify under CEPA). In Chaoju's case, the 10% rate is the baseline assumption.

**From Cayman holdco to shareholders**: Cayman Islands has no withholding tax on dividends paid to foreign shareholders. This is a neutral feature -- the Cayman level is pass-through.

**For non-Chinese offshore holders (e.g., a Chinese-resident investor holding via HKEX)**: dividends received are gross of the 10% PRC tax that was withheld at the subsidiary level. There is typically no additional HK or shareholder-country withholding on the HKEX-level distribution.

**For a US-based investor** (if structured through an IRA or taxable account holding HK-listed H-shares via a US brokerage): US tax treatment is typically as qualified dividends at favorable long-term capital gains rates (assuming holding period met), with foreign tax credit available for the 10% PRC withholding already suffered at the subsidiary level.

**For a China-resident investor**: dividends received from a Cayman-domiciled HKEX issuer are generally treated as foreign-source income. Mainland China residents may be subject to the 20% individual income tax on dividend income, with foreign tax credit potentially available for the 10% PRC withholding already applied at the subsidiary level (effectively a wash).

### Currency Considerations

The underlying business is fully RMB-denominated: revenue, costs, and most debt are in CNY. The Cayman holding company transacts in HKD for the HKEX listing. Dividends are declared and paid in HKD per-share amounts, with the underlying source in RMB subject to conversion at the prevailing rate. For offshore dividend investors, this creates a modest currency mismatch: if RMB depreciates vs HKD, the HKD-denominated dividend per share tends to be lower over time (all else equal) than it would be in a RMB-stable environment.

### Key Tax/Structural Risks

- **Mainland-Cayman profit repatriation friction**: the 10% PRC withholding is a baseline cost; additional constraints (quota limits, foreign exchange controls) apply episodically and can delay or reduce dividend distributions
- **H-share Stock Connect mechanisms**: Chaoju is available through HKEX and, for qualifying institutional investors, via the Shanghai-Hong Kong or Shenzhen-Hong Kong Stock Connect programs. This provides some demand floor from mainland investors looking for HK exposure
- **Potential delisting risk**: the ongoing US-China capital markets tensions have largely focused on US-listed Chinese ADRs rather than HK-listed H-shares, but investors should monitor for any policy shifts that would restrict offshore Chinese equities
- **ESG and sanctions risk**: Chinese healthcare operators have periodically been mentioned in sanctions/ESG discussions related to specific practices; Chaoju has no known exposure to controversial areas but monitoring is prudent
