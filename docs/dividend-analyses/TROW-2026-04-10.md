## PART I -- Executive Summary

T. Rowe Price Group is an 88-year-old Baltimore-headquartered pure-play active investment management firm with $1,775.6 billion in assets under management at year-end 2025. The company is a Dividend Aristocrat with 39 consecutive annual dividend increases since its 1986 IPO, most recently declaring $5.08 per share for fiscal 2025, representing a yield of approximately 5.5% at the reference price of roughly $92 per share. The payout ratio on GAAP earnings is approximately 55%, well within sustainable territory.

The single most important development over the past three years is the accelerating structural decline of TROW's equity franchise -- $212.3 billion in cumulative equity outflows from 2023 through 2025, with no sign of reversal. Equity AUM, which carries the firm's highest fee rates, has declined from 56.8% of total AUM at year-end 2022 to 49.5% at year-end 2025. This mix shift is compressing the effective fee rate, which fell from 41.9 basis points in 2023 to an annualized 38.8 basis points in Q4 2025 -- a loss of 3.1 basis points across the firm's $1.78 trillion base.

And yet, the investment case for income investors is compelling precisely because the market has already priced in terminal decline. At roughly 10x GAAP earnings and 9.5x non-GAAP earnings, TROW trades at the lowest sustained valuation in its public-company history. The balance sheet is a fortress: zero long-term corporate debt, $3.84 billion of cash and discretionary investments, and $7.60 billion of total Group-level cash and investments against an annual dividend cost of $1.14 billion. The target-date retirement franchise ($561.4 billion, 31.6% of AUM), which is effectively immune to passive displacement because TROW is the default allocation engine inside millions of 401(k) plans, provides a structural revenue floor. The OHA alternatives platform ($58.5 billion of AUM plus $21.6 billion of unfunded commitments) is an optionality layer that, if it scales, reprices the stock materially higher. The 2025 restructuring ($177.3 million charge, 4.7% headcount reduction) signals management is belatedly confronting the cost structure. The dividend growth rate has decelerated to 2-3% per annum, which is the correct calibration for a business in secular transition. At $92, you are buying a 5.5% yield on a balance sheet that can sustain the dividend from cash reserves alone for 3.4 years with zero future earnings.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield / Payout ratio / Streak: ~$92 / ~5.5% / ~55% GAAP / 39 years
- Last dividend hike: February 2025, $5.08/share (+2.4% from $4.96)
- 5y dividend CAGR: ~7.1% (2020-2025)
- 10y dividend CAGR: ~10.0% (2016-2025: $2.16 to $5.08)
- Net debt / EBITDA: Net cash position (negative net debt)
- Free cash flow coverage of dividend: 1.3x (2025)

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

T. Rowe Price Group, Inc. is a financial services holding company that provides global investment advisory services through its subsidiaries to investors worldwide. Founded in 1937 by Thomas Rowe Price, Jr., the intellectual father of growth-stock investing, the common stock has traded on the NASDAQ Global Select Market under the symbol TROW since the 2000 corporate restructuring.

The business model is elegantly simple: TROW charges investment advisory fees -- overwhelmingly based on AUM levels rather than performance -- and layers on administrative, distribution, and recordkeeping services. Nearly 55% of advisory fees come from sponsored U.S. mutual funds; the balance from collective investment trusts, subadvised accounts, separately managed accounts, ETFs, and international vehicles. There is essentially one product: investment management judgment, sold across different wrappers.

- **Sector / Industry:** Financial Services / Asset Management
- **Business segments:** Single reportable segment -- investment advisory services
- **Geographic exposure:** Americas >91% of AUM (U.S. domiciled); EMEA & APAC ~8.8%; 60 countries served
- **Top customers / customer concentration:** No single client represents a material concentration risk. Revenue split: ~55% from sponsored U.S. mutual funds, remainder from institutional and intermediary channels
- **Main competitors:** BlackRock (BLK), Franklin Templeton (BEN), Invesco (IVZ), Vanguard (private), Capital Group (private), Fidelity (private), MFS
- **Recent corporate actions:** OHA acquisition (December 2021, ~$4.2B); July 2025 restructuring (headcount -4.7%); Goldman Sachs strategic collaboration announced Q4 2025; First Abu Dhabi Bank partnership announced January 2026

## PART IV -- Dividend Deep Dive

### The Streak: 39 Consecutive Years of Increases

T. Rowe Price has raised its dividend every year since approximately 1986 (coinciding with its IPO). The 2025 declaration of $5.08 per share marked the 39th consecutive annual increase. At the reference price of roughly $92, the yield is approximately 5.5% -- among the highest in the company's modern history and well above the 10-year average yield of roughly 2.5-3.5%.

- **Current yield:** ~5.5%
- **Payout ratio (EPS basis):** ~55% GAAP ($5.08 / $9.24), ~52% non-GAAP ($5.08 / $9.72)
- **Payout ratio (FCF basis):** ~77% ($1,143M dividends / $1,480M FCF)
- **Dividend streak:** 39 years of uninterrupted growth
- **5y dividend CAGR:** ~7.1% (2020: $3.60 to 2025: $5.08)
- **10y dividend CAGR:** ~10.0% (2016: $2.16 to 2025: $5.08)
- **Last hike:** February 2025, +2.4% ($4.96 to $5.08)
- **Pattern of hikes:** Annual, declared in February. Growth decelerated from 18-20% (2019-2021) to 1.6-2.4% (2023-2025). No special or supplemental dividends in recent years.
- **Dividend coverage by FCF:** 1.3x (2025)
- **Currency of payment:** USD
- **Withholding tax for non-US holders:** Standard 30% (reduced to 10% under US-China treaty for China residents)

**Analysis of the trajectory:**

The dividend growth rate has decelerated sharply. From 2019-2021, TROW raised the dividend 18-20% annually as AUM surged with the post-COVID bull market and earnings exploded to $3.08B. The 2022 bear market slashed earnings to $1.56B while the dividend went to $4.80 -- creating a transient 70% payout ratio. Management responded rationally: they slowed dividend growth to 1.6-2.4% annually in 2023-2025, gradually normalizing the payout ratio back toward 55%.

The 2023 FCF scare deserves attention. FCF coverage dipped to 0.8x in 2023 ($910M FCF vs. $1,120M dividends) -- the first time in decades that free cash flow failed to cover the dividend. The cause: depressed operating cash flow from lower earnings (bear market hangover on AUM), elevated CapEx ($310M for the new Baltimore headquarters), and unfavorable working capital timing. Management responded by raising the dividend only 1.7% in 2023 -- the smallest increase in years. The recovery to 1.3x coverage in 2025 reflects lower CapEx ($274M as the HQ was completed) and modestly higher operating cash flow ($2,490M attributable to T. Rowe Price Group in 2025 vs. $2,314M in 2024 vs. $2,059M in 2023).

TROW's dividend growth rate is now anchored at 2-3% per annum. At current earnings power (~$9.25-9.75 non-GAAP EPS), the $5.08 DPS represents a 52-55% payout ratio with headroom for modest increases even if earnings stagnate. My estimate for the February 2027 declaration: $5.20/share (+2.4%), representing the 40th consecutive increase.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

The dividend is safe for the next 2-3 years under virtually any scenario. The fortress balance sheet ($3.38B cash, $7.60B total Group cash and investments, zero long-term corporate debt) provides a liquidity cushion that covers 3.0 years of dividends from cash alone and 6.7 years from total Group investments -- with zero future earnings assumed. Even in a severe recession that contracted AUM by 25-30% and compressed revenue to approximately $5.5B, management would eliminate buybacks ($624.6M of annual flexibility) and reduce discretionary spending long before touching the dividend. The Aristocrat status (39 years) is a powerful institutional incentive -- boards of directors and management teams almost never voluntarily surrender such a streak.

The longer-term risk (5+ year horizon) is real but manageable: if equity outflows accelerate to $100B+ annually and a sustained bear market contracts AUM simultaneously, the payout ratio would approach 80-90% and the streak would face genuine pressure. That scenario requires both persistent outflows and a multi-year market decline -- possible but not the base case.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

### The Passive Headwind -- Quantified

TROW's competitive position must be evaluated against the single most important structural trend in asset management: the relentless shift from active to passive. This is not a TROW-specific problem -- it is industry-wide -- but TROW is more exposed than most because it is a pure-play active manager with no index fund franchise.

**Investment performance (2025 10-K):**

| Period | % of Equity Composites Beating Benchmarks | % of All Funds Beating Morningstar Median | % of All Funds Beating Passive Peer Median |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Year | 17% | 49% | 39% |
| 3 Year | 28% | 56% | 47% |
| 5 Year | 26% | 46% | 43% |
| 10 Year | 45% | 61% | 48% |

These numbers are brutal for the equity franchise. Only 17% of equity composites beat their benchmarks over 1 year and 26% over 5 years. The passive peer comparison -- which is the metric institutional allocators actually use -- shows 39% outperforming over 1 year and 43% over 5 years. This is below random chance. However, AUM-weighted performance is materially better: 42% of fund AUM outperformed Morningstar median over 1 year and 72% over 3 years, reflecting the fact that TROW's large flagship funds (Blue Chip Growth, Capital Appreciation, Growth Stock) have stronger records than smaller strategies.

The apparent contradiction between poor composite performance and strong Morningstar ratings (60.4% of AUM in rated U.S. funds carried 4 or 5 stars at year-end 2025 vs. 32.5% for the Morningstar population) reflects survivorship bias (TROW merges or closes underperforming funds), the AUM-weighting effect, and the relative nature of Morningstar ratings (rated against other active managers, many of whom also underperform passive).

- **Moat type:** Switching costs (target-date/retirement), brand reputation, distribution scale
- **Moat trajectory:** Narrowing in equity; stable in retirement/multi-asset; widening in alternatives
- **Pricing power evidence:** Effective fee rate declined from 41.9 bps (2023) to 38.8 bps (Q4 2025) -- pricing power is eroding as mix shifts toward lower-fee vehicles and strategies
- **ROIC trend (5y):** Structural decline from the 2021 peak as OHA goodwill/intangibles dilute invested capital; still attractive in absolute terms given the asset-light model

### The Structural Moats

1. **Target-Date Retirement Franchise.** $561.4B of AUM (31.6% of total), with $5.2B of net inflows in 2025, $16.3B in 2024, and $13.1B in 2023. This is TROW's most defensible asset. Once a target-date suite is embedded as the qualified default investment alternative (QDIA) in a 401(k) plan, switching costs are enormous: plan sponsors must go through fiduciary review, participant communication, and blackout periods. The Secure 2.0 Act's auto-enrollment mandate is a secular tailwind. TROW is the largest fully active target-date provider, second only to Vanguard in total target-date AUM. The Goldman Sachs partnership to create public-private retirement date offerings is an important strategic evolution. The 2025 slowdown in target-date inflows ($5.2B vs. $16.3B in 2024) reflects Q4 losses (some M&A-driven, some competitive losses to passive/blend alternatives) that bear watching. On the Q4 earnings call, CEO Sharps noted that fully active target-date funds are losing share to passive and blend categories, but that TROW's blend offerings are gaining share in the fastest-growing segment.

2. **Brand and Reputation.** 88 years of continuous operation, consistently ranked among Fortune's Most Admired Companies and Newsweek's Greatest Companies. The T. Rowe Price brand connotes conservative professionalism -- this matters in the institutional channel where the cost of hiring the wrong manager is career risk for the allocator.

3. **OHA Alternatives Platform.** $58.5B of AUM plus $21.6B of unfunded commitments. Private credit, leveraged loans, mezzanine, structured products, stressed/distressed, CLOs, special situations, BDCs. OHA had a record fundraising year in 2025 with over $16 billion of capital raised across the platform, led by private lending strategies. Alternatives cannot be indexed, fee rates are higher (estimated 55-70 bps), and relationships are stickier.

4. **ETF Expansion.** 30 ETFs at year-end 2025 with over $21 billion AUM, having launched 13 ETFs in 2025. Net ETF inflows were $10.5 billion in 2025 including $1.8 billion in Q4 alone. The two new active core ETFs (U.S. and international) combining quantitative and fundamental research are designed to compete directly with passive.

5. **Distribution Breadth.** 5 channels (Americas intermediaries, EMEA/APAC intermediaries, direct U.S. investors, U.S. defined contribution, institutional), 60 countries. No single client represents a dangerous concentration. Assets under administration of $314 billion at year-end 2025, of which $178 billion are managed AUM.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

1. **Dividends:** $1,143.4M declared in 2025, representing ~77% of FCF and ~55% of GAAP net income. The dividend is the unquestioned top priority of capital allocation.

2. **Buybacks:** $624.6M in 2025 (6.175M shares at ~$101 avg), up from $334.5M in 2024. Total of 12.2M shares remaining under the December 2024 authorization. Opportunistic: management accelerated repurchases when the stock was cheap. Net of SBC dilution ($216.9M), true capital return via buybacks was approximately $407.7M.

3. **Capex:** $274.2M in 2025 (down from $423.4M in 2024 as the new Baltimore headquarters at 1307 Point Street was completed in early 2025). 2026 guidance: approximately $270M, with more than 75% for technology initiatives. The technology focus ($723.6M total technology, occupancy, and facility costs in 2025, up 12.3%) is unlikely to be cut -- AI, cloud, and digital capabilities require ongoing investment.

4. **M&A:** No new acquisitions in 2025. OHA ($4.2B, December 2021) remains the largest deal in TROW's history. Remaining OHA commitment: $287M of the original $500M pledge for product launches. The Goldman Sachs collaboration and First Abu Dhabi Bank partnership are partnership-based rather than acquisition-based -- a more capital-efficient approach.

5. **Debt paydown:** Not applicable -- there is no long-term corporate debt to pay down. The $21.8M of CLO repurchase agreements at year-end 2025 (down from $59.1M at year-end 2024) is collateralized financing embedded in the alternatives business.

**Capital allocation grade:** B+ -- The discipline is strong (zero corporate debt, consistent dividends, opportunistic buybacks), but the OHA acquisition at the top of the cycle ($4.2B, earnout now marked to zero) is a mark against management's deal-making judgment. The partnership-based approach (Goldman Sachs, FAB) is more sensible.

## PART VII -- Management Assessment

- **CEO:** Robert W. Sharps. Chair, CEO, and President since January 2022. Previously CIO and Head of Investments. Career TROW employee. Compensation: disclosed in proxy (see annual meeting filing). Sharps has been candid on earnings calls about the flow challenges, acknowledging that "flows in the fourth quarter were meaningfully softer than we anticipated, especially in the month of December."
- **CFO:** Jennifer Benson Dardis. Vice President, CFO, and Treasurer.
- **Head of Global Investments:** Eric Lanoue Veiel.
- **Insider ownership:** Common stock owned outright by associates and directors, combined with outstanding vested stock options and unvested restricted stock awards, total nearly 5% of outstanding stock at December 31, 2025 (6% at December 31, 2024). This is meaningful skin in the game for a company of this size.
- **Comp structure:** LTI plans include restricted stock units (average 5-year vesting), performance-based RSUs (3-year performance period + 2-year time-based), and restricted fund units (introduced 2024, allowing 50% of LTI in fund units). No stock options granted to employees since 2015.
- **Guidance track record:** Management does not provide formal EPS guidance, but does provide operating expense guidance. The 2025 non-GAAP operating expense growth of 3.4% came within the 2-4% guidance range. The 2026 guidance of 3-6% growth (from the $4,608M non-GAAP base) provides a realistic range. On the Q4 call, Sharps was forthright about the challenge: "the biggest factor in any single year on our operating margin is equity market return."

**Assessment:** Management is competent, transparent, and appropriately conservative. The restructuring was necessary but overdue -- it should have been initiated 12-18 months earlier. The strategic pivot toward ETFs, alternatives, blend target-date products, and global partnerships is directionally correct. The risk is execution: TROW must simultaneously defend its retirement franchise, grow alternatives, build an ETF business, and manage costs -- all while the core equity AUM erodes. This is a difficult balancing act.

## AUM DYNAMICS -- The Core Revenue Driver

Understanding TROW requires understanding AUM dynamics, because more than 90% of net revenue is a direct function of AUM levels and composition. Investment advisory fees of $6,602.3M in 2025 were earned on average AUM of $1,677.3B at an average annualized effective fee rate that declined throughout the year.

## OHA AND THE ALTERNATIVES PIVOT

The acquisition of Oak Hill Advisors in December 2021 was a $4.2B strategic bet that the future of T. Rowe Price lies in alternatives. Four-plus years in, the results are mixed but directionally promising.

**OHA by the numbers:**
- AUM: $58.5B at year-end 2025 (3.3% of total TROW AUM)
- Unfunded commitments: $21.6B (up from $16.2B at YE 2024, $11.6B at YE 2023) -- this represents future fee-generating AUM
- 2025 fundraising: record year with over $16 billion of capital raised, led by private lending strategies (per Q4 earnings call)
- Strategies: private credit, leveraged loans, mezzanine, real assets/CRE, structured products, stressed/distressed, CLOs, special situations, BDCs
- Capital allocation-based income (carried interest): $149.5M accrued in 2025 ($117.8M realized)
- Contingent consideration (earnout): marked to zero as of year-end 2025 -- earnout eliminated
- Acquisition-related amortization: $111.3M in 2025 (declining; remaining weighted avg amortization period: 2.8 years)
- TROW remaining commitment to OHA products: $287M of the original $500M pledge
- T. Rowe Price private equity fund: first close in January 2026, targeting ~25 category-leading private companies, leveraging an 18-year track record of investing over $24 billion across approximately 300 private companies

**The bearish read:** The earnout going to zero is a clear signal that OHA has underperformed management's acquisition-date expectations. At an estimated 55-70 bps effective fee rate on $58.5B of AUM, OHA generates roughly $350-400M of incremental advisory revenue -- approximately a 10% return on the $4.2B acquisition price before all the related costs. Total acquisition-related expenses (amortization + retention + impairments) have exceeded $400M cumulative since 2022.

**The bullish read:** OHA is a platform play. The unfunded commitments of $21.6B are a pipeline that converts to fee-generating AUM over the next 2-4 years, implying ultimate AUM of approximately $80B+ from existing vintages alone. The record $16B fundraising year in 2025 shows the platform is scaling. TROW's distribution network gives OHA access to institutional and retirement channels that a standalone alternatives firm could not easily reach. The private credit boom has structural tailwinds (banks retreating from mid-market lending, regulatory capital constraints). The carried interest layer ($117.8M realized in 2025) adds a performance-fee kicker above management fees. The remaining weighted average amortization period of 2.8 years means that by late 2028, the $111.3M annual acquisition-related amortization drag will largely cease -- creating a natural non-GAAP to GAAP earnings convergence.

## RESTRUCTURING AND COST MANAGEMENT

The $177.3M restructuring charge in 2025 is the first major expense restructuring in TROW's modern history and signals a recognition that the cost base is too high for the revenue trajectory.

**Components of the restructuring:**
- $127.3M: Accelerated depreciation and impairment of certain owned real estate (buildings to be disposed in 2026)
- ~$50.0M: Compensation-related costs, primarily severance (July 2025 workforce action)

**Headcount trajectory:**
- December 2024: 8,158 employees
- H1 2025 average: 8,089
- September 2025: 7,830 (post-workforce action)
- December 2025: 7,773

The approximately 385 headcount reduction (4.7% of the workforce) is meaningful but modest. Management is also outsourcing certain technology capabilities through vendor partnerships and plans to dispose of owned buildings in 2026. Key organizational changes include: creation of the technology, data, and operations function to integrate digital capabilities, and the global strategy function to sharpen strategic vision.

**2026 expense guidance (from Q4 2025 earnings call):** Non-GAAP operating expenses (excluding carried interest compensation) expected to grow 3-6% from the 2025 base of $4,608.0M, implying $4,746-4,884M. CFO Dardis broke this into: (1) controllable expenses (two-thirds of total) managed toward low single-digit growth, balanced between cost savings and investment in growth areas (ETFs, SMAs, models, alternatives, partnerships, advice), and (2) market-driven expenses (one-third) growing faster due to distribution costs rising with AUM and year-end compensation accounting.

**The margin problem:** TROW's non-GAAP operating margin declined from 39.3% (2024) to 37.2% (2025) despite the restructuring. Revenue growth of approximately 3.1% in 2025 was barely outpacing non-GAAP expense growth of approximately 3.7%. The fundamental challenge: technology spending ($723.6M in 2025, up 12.3%) is the fastest-growing cost line and unlikely to be cut -- AI, cloud, and digital investments are table stakes. Distribution and servicing costs ($383.5M, up 8.3%) rise mechanically with intermediary-distributed AUM. Only compensation (up 1.6%) showed restraint, partly because of the July 2025 workforce action.

## PART X -- Risks & Stress Tests

### Risks to the Dividend (ordered by severity)

1. **Prolonged bear market (HIGH IMPACT, MEDIUM PROBABILITY).** A 30%+ decline in equity markets would reduce AUM to approximately $1.2-1.3T and revenue to approximately $5.0-5.5B. At the current $1.14B annual dividend run-rate, the payout ratio would exceed 80%. Management would first eliminate buybacks ($624.6M of flexibility) and could sustain the dividend from cash reserves for 2-3 years, but a multi-year bear market would force a cut. The 2022 stress test is instructive: AUM fell 24.4% ($1,688B to $1,275B), revenue fell 15.4%, and the payout ratio spiked to 70% -- painful but survivable because the bear market lasted only one year.

2. **Accelerating equity outflows (MEDIUM-HIGH IMPACT, HIGH PROBABILITY).** Equity outflows of $74.9B in 2025 were worse than 2024's $52.0B, despite improving investment performance in certain flagship strategies. If outflows accelerate to $100B+ annually, the fee rate compression and revenue impact would be severe even in a flat market. This is the most likely path to dividend stress. On the Q4 earnings call, management noted that December 2025 was "meaningfully softer than anticipated" with "a handful of institutional losses and some rebalancing."

3. **Fee pressure intensification (MEDIUM IMPACT, HIGH PROBABILITY).** The effective fee rate declined from 41.9 bps (2023) to 38.8 bps (Q4 2025) -- a 3.1 bps decline in approximately two years. Every basis point of fee decline on $1.78T of AUM equals approximately $178M of annual revenue loss. The 3.1 bps decline represents roughly $550M of annual revenue erosion, partially offset by AUM growth. CFO Dardis noted on the Q4 call that "client demand increasingly shifts toward lower-priced vehicles and strategies."

4. **OHA underperformance (MEDIUM IMPACT, MEDIUM PROBABILITY).** The earnout going to zero is a confirmed underperformance signal. Total acquisition-related costs (amortization + retention + impairments) have exceeded $400M cumulative since 2022. If fundraising stalls or credit markets deteriorate, the alternatives thesis fails and TROW is left as a pure-play traditional active manager in a passive world.

5. **Target-date franchise erosion (MEDIUM IMPACT, LOW-MEDIUM PROBABILITY).** The 2025 slowdown in target-date net inflows ($5.2B vs. $16.3B in 2024) and Q4 net outflows are a new concern. Fully active target-date funds are losing share to passive and blend alternatives. TROW's blend offerings are growing but may not fully offset the active-to-blend migration within its own franchise. Any loss of a major plan sponsor relationship could represent billions in AUM outflows.

6. **Regulatory risk (LOW-MEDIUM IMPACT, LOW-MEDIUM PROBABILITY).** DOL fiduciary rules, SEC marketing regulations, and potential changes to retirement plan regulations (including possible passive mandates in QDIA designations or new rules around private alternatives in DC plans) could affect TROW's retirement business.

## PART XI -- Thesis & Verdict

**Thesis (one paragraph):**

T. Rowe Price is a once-great active management franchise in the early stages of a multi-decade structural transition. The equity franchise that built the firm is in secular decline, hemorrhaging assets at $50-75B per year as the passive revolution consumes active management market share. But the target-date retirement franchise ($561B) is a genuinely defensible annuity stream, the OHA alternatives platform provides real optionality, the ETF business is scaling rapidly ($21B AUM, $10.5B net inflows in 2025), and the zero-debt balance sheet provides a multi-year liquidity cushion that makes dividend cuts virtually impossible in any reasonable scenario. At 10x earnings and 5.5% yield, the market is pricing in terminal decline of the franchise. If the reality is merely slow attrition offset by target-date growth, alternatives scaling, and ETF inflows -- which is the base case -- the stock reprices meaningfully higher over time. This is not a growth compounder; it is a high-yield income vehicle with a clean balance sheet and a franchise in transition.

**Verdict:** ACCUMULATE below $88. HOLD at $92. Do not chase above $105.

**Position sizing:** 2-3% of a dividend portfolio. This is not a core holding -- the flow trajectory introduces more uncertainty than a typical Aristocrat. But the yield, balance sheet, and valuation make it attractive as an income vehicle.

**Monitoring checklist:**
