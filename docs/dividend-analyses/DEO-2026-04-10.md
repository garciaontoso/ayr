## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE below $75. HOLD at $77.46. -- Dividend Safety: 7/10 -- Confidence: Medium-High**

Diageo is the world's largest international spirits company by a wide margin -- 1.4x larger than its nearest competitor by retail sales value, with 13 billion-dollar brands sold in approximately 180 countries -- and the stock is now trading at what appears to be a generational discount. At $77.46 per ADR, the shares are down roughly 45% from their 2022 highs near $140 and sit at levels not seen since 2018. The question is not whether this is a great business; it is whether the dividend is safe during a cyclical trough, whether the new management team can reignite growth, and whether the stock has further to fall before the turnaround materializes.

On dividend safety, the picture is mixed but ultimately reassuring. Management held the dividend flat at 103.48 cents per share for FY2025 (year ended June 30, 2025) -- the first freeze in over two decades. That decision was deliberate, not forced: free cash flow of $2.75B covers the implied dividend outflow comfortably, and the Accelerate program targets $3B+ of sustainable annual FCF from FY2026 onward. The payout ratio on reported EPS of $4.24 (distorted by $1.37B of exceptional impairment charges) looks alarming at ~97%; but the adjusted payout on EPS-before-exceptionals of $6.58 (164.2c per ordinary share, or ~$6.58 per ADR at 4:1 ratio) is a far more reasonable 63%. The dividend freeze was prudent capital allocation during a transition, not a signal of fundamental distress.

The harder question is what comes next. Debra Crew departed as CEO in July 2025, leaving Nik Jhangiani (the former CFO, with less than a year in that role) as Interim CEO while a search for a permanent appointment proceeds. The board has also launched the Accelerate restructuring program targeting $625M of cost savings over three years and a return to the leverage target of 2.5-3.0x net debt/EBITDA by FY2028 (from ~3.2x currently). Net debt stands at $21.85B -- not dangerous for a business of this quality, but uncomfortably high for a company whose organic growth was only 1.7% in FY2025 and whose ROIC declined from 15.8% to 13.7%. The bull case requires either an acceleration in organic revenue growth (driven by tequila, Guinness, and emerging markets) or multiple re-rating as the market recognizes the structural undervaluation. Both are plausible on a 2-3 year horizon.

The FMP DCF output of $500.19 per share is laughably high and should be disregarded -- it likely reflects a currency or share-class mismatch. The sell-side consensus target of $124 per ADR implies approximately 60% upside, which is more reasonable but requires meaningful earnings recovery. At 18x normalized earnings and ~3.3% dividend yield, the risk/reward is attractive for patient income investors, but the near-term catalysts are uncertain.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**
1. **Portfolio quality is genuinely unmatched in spirits.** 13 billion-dollar brands, #1 position in international spirits, #1 in tequila globally (Don Julio grew 41.9% in FY2025 US), #1 in scotch, and Guinness is now the fastest-growing major beer brand in the US on-trade. This is not a one-brand company -- the portfolio breadth provides resilience across categories and geographies.
2. **Cyclical trough in premium spirits creates a buying window.** Consumer wallet pressure, post-COVID normalization, and China weakness have compressed margins and sentiment. But spirits per-capita consumption has been broadly stable (CAGR -0.4% 2009-2024), while premiumization has driven premium-and-above from 26% to 35% of category value over ten years. The structural tailwinds have not reversed -- the cycle has.
3. **Accelerate program creates a credible path to margin recovery.** $625M of cost savings over three years, $3B+ FCF target from FY2026, capex declining from 7.7% of net sales to mid-single-digits, and selective disposals (Guinness Nigeria, Guinness Ghana, Ciroc North America, Distill Ventures) all point toward a leaner, more cash-generative business by FY2028.

**Top 3 reasons not to add at current price:**
1. **No permanent CEO and the talent bench is thin.** Jhangiani has been at Diageo for less than a year. The CFO chair is now occupied by an interim appointee (Deirdre Mahlan, a returning veteran). The leadership vacuum is real and the search for a permanent CEO introduces uncertainty about strategic direction.
2. **GLP-1 overhang is unresolved.** The board acknowledges monitoring weight-loss drugs as a potential headwind. Their own research says cannabis and GLP-1s have "not yet shown significant disruption to spirits consumption" -- but the operative word is "yet." If Ozempic/Wegovy reduce alcohol consumption by even 3-5% among heavy users, the volume impact on premium spirits could be meaningful.
3. **Leverage is elevated with limited buyback support.** Net debt of $21.85B against $6.7B of adjusted EBITDA implies ~3.25x leverage. Share buybacks were zero in FY2025 (vs $1.67B in FY2024). Until leverage declines below 2.8x, capital return will be limited to dividends only.

**Next 12 months catalysts (high-probability):**
- **Permanent CEO announcement** (expected H1 FY2026, i.e., before December 2025)
- **FY2026 results showing first full year of Accelerate benefits** (capex reduction, cost savings dropping to bottom line)
- **Tariff resolution or mitigation clarity** (UK/EU imports to US, estimated $200M annualized impact pre-mitigation)
- **FIFA 2026 World Cup activation** (Diageo is official spirits partner in the Americas -- major brand-building event)
- **Dividend decision for FY2026** (likely modest increase to signal progressive policy resumption)

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Diageo plc is a UK-incorporated, London-headquartered global leader in beverage alcohol. The company was formed in 1997 through the merger of Grand Metropolitan and Guinness plc and traces its corporate lineage to Arthur Guinness Son and Company Limited, incorporated in 1886. ADRs trade on the NYSE under the ticker DEO; each ADR represents four ordinary shares. As of June 30, 2025, there were 2.432 billion ordinary shares outstanding.

The portfolio spans scotch whisky (Johnnie Walker, Buchanan's, J&B), vodka (Smirnoff, Ciroc, Ketel One), tequila (Don Julio, Casamigos, Lobos 1707), rum (Captain Morgan), gin (Tanqueray, Gordon's), liqueur (Baileys), beer (Guinness), Canadian whisky (Crown Royal), American whiskey (Bulleit), and a growing non-alcoholic range (Seedlip, Guinness 0.0, Ritual Zero Proof). Diageo is the #1 international spirits company by retail sales value (IWSR 2024), the #3 player in total beverage alcohol globally, and the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits player (4x larger than the nearest competitor).

**Key market dynamics and industry context:**

The total beverage alcohol (TBA) market exhibits several long-term tailwinds that underpin Diageo's investment case: a growing legal-purchase-age (LPA+) population (600 million new LPA+ consumers expected by 2035), rising middle class in emerging markets, premiumization (consumers drinking better, not more), spirits gaining share from beer and wine, and increasing spirits household penetration in the US. Against these tailwinds, the near-term headwinds include pressured consumer wallets (particularly in the US and China), cannabis legalization in certain markets, GLP-1 weight-loss drug adoption, and Gen Z consumption patterns that skew toward moderation. Diageo's board and management explicitly monitor all of these factors. Their proprietary survey of 21 markets (cited in the FY2025 20-F) provides real-time consumer sentiment data that informs brand investment and activation decisions.

Notably, per-capita international spirits consumption (excluding baijiu and RTDs) has remained broadly stable over the past 15 years (CAGR -0.4% 2009-2024), even as total beverage alcohol per-capita consumption declined by 1.2% over the same period. This means spirits are gaining share of a moderating market -- a structural advantage for a spirits-led portfolio company like Diageo.

## PART III -- HISTORICAL REVIEW: 10-YEAR STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 -- The Ivan Menezes Era: Premiumization and Portfolio Curation (2013-2023).** Ivan Menezes became CEO in July 2013 and drove a decade-long premiumization strategy that reshaped Diageo from a volume-driven spirits conglomerate into a price/mix-led portfolio company. Key moves included acquiring Don Julio (2014, full control), Casamigos (2017, ~$1B), Aviation American Gin (2020, $610M), and Chase Distillery. Tequila was identified early as the fastest-growing spirits category, and Diageo's bet was prescient: tequila organic net sales grew 18% in FY2025 alone. During this era, the dividend grew every year, the payout ratio remained below 70%, and share buybacks averaged approximately $1.5-2.0B annually. Revenue grew from roughly $12.7B (2017) to $20.6B (2023), driven by both organic growth and FX tailwinds. Operating margins hovered in the 28-30% range on an organic basis.

**Phase 2 -- Post-COVID Normalization and Inventory Correction (2023-2024).** The FY2023 hangover was severe. During COVID, consumers traded up aggressively and channel-stuffed pantries; the subsequent normalization hit premium spirits disproportionately. North American distributor inventory destocking, China economic weakness, and Latin American volatility combined to produce the first meaningful organic revenue decline in years. Reported net sales peaked at $20.6B in FY2023, then declined to $20.3B in FY2024 with organic operating profit falling. Management issued a rare profit warning in January 2024. The stock began its decline from the $140s toward the $80s.

**Phase 3 -- Leadership Transition and Accelerate (2024-2025).** Debra Crew, who had been elevated to CEO in June 2023 after Menezes's departure, left in July 2025 after roughly two years at the helm. Her tenure saw the worst TSR period in Diageo's modern history (-24% in FY2025 alone). But it also saw the launch of Accelerate -- the most ambitious restructuring program in Diageo's history. The Ciroc North America stake was exchanged for global Lobos 1707 Tequila ownership (an elegant asset swap). Guinness Nigeria and Guinness Ghana were divested as part of an Africa asset-light pivot. Distill Ventures was wound down with a $458M impairment. Aviation American Gin took a $231M impairment write-down. The total exceptional charge in FY2025 was $1.37B -- the cost of portfolio rationalization. The strategic logic is sound: concentrate resources on the brands that can drive above-market growth (Don Julio, Guinness, Crown Royal, Buchanan's, Johnnie Walker premium expressions) and cut the tail.

## PART VI -- BRAND-BY-BRAND PERFORMANCE REVIEW (FY2025)

Understanding Diageo requires understanding the individual brand trajectories, because the portfolio is the moat.

**Don Julio (Tequila) -- THE STAR:** Net sales grew 41.9% in US Spirits, making it the single largest growth driver in the portfolio. Don Julio Reposado was the standout variant. The brand gained both spirits industry and tequila category share. Shipments grew ahead of depletions growth of 36% as distributors replenished inventory to accommodate strong consumer demand. Don Julio 1942 launched its first-ever global product collaboration (with DJ Peggy Gou) and sold out at events across the US, Asia Pacific, and Europe. Diageo is the #1 tequila player globally, and the broader tequila category grew 18% organically in FY2025. This is the franchise's growth engine.

**Guinness (Beer) -- THE BREAKOUT:** Guinness has added over 3.8 million new LPA+ (legal purchase age) drinkers since 2019. It became the #1 beer in football occasions in Great Britain. In the US, it was the fastest-growing major beer brand in the on-trade by volume in H2 FY2025, and the #1 draft beer in major metropolitan areas (Boston, New York). Guinness 0.0 became Great Britain's #1 non-alcoholic beer. Guinness has expanded to 88 markets. The "A Lovely Day" US campaign received 474 million impressions. Diageo Beer Company USA net sales grew 4.8%. Guinness is no longer just an Irish stout -- it is becoming a global lifestyle brand, and the US represents a massive runway.

**Crown Royal (Canadian Whisky):** Net sales grew 3.8% in the US, driven by continued strong consumer demand for Crown Royal Blackberry (launched H2 FY2024). The innovation supported recruitment of consumers into spirits, the category, and the Crown Royal trademark. This is a solid, mid-single-digit grower with strong brand equity.

**Johnnie Walker (Scotch):** US net sales declined 10.6% due to overall scotch category weakness. However, the Johnnie Walker trademark gained share of the scotch category and held share of total spirits, led by Black Label and Red Label. Johnnie Walker Black Ruby launched in Latin America with 72% of volume sourced from outside scotch -- demonstrating the ability to recruit non-scotch drinkers. The global rollout of Black Ruby is underway. Scotch is cyclical; Johnnie Walker's brand equity is structural.

**Smirnoff (Vodka):** US net sales declined 4.5% due to RTD format competition and overall category weakness. Smirnoff lost category share, though Ketel One gained. Vodka is a declining category in the US relative to tequila and whiskey; Smirnoff's value positioning makes it vulnerable to trade-down pressure.

**Captain Morgan (Rum):** US net sales declined 9.3% due to rum category weakness. The primary variant, Captain Morgan Original Spiced, gained share of the category despite the overall decline. Rum remains a challenged category.

**Casamigos (Tequila):** US net sales declined 18% as increased category competition drove lower demand. This is the underperformer within the tequila portfolio -- the George Clooney-founded brand has faced margin pressure and brand fatigue. The Ciroc-for-Lobos swap suggests management is rationalizing the tequila portfolio around Don Julio as the primary growth platform.

## PART VII -- COMPETITIVE POSITIONING AND MOAT

Diageo's economic moat is wide and durable, built on three pillars:

1. **Brand portfolio breadth.** No other spirits company has 13 billion-dollar brands across scotch, tequila, vodka, beer, rum, gin, and liqueur. Pernod Ricard is the nearest competitor at roughly 70% of Diageo's international spirits revenue. Brown-Forman, Beam Suntory, and Bacardi are all significantly smaller. This breadth allows Diageo to capture share regardless of which category is trending -- when vodka declines, tequila accelerates; when scotch softens, Guinness surges.

2. **Distribution network and route-to-market.** Selling in ~180 countries with 110+ manufacturing sites creates enormous barriers to entry. A new spirits brand can achieve regional distribution; global distribution at Diageo's scale is essentially unreplicable. Diageo operates five different route-to-consumer models: wholesalers/distributors (including US three-tier and Canadian control boards), modern trade (direct to multi-outlet retailers), eMarketplace (third-party digital platforms), direct-to-consumer (Thebar.com and similar), and direct-to-store (e.g., Ireland beer distribution). This multi-model approach ensures coverage across all retail channels.

3. **Aging inventory as a balance sheet asset.** Scotch whisky, bourbon, and tequila require years of maturation. Diageo's maturing stock inventory (valued at billions on the balance sheet) represents both a competitive barrier and a future profit stream. No competitor can accelerate this -- time is the moat. The Scotch Intelligence Platform (SIP), deployed in FY2025, uses AI and data to optimize pre-bottling allocations, maturation yields (targeting the "angel's share"), and cask-to-bottle logistics -- extracting incremental value from the aging inventory.

4. **Non-alcoholic spirits leadership.** Diageo is the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits player, more than 4x larger than any competitor (IWSR 2024). The portfolio includes Seedlip, Ritual Zero Proof (acquired FY2025, #1 non-alcoholic spirits brand in the US), Guinness 0.0, Tanqueray 0.0, Gordon's 0.0, and Captain Morgan 0.0. The non-alcoholic portfolio grew approximately 40% in FY2025. This positions Diageo uniquely to benefit from the moderation trend rather than be victimized by it -- a structural hedge that no competitor can match at similar scale.

Against Pernod Ricard (largest peer), Diageo wins on tequila (Don Julio vs Pernod's weak tequila portfolio), beer (Guinness has no Pernod equivalent), North American breadth (Crown Royal, Bulleit, Captain Morgan), and non-alcoholic spirits. Pernod wins on cognac (Martell), Indian whisky (Royal Stag), and arguably gin (Pernod's portfolio is competitive). Both are well-positioned for premiumization, but Diageo's category diversity provides superior resilience during category rotations.

Against Brown-Forman (Jack Daniel's): Brown-Forman is essentially a one-brand company (Jack Daniel's represents approximately 60% of sales). Diageo's diversification advantage is overwhelming, though Brown-Forman's Tennessee whiskey position is entrenched and well-defended.

Against Beam Suntory (Jim Beam, Maker's Mark, Japanese whisky): Beam has strengths in bourbon and Japanese whisky but lacks Diageo's tequila, beer, and vodka positions. Diageo's 4.5% share of global TBA (IWSR 2024) compares to substantially smaller shares for all peers except Pernod.

## PART VIII -- MANAGEMENT AND GOVERNANCE

The current leadership situation is suboptimal. Debra Crew departed as CEO in July 2025 after two years. Nik Jhangiani serves as Interim CEO; he joined as CFO in September 2024 with 30 years of finance experience (Coca-Cola system, consumer goods), but has less than 12 months of Diageo tenure. Deirdre Mahlan has returned as Interim CFO -- she is a 27-year Diageo veteran who previously served as CFO and North America president. Sir John Manzoni became Chair in February 2025 after three years as a Non-Executive Director.

The board has initiated a comprehensive CEO search including internal and external candidates. The search outcome is the single largest near-term unknown for equity holders. History suggests Diageo prefers internal appointments (Menezes was a lifer, Crew was internal), but the current bench is thin following multiple Executive Committee changes in the past 18 months.

Capital allocation priorities for FY2026 are clear: (1) deleveraging toward 2.5-3.0x target, (2) maintaining progressive dividend policy, (3) selective disposals of non-core assets, (4) reduced CapEx. Share buybacks are off the table until leverage improves. This is the right sequencing.

## PART IX -- RISK FACTORS

1. **CEO search and leadership transition risk.** A prolonged vacancy or a poor external hire could derail the Accelerate program momentum and reset strategy. This is a 12-month risk window.

2. **Tariff exposure.** UK and European imports to the US face tariffs. Diageo estimates the unmitigated annualized impact at approximately $200M. Mitigation (supply chain re-routing, inventory management) is expected to offset roughly half. The remainder will compress margins unless passed through in pricing.

3. **China economic weakness.** Greater China has been a drag on Asia Pacific results for two consecutive years. Consumer downtrading and competitive pressure on scotch are structural until the macro environment improves.

4. **GLP-1 and moderation headwinds.** While Diageo's own research shows limited disruption to date, the long-term impact of GLP-1 drugs on alcohol consumption is genuinely unknowable. Diageo's position in non-alcoholic spirits (world's largest) is a partial hedge, but the core business remains fundamentally tied to alcohol consumption.

5. **FX volatility.** Diageo reports in GBP, earns in 50+ currencies, and pays ADR dividends in USD. A strengthening GBP reduces reported USD earnings; a weakening GBP inflates reported debt. FX movements added $119M of headwind to FY2025 reported revenue.

6. **Moet Hennessy associate income decline.** Diageo holds a 34% stake in Moet Hennessy (LVMH wines and spirits). Associate income declined 53.4% to $193M in FY2025 from $414M in FY2024, driven by the luxury slowdown. This directly reduces EPS with no corresponding cash flow from the holding.

## PART XI -- TAX CONSIDERATIONS FOR US INVESTORS

DEO ADRs are subject to UK withholding tax on dividends. For US tax residents, the UK-US tax treaty generally provides for a 15% withholding rate on dividends (reduced from the standard 20% UK rate). US investors can claim a foreign tax credit on their US tax return for UK withholding tax paid. Dividends are generally treated as qualified dividend income for US federal tax purposes, eligible for preferential tax rates for qualifying US holders.

For the user's specific situation (China tax resident), the UK-China tax treaty should be consulted for applicable withholding rates on UK-source dividends. The standard UK rate for non-treaty countries is 20% (for non-individual shareholders) or the treaty-reduced rate.
