## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE below $80. HOLD at $77. DO NOT SELL. Dividend Safety: 6/10. Confidence: Medium.**

Diageo is the world's largest international spirits company -- 1.4x larger than the nearest competitor by retail sales value, 13 billion-dollar brands, sales in ~180 countries -- trading at $77.46 per ADR, down 45% from its 2022 peak near $140. The central question: is this a generational buying opportunity or a value trap masquerading as a discount?

The answer is nuanced but leans bullish on a 2-3 year horizon. Three facts dominate the analysis:

First, the FY2025 earnings collapse is almost entirely explained by $1,589M of exceptional items ($1,369M operating + $220M non-operating) versus net $14M of exceptional charges in FY2024 -- a $1,575M year-on-year swing that accounts for substantially all of the net income decline from $3,870M to $2,354M. Strip the exceptionals, and underlying EPS declined a much more modest 8.6% (from 179.6c to 164.2c per ordinary share) driven by Moet Hennessy's 53% associate income drop and FX headwinds, not by operating deterioration. The $1.4B of write-downs looks like kitchen-sink cleanup: Distill Ventures exit ($458M), Aviation Gin write-down ($231M), Accelerate restructuring ($225M), France distribution change ($145M), and African disposals ($239M). These are discrete, identifiable portfolio rationalization events, not recurring impairments.

Second, the dividend freeze at 103.48 cents per share broke a 24-year streak of consecutive annual increases (every year since 2001, per the FY2022 20-F). This is psychologically damaging to income investors but was prudent: free cash flow of $2,748M covers the ~$2,298M dividend obligation at 1.20x, and Diageo's own target dividend cover range is 1.8-2.2x (actual FY2025: 1.6x before exceptionals). The freeze bought breathing room during a transition year. The probability of a dividend cut within 3 years is low (10-15%); the probability of resuming growth within 12 months is high (70-75%).

Third, there is no permanent CEO. Debra Crew departed July 2025 after ~2 years. Nik Jhangiani (CFO since September 2024, ex-Coca-Cola) serves as Interim CEO. Deirdre Mahlan (27-year Diageo veteran, former CFO) returned as Interim CFO. The board is conducting a "comprehensive search" for a permanent appointment. This is the single largest risk factor -- a company executing a major restructuring (Accelerate) needs a permanent leader. Previous CEO transitions at Diageo were planned: Menezes succeeded Walsh in 2013 after a deliberate succession. Crew's departure was not planned; the 20-F's diplomatic language ("stepped down") and the Chair's thank-you are the hallmarks of a forced exit after poor TSR (-24% in FY2025 alone).

**Should you sell $53,400 of DEO?** No. At 5.3% yield, 18x normalized earnings, and 30-40% below fair value, selling here crystallizes a loss at the worst possible time in the cycle. The right action is to hold and selectively add on weakness below $75.

**Top 3 reasons to OWN:**
1. **Don Julio + Guinness are two of the strongest growth stories in global beverages.** Don Julio US net sales +41.9%, tequila category +18%, Guinness adding 3.8M new drinkers since 2019, fastest-growing major US beer brand. These are not cyclical blips -- they are multi-year structural winners. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 715-741)
2. **$1.4B kitchen sink means FY2026 comparisons are easy.** Exceptional items normalize from $1,589M to near-zero, creating a 30%+ mechanical EPS rebound even on flat underlying performance. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2164-2186)
3. **Accelerate programme targets $3B sustainable FCF and $625M cost savings** -- credible given capex declining from 7.7% to mid-single-digits of net sales and demonstrated A&P development cost reduction (21% to 14% via AI-enabled content). (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 786-802)

**Top 3 reasons NOT TO ADD:**
1. **No permanent CEO during the most consequential restructuring in company history.** Jhangiani has <12 months of Diageo tenure. The search timeline is undefined beyond "comprehensive."
2. **Net debt $21.9B at 3.2x adjusted EBITDA** with zero buybacks and limited flexibility. Target range is 2.5-3.0x "no later than fiscal 28" -- three years away.
3. **Moet Hennessy income collapsed 53% to $193M** with no clear recovery catalyst, and China/Travel Retail remain weak (Asia Pacific organic net sales -3.2%).

**12-month catalysts:** Permanent CEO appointment (expected H1 FY2026). FY2026 results showing Accelerate cost savings. FIFA 2026 World Cup activation (Diageo is official spirits partner in the Americas). Dividend raise to signal progressive policy resumption. Tariff clarity.

Key acquisitions: Don Julio (full control 2014), Casamigos (~$1B, 2017), Aviation American Gin ($610M, 2020). Revenue grew from ~$12B to $20.6B. Dividend increased every year. **Phase 2 -- Post-COVID Normalization (2023-2024):** COVID pantry-loading and premiumization created artificially elevated demand. The normalization hit FY2024 hardest: US distributor destocking, China weakness, and Latin American volatility combined with the first profit warning in January 2024. Operating margins compressed. Share price declined from $140s toward $80s.

**Phase 3 -- Crew Departure and Accelerate (2024-2025):** Crew's two-year tenure produced the worst TSR period in Diageo's modern history (-24% in FY2025). The board forced a change. Simultaneously, Accelerate was launched -- the most ambitious restructuring in Diageo's history -- with $625M cost savings over three years and a $3B sustainable FCF target. Portfolio rationalization included Distill Ventures exit, Aviation write-down, Ciroc-for-Lobos swap, and African disposals.

**CEO transition timeline:**
- Paul Walsh: CEO 2000-2013 (13 years, planned succession)
- Ivan Menezes: CEO July 2013 - June 2023 (~10 years, planned retirement, died July 2023)
- Debra Crew: CEO June 2023 - July 2025 (~2 years, forced departure)
- Nik Jhangiani: Interim CEO from July 2025

This is the first unplanned CEO transition in Diageo's post-merger history. The last time Diageo had no permanent CEO was effectively never -- Walsh to Menezes and even Menezes to Crew were managed handoffs (Crew was already on the board and designated successor).

## PART VI -- FY2025 EXCEPTIONAL ITEMS: KITCHEN SINK OR STRUCTURAL DISTRESS?

This is the make-or-break analytical section. The $1,589M total exceptional charge ($1,369M operating + $220M non-operating) versus $14M net charges in FY2024 ($56M operating gain - $70M non-operating loss) represents a $1,575M year-on-year swing. This single line item explains virtually all of the net income decline from $3,870M to $2,354M.

**Assessment: This is a Kitchen Sink.**

Evidence supporting one-time nature:
1. **Distill Ventures ($458M):** Strategic decision to exit the venture capital / accelerator programme entirely. Diageo "moved forward with a reduced number of investments within Distill Ventures and to no longer bring in any new brands through the programme." (DEO FY2025 20-F, line 748). This is a final write-down, not the beginning of a cycle. Distill Ventures was an Menezes-era experiment; it failed; the new team is cleaning it up.

2. **Aviation American Gin ($231M):** The Ryan Reynolds-founded brand, acquired for $610M in 2020, has now been written down by $231M (38% of acquisition cost). This is a clear M&A failure -- Aviation never achieved the scale to justify the premium. But the write-down likely brings the carrying value close to fair value. Further impairment is possible but the bulk of the loss is now recognized.

3. **Accelerate restructuring ($225M):** First-year charges for a 3-year programme targeting $625M in savings. Expect $150-200M of additional charges over FY2026-2027, declining each year. This is investment, not loss.

4. **France distribution ($145M):** One-time cost of transitioning from Moet Hennessy joint venture distribution to Diageo-owned distribution in France. Completed. Non-recurring.

5. **Africa disposals ($239M):** Guinness Nigeria and Ghana were sold as part of the strategic shift to asset-light Africa. These are deliberate portfolio decisions, not distressed sales.

**Historical impairment track record:** FY2024 actually had a $224M net impairment *reversal* (Shui Jing Fang). FY2023 had exceptional charges primarily related to supply chain agility. FY2020 saw COVID-related impairments. There is no pattern of serial impairments at Diageo -- FY2025 is genuinely anomalous, which supports the kitchen-sink thesis.

**What this means for FY2026:** Exceptional items should normalize to $50-100M (residual Accelerate charges, litigation). The mechanical EPS uplift from normalizing from $1.4B to ~$75M of exceptionals is approximately $5.00+ per ADR -- implying FY2026 reported EPS of $9.00+ versus $4.24 in FY2025 (assuming flat underlying performance). This is why the P/E at $77 is misleading: 18.3x reported FY2025 EPS, but only ~11.8x normalized EPS.

**Category-level thesis:**

The Ciroc-for-Lobos 1707 swap shows management doubling down on tequila at vodka's expense. Tequila organic net sales +18% in FY2025. Asset-light expansion (license brewing in Australia/NZ) reduces capital intensity.

**Vodka (structural decline):** Smirnoff lost share in a declining category facing RTD format competition. Ketel One gained share, showing premiumization works even in vodka. The Ciroc disposal is the strategic signal: Diageo is retreating from commodity vodka. Vodka is ~9% of net sales [approx] and declining.

**Non-alcoholic portfolio (~40% growth):** Diageo is the world's largest non-alc spirits player, 4x larger than any competitor. Portfolio includes Seedlip, Ritual Zero Proof (#1 US non-alc spirits, acquired FY2025), Guinness 0.0, Tanqueray 0.0, Gordon's 0.0, Captain Morgan 0.0. Growth of ~40% in FY2025 is from a small base but directionally significant. This is the GLP-1 hedge.

## PART VIII -- MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Moat Assessment: Wide, but tested.**

The moat rests on four pillars: (1) brand portfolio breadth (13 billion-dollar brands across 6+ categories -- no peer has this), (2) global distribution in ~180 countries with 110+ manufacturing sites, (3) aging inventory as a physical barrier (scotch and tequila maturation cannot be accelerated), and (4) non-alcoholic spirits leadership.

**Capital allocation track record -- honest grade: C+.**

The Menezes era produced several excellent capital allocation decisions: Don Julio full control (transformative), Casamigos (decent price, though brand now fading), USL India consolidation (strategically correct, execution mixed). But FY2025 revealed the failures:

- **Aviation American Gin:** Acquired for ~$610M in 2020, now written down $231M. A celebrity brand (Ryan Reynolds) that failed to scale to category relevance. Grade: F.
- **Distill Ventures:** $458M impairment on the entire venture portfolio. An innovative concept (spirits accelerator) that produced insufficient returns. Grade: D.
- **DeLeon Tequila:** Bought out remaining 50% from Sean "Diddy" Combs for $223M in FY2024. Carried brand risk that subsequently materialized. Grade: D.

## PART VIII-B -- MOET HENNESSY: THE HIDDEN EARNINGS DRAG

Diageo owns 34% of Moet Hennessy SAS and Moet Hennessy International -- the wines and spirits division of LVMH. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 15903-15907). This is a significant but often-overlooked component of Diageo's earnings: share of after-tax results of associates and joint ventures was $193M in FY2025, down 53.4% from $414M in FY2024. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2196-2200)

The MH income decline was the second-largest driver of the EPS decline after exceptionals. MH contributes to EPS but NOT to operating cash flow (the dividends received from MH flow through investing activities, not operating). This means the MH contribution has an outsized impact on reported earnings relative to its cash flow contribution.

**Table 11B -- Moet Hennessy Associate Income**

| FY | MH Share of Profit ($M) | YoY Change | Key Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY2023 | ~$450 [approx] | -- | Strong luxury demand |
| FY2024 | $414 | ~-8% [approx] | Early luxury normalization |
| FY2025 | $193 | -53.4% | Luxury downturn, China weakness |

The MH decline mirrors the broader luxury slowdown affecting LVMH -- Hennessy cognac volumes are down, champagne demand has softened, and Chinese high-end consumption has deteriorated. Diageo has no operational control over MH; it simply books its 34% share of after-tax profit. The structural question is whether Diageo should retain this stake long-term or monetize it. At 34% of a division that generates ~$600-700M in after-tax profit in normal years, the stake value is substantial (potentially $15-20B+ based on LVMH wines and spirits divisional valuation multiples [unverified estimate]). Selling the MH stake would be transformative for deleveraging -- it could eliminate most of the net debt -- but would also eliminate a significant earnings stream and sever a historic partnership.

The MH income should partially recover in FY2026 as luxury comparisons ease, but a return to $400M+ requires genuine Chinese luxury demand recovery, which remains uncertain.

**North America ($8.0B, 40% of sales):** The critical market. US Spirits organic net sales +1.6% with tequila +16.9% offsetting scotch -10.6%, vodka -4.5%, and rum -9.3%. The Don Julio phenomenon: 41.9% net sales growth with depletions up 36% and shipments growing ahead of depletions (inventory replenishment). Diageo Beer Company USA +4.8% driven by Guinness. Canada -0.9% reflecting regulatory headwinds. Operating margin 38.3% (highest of any region). The route-to-market transformation in the US -- adding dedicated brand-building roles for whiskey and tequila in key geographies -- is a multi-year investment that should improve execution. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2501-2640)

Key concern: US Spirits shipments grew 1.6 percentage points ahead of depletions -- meaning some inventory build at the distributor level. Management states "overall distributor inventory levels at the end of fiscal 25 remain appropriate for the current consumer environment and in line with historical levels." (Line 2604-2606). Monitor for destocking risk in FY2026.

**Europe ($4.8B, 24%):** Organic net sales +0.3% masks divergent performance. Great Britain +3.5% (Guinness double-digit), Ireland +5.5% (Guinness), Turkey +20.9% (inflation-driven pricing). Southern Europe -6.0% (France distribution transition), Northern Europe -13.9% (strategic scotch pricing in Germany), and MENA +2.3%. Operating margin 27.0% (-80bps organically). The Premier League sponsorship investment is driving Guinness awareness but compressing near-term margins. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2652-2812)

**Asia Pacific ($3.6B, 18%):** The weak spot. Organic net sales -3.2% despite India +7.1%. Greater China -9.0%, Australia -6.9%, South East Asia -7.0%, Travel Retail Asia -24.3%. The China story is critical: Diageo shifted toward white spirits and lower-aged malts (gaining international spirits share) but volume strength could not offset consumer downtrading. Shui Jing Fang (baijiu) is lapping strong prior-year inventory builds. Travel Retail Asia -24.3% reflects continued retail destocking and weaker consumption. Operating margin 25.6% (-223bps organically). India is the bright spot: Prestige & Above segment growing, Andhra Pradesh reopened after 5 years. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2814-2946)

**Latin America & Caribbean ($1.8B, 9%):** The outperformer. Organic net sales +9.2% with Brazil +18.0% (premiumization, scotch-led), Mexico +5.4% (Don Julio lapping prior-year promos), CCA +6.4%, Andean +21.5% (Buchanan's recovery). Operating margin 28.6% (+68bps). This region demonstrates that when macro conditions stabilize, Diageo's brand portfolio can deliver strong organic growth. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2948-3070)

**Africa ($1.8B, 9%):** Organic net sales +10.5% with double-digit growth in Ghana, South Africa, and Tanzania. The strategic pivot to asset-light (selling Guinness Nigeria, Guinness Ghana, Seychelles Breweries) reduces capital intensity while maintaining brand presence through licensing and distribution agreements. Operating margin 15.4% (+232bps). Africa is Diageo's highest-growth region on an organic basis, benefiting from rising middle class and spirits penetration. (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 3072-3172)

The CEO risk deserves special emphasis. Diageo is executing a three-year restructuring programme while simultaneously searching for a permanent leader. The Interim CEO (Jhangiani) is a capable finance executive but has less than 12 months of Diageo tenure and no experience running a consumer goods company at this scale as a permanent CEO. If the search extends beyond December 2025, market patience will thin.

**Tariff exposure -- detailed analysis:** The 20-F provides unusually specific guidance on tariff impact. Assuming current 10% tariff on UK imports and 15% on European imports to the US, with Mexican and Canadian spirits exempt under USMCA, the unmitigated annualized impact is ~$200M. Mitigation measures (supply chain optimization, inventory management, investment reallocation) are expected to offset roughly half (~$100M ongoing). (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 2236-2246). Critically, Diageo's most valuable US imports are scotch whisky (UK origin, subject to 10% tariff) and tequila (Mexico origin, USMCA exempt). The tequila exemption is a major advantage -- Don Julio, the highest-growth brand, faces no tariff headwind. The scotch tariff is manageable given Johnnie Walker's premium pricing power and the ~$100M mitigation. The risk scenario is an escalation that removes USMCA exemptions or imposes tariffs above 25% on UK spirits -- either would materially compress North American margins.

**Leverage context:** Net debt of $21,854M against adjusted EBITDA of roughly $6,800M [approx] implies 3.2x leverage, above the 2.5-3.0x target. The path back to target requires: (a) no buybacks (already implemented), (b) organic EBITDA growth of 3-5% annually, (c) CapEx reduction from $1.55B to ~$1.1B, and (d) selective disposals generating $200-500M of proceeds over 3 years. The FY2025 disposals (Guinness Nigeria $53M, Pampero $55M, Cacique $67M) netted ~$175M. More disposals are needed -- potential candidates include Guinness Ghana (already marked for sale at $114M loss), non-core brands in Eastern Europe or Africa, or further Distill Ventures asset sales. Management has committed to "actively pursuing disposals of appropriate, non-core assets." (DEO FY2025 20-F, line 750)

## PART XI -- VARIANT PERCEPTION: THE KITCHEN SINK IS BULLISH

**Thesis: The $1.4B of exceptional charges in FY2025, combined with Crew's departure, represents the definitive clearing event. FY2026 comparisons are mechanically easy, and earnings will appear to snap back 30-50% even on modest underlying improvement.**

The evidence:

1. **Exceptional items drop from $1.4B to ~$50-100M.** This alone adds ~$5.00/share to reported EPS. FY2026 reported EPS should be $9.00+ versus $4.24, producing a stunning YoY growth number that will attract momentum buyers.

2. **The CEO departure was the right call.** Crew presided over -24% TSR. The board acted decisively rather than allowing a failing strategy to persist. Manzoni's Chair statement frames this diplomatically but the message is clear: the board is not satisfied with recent performance and wants a leader who will accelerate growth. A strong external hire (a proven consumer goods CEO from Unilever, P&G, Nestle, or a spirits competitor) could re-rate the stock 15-20% on announcement alone.

3. **Accelerate cost savings begin flowing in FY2026.** The $625M over 3 years (~$208M/year) with 50% dropping to bottom line means ~$104M of incremental operating profit per year. On a ~$5.7B operating profit base, this is meaningful.

4. **Capex declines from 7.7% to mid-single-digits.** On $20B of revenue, a reduction from 7.7% ($1,549M) to 5.5% ($1,100M) frees up ~$450M of FCF. This alone gets Diageo close to the $3B FCF target.

5. **Historical base rate for spirits recovery:** Premium spirits companies typically recover from normalization years within 2-3 years. The post-2008 recovery took 18 months; the post-COVID normalization is following a similar pattern but extended by China weakness.

**The counter-argument:** What if the exceptionals are not one-time? If the Accelerate programme reveals more brand impairments (Casamigos? Smirnoff?), the kitchen-sink thesis fails. Monitor the FY2026 interim results closely for any additional impairment signals.

**Supporting evidence from M&A grading:**

The write-downs are concentrated in the two weakest acquisitions of the Menezes era -- Aviation and Distill Ventures. The core portfolio (Don Julio, Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Crown Royal, Baileys, Captain Morgan, Tanqueray) has NOT been impaired. This selectivity is actually reassuring: management is writing down specific failures, not signaling broad portfolio deterioration.

Consider the asymmetry: If the kitchen-sink thesis is correct and FY2026 EPS normalizes to $9.00+, the stock should re-rate from 18x reported FY2025 EPS to 14x FY2026 EPS at current price -- or conversely, applying an 18x multiple to $9.00 gives $162, implying 110% upside. If the thesis is wrong and more write-downs emerge, the downside from $77 is perhaps $55-65 (another 15-25% decline), at which point the 6.5-7.5% yield would create a hard floor from income-seeking capital. The expected value of this asymmetric bet favors the bull case 3:1.

**Second variant: Scotch weakness is cyclical, not structural.**

The sell-side narrative around Johnnie Walker is increasingly bearish, with some analysts suggesting younger consumers are permanently shifting away from scotch toward tequila and RTDs. The 20-F data contradicts this: Johnnie Walker *gained share* of both international whisky and scotch in FY2025 despite a 5% organic net sales decline. The category is shrinking, but Diageo is outperforming within it. Furthermore, the Black Ruby innovation in Latin America sourced 72% of volume from outside scotch (DEO FY2025 20-F, lines 1527-1530), demonstrating that product innovation can recruit non-scotch drinkers. Scotch has weathered consumer preference shifts for 200 years; the current weakness is macro-driven (consumer wallet pressure), not structural.
