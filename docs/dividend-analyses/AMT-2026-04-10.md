## PART I -- Executive Summary

American Tower Corporation is the world's largest publicly traded independent tower REIT, operating 149,686 communications sites across more than 20 countries and 30 CoreSite data center facilities in 11 United States markets. The company functions as an infrastructure toll collector: wireless carriers pay recurring rent under long-term leases with contractual 3% annual escalators domestically and inflation-linked escalators internationally, producing one of the most predictable revenue streams in all of real estate. As of December 31, 2025, AMT had over $54 billion in contracted non-cancellable tenant lease revenue stretching across future periods. That single statistic -- verified in the 2025 10-K, Item 7 Executive Overview -- encapsulates the durability of the business model.

The dividend has been raised for 15 consecutive years, placing AMT on the trajectory toward Aristocrat status in a decade. The current annualized payout of $6.80 per share yields approximately 3.9% at recent prices near $174, which is near the highest yield AMT has offered in the past decade. The AFFO payout ratio stands at approximately 63%, which is healthy by REIT standards and provides a meaningful cushion against moderate AFFO volatility. Full-year 2025 AFFO attributable to common stockholders reached $5.042 billion, or approximately $10.76 per share, representing 8% year-over-year growth as adjusted -- a figure confirmed by CFO Rod Smith during the Q4 2025 earnings call.

However, the story for 2026 has become more complicated. DISH Network defaulted on its contractual obligations in January 2026, forcing AMT to remove approximately 2% of consolidated property revenue and 4% of U.S. and Canada property revenue from its run-rate outlook. In Latin America, elevated carrier consolidation churn in Brazil and an ongoing arbitration with AT&T Mexico (representing approximately $300 million of annual tenant revenue) cloud near-term organic growth. Management guided 2026 consolidated organic tenant billings growth to approximately 1%, or approximately 4% excluding DISH churn. AFFO per share growth guidance of approximately 1% (approximately 5% normalized for DISH and FX) represents a visible deceleration from 2025's 8%.

For dividend investors, the calculus is straightforward: AMT remains a durable compounder with a well-protected distribution, but the 2026 deceleration and elevated leverage argue against aggressive accumulation at current prices. The stock becomes compelling on dips below $165 where the yield exceeds 4.1% and provides a margin of safety.

**Key numbers at a glance**

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

American Tower Corporation is a Delaware-incorporated REIT headquartered at 222 Berkeley Street, Boston, Massachusetts. It is one of the largest global real estate investment trusts and the leading independent owner, operator, and developer of multitenant communications real estate.

The primary business -- accounting for 97% of total revenues in FY2025 -- is the leasing of space on communications sites to wireless service providers, broadcasters, government agencies, and other tenants. The remaining 3% comes from tower-related services (site application, zoning, permitting, structural analysis, and construction management). Services revenue grew from $143 million in FY2023 to $194 million in FY2024 to $340 million in FY2025, reflecting elevated carrier construction activity during 5G densification.

- 42,224 communications sites in U.S. and Canada (including 26,940 owned and 14,855 operated towers, plus 862 DAS sites and property interests)

## PART III -- Historical Review (2016-2025)

**Phase 1 -- Domestic Dominance plus International Land Grab (2016-2019).** American Tower entered 2016 with approximately 90,000 towers worldwide and revenue of $5.79 billion. The playbook was expansion: acquire tower portfolios internationally (particularly India via Viom Networks, Latin America, Africa), layer on colocation tenants at incremental margins, and let the 3% domestic escalator compound. Revenue grew from $5.79 billion to $7.58 billion (9.4% CAGR). The dividend grew at 15-20% annually, funded by explosive AFFO growth. This was AMT's golden era for dividend growth investors.

**Phase 2 -- COVID Resilience plus CoreSite Acquisition (2020-2021).** The pandemic barely affected AMT -- revenue grew 6.1% in 2020 to $8.04 billion and 16.3% in 2021 to $9.36 billion. Wireless carriers accelerated 5G deployments. The transformational move was the $10.1 billion CoreSite acquisition (closed December 2021), adding 25 data center facilities and introducing a second growth vertical. The strategic logic: convergence of tower and data center assets as 5G and edge computing merge. Financing was primarily debt, pushing net leverage above 7x.

**Phase 3 -- Rate Shock plus Portfolio Rationalization (2022-2024).** The Fed's 525bps tightening cycle crushed AMT's stock from $280+ (January 2022) to below $170 (October 2023) -- a 40% drawdown. Interest expense climbed from $871 million in 2021 to $1,136 million in 2022 to $1,388 million in 2023 to $1,405 million in 2024. Meanwhile, India became structurally challenged: Reliance Jio's aggressive pricing, VIL payment shortfalls, and INR depreciation made Indian towers low-returning assets. Management pivoted to portfolio rationalization, selling Poland fiber (2023), Australia/NZ/India (2024), and South Africa fiber (2025).

**Phase 4 -- Refocus and Deleveraging (2025-present).** With India divested and international assets pruned, Vondran's AMT is a simpler, more focused company. FY2025 revenue of $10.645 billion grew 5.1% despite asset sales. Operating cash flow of $5.464 billion set a record. AFFO per share as adjusted grew 8%. The company brought leverage back to 4.9x (within the 3-5x target range), resumed share repurchases, and guided for continued 200-300 basis points of tower cash EBITDA margin expansion through 2030.

## PART VI -- Balance Sheet and Leverage Analysis

American Tower's balance sheet is, by design, highly leveraged. This is structurally appropriate for a tower REIT -- long-duration, contractual cash flows with minimal cyclicality support more debt than traditional businesses. But the absolute quantum of debt demands detailed scrutiny.

**As of December 31, 2025 (per 10-K consolidated balance sheet):**
- Cash and cash equivalents: $1,474.8M
- Restricted cash: $130.4M ($69.0M in securitization reserve accounts)
- Current portion of long-term obligations: $3,387.8M
- Long-term obligations: $33,832.5M
- Total indebtedness: $37,220.3M (current + long-term)
- Total American Tower Corporation equity: $3,652.5M
- Noncontrolling interests: $6,702.8M
- Total equity: $10,355.3M
- Total assets: $63,190.4M

**Leverage trajectory:**
- Management stated leverage at 4.9x net debt / adjusted EBITDA at year-end 2025, within the 3-5x target range -- a significant achievement after exceeding 7x in 2023.
- The deleveraging was accomplished through India divestiture proceeds, strong AFFO generation, and disciplined capital allocation.

**Debt structure (verified from 10-K exhibit list and MD&A):**
AMT has issued 30+ tranches of senior unsecured notes across four indentures (2013, 2019, 2022, 2025), with maturities laddered from 2026 through 2051. The key features:
- Euro-denominated notes provide natural hedge for European operations
- Most recent issuances in 2025: 4.900% Notes due 2030 ($850M), 5.350% Notes due 2035 ($725M), 3.625% Notes due 2032 (EUR 500M), 4.700% Notes due 2032 ($850M)
- Total 2025 senior note issuance: $3.0 billion in registered public offerings
- Secured Tower Revenue Securities (ABS): $1.3B Series 2023-1 and $500M Series 2018-1 remain outstanding, backed by pledged U.S. tower assets
- Credit facilities: $6.0B multicurrency revolving facility and $4.0B revolving facility, both recently amended to extend maturities

**2025 refinancing activity (per 10-K Liquidity section):**
During FY2025, AMT repaid five maturing senior note tranches totaling approximately $3.2 billion:
- 2.950% Notes ($650M, January 2025)
- 2.400% Notes ($750M, March 2025)
- 1.375% Notes (EUR 500M, April 2025)
- 4.000% Notes ($750M, May 2025)
- 1.300% Notes ($500M, September 2025)
Plus the $525M Secured Tower Revenue Notes, Series 2015-2 (June 2025).

The weighted-average coupon on redeemed notes was approximately 2.3%, while the weighted-average coupon on new issuances was approximately 4.7%. This 240bps refinancing spread on approximately $3.5 billion of refinanced debt adds approximately $84 million in annual interest expense -- a headwind that will persist as more low-coupon 2020-2021 vintage notes mature in 2026-2028.

**Interest expense trajectory (per 10-K consolidated statements of operations):**
- 2021: $871M | 2022: $1,136M | 2023: $1,388M | 2024: $1,405M | 2025: $1,359M

The 2025 decline of $46M was driven by lower average debt outstanding after India divestiture proceeds repaid revolver balances. However, FMP reports interest expense of $1,018M for FY2025. The discrepancy arises because FMP may exclude certain non-cash interest components. The 10-K figure of $1,359.4M from the consolidated P&L is the authoritative source and includes interest income offset of $134.0M, yielding net interest expense of $1,225.4M.

**Debt maturity wall -- the critical risk factor:** Over the 2026-2028 window, multiple tranches of low-coupon notes will mature, including 0.40%-1.95% Euro notes and 1.30%-2.95% USD notes. Each $1 billion tranche refinanced at 4.5% instead of 1.5% adds $30 million in incremental annual interest expense. Management's $11.1 billion liquidity position (comprising $9.6B available under credit facilities net of letters of credit, plus $1.5B cash) provides optionality to retire rather than refinance debt, but that consumes capital that could fund growth or buybacks.

Subsequent to year-end, AMT repaid the 4.400% Notes ($500M) in February 2026 and made additional borrowings of $600M under the credit facility plus $135M net under the multicurrency facility.

**Credit ratings:** Investment-grade maintained. This is essential -- any downgrade to high-yield would be catastrophic for AMT's perpetual access to the capital markets.

## PART VII -- Competitive Position and Moat Analysis

American Tower operates in a natural oligopoly with one of the widest moats in infrastructure.

**Moat type:** Regulatory barriers (zoning/permitting), switching costs, scale economics
**Moat trajectory:** Stable domestically, slightly narrowing internationally (divestitures reduce geographic diversification)

**Structural moat characteristics:**

1. **Zoning and permitting barriers create de facto geographic monopolies.** Each tower site is protected by local zoning restrictions that effectively prevent competitors from building within the same coverage area. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 prohibits discriminatory state/local actions but does not eliminate the permitting burden for new entrants. Communities oppose new tower construction, and the FAA/FCC pre-approval process adds further friction.

2. **Switching costs are prohibitive.** Moving equipment from one tower to another requires physical construction, network re-optimization, potential coverage gaps, and regulatory filings. The 2% churn rate (per 10-K) reflects the near-impossibility of switching. Management stated during the Q4 2025 call that the business model produces "98% renewal rates."

3. **Incremental economics are exceptionally powerful.** Each new tenant added to an existing tower generates 80%+ incremental margin because fixed costs (land lease, maintenance, insurance) are already absorbed. This is the fundamental driver of tower economics and explains why tower REIT margins are structurally higher than nearly all other real estate verticals.

4. **$54 billion of contracted, non-cancellable lease revenue** provides visibility unlike any other REIT sub-sector. This backlog, disclosed in the 10-K Executive Overview, extends years into the future with embedded 3% annual escalators domestically and CPI-linked escalators internationally.

**Pricing power evidence:**
- U.S. contractual escalations averaged approximately 3% in FY2025
- CoreSite achieving mark-to-market increases on renewing leases above historical norms, per CEO Vondran on Q4 call
- Data center mid-teens stabilized yields on new deployments maintained despite inflationary cost pressures

**Competitive risks to monitor:**
- **Carrier consolidation:** The T-Mobile/Sprint merger (2020) drove churn for several years. The DISH default and effective exit from network deployment in 2025-2026 removes a customer. T-Mobile's acquisition of U.S. Cellular could reduce the customer base further. Each consolidation event reduces tenant count at the margin.
- **Small cells and DAS:** Urban densification increasingly uses small cells. Crown Castle has invested more heavily in small cells. If small cells commoditize urban coverage, macro tower pricing power could erode -- though AMT operates 862 DAS networks of its own.
- **Open RAN and network sharing:** Technologies that allow carriers to share infrastructure more efficiently could theoretically reduce tower demand. The 10-K Risk Factors explicitly cite this: "extensive sharing of site infrastructure through RAN sharing, roaming or resale arrangements...may cause new lease activity to slow."
- **LEO satellite constellations:** CEO Vondran addressed this directly on the Q4 call: "towers will always be the cheapest and best way of deploying the level of content, the volume of mobile data that consumers demand. And the satellites just can't compete with that." AMT's investment in AST SpaceMobile (with Board seat retained after partial stake sale) provides intelligence on this evolving technology.

## PART VIII -- Management Assessment

**Steven O. Vondran, President and CEO (appointed early 2024):** A 24-year AMT veteran who rose through legal and operational roles, Vondran brings deep institutional knowledge and operational credibility. His early strategic decisions -- accelerating the India exit, resuming share repurchases, creating a global COO role, emphasizing developed markets and data centers -- are directionally correct and aligned with shareholder interests.

On the Q4 2025 call, Vondran's language was notably confident: "we had a great year and an excellent fourth quarter" and "we're well positioned to deliver our goal of industry-leading attributable AFFO per share growth." His articulation of the four cost-reduction initiatives (land optimization, unified sourcing, global standard of care, technology simplification) and the 200-300 basis point margin expansion target through 2030 demonstrates operational rigor.

**Rodney M. Smith, CFO:** Joined 2009. Provided detailed financial guidance on the Q4 call with clear explanations of DISH impact, organic growth components, and AFFO bridge items. Smith's language on leverage was particularly reassuring: "we've regained full financial flexibility" after reaching 4.9x.

**Eugene M. Noel, COO (new global role):** The creation of this position signals management's pivot from acquisition-driven growth to operational efficiency -- the right strategic emphasis for a maturing tower portfolio.

**Compensation structure:** Equity-heavy with RSU and performance-based awards. The 2025 10-K discloses that 2025 human capital goals for the executive team focused on "developing talent." Stock-based compensation expense was $174.2M in FY2025.

**Insider ownership:** Modest in percentage terms given the $81B market cap, but meaningful in absolute dollar value. No dual-class structure. No poison pill.

**Guidance track record (FY2025):** Management delivered 8% AFFO per share as adjusted growth, at the high end of the mid- to high-single-digit long-term target. The India divestiture was executed smoothly. The South Africa fiber sale was completed during 2025. Share repurchases resumed at the largest quarterly level since 2017. On these measures, execution was strong.

**Capital allocation grade: B+.** The portfolio rationalization (exiting India, ANZ, Poland, SA fiber) was the right call, and deleveraging from 7x+ to 4.9x was a meaningful achievement. The resumption of buybacks at approximately $180 average price appears reasonable. Deduction from an "A" grade reflects: (a) the CoreSite acquisition at $10.1B was well-timed in terms of secular trends but poorly timed in terms of financing costs, loading the balance sheet with debt at the start of a rate cycle; (b) the DISH relationship, while not management's fault, represents a customer credit misjudgment.

**International strategy under Vondran -- a dividend investor's perspective:** The pivot from "growth through international expansion" to "developed-market focus plus capital recycling" is the single most important strategic shift for AMT's dividend trajectory. During the 2016-2021 era, AMT deployed billions into emerging-market towers (India, Africa, LatAm) that generated impressive top-line growth but faced currency headwinds, sovereign risk, and lower risk-adjusted returns. The India exit crystallized the lesson: a position that produced $911 million in 2024 revenue (before divestiture, per 10-K discontinued operations table) but required a $1.2 billion loss on sale was, in hindsight, a capital misallocation relative to what domestic towers or CoreSite expansion could have delivered.

Vondran has been explicit that future M&A will focus on developed markets. On the Q4 2025 call: "our capital allocation strategy is to focus on developed markets. You should not expect to see us participating in M&A in emerging markets." This discipline, if maintained, will improve earnings quality, reduce FX volatility (FY2025 saw $809.4 million in unrealized FX losses per the consolidated P&L), and support a more predictable dividend growth trajectory. The 2025 financials demonstrate the benefit: while FX losses hit the GAAP P&L, underlying AFFO growth of 10.3% from continuing operations was robust.

The remaining international tower portfolio (Africa, LatAm, Europe) totaling approximately 107,000 towers will be managed for cash flow harvesting rather than aggressive growth investment. Management expects international towers to deliver faster organic growth than U.S. towers over time as less mature portfolios lease up, but incremental CapEx will be modest. This "harvest and redeploy" approach is the correct framework for a company transitioning from a growth REIT to a mature infrastructure compounder.

**Operational efficiency as the new growth lever:** Perhaps the most notable development from the Q4 2025 call was the introduction of a concrete operational efficiency target: 200-300 basis points of tower cash EBITDA margin expansion by 2030. The four initiatives -- global land optimization program, unified sourcing and supply chain, expanded standard of care from U.S. operations to global portfolio, and technology platform simplification -- are not glamorous but represent real margin improvement potential. CEO Vondran described this as a "stretch target" that could be augmented by AI-driven efficiencies in subsequent years. Current tower SG&A is approximately 4.5% of revenue, which management described as "best-in-class" among peers. The new COO role (Eugene Noel) is specifically tasked with bending the direct cost curve downward -- managing land expense, repair and maintenance, and power costs at a slower growth rate than revenue. If successful, this operational efficiency program provides a second source of AFFO per share growth independent of organic revenue, which is exactly what a decelerating-growth REIT needs to sustain dividend increases.

## PART IX -- The Data Center Thesis: CoreSite Deep Dive

CoreSite deserves dedicated analysis because it represents AMT's primary growth vector and the key justification for a premium multiple among tower REITs.

**What AMT acquired:** CoreSite Realty Corporation, closed December 2021 for approximately $10.1B (including assumed debt). 30 data center facilities across 11 U.S. markets. Focus: network-dense, carrier-neutral colocation and interconnection. These are not hyperscale campuses -- they are network convergence points where hundreds of carriers, cloud providers, and enterprises interconnect. The interconnection density creates the same switching-cost moat that tower colocation enjoys.

**Revenue trajectory:**
| Year | Data Centers Revenue ($M) | YoY Growth |
|------|--------------------------|------------|
| 2021 (partial, Dec only) | 23.2 | -- |
| 2022 | 766.6 | -- |
| 2023 | 834.7 | +8.9% |
| 2024 | 924.8 | +10.8% |
| 2025 | 1,053.1 | +13.9% |

Source: 2025 10-K segment data. The acceleration from 10.8% to 13.9% growth is significant and coincides with AI compute demand explosion.

**Segment gross margin trajectory (excluding D&A):**
- FY2023: $487.1M (58.3% of segment revenue of $834.7M)
- FY2024: $534.0M (57.7% of $924.8M)
- FY2025: $650.7M (61.8% of $1,053.1M)

The 410bps margin expansion in FY2025 was partially driven by a one-time property tax reversal ($26.0M) and a legal settlement, per the 10-K. Normalized margins are approximately 59%, still showing improvement.

**Q4 2025 earnings call commentary on CoreSite:** CEO Vondran stated this was CoreSite's "fourth consecutive year of record sales growth" and the first year where "AI really manifest itself as a huge use case." Inferencing and machine learning are the fastest-growing new use cases. Vondran noted "we have more demand for it than we can meet with our supply" and that CoreSite is "curating our customer mix" to maintain the interconnection-rich ecosystem. New campuses are being explored in the San Francisco Bay Area and potentially Dallas-Fort Worth, with a 2-3 year timeline from groundbreaking to revenue generation.

Management guided 2026 data center revenue growth of approximately 13%, with cash margins expected to decline approximately 270bps year-over-year due to non-recurrence of 2025 one-time benefits. Normalized margins expected to hold steady as strong lease-up offsets new capacity being brought online.

**CapEx commitment:** Approximately $609M was spent on data center assets in FY2025, and approximately $700M is planned for FY2026. This is significant -- over one-third of total discretionary CapEx is now directed at CoreSite.

**The bull case:** If data center revenue compounds at 13% annually for five years, it reaches approximately $1.9B by 2030 -- roughly 17% of today's consolidated revenue base. Data center EBITDA margins comparable to tower margins (55-65%) mean CoreSite's incremental AFFO contribution would be meaningful, potentially adding 2-3 percentage points to consolidated AFFO per share growth.

**The bear case:** Equinix operates 200+ global facilities versus CoreSite's 30. Digital Realty, hyperscaler-owned facilities (AWS, Google, Microsoft), and new entrants could pressure pricing. Power availability constraints in key markets (Northern Virginia, Silicon Valley) could limit expansion. The AI compute cycle may prove more volatile than secular wireless growth.

## PART XII -- Investment Conclusion and Action Plan

**Thesis (one paragraph):** American Tower is a toll-booth business on the physical layer of global wireless connectivity, protected by zoning barriers, switching costs, and contractual escalators. The 15-year dividend growth streak, 63% AFFO payout ratio, and $54 billion of contracted non-cancellable revenue provide exceptional visibility. The CoreSite data center platform adds a second secular growth vector. However, 2026 is a transition year with DISH default, LatAm churn, and refinancing headwinds suppressing reported growth to approximately 1% AFFO per share. The stock is fairly valued at current prices for investors who must see normalized 5%+ AFFO growth before adding exposure. For patient capital, the ~3.9% yield plus 4-6% expected annual dividend growth produces an 8-10% total return from income and growth alone, with significant upside potential if rates decline and the 2027 growth re-acceleration materializes.

**Verdict: HOLD at ~$174. ACCUMULATE below $165.**

**Position sizing:** 2-3% of a diversified dividend portfolio. Do not overweight -- the leverage profile and customer concentration merit a measured allocation despite the business quality.

**2026 expected distributions:** Management guided approximately 5% dividend growth, implying approximately $7.14 per share annualized ($1.785 per quarter), subject to Board approval.

**Monitoring checklist:**
- AFFO per share and YoY growth: The single most important fundamental indicator. 2026 guide is approximately 1% growth; normalized for DISH is approximately 5%. If AFFO per share from continuing operations declines below $10.50, reassess.
- Organic tenant billings growth by segment: Should be 4%+ excluding DISH in U.S.; 8%+ in Africa/APAC; monitor LatAm for signs of churn abating earlier than expected.
- CoreSite revenue growth and interconnection revenue: The leading indicator for the data center thesis. Should be 12%+ annually.
- Net leverage (net debt / adjusted EBITDA): Management targeting 3-5x. Progress below 4.5x would be a positive catalyst; any movement above 5.5x would be concerning.
- Interest expense and weighted-average cost of debt: Monitor each new issuance for coupon trends. If new issuances consistently exceed 5.5%, the refinancing headwind is worse than expected.
- Churn rate: Should remain at or below 2% (excluding DISH). Anything above 3% signals carrier network disruption.
- DISH litigation outcome: Potential $200M/year revenue recovery through 2035; any settlement or legal victory is upside.
- AT&T Mexico arbitration: Hearing scheduled August 2026. Resolution impacts approximately $300M in annual revenue.

**Exit triggers:**
- AFFO per share declines for two consecutive fiscal years (not quarters) on a continuing operations basis
- Leverage exceeds 6.0x with no credible path to reduction
- A second major U.S. carrier defaults or exits
- Management cuts or freezes the dividend
