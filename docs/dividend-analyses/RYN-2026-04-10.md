## TL;DR — 10 Lines

1. **RYN is mid-transition between two identities.** Old identity: leveraged pure-play U.S.+NZ timberland REIT with a $1.08 "frozen" dividend (2017–2022). New identity (post-1/30/26): ~4M-acre U.S.-only timber + wood products REIT with a Weyerhaeuser-lite flex-dividend framework.
2. **The 2023–2025 deleveraging plan WORKED.** Target was $1B of dispositions to bring Net Debt/Adj EBITDA ≤3.0x. They delivered **$1.45B**: 2023 dispositions ($242M, 55K acres), 2024 Large Dispositions ($495M, 199K acres in OK + WA), plus the **$710M New Zealand exit** (June 30, 2025). Result: **Net Debt/Adj EBITDA collapsed from 4.6x (2023) to 0.8x (2025)**. Cash on balance sheet ballooned to $842.9M.
3. **Proceeds were NOT used for a bigger base dividend** — they were used for (a) special dividends (2024: $1.80; 2025: $1.40, both 75% stock / 25% cash), (b) $70.5M of 2025 buybacks at $24.29 avg, (c) $229.5M of remaining buyback authorization, and (d) setting up dry powder for the PCH merger. Management clearly prefers **return capital, not grow it**.
4. **The ordinary dividend adjustment to $0.26/qtr ($1.04 annual) is NOT a cut** — it is the mechanical re-basing after the ~7.5M special-dividend share issuance. Aggregate ordinary dividend $ stays roughly flat, yield on current price is ~**5.0%**.
5. **Post-merger dividend run-rate:** management guides to ~$317M/yr on common shares = $1.04/sh × ~305M shares. **This is well below what the combined EBITDA can support** (2026E ~$515M EBITDA per FMP consensus). The flex comes through specials + buybacks, not base-rate hikes.
6. **Timber cycle timing is dreadful — and that's the point.** 2025 Southern pine stumpage hit $18.83/ton (−12% YoY) on salvage volume + soft mill demand. PNW delivered sawtimber at $93.37. Housing starts remain depressed. Lumber tariffs (10% Section 232 duty since Oct 14, 2025) add upside optionality. **Buying at cycle trough** is a legitimate reason to own RYN here.
7. **Land Use / HBU is the hidden alpha engine.** Rural HBU sold at $5,475/acre vs. timberland basis of ~$1,200/acre book. Improved Development (Wildlight, Heartwood, Chenal Valley via PCH) is the long-tail optionality that pure lumber plays don't capture.
8. **NAV vs. market cap is interesting but not screaming cheap.** On pre-merger figures, using comparable transaction pricing of **~$1,800–$2,500/acre** for southern timberland and **~$3,000–$4,500** for PNW, plus HBU premium, we arrive at an NAV range of roughly **$5.5–$7.5B standalone** vs. a pre-merger EV of ~$4.1B (Dec 2024 enterprise value). That is a **meaningful discount**, but the discount has compressed post the RYN+PCH merger, which was priced at approximately par on an NAV basis.
9. **vs. Weyerhaeuser (WY):** WY is the larger, more diversified (timber + wood products + real estate), better-known name with a variable-dividend framework that RYN is now copying. **RYN is the higher-beta way to play the same thesis.** Smaller, more recent deleveraging story, cleaner post-merger, but less scale. WY also pays a more reliable "base" component. I'd own both.
10. **Verdict: BUY for a 5-year holder who understands specials.** It's NOT a widows-and-orphans name. The base dividend yields ~5% at current price with ROC-heavy tax characterization, but the *real* return comes from cycle recovery + HBU + buybacks + occasional specials. **Rate this "sleep well in a rate-cut + housing-recovery scenario"; otherwise it's dead money for 12–24 months.**

## PART I — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Rayonier is the second-largest publicly-traded pure-play timberland REIT in the U.S., and as of January 30, 2026 it is, in a very real sense, a **new company**. The merger-of-equals with PotlatchDeltic closed that day (1.8185 RYN shares + $0.61 cash per PCH share; ~140.9M new shares issued), instantly doubling the share count and adding a full wood products manufacturing business (lumber mills) to what had been a pure "trees plus real estate" portfolio. Any analyst writing about RYN on April 10, 2026 is therefore writing about **two things at once**: the legacy RYN track record (10-K 2025) and the pro-forma combined entity that will produce the Q1 2026 10-Q and beyond.

**The thesis in one paragraph:** Between 2023 and 2026 Mark McHugh ran three strategic plays in sequence — (1) the November 2023 "Initiatives to Enhance Shareholder Value" $1B asset disposition plan to get net leverage from 4.6x to ≤3.0x, (2) the June 2025 exit from New Zealand ($710M cash, $404.5M book gain), and (3) the January 2026 merger-of-equals with PotlatchDeltic to create a ~4M-acre U.S.-focused scale player with wood products exposure. The first two plays are **done and verified** in the 10-K. Net leverage is now 0.8x, cash is $842.9M, and the NZ exit crystallized value at what looks like a decent cycle-neutral price. The third play is in progress and cannot yet be judged.

For a dividend investor, the key question is: **what is RYN's steady-state dividend framework?** The answer from the 10-K is unusually explicit:
- **Base quarterly:** $0.26/share post-merger ($1.04 annual; $317M annual cost on ~305M shares) — adjusted downward from $0.2725 purely to neutralize the 7.5M share issuance from the Dec 2025 special dividend.
- **Specials:** Declared opportunistically. 2024: $1.80 (paid Jan 2025, 25% cash / 75% stock). 2025: $1.40 (paid Dec 2025, 25% cash / 75% stock). 2023: $0.20 (all cash).
- **Buybacks:** $300M authorization from Dec 2024; $70.5M executed in 2025 at $24.29 avg (before merger-window blackout); $229.5M remaining.

The REIT distribution requirement (90% of taxable income, effectively 100% for tax-advantaged REIT status) is being satisfied primarily through *stock specials*, not cash. This is a smart REIT-mechanic trick: it meets the taxable income distribution test without draining cash, it signals "we will NOT starve the balance sheet", and it penalizes nobody who doesn't want cash drag. **But it is not a "dividend growth story" in the traditional sense.** The base rate has been roughly flat at $1.08–$1.14/yr since 2017. Dividend growth investors should calibrate expectations accordingly.

**My opinionated read:** RYN is not a "Keep buying, hold forever" staple. It is a **tactical long** for investors who believe (a) housing starts bottom in 2026–2027, (b) Canadian lumber duty escalation + Section 232 tariffs create a multi-year U.S. log price tailwind, (c) the PCH merger produces $40–$60M in synergies by year 3, and (d) the special-dividend framework continues to return excess cash. At $20.92 (trough pricing, near 52-week lows) the risk/reward favors accumulation. It is decidedly **NOT** a Kimberly-Clark-style "set and forget" holding, and anyone using it that way will be disappointed by the flat base rate and the complexity of stock specials.

**Opinionated reading of the arc:**

1. **2016–2022 was the "do nothing" era.** Dividend was essentially frozen at $1.08 for six straight years (FY2017 through FY2021 inclusive per the 10-Ks). Leverage drifted up from ~2.6x to 4.7x as RYN funded Pope and other acquisitions without commensurate EBITDA growth. Return on invested capital was mediocre.

2. **2022 was the cycle peak.** Share price touched $41, COVID lumber boom flattered results, and then cyclical reality arrived. Revenue (continuing ops) peaked at $1.11B in 2021, EBIT at $270M. 2022 net income was just $107M and operating income dropped to $166M. Management saw the writing on the wall.

3. **2023–2025 was a masterclass in controlled monetization.** Under pressure from activist-adjacent investors and wider REIT peers trading at steeper discounts, McHugh & team announced a $1B target and blew through it with $1.45B of dispositions. Net debt went from $1,246M (2023) to $230M (2025). That is a **$1B reduction in net debt in two years**, a genuinely rare accomplishment in REITland.

4. **The NZ exit was the non-obvious smart move.** NZ timberlands had always been the weak part of the RYN story — currency-exposed, requiring 77%/23% partnership governance overhead, with inferior per-ton economics despite carbon credit optionality. Cutting the cord at $710M (pre-fees $698.6M net) was sold as "focus on U.S.", but I read it as also reducing complexity ahead of the PCH merger negotiations. Win for management.

5. **The PCH merger is the big unknown.** Merger-of-equals, roughly 50/50 economic split, Rayonier the legal/accounting acquirer but without a premium being paid either direction. Adding PCH's wood products (CoCEO Eric Cremers' baby) gives the combined company a natural cyclical hedge — lumber mills print money when logs are cheap, while log sales struggle. That is not the same as "growth", but it smooths the curve. Whether PCH's Idaho + Southern exposure adds diversification or duplicates existing footprint is the question I cannot answer from a 10-K alone.

## PART V — DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE: BASE + SPECIAL ANALYSIS

This is where RYN is genuinely different from the Kimberly-Clarks and Procter & Gambles of the dividend world, and where most "dividend screeners" get it wrong.

### V.1 — The base ordinary dividend

Per the 10-K (p. 1080): *"Common share ordinary cash dividends during the years ended December 31, 2025, 2024 and 2023 aggregated to $1.09, $1.14 and $1.14, respectively."*

The base has been essentially frozen at $1.08–$1.14 for **nine years**. Anyone who describes RYN as a "dividend growth stock" is miscalibrated. Some will say "well, they pay *total* distributions well above $1.14" — that is true, but the specials are **lumpy, unpredictable, and 75% stock**, so they are not a substitute for base-rate growth.

**Post-merger mechanical adjustment (p. 4161):** *"On February 23, 2026, the Company's board of directors declared a first quarter cash dividend of $0.26 per common share. The adjustment in the quarterly dividend from $0.2725 per share to $0.26 per share reflects the 7.5 million incremental shares issued to shareholders as part of the Company's recent special dividend."* So the new run-rate is $0.26 × 4 = **$1.04 annualized**. At $20.92 = **4.97% yield**. Attractive-looking on screens, but misleading if the investor thinks this is a 5% yielder with growth. It is a 5% yielder with **zero growth + optional specials**.

### V.2 — The 2023 $0.20 "spillover" special

*"The year ended December 31, 2023 excludes an additional cash dividend of $0.20 per common share, paid January 12, 2024, to shareholders of record on December 29, 2023."* (p. 1082). This was a small REIT-spillover payment — the kind of thing a REIT does when taxable income slightly exceeds the 90% distribution already made. Not a strategic signal.

### V.3 — The 2024 $1.80 mega-special (25% cash / 75% stock)

*"The year ended December 31, 2024 excludes an additional dividend of $1.80 per common share, paid January 30, 2025... Both the 2025 and 2024 special dividends consisted of a combination of cash and the Company's common shares, with the cash component (excluding cash for fractional shares) not exceeding 25% of the aggregate value."* (p. 1081).

**What happened:** 2024 generated $495M of Large Dispositions in OK + WA. That produced a huge spike in REIT taxable income (Large Disposition operating income was $291M). Under REIT rules, 90%+ of this had to be distributed. The company chose the IRS-approved "cash + stock" special dividend mechanic to satisfy the distribution requirement **without draining balance-sheet cash**. Cash component was roughly $0.45/share × 152M = ~$68M cash. Stock component was effectively a ~3% dilutive share issuance at net book value.

**Why this matters for dividend investors:** the stock portion is NOT cashable dividend income for an investor who wants income. It's a forced small-scale share grant. In after-tax terms it is roughly equivalent to receiving a bigger dividend and being forced to immediately reinvest 75% of it in shares — which is what most dividend re-investors do anyway, but it's not "cash yield."

### V.4 — The 2025 $1.40 mega-special (25% cash / 75% stock)

Same mechanic, driven this time by the NZ JV gain ($404.5M on disposal). $0.35/share cash (~$55M) and $1.05/share stock. The 10-K is explicit: *"This distribution consisted of $0.35 per unit in cash and $1.05 per unit in Redeemable Operating Partnership Units."* (p. 1118). ~7.5M incremental shares were issued from this special, triggering the $0.2725 → $0.26 mechanical rebase.

### V.5 — Why the "cash + stock" special dividend format is actually clever

**Senior-investor Q: Isn't this just dilution disguised as a dividend?** Mostly no, actually. Here's the mechanic:

1. REIT has $400M of non-recurring income (asset sale gain) that must be distributed.
2. Option A: pay $400M cash → drains balance sheet, can't be reinvested in buybacks or debt reduction.
3. Option B: pay $100M cash + $300M in shares at market → satisfies IRS distribution test, preserves $300M of balance-sheet firepower, and the share issuance is at **current market price**, so no value is destroyed or created vs. what a proportional rights offering would do. Existing holders receive the pro-rata share — they are NOT diluted if they hold.
4. The only "loss" is tax timing: the full $400M is taxable to the recipient as a REIT dividend in the year received, even though only $100M was cash. Investors may need to sell shares to cover the tax bill, creating a small amount of forced selling.

**Net assessment:** The format is correct for a cyclical REIT with chunky one-time gains. Weyerhaeuser, Iron Mountain, and several others use the same structure. My opinion: thumbs up on the mechanic, and it is a MATERIALLY different animal than a conventional cash-only special.

### V.6 — Payout ratios, three ways

| Metric | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ordinary cash div paid / share | $1.14 | $1.14 | $1.09 |
| EPS continuing ops | $1.05 | $2.24 | $0.47 |
| EPS including discontinued | $1.17 | $2.39 | $3.03 |

CAD-based coverage has trended from 125% (uncovered) in 2023 to 87% (covered with room) in 2025. That is the direction you want — **coverage is improving, not deteriorating**. Post-merger coverage should be at worst stable, at best improving, if we believe the FMP consensus 2026E EBITDA of $515M.

### V.7 — Is there dividend risk?

**Short answer: little to none for the base. Specials are unpredictable by design.**

Arguments that the base is safe:
1. Net debt/EBITDA is 0.8x — by far the lowest in the timber REIT peer group. BBB upgrade from BBB- in 2025 was earned.
2. $842.9M cash, $199.3M undrawn revolver (pre-merger) — more than a full year of dividends in cash on hand.
3. Base dividend is $317M/yr post-merger vs. guided 2026 CapEx $104–108M and RE dev investment $40–44M, implying a very manageable capital plan.
4. Management explicitly guided to $317M common dividend spend in 2026 — they are committing publicly.
5. Tax law: as a REIT, they MUST distribute 90% of taxable income. The base rate is below the taxable-income floor in most years, so it is structurally defended.

Arguments against base safety:
1. If combined-company EBITDA falls materially below $300M (extreme recession scenario), cash conversion could dip and specials disappear, then the base starts looking tight.
2. The 2016-Incremental Term Loan ($200M) matures in 2026 (already classified as current). Refinancing is easy given the August 2025 $1.81B Second Amended Credit Agreement and low leverage, but the refi happens at roughly 5.6% SOFR + spread vs. the legacy 2.75% senior notes — a small margin drag.
3. The PCH merger brings operational integration risk. If synergies fail to materialize, the "pro forma" story wobbles.

My verdict: **base is safe. Specials come and go. Don't bet the retirement on specials, but price them into your expected return at some fractional probability (30–50% of years).**

## PART VII — TIMBER CYCLE + HBU MARGIN STORY

### VII.1 — Where are we in the lumber cycle?

Framework for thinking about timber cycles:

- **Demand drivers:** U.S. housing starts (single-family is the big one, ~80% of lumber consumption), R&R (repair and remodel), paper/pulp, bioenergy, industrial packaging.
- **Supply drivers:** Canadian imports (subject to duties), U.S. domestic harvest, imports from other countries, South African/Russian/European dynamics.
- **Price signals:** Lumber futures (CME), NBSK pulp, container board, OSB.

**Current setup (April 2026, from RYN 10-K 2025 MD&A):**
1. U.S. housing starts depressed but not crashed — mortgage rates elevated but showing signs of easing.
2. Canadian softwood lumber duty 6th administrative review added to burden; 7th review (expected H2 2026) could ease them.
3. **Executive Order 14223 + Section 232 tariff of 10% on softwood timber and lumber imports** (effective Oct 14, 2025) is a brand-new tailwind for U.S. timber owners. This is a significant structural win that was NOT in place a year ago.
4. **Executive Order 14225** (Immediate Expansion of American Timber Production, March 2025) could increase federal land harvests, mainly PNW — adds supply risk but "logistical, legal and infrastructure-related challenges will likely limit near-term market impacts."
5. Southern pulp mill closures continue, suppressing pulpwood pricing (as evidenced by the 22% drop in 2025).
6. Lumber prices are trending higher entering 2026 per the RYN 2026 outlook section.

**My opinionated cycle call:** We are somewhere between the *trough* and *early recovery*. The combo of (a) Canadian duty ratchet + Section 232 tariffs, (b) demographic tailwind for new household formation, (c) modest rate cut expectations, and (d) structurally depressed U.S. housing starts still below equilibrium suggests 2026–2028 will be a **gradually improving** period for lumber/timber prices. This is exactly the kind of cycle positioning where timber REITs generate outsized returns. **But it is NOT a V-shaped recovery.** Patient investors win; those with 12-month horizons will be bored.

### VII.2 — The HBU (Higher-and-Better-Use) margin story

This is the *hidden* part of the RYN thesis that mainstream energy/materials investors tend to miss.

**Definition:** HBU land is timberland that is worth *more* for some non-timber purpose (rural recreation, residential development, commercial, conservation) than it is worth as pure working forest. RYN sells HBU land at substantial premiums to their timber-hold basis.

**2025 HBU pricing from the 10-K Real Estate Overview:**
- Improved Development: **$79,351/acre** (Wildlight FL, Heartwood GA at cost of development)
- Unimproved Development: $13,155/acre
- **Rural: $5,475/acre**
- Timberland & Non-Strategic: $2,477/acre
- Weighted Average (excl. Large Dispositions): $4,906/acre

**2025 HBU acres sold:**
- Rural: 8,873 acres at $5,475/acre = $48.6M
- Timberland & Non-Strategic: 21,601 acres at $2,477/acre = $53.5M
- Improved Development: small acres but $47.2M in sales

**Implied margin on Rural HBU vs. timberland basis:** If depletion cost basis on Southern timber averages roughly $700–$1,500/acre, and Rural HBU sells for $5,475/acre, the gross margin is **approximately 75–90%**. That is not a typo. **Rural HBU is the highest-margin business inside RYN**, hidden inside a "Real Estate" segment line that pulls down corporate optics when large dispositions come through at low per-acre prices.

**Improved development is even more interesting:** Wildlight (FL) and Heartwood (GA) are mixed-use master-planned communities. Improved development sold at $79,351/acre in 2025. With the PCH merger, Chenal Valley (Little Rock, AR) gets added as a third project. These are multi-decade community-development efforts — they throw off ~$30–50M/yr and growing. I would not be surprised to see improved development revenues double over the next 5 years as Wildlight matures and Heartwood scales.

**Opinionated read:** A reasonable back-of-envelope way to value RYN's land:
- Core working forest (1.8M acres post-divestitures) at $2,200/acre (south) + $3,500/acre (PNW weighted) ≈ **$4.4B + $1.1B = $5.5B**
- Rural HBU optionality (say 5% of holdings at $5,000+/acre premium) ≈ **$450M**
- Improved Development NPV (Wildlight + Heartwood + Chenal) ≈ **$600–900M**
- **Total standalone NAV estimate: ~$6.5–7.0B**
- Less net debt (~$230M) = **equity NAV ~$6.3–6.8B**, or roughly **$39–42/share on 161.4M pre-merger shares**

Market is at **$20.92 × 161.4M = $3.4B** equity pre-merger — **nearly 50% NAV discount**. Post-merger with PCH added at roughly par, the combined entity trades at **$20.92 × 305M = $6.4B** vs. a combined NAV that could reasonably be in the **$10–13B range**. That's still a ~40% discount to NAV. **That is a wide discount and the main reason I think this is a BUY.**

Big caveat: NAV is a floor value, not a trading multiple. Timber REITs have historically traded at 10–30% discounts to their own best-estimate NAVs because the public-market "liquidity discount" is real, and you never get NAV unless the entire company is sold in a private transaction (which has happened — see Rayonier Private Timber Fund, Plum Creek pre-WY merger, etc.). So while the *numerical* discount is wide, the *practical* discount investors will realize is more likely in the range of **15–25% above current price** over a 2–3 year horizon — call it **$26–32/share** — unless a take-private transaction materializes.

The 10-K devotes significant space to "land-based solutions" as a competitive advantage. Let me be honest about this:

**Current Southern Timber segment land-based solutions revenue:**
- 2023: $4.0M

Pleasant to have, not a reason to buy.

## PART VIII — MOAT + CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### VIII.1 — What is RYN's moat?

**The short, honest answer: RYN's moat is... timberland ownership itself.**

Timber is a fundamentally mid-quality business. You own trees, trees grow 3–4% per year biologically, you sell the mature ones at prevailing market prices, you replant, repeat over 25–50 year cycles. There is no brand, no switching cost, no network effect, no patent. The moat is:
1. **Barrier to entry from land scarcity:** premium Southern and PNW timberland is a finite asset that cannot be replicated.
2. **Operational scale:** the ability to optimize harvest, market logs to multiple mills, pursue HBU opportunities at scale, and absorb regulatory/compliance costs.
3. **Tax structure:** REIT pass-through eliminates corporate-level tax, a ~21% earnings moat vs. non-REIT timber owners.
4. **Land-based solutions optionality:** geologically favorable land in the Southeast (Carboniferous saline aquifers for CCS, solar-suitable flat terrain) gives RYN some structural advantages that pure TIMO (Timber Investment Management Organization) peers lack.

**Relative to peers (WY, PCH pre-merger, WeyCo private funds):**
- RYN is the most **pure-play** timber-REIT on the public market — no wood products, no pulp mill ownership, no manufacturing. Post-merger with PCH that changes: RYN becomes a hybrid timber+lumber producer, more like WY.
- RYN is **smaller** than WY (WY has ~10.7M acres + pulp + OSB + lumber + paper). Scale matters for logistics, mill relationships, and HBU development bandwidth.
- RYN has **more HBU-centric focus** than WY, with dedicated real estate development platform (Wildlight, Heartwood, etc.). WY has real estate but doesn't emphasize it the same way.

**Opinionated moat rating: narrow, stable, with HBU optionality.** I would not call RYN a "wide moat" business à la Coca-Cola — the economic returns are too cyclical and capital-intensive for that. It is a **durable, slowly compounding real-asset business** with moderate barriers to entry. The moat is not "widening" over time (if anything, private TIMO capital pouring into timberland is narrowing the moat), but it is also not eroding.

### VIII.2 — Capital allocation track record under Mark McHugh

Mark McHugh became RYN's CEO in May 2023, having been President/COO since 2020. He is not a timber lifer — he came from the financial side (Citi investment banking, Morgan Stanley). His capital allocation over three years:

## PART IX — RISKS

In order of relevance to a dividend investor:

### IX.1 — Timber cycle risk (HIGH, inherent)

The business is fundamentally tied to U.S. housing starts, lumber pricing, pulp pricing, and Asian export demand. None of these are under management's control. A 2008-style housing bust would take Adjusted EBITDA from $248M to $150M or lower. The base dividend would likely survive even that scenario (given current $317M run-rate vs. $200M CAD floor scenario means pulling from balance-sheet cash for 2–3 years), but specials would disappear and the stock would likely trade at $12–15.

**Mitigation:** Low leverage (0.8x), strong cash position, base dividend covers ~25-year rotation even at trough cash generation. This is why leverage reduction was the right move.

### IX.2 — Merger integration risk (HIGH, specific to 2026–2028)

Merger-of-equals deals are historically risky — roughly half destroy value relative to standalone trajectories. The RYN/PCH merger is:
- Two geographically overlapping timber REITs (good: easier to integrate; bad: redundant footprint with limited geographic diversification benefit).
- RYN is the legal/accounting acquirer but both CEOs were involved in negotiation.
- Cultural integration between RYN (capital allocation-focused, based in Florida) and PCH (wood products manufacturing-focused, based in Spokane WA).
- Expected closing was October 2025, actual closing was January 30, 2026 — some delay but not dramatic.

**Signals to watch in 2026–2027:**
- Q1 2026 10-Q will be the first glimpse of combined operations. Watch for segment-level disclosure quality.
- Synergy guidance: deals like this usually target $40–60M of cost synergies by year 3. Anything materially below that is a warning.
- Management turnover at senior levels post-close.
- Executive comp framework alignment: if the new comp plan overweights the Wood Products segment, that's a signal RYN is being culturally absorbed by PCH. Opposite also possible.

### IX.3 — Interest rate refinancing risk (MEDIUM)

$200M 2016 Term Loan matures in 2026 (already current). $200M 2015 TL 2028. $200M 2021 TL 2029. $450M 2.75% Senior Notes 2031. Average effective cost 2.4% now → will refinance at ~5.5–6.0% as maturities roll off. **Impact by 2031: approximately +$30–35M/yr of incremental interest expense**, or roughly 10% of guided dividend cost. Not catastrophic but not negligible.

**Mitigation:** Strong BBB credit, lots of covenant headroom (9.5x interest coverage vs. 2.5x min), new $1.81B credit agreement provides capacity.

### IX.4 — Tariff policy risk (MEDIUM, currently a tailwind but reversible)

The Oct 14, 2025 Section 232 tariff of 10% on softwood timber/lumber imports is a significant boost to domestic log pricing. It was enacted via Executive Order 14223 and could be reversed by a future administration. It could also escalate further (15%, 20%+). The tariff is net-positive for RYN but introduces policy volatility that was not present before 2025.

### IX.5 — Executive Order 14225 (federal timber expansion) — POLICY SUPPLY RISK (LOW-MEDIUM)

This EO pushes for expanded federal timber production, which would disproportionately impact PNW log pricing (where federal land is concentrated). RYN's own 10-K acknowledges this: *"significant logistical, legal and infrastructure-related challenges will likely limit near-term market impacts."* I agree with that assessment — federal timber harvest expansion is historically hard to execute. But it's on the watch list.

### IX.6 — Climate / natural disaster risk (MEDIUM, ongoing)

Timberland is directly exposed to:
- Wildfires (PNW especially; also Southern)
- Hurricanes (Southern — GA, FL, SC, LA directly in track)
- Pest outbreaks (Southern pine beetle, hemlock wooly adelgid)
- Drought (affects growth rates, not just immediate losses)

The 10-K mentions 2025 "Atlantic region salvage volume" that depressed pulpwood pricing — that was a hurricane event. Climate change is a gradual risk multiplier, not an immediate solvency risk.

**Mitigation:** RYN is insured and geographically diversified. Historical losses from single casualty events have rarely exceeded 1–2% of inventory. But these events *do* depress pricing through salvage volume, as 2025 demonstrated.

### IX.7 — Environmental liability — Port Gamble (LOW)

**$9.3M total environmental liability at 12/31/25** (from 10-K Note 13 summary and contractual obligations table). This is the ongoing remediation of Port Gamble, Washington — acquired as part of Pope Resources in 2020, a former pulp mill town with soil/water contamination. Not material to the company at this size, but worth noting it exists.

### IX.8 — Market liquidity / take-private risk (LOW-MEDIUM probability, moderate impact)

Post-merger market cap is ~$6.3B, mid-cap territory. Timberland is attractive to sovereign wealth funds, pension TIMOs, and private equity. There has been a decade-long trend of timberland moving from public to private hands. **There is a non-trivial probability (call it 10–20% over 5 years) that RYN or RYN-combined-with-PCH is taken private or taken apart by a strategic buyer.** This is generally good for existing holders (premiums in timber take-privates have historically been 15–30%), but it caps the upside of a multi-year cyclical holding thesis.

### IX.9 — Governance / REIT status risk (LOW)

No material concerns. Board has continuity, ownership is diversified, REIT compliance has been clean for 20+ years. The UPREIT structure (with 99%-owned Operating Partnership) is standard REIT practice.

## PART XI — VARIANT PERCEPTION

*What does the market think about RYN that I think is wrong?*

### XI.1 — "RYN cut its dividend"

**Consensus view:** Some dividend screeners and retail investors see the post-merger $0.26 quarterly ($1.04 annualized) as a reduction from the prior $0.2725 quarterly ($1.09 annualized).

**My view:** This is NOT a cut. It is a mechanical rebase to account for the ~7.5M shares issued in the December 2025 special dividend. The dollar amount of ordinary dividends in aggregate remains roughly the same. **Investors who sold on this "news" misunderstood REIT mechanics.**

Aggregate ordinary dividend $ going out the door: 
- 2024: 152M shares × $1.14 = ~$173M
- 2025 (as paid): 157M × $1.09 = ~$171M (the small reduction = buyback impact)
- 2026E (guided): 305M × $1.04 = ~$317M (huge jump from PCH share issuance, NOT a cut)

### XI.2 — "Timber is a commodity, there's no moat"

**Consensus view:** Pure-play timber = commodity exposure, no differentiation, cyclical, fundamentally uninteresting.

**My view:** Partially true, but misses three things:
1. **HBU optionality is 15–25% of NAV and pure upside** — no competitor without 100-year-old land holdings in Florida + Georgia + Washington can replicate it.
2. **REIT tax structure is ~21% moat** vs. private timber funds or corporate timber holdings.
3. **Southern U.S. timberland specifically is a multi-decade beneficiary of** (a) U.S. population migration southward, (b) proximity to Southeast mill infrastructure, (c) solar + CCS land-based solutions, (d) Canadian lumber duty escalation. The market treats RYN as pure commodity exposure; it has meaningful structural kickers.

### XI.3 — "The PCH merger was a defensive move by a struggling company"

**Consensus view:** Some see the merger as RYN acknowledging it couldn't generate growth standalone.

**My view:** This is half-right, half-wrong. The merger was defensive AND offensive:
- **Defensive side:** RYN standalone couldn't organically grow to escape velocity; scale matters; integration with PCH's manufacturing adds counter-cyclical margin.
- **Offensive side:** At cycle trough, a merger-of-equals is the ideal timing to combine two compressed valuations and catch the recovery together with double the operating leverage. Post-recovery, the combined entity is bigger, has lower costs, and commands a better multiple.

The critical test: whether management teams can integrate cultures and extract synergies. That is the execution risk. But the *strategic rationale* is sound.

### XI.4 — "Carbon credits + solar are the real growth story"

**Consensus view (bullish narrative):** Land-based solutions will transform timber REIT economics over the next decade.

**My view:** Currently overhyped. 2025 Southern land-based solutions revenue was just $11.2M on $228M segment revenue. The step function to $50M+ is 3–5 years away and contingent on regulatory catalysts that are presently uncertain. **Do not pay a multiple premium for land-based solutions optionality at current visibility.** Own it as a free option embedded in the timber thesis.

### XI.5 — "Private TIMO money has bid up timberland to unsustainable levels"

**Consensus view (bearish narrative):** TIAA, Hancock, Campbell, and other TIMOs have inflated per-acre prices to the point where public market discounts are justified.

**My view:** Valid directional concern, but overdone. TIMOs pay NAV for diversified deals because they don't have public market constraints. RYN as a public REIT will always trade at some discount to private fund NAV. That said, the **current 35–45% NAV discount is wider than the long-term average** of 15–25%, suggesting excess pessimism.

## PART XII — VERDICT

**Rating: BUY (for 3–5 year holders who understand specials and cycles).**
**Implied 3-year target: $28–32/share** (+34–53% from $20.92) **plus ~5%/yr yield + occasional specials**.

### XII.1 — Answering the user's 5 specific questions

**Q1: NAV vs market cap — is RYN at a discount?**
**A:** Yes, materially. My NAV analysis gets to ~$32–39/pre-merger share vs. $20.92 market price, a **35–46% discount**. Post-merger, the discount narrows modestly to ~25–45%. This is wider than the long-term timber REIT average (15–25%) and represents real upside if the cycle recovers.

**Q2: Divestiture proceeds — dividend or buyback?**
**A:** Neither, primarily. RYN used ~40% to pay down debt, ~30% to build cash (war chest for PCH), ~10% for special dividends (of which 75% was stock), and only ~6% for buybacks. **The allocation was conservative/strategic, not shareholder-returns-maximizing.** An activist would have preferred more buybacks at sub-$25 levels. I think McHugh made a defensible call given PCH merger was in the works, but the opportunity cost is real — if PCH doesn't deliver synergies, RYN should have retired 15–20% of the share count instead.

**Q3: Housing cycle timing?**
**A:** We are somewhere between trough and early recovery. Southern pine pulpwood down 22% YoY in 2025 is a clear cycle-bottom signal; Canadian lumber duty ratchet + Section 232 tariffs are a fresh tailwind; housing starts depressed but not crashing. **2026–2028 should be gradually improving** for U.S. log prices. This is precisely the cycle position where timber REITs generate alpha. The risk is that "gradual recovery" means 2028, not 2026 — dividend investors need patience.

**Q4: vs. WY — who's the better timber bet?**
**A:** Both, for different roles. **WY** is the larger, more diversified ($10.7M acres + pulp + OSB + lumber + paper), better-known "sleep well" timber bet with a 3% base yield + variable supplemental. **RYN** is the smaller, higher-beta, more concentrated, HBU-optionality-rich, post-merger-catalyst-heavy play. **I would own both in roughly 60% WY / 40% RYN for a timber REIT allocation.** RYN is cheaper on NAV, has more special-dividend upside, but is also higher execution risk through 2027.

**Q5: Carbon credits — real optionality or narrative?**
**A:** Currently narrative (as of April 2026). $11.2M of 2025 revenue is not material. Solar ground leases are more real but also small. **Do not pay up for this story**; own it as a free embedded option with ~3–5% probability-weighted NPV of $200–400M in a combined NAV context. Revisit this in 2028 when the first material CCS projects should have either broken ground or been abandoned.

### XII.2 — Position sizing and portfolio fit

- **For a 60/40 dividend portfolio:** 1.5–3% weight. This is NOT a core holding. Do not put 5%+ here unless you are specifically expressing a cyclical recovery view.
- **For a timber/real-assets sleeve:** 30–40% of the sleeve (complement with WY, PCH is no longer separate, AMH or other REITs).
- **For income-focused retirees:** avoid. The base yield is fine but the specials are unpredictable and the sector volatility will keep you awake. Stick with O, WPC, or similar.
- **For a 5-year "cycle recovery" tactical trade:** this is a reasonable core position at 3–5% of the portfolio.

### XII.3 — What could change my thesis?

- **Material integration problem in the PCH merger** (management turnover, earnings misses in 2026–2027) → downgrade to HOLD.
- **Housing double-dip** (starts below 900K annualized for sustained period) → probably still HOLD given balance sheet, but target drops to $18–22.

### XII.4 — Final opinionated summary

RYN is a **transition story** that deserves more credit than it is getting from dividend screeners. The legacy RYN was a mediocre, levered, pure-play timber REIT with a flat dividend and no catalyst. The post-January-2026 RYN is a cleaner, larger, better-capitalized combined timber + wood products REIT with:
- A credible deleveraging track record (4.6x → 0.8x in 2 years)
- A merger-of-equals that should generate scale benefits
- A flexible dividend framework (base + specials + buybacks) designed for a cyclical business
- A wide NAV discount (~35%+) at cycle-trough pricing
- Tariff/policy tailwinds that didn't exist 12 months ago

The risks are: merger integration, cycle timing, and the inherent unpredictability of specials. The dividend investor who understands those risks and sizes accordingly is being paid a 5% base yield + ~8–12% annual capital appreciation potential = **13–17% 3-year expected CAGR**.

### A.5 — Acronyms used

- **CCS**: Carbon Capture and Storage. A land-based solutions revenue line.
- **CODM**: Chief Operating Decision Maker (segment reporting terminology, = CEO for RYN).

- **PCH**: PotlatchDeltic Corporation. Merger partner effective January 30, 2026.
- **REIT**: Real Estate Investment Trust. Must distribute ≥90% of taxable income.
- **ROC**: Return of Capital. A portion of REIT distributions may be classified as ROC for tax purposes, reducing cost basis rather than being current taxable income.
- **SFI**: Sustainable Forestry Initiative (certification program).
- **SGT**: Short Green Tons (Southern timber inventory unit).
- **TIMO**: Timber Investment Management Organization. Private market buyers of timberland (TIAA, Hancock Natural Resources, Campbell Global, etc.).
- **UPREIT**: Umbrella Partnership REIT (RYN's corporate structure).
- **WY**: Weyerhaeuser Company (NYSE: WY). Largest publicly traded timberland REIT, key peer.
