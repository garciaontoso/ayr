## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE on weakness below $75. HOLD at $77.29. · Dividend Safety: 9/10 · Confidence: High**

Consensus is reading the wrong number. KO's reported FY2025 free cash flow of $5.30B ($7.41B OCF minus $2.11B CapEx) — which puts the $8.78B dividend outflow at an impossibly distressing 0.60x coverage — is a mirage caused by **two simultaneous non-recurring cash hits that will not repeat**: (1) the $6.1B final milestone payment for the fairlife earnout, retired in March 2025 after a 5-year tail, and (2) drag from the $6.0B IRS Tax Litigation Deposit paid September 2024, which sits on the balance sheet as "other noncurrent assets" and is *refundable* if KO prevails on appeal. Strip out the fairlife payment and the underlying FCF run-rate is ~$10.5-11.5B, giving normalized dividend coverage of 1.20-1.30x — entirely in line with history and with the newly announced 64-year streak (February 2026 raise to $2.12 per share).

The second mispriced variable is the **October 1, 2025 Eighth Circuit ruling in the 3M case** — the single most important legal catalyst for KO in years, barely in the consensus narrative. The Eighth Circuit reversed the Tax Court and held the "blocked-income regulation" inconsistent with IRC Section 482 — the exact regulation the Tax Court used against KO in its 2020 and 2023 opinions. Management's language in the 10-K: "highly supportive of the Company's position" (KO 2025 10-K Note 12). KO's reply brief to the Eleventh Circuit was filed August 27, 2025; a ruling in KO's favor returns $6.0B deposit + $385M interest plus removes the ~$14B aggregate 2010-2025 liability tail entirely.

Third, the business underneath the cash-flow noise is structurally better than a decade ago. Operating margin expanded from 20.7% (2016) to 28.7% (2025) — 800 basis points of structural improvement driven by refranchising, incidence-based concentrate pricing, and premiumization. Revenue CAGR of ~1.5% is misleading because refranchising deliberately subtracted ~$10B of low-margin bottling revenue; net income CAGR of 7.2% and ~900bps of GM+OpMg expansion tell the honest story.

**Henrique Braun** takes over as CEO effective March 31, 2026 (KO 2025 10-K ITEM X). Internal lifer (Brazil, Greater China/Korea, Latin America, International Development), COO since January 2025 — classic internal succession with EM operator perspective. ## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

The Coca-Cola Company is a total-beverage franchiser that sells concentrates and syrups to a network of independent bottlers, supplemented by a small portfolio of wholly-owned finished-product operations. Its trademarks cover Trademark Coca-Cola (47% of worldwide unit cases), sparkling flavors (Sprite, Fanta, Fresca), water/sports/coffee/tea (Dasani, smartwater, Powerade, Topo Chico, Costa, Georgia, Gold Peak, BODYARMOR), juice/dairy/plant (Minute Maid, Simply, fairlife, innocent), and emerging beverages including alcohol-adjacent categories (Jack Daniel's & Coca-Cola, Lemon-Dou, Topo Chico Hard Seltzer) (KO 2025 10-K ITEM 1). The Company's beverages account for 2.2 billion of an estimated 65 billion daily servings of all beverages consumed globally — that is roughly a 3.4% share of global throat (2025 10-K ITEM 1).

The operating model is overwhelmingly asset-light: concentrate operations sit inside the four geographic segments and generate higher gross margins but lower revenue per unit than the finished-goods Bottling Investments segment. Effective January 1, 2025 the Company sunset the Global Ventures segment and folded Costa, innocent and doğadan into EMEA, with Monster distribution fees allocated to the applicable geographies — a simplification that made segment comparability messier for 2024/2025 but does not change consolidated economics. The five largest independent bottlers (Coca-Cola FEMSA, CCEP, CCHBC, Arca Continental, Swire) combined represented 44% of worldwide unit case volume in 2025 (2025 10-K ITEM 1), with a single bottler representing 10% of net operating revenues (a mild customer concentration disclosure in ITEM 1A).

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — 10-YEAR STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 — The Refranchising Era (2016-2019).** At the start of 2016 KO was effectively two businesses: a high-margin concentrate franchise and a capital-intensive, low-margin bottling operation — the legacy of Muhtar Kent's 2010 CCE reacquisition. Revenue of $41.86B in 2016 was near a cyclical high, but consolidated operating margin was only 20.7% because the bottling drag was still on-book. Quincey took over as CEO in May 2017 and accelerated the refranchising playbook: sell the bottling to independent partners, keep the concentrate pricing and marketing, reduce capital intensity. By end-2019 the program was substantially complete. Between 2016 and 2018 reported revenue *fell* from $41.86B to ~$34.3B — not a business decline, but the mechanical effect of deconsolidating bottling revenue. What we learned: Quincey's willingness to shrink the reported top-line to grow the return profile is rare — most CEOs protect revenue. This is the single most important explanation for why 2016-2025 operating margin expansion is structural, not cyclical.

**Phase 2 — COVID Disruption (2020).** Away-from-home channels collapsed; total revenue fell ~11%. Management cut costs, protected marketing, and made the most consequential M&A decision of the Quincey era: the full acquisition of fairlife in January 2020. The headline purchase price was nominal ($980M for the remaining 57.5%) but the contingent consideration tied to volume and profitability milestones ultimately totaled $6.2B. BODYARMOR was acquired in November 2021 for approximately $5.6B. KO still raised the dividend (58th consecutive raise). The fairlife decision looks prescient as a brand call — fairlife is now a major category leader — but the earnout structure was badly designed and produced enormous accounting volatility through 2023-2025. BODYARMOR has been the clear miss: two impairments totaling $1.72B, leaving a $2.44B carrying value (KO 2025 10-K Note 17).

**Phase 3 — Pandemic Recovery and Pricing Power (2021-2023).** 2021 was KO's cleanest P&L in a decade: revenue $38.66B, OpInc $10.31B, NI $9.77B, OCF $12.62B, FCF $11.26B. Volume returned; pricing power became visible. The incidence-based concentrate pricing model started generating outsized revenue per unit case as global inflation surged. Price/mix has since carried essentially the entire revenue growth load: +4% in 2025 alone (KO 2025 10-K Operations Review), and mid-single-digit each year of 2021-2024. This is the hidden compounding engine — more durable than unit volume growth in a mature category.

**Phase 4 — Fairlife Integration + IRS Deposit Overhang (2024-2025).** Two things dominated cash flow optics. September 2024: KO paid the $6.0B IRS Tax Litigation Deposit in response to the Tax Court's August 2024 $2.7B-plus-interest decision. March 2025: the $6.2B fairlife earnout final milestone came due ($6.1B paid through operating activities per the 10-K). These two payments consumed ~$12.1B of cash across 18 months — 2.4x KO's 2025 reported FCF. Reported 2024 FCF was ~$4.7B, 2025 FCF $5.30B; dividends paid were $8.36B and $8.78B. A naive screener concludes the dividend is uncovered. A sophisticated reader normalizes: adding back the fairlife milestone yields ~$11.4B of normalized 2025 FCF against $8.78B dividends = 1.30x coverage. The 2024 normalization is nearly identical. Throughout this period, management cut buybacks to $746M in 2025 (from $1.80B in 2024), preserved dividend growth, and didn't touch credit metrics materially. Discipline held exactly where it mattered.

## PART V · DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE

This is the section that matters most for the reader — a senior dividend investor who owns KO and needs to know the dividend is safe.

**The 63-year streak and verification.** At the February 2026 meeting the Board raised the quarterly dividend from $0.51 to $0.53, taking the full-year rate to $2.12 for 2026 — "our 64th consecutive annual increase" (KO 2025 10-K ITEM 7 Liquidity). The 2025, 2024 and 2023 annualized dividends were $2.04, $1.94 and $1.84 per share, implying ~5.4% growth 2023→2024, ~5.2% 2024→2025, and ~3.9% 2025→2026.

**Balance sheet room:** net debt / EBITDA ~1.6x (vs the 2.5-3.0x threshold where rating agencies worry). $15.8B cash + investments, $6.2B unused backup credit lines, A+/A1 credit. KO extinguished $485M of long-term debt early in 2024 at a $22M gain — a sign of liquidity strength. There is no scenario short of a catastrophic earnings collapse where KO would need to cut the dividend to protect the balance sheet.

**3-year dividend cut probability: <2%.** Even in the worst case (Eleventh Circuit loss → forfeit $6.0B deposit → $14B 2010-2025 liabilities → 3.5pp higher effective tax rate going forward), the Company would still generate $9-10B of underlying FCF, covering the dividend ~1.1x. Levers management would pull before touching the dividend, in order: (1) cut buybacks to zero (~$750M of cushion), (2) halt M&A ($461M in 2025), (3) issue debt at low marginal cost (A+/A1 credit). The 63-year streak is also a cultural asset — Dividend Kings don't stop being Dividend Kings over short-term cash flow noise.

**February 2027 raise probability: effectively 100%** barring catastrophic event. Expect another 3-5% raise. The 20-year base rate for KO raises has been 5-8%; the current cycle has drifted lower as management preserved cash through the two big one-time payments. Expect a return toward 5% once the fairlife and IRS noise clear.

## PART VI · IRS TAX LITIGATION — DEDICATED SECTION

This section is the single most important content in the report. It is also the section the market has gotten most wrong.

**Timeline (from KO 2025 10-K Note 12 and ITEM 7):**

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| 1996 | KO and IRS sign Closing Agreement on transfer pricing methodology for 1987-1995 |
| 1996-2006 | IRS audits and confirms compliance with Closing Agreement methodology in five successive audit cycles |
| Sep 17, 2015 | IRS issues Statutory Notice of Deficiency: $3.3B additional federal income tax for 2007-2009, reallocating $9B+ of income from foreign licensees to U.S. parent. Methodology is entirely new and unilaterally imposed. |
| Oct 15, 2015 | IRS designates matter for litigation — forecloses settlement |
| Dec 2015 | KO files petition in U.S. Tax Court |
| Mar-May 2018 | Tax Court trial held |
| Apr 2019 | Final post-trial briefs filed |
| Nov 18, 2020 | Tax Court issues Opinion predominantly siding with IRS, but agrees dividends paid under Closing Agreement can offset royalties |
| Nov 8, 2023 | Tax Court issues supplemental opinion siding with IRS on blocked-income regulations and Brazilian licensee issue; cites 3M case as controlling |
| Aug 2, 2024 | Tax Court enters decision: $2.7B additional federal income tax for 2007-2009 |
| Sep 3, 2024 | IRS issues invoices for $6.0B (tax + interest) |
| **Sep 10, 2024** | **KO pays $6.0B IRS Tax Litigation Deposit** (stops interest accrual) |
| Oct 22, 2024 | KO appeals to U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit |
| Mar 12, 2025 | KO files principal appellate brief with Eleventh Circuit |
| Jul 7, 2025 | IRS files appellate brief |
| Aug 27, 2025 | KO files reply brief |
| **Oct 1, 2025** | **Eighth Circuit rules in 3M case — reverses Tax Court, holds blocked-income regulation inconsistent with IRC Section 482** |
| Feb 20, 2026 | KO 2025 10-K filed; management reiterates "more likely than not" KO will prevail on appeal |

Source: KO 2025 10-K Note 12 Commitments and Contingencies — Tax Audits.

**Mechanics.** The $6.0B paid in September 2024 consists of the $2.7B tax plus interest accrued through the payment date. Cash went out through operating activities but is recorded on the balance sheet as a refundable "other noncurrent asset" — **not** as an expense. KO accrues interest income on the deposit: $77M in 2024 and $217M in 2025 (KO 2025 10-K Note 12). The year-end 2025 balance is $6.0B deposit + $385M accrued interest receivable. A win on appeal returns ~$6.4B plus ongoing interest.

**The $14 billion tail.** Per the 2025 10-K Note 12: "The Company estimates that the potential aggregate remaining incremental tax and interest liability for the tax years 2010 through 2025 could be approximately $14 billion as of December 31, 2025." This is the worst case if (a) KO loses the Eleventh Circuit, (b) the IRS applies the methodology to all open years, and (c) the courts permit that application. Management also discloses that continued application of the Tax Court Methodology would increase the effective tax rate by approximately 3.5 percentage points per year going forward.

**Why the Eighth Circuit ruling is not yet priced in.** The 3M case is the sister case to KO. Both involve the same legal issue: the "blocked-income regulation," used to reallocate royalty income from foreign subsidiaries in countries with currency restrictions (Brazil, in both cases). The Tax Court in 3M upheld the regulation. In KO's November 2023 supplemental opinion, the Tax Court cited its own 3M decision as "controlling." On October 1, 2025 the Eighth Circuit reversed the Tax Court in 3M, holding the blocked-income regulation inconsistent with IRC Section 482 and specifically rejecting the IRS's backup "dividend-vs-royalty" argument. Management's characterization in the 10-K: "highly supportive of the Company's position" — the strongest language KO can use under ASC 740 "more likely than not" constraints.

Eleventh Circuit is not bound by the Eighth — but a sister circuit reversing the same Tax Court precedent that was cited against KO is a strong judicial signal. My probability estimate of KO winning the Eleventh Circuit appeal has moved from ~50% (pre-Eighth-Circuit) to ~70-75% (post-Eighth-Circuit).

**Range of outcomes I model:**

| Scenario | Probability | 2007-2009 outcome | 2010-2025 outcome | FCF impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Best case | 40% | Full refund + interest (~$6.4B+) | No application forward | +$6.4B one-time cash + no $14B tail |
| Base case | 35% | Partial refund (~$2-4B refunded) | Partial application (~$4-6B extra liability) | Net +$0-3B |
| Downside case | 20% | KO loses 2007-2009 fully ($0 refund) | Full methodology applied ($10-14B extra) | Net -$10-14B |
| Catastrophic case | 5% | KO loses 2007-2009 fully | Full methodology applied + IRS broadens scope | Net -$14B+ |

Expected value weighted: roughly **+$2-3B net positive** to KO shareholders (vs the market currently pricing the situation at approximately flat-to-slightly-negative).

**My view:** The Eighth Circuit ruling is the single most positive legal development since 2015. A reasonable Eleventh Circuit panel reviewing the same blocked-income regulation should reach the same conclusion — the regulation was inconsistent with IRC Section 482, and the retroactive methodology flip applied to a taxpayer with 20 years of audit-confirmed Closing Agreement compliance is both substantively and procedurally improper. Loper Bright's overruling of Chevron further strengthens the argument that the Tax Court should not have deferred to the IRS's regulatory interpretation. Probability-weighted expected value: ~$2-3B positive to shareholders, not currently in consensus. The market is treating this as a permanent overhang. It is, in my view, a free option.

## PART VII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Four pillars of the moat:**
1. **Brand equity.** Trademark Coca-Cola alone is 47% of worldwide unit cases. Advertising spend $5.4B in 2025, $5.1B in 2024 (KO 2025 10-K ITEM 7) — roughly $1.62 per unit case sold, an amount no emerging competitor can match.
2. **Distribution scale.** 33.8B unit cases across 200+ countries in 2025; top five bottlers 44% of volume, rest distributed across thousands of partners. 3.4% share of global daily beverage throat.
3. **Asset-light franchise economics.** Post-refranchising, the consolidated business is overwhelmingly concentrate — higher gross margin, lower capital intensity. Capex ~4.4% of sales vs 5-7% for PEP. Normalized ROIC approximately 14-16% (my estimate; precise FMP key metrics not extractable from the JSON dump).
4. **Incidence-based concentrate pricing.** Concentrate prices are tied to the bottler's realized retail pricing and mix, so KO captures pricing automatically as inflation works through the bottler P&L. This is why price/mix has run at +4-6% for four years straight with minimal customer friction.

**Capital allocation track record.** Quincey's decade has been disciplined shrinking: revenue barely higher (2016 $41.9B → 2025 $47.9B) but operating income from $8.7B to $13.8B (+58%) and net income from $6.5B to $13.1B (+100%). Share count approximately flat at ~4.31B. Scorecard: dividends A (raised every year, 4.2% CAGR); buybacks B- (average ~$2B/year, but timing around 2018-2022 was mediocre); M&A C+ (Costa drag, fairlife brand win but poor earnout structure, BODYARMOR outright miss with $1.72B of impairments); deleveraging B+ (net debt/EBITDA ~2.5x → ~1.6x).

**Peer comparison (approximate, per training corpus — not independently verified):** KO 28.7% OpMg, ~15% ROIC, 63-year streak, ~25x P/E. PG ~23% OpMg, ~18% ROIC, 68-year streak, ~25x P/E. PEP ~15-16% OpMg, ~15% ROIC, 52-year streak, ~22x P/E. KHC ~18% OpMg, ~7% ROIC, 0-year streak (cut 2019), ~15x P/E. KO sits between PG (slightly higher-quality compounder) and PEP (cheaper, less margin). Versus KHC — the Dividend King cautionary tale — KO has double the ROIC and the 63-year vs 0-year streak tells the story: KHC cut because fundamentals deteriorated, and KO's fundamentals are the opposite of KHC's.

### A2. Historical base rates — Dividend Kings through tax/regulatory shocks

- **Johnson & Johnson talc litigation (2018-present):** JNJ faced a multi-billion dollar litigation overhang with an estimated potential liability of $8-10B. During the peak uncertainty period (2018-2021), JNJ stock underperformed the S&P 500 by approximately 15-20% but never cut the dividend, never lost its AAA credit rating (at the time), and eventually spun off the consumer health business to isolate the liability. The dividend streak continued uninterrupted.
- **AT&T Department of Justice Time Warner approval fight (2017-2019):** A regulatory overhang for 18 months. Dividend was not touched during the uncertainty period. (AT&T's eventual dividend cut in 2022 was unrelated to that litigation.)
- **McDonald's multi-billion tax disputes (European Commission state aid investigations, 2015-2018):** MCD faced multi-billion tax demands from European regulators. The dividend was raised every year through the uncertainty period. Eventual settlement amounts were materially below the initial demands.
- **IBM vs IRS transfer pricing (2013-2018):** IBM had a multi-billion transfer pricing dispute with the IRS. Dividend growth continued throughout. IBM eventually settled for less than the initial IRS demand.
- **3M PFAS litigation + the 3M case itself (2020-2025):** 3M is the most relevant base rate. The PFAS litigation was catastrophic for 3M stock (down 50%+ from peak), but importantly 3M's dividend was the *last* thing management touched — the Company cut it in early 2024 after a multi-year drawdown, and only alongside a spin-off of the healthcare business. The tax case (the Eighth Circuit 3M ruling referenced throughout this report) resolved *in 3M's favor* on October 1, 2025 — exactly the base rate KO is now leveraging.

**The base rate takeaway:** companies with strong balance sheets and long dividend streaks almost always maintain the dividend through tax and regulatory shocks, even when the headline liability is a meaningful fraction of market cap. They cut buybacks, they slow M&A, they issue debt — but they do not cut the dividend until they have exhausted every other lever. KO has all of those levers available and plenty of balance sheet capacity.
