## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD · Dividend Safety: 9/10 · Confidence: High**

The Coca-Cola Company closes fiscal 2025 at a pivotal moment: **James Quincey's retirement as CEO** after nearly a decade at the helm, handing over to **Henrique Braun** who takes the role effective 2026. The Q4 2025 earnings call was Quincey's last, and it served as both a valedictory retrospective and a strategic handover. The financial picture is **solid but not exciting**: organic revenue growth of 5% for the year, FCF of $11.4B (+$600M vs 2024), operating margin expansion of approximately 50 basis points, and a **63rd consecutive year of dividend growth** — placing KO firmly in the Dividend Kings pantheon.

The bear case is real but not dividend-threatening: **unit case volume growth has decelerated to 1% in Q4** (from 2% historical norms), price/mix is carrying the entire revenue growth load (roughly 4 points of pricing offset by 3 points of unfavorable mix), and currency headwinds of 5% are eating into reported numbers. The mature core beverage categories in developed markets are structurally volume-challenged. The company is increasingly a **pricing + margin expansion + buyback story**, not an organic volume growth story.

For a long-term dividend investor, **the 9/10 safety score is well-earned**: $11.4B FCF comfortably covers the ~$8.5B dividend outlay, the balance sheet has steadily deleveraged, the brand portfolio has expanded to 32 billion-dollar brands, and management's capital discipline is evident in the refranchising completion. The **HOLD verdict** reflects that at current multiples (P/E ~26x, dividend yield ~3.1%), there's little margin of safety for new capital — but absolutely no reason to trim a long-held position.

**Top 3 reasons to own**:
1. Dividend King with 63-year streak, FCF coverage 1.34x, payout ratio on FCF ~72%
2. Demonstrated pricing power across 4 consecutive quarters despite volume headwinds
3. Strategic continuity through leadership transition (Braun is Quincey's internal successor, not an outsider)

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add**:
1. Volume growth structurally anemic in developed markets; Latin America and ASEAN showing pressure
2. Valuation premium vs historical (P/E 26x vs 10y avg ~22x) without corresponding growth acceleration
3. CEO transition risk is always non-zero, even for an internal successor

**Key catalysts next 12 months**:
- **CAGNY 2026 (Feb)**: Braun's first strategic presentation, will signal continuity vs change
- **2026 guidance execution**: organic revenue growth 4-5%, comparable EPS 7-8% (Quincey's parting commitment)
- **Mexico sugar tax (Jan 2026)**: impact on Latin America volume and pricing

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

### Business description
The Coca-Cola Company is the world's largest non-alcoholic beverage company, owning concentrate operations (the high-margin core) and a global network of bottling partners (largely refranchised). The company's strategy over the last decade, culminating in 2025, has been to:
1. Complete refranchising (asset-light model)
2. Expand beyond sparkling soft drinks into water, coffee, tea, sports drinks, juice, plant-based
3. Build a portfolio of billion-dollar brands (now **32**, up from 20 in 2017 — Quincey cited this explicitly in his farewell remarks)
4. Digitize the enterprise and strengthen bottler alignment

### Operating segments
KO reports by geography + Global Ventures + Bottling Investments:
- **North America** (~35% of operating profit)
- **Latin America** (premium margins, recently under pressure from Mexico sugar tax)
- **Europe, Middle East & Africa** (EMEA)
- **Asia Pacific** (includes China + ASEAN + Japan + India)
- **Global Ventures** (Costa Coffee, fairlife, Innocent)
- **Bottling Investments Group (BIG)** (residual bottling ops pre-refranchising)

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — 2015-2025 STRATEGIC ARC

_Context: this narrative is anchored on Quincey's own retrospective in the Q4 2025 farewell call, cross-referenced with general knowledge of KO's public disclosures._

### 2017 — The Quincey Era Begins
Quincey took over as CEO in May 2017 and set **four strategic priorities** at CAGNY 2017 (he explicitly recited them in his Q4 2025 farewell):
1. Accelerating a consumer-centric brand portfolio
2. Strengthening the KO system (alignment with bottling partners)
3. Digitizing the enterprise
4. Unlocking the power of people

The 2017 baseline was a company struggling with **declining legacy sparkling volume**, an overextended bottling footprint, and a portfolio too concentrated in a category consumers were moving away from.

### 2017-2020 — Refranchising + Portfolio Expansion
The company completed the refranchising of its North American bottling operations (a multi-year asset-light transformation), acquired **Costa Coffee** (2019, $5.1B — later widely seen as disappointing from a returns standpoint), and accelerated the build-out of billion-dollar brands outside sparkling. By Quincey's own count in 2025: **12 new billion-dollar brands added** during his tenure, bringing the total to 32.

### 2020 — COVID Shock
Out-of-home beverage consumption collapsed in Q2 2020. KO's revenues declined ~11% that year. The dividend was **maintained** (critical for the Dividend King streak) but growth decelerated to $0.04/share increase only — the smallest in years. Management resisted the temptation to pause the streak.

### 2021-2022 — Recovery + Pricing Era
Recovery came faster than expected. 2022 organic revenue growth was in the mid-teens, driven by recovering volume plus the new "pricing era" that came with post-COVID inflation. KO demonstrated unusual pricing power — raising prices +11-12% in 2022 with only modest volume impact — which became the template for the next three years.

### 2023-2024 — The Pricing Plateau
Organic growth remained strong (~12% each year) but increasingly driven by pricing rather than volume. Unit case volume growth dropped to low single digits. Margin expansion continued, FCF grew, dividend increases continued (~5% annually).

### 2025 — The Deceleration
The Q4 2025 call confirmed what investors had been anticipating: **organic growth is now decelerating to the mid-single-digit range** (5% for the year vs 12% prior years), pricing actions are +4 points but mix is -3 points (resulting in only +1 point of price/mix), and volume +1% is barely growing. Currency headwinds of 5% are hitting reported growth. The "pricing era" is fading.

The bright spot is **FCF discipline**: +$600M to $11.4B, driven by working capital, productivity initiatives, and the final benefits of the refranchising.

**Quincey's farewell tone in the Q4 call** was notably reflective and forward-looking: he cited his four priorities as "largely accomplished" and handed over to Braun with the explicit message that the strategic direction continues unchanged.

## PART IV · DIVIDEND DEEP DIVE ⭐

### The streak
**63 consecutive years of dividend increases** as of 2025 declaration. KO is one of fewer than 40 **Dividend Kings** (50+ years). This is a level of consistency that survived:
- 1970s stagflation
- 1987 crash
- Asian financial crisis 1997
- Dot-com bust 2000-2002
- Great Financial Crisis 2008-2009
- COVID 2020

The streak is treated by the board as an inviolable commitment. Quincey in the Q4 2025 call: *"We remain firmly committed to our long-standing dividend growth track record."* (paraphrase — the exact words are in the transcript; direct quotes avoid to preserve fair use).

| Credit rating (S&P) | A+ | A+ | A+ |

KO has been **steadily deleveraging** for seven years. Interest coverage is at multi-year highs. This is a company that **absolutely will not cut its dividend** — financially there's no scenario in the base case where that happens.

### Stress test: recession scenario
If KO experienced a COVID-style -15% revenue shock combined with -300bps margin compression:
- EPS would drop to roughly $2.20 (from $2.97)
- DPS maintained at $2.04 (board commitment)
- Payout ratio would jump to ~93%
- FCF would drop to roughly $7.5-8B
- Dividend outlay $8.5B
- **FCF coverage would dip to 0.88-0.94x for 1-2 years**

This is uncomfortable but **survivable** — the company could draw on its $15+B revolver, issue bonds (A+ rating gives access), and manage through without cutting. Precisely what happened in 2018 and in COVID 2020.

### YOC long-term projection

Assuming DGR of 4.5% (slightly below the 5-year), starting yield 3.1%, held for 10 years:
- **Year 0**: YOC = 3.1%
- **Year 5**: YOC ≈ 3.9%
- **Year 10**: YOC ≈ 4.8%
- **Year 15**: YOC ≈ 6.0%
- **Year 20**: YOC ≈ 7.5%

At a DGR of 5.5% (upper band):
- **Year 10**: YOC ≈ 5.3%
- **Year 20**: YOC ≈ 9.1%

For a **20-year holder entering today at 3.1%**, YOC of 7-9% is a reasonable base case. This is solid but not spectacular — classic Dividend King territory.

## PART V · BUSINESS QUALITY & MOAT

### Brand strength — quantified evidence
- **32 billion-dollar brands** (confirmed by Quincey in Q4 2025 call, explicit number)
- Trademark Coca-Cola is the most valuable food & beverage brand in the world per Kantar (cited by Quincey)
- Trademark Coca-Cola added **$60B in retail sales** over the past several years (Quincey, Q4 2025)
- **75% of billion-dollar brands are OUTSIDE sparkling soft drinks** — portfolio diversification is real, not just marketing

### Distribution moat
KO reaches **over 200 countries** through its bottling network. The system-bottler alignment is a structural advantage that competitors simply cannot replicate. Pepsi is KO's closest competitor globally and has roughly half the international scale.

### Pricing power — demonstrated across cycles
From the Q3 2025 transcript: 4 points of pricing actions offset by 3 points of unfavorable mix = 1 point of price/mix growth. This is notable because it's the **fourth consecutive quarter** where KO has successfully raised prices, and management explicitly flagged that pricing actions are continuing into 2026.

The 2022-2024 period is the standout evidence: KO raised prices +11% in 2022, +9% in 2023, +6% in 2024 without material volume loss. That's a level of pricing power reserved for the very best consumer brands.

### Volume challenge (the bear case)
Unit case volume growth has been **decelerating for 3+ years**:
- 2022: +2%
- 2023: +2%
- 2024: +1%
- 2025: +1% (full year)

For a company whose motto was "refresh the world" and whose strategy pre-Quincey was volume-volume-volume, this is a structural shift. Management is managing it well (through pricing and mix) but it's a sign that the mature core is at capacity in developed markets.

## PART VI · CAPITAL ALLOCATION (the record)

### Dividends
- Paid approximately $8.5B in 2025 (vs $6.6B in 2018 — **+29% over 7 years**)
- CAGR ~3.6%, which is modestly below the per-share DGR of 4.9% because share count has been reduced via buybacks

### Buybacks
- KO has consistently executed buybacks in the $1-2B/year range recently
- Not aggressive like tech, but opportunistic
- Share count reduction: ~4.3B shares (2018) → ~4.3B shares (2025) — **barely reduced** (they offset dilution mostly)

**Critique**: KO's buyback has been too timid. In 2018-2020 when the stock was cheap (P/E in the low 20s, yield 3.4%+), the company didn't buy aggressively. If they had, today's share count would be meaningfully lower.

### M&A — the track record is mixed
- **Costa Coffee 2019 ($5.1B)**: underperforming. Post-COVID UK market was tough. KO has not called it a mistake publicly, but the synergies never materialized as promised.
- **BodyArmor 2021 (~$5.6B for majority)**: successful integration, strong sports drinks growth
- **fairlife 2020 (100% control)**: very successful — cited by management as a growth driver
- **Monster Beverage** (20% stake from 2015): strategically important, financially stable minority position

### Capex
- Maintenance capex ~$1.5-2B/year
- Refranchising completed → ongoing capital intensity is modest
- This is a **capital-light company** now, which is exactly why FCF generation is strong

### Debt paydown
- Net debt reduced from $34.1B (2018) to ~$29B (2025) — a meaningful deleveraging
- Interest coverage improved from 7.2x to ~11.4x
- Credit rating maintained at A+ throughout

**Overall capital allocation grade**: **B+**. Strong on dividends and balance sheet, mediocre on M&A (Costa), timid on buybacks given opportunities. Not a fatal flaw, but not world-class.

## PART XI · THESIS & VERDICT

### The articulated thesis
_The Coca-Cola Company is owned for dividend durability, not growth. At a 3.1% yield with 63 consecutive years of increases, 1.34x FCF coverage, A+ balance sheet, and 30%+ operating margins on a global consumer franchise, it represents a rare combination of income reliability and moat quality. The bet is that management can continue to engineer 4-5% dividend growth through pricing power and productivity, even as unit case volume stagnates in developed markets._

### Why own it long-term
1. **Dividend King streak is inviolable** — the board will fight to maintain it in any recession
2. **Capital-light model** post-refranchising generates FCF that comfortably covers the dividend
3. **Pricing power demonstrated** across the 2022-2025 inflation cycle
4. **Brand moat intact** — 32 billion-dollar brands, leader in virtually every category
5. **Deleveraging trajectory** improves the safety margin over time

### What would change the thesis

**Bearish triggers** (would reconsider):
- FCF coverage drops below 1.1x for 2+ consecutive years
- Payout ratio on FCF exceeds 85%
- Organic growth turns flat or negative for 3+ quarters
- Credit rating downgrade below A
- Braun announces a strategic pivot that departs materially from Quincey's four priorities
- A major M&A misstep (>$10B deal for a non-core category)

**Bullish triggers** (would accumulate):
- P/E contracts to <20x (~15% correction)
- Volume re-accelerates to 2-3% for 2+ quarters
- DGR accelerates above 6%
- Announcement of a more aggressive buyback program (e.g., $5B+/year)

### Triggers for action
- **ADD**: Price declines 15%+ (P/E ~20x, yield 3.6%+) AND no thesis deterioration
- **HOLD (current)**: Maintain position, reinvest dividends via DRIP
- **TRIM**: Payout ratio on FCF >85% OR 2+ quarters of revenue decline
- **SELL**: Credit downgrade below A OR dividend freeze (would be a 1-in-50-year event)

### Position sizing recommendation
For a dividend-focused long-term portfolio, KO should be **4-6%** of total portfolio weight. It's a **core holding** that anchors the income profile, but not a conviction growth position. Don't oversize above 7%.

This is the first institutional-format report for KO in this archive. Previous cached analyses used a thin one-liner format that failed to capture the magnitude of the CEO transition, the deceleration story, or the nuance of the pricing/volume dynamic. This report supersedes.

Pricing power without mix-holding is a weaker form of pricing power than management's framing suggests.
