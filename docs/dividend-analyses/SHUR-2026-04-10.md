## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Shurgard Self Storage is the only publicly traded pure-play self-storage REIT in continental Europe, operating 267+ facilities across seven countries under the Shurgard brand. This is a business that American investors instinctively understand -- it is the European cousin of Public Storage (PSA), Extra Space (EXR), and CubeSmart (CUBE), businesses that have generated extraordinary long-term shareholder returns through the combination of pricing power, low capex requirements, high operating leverage, and sticky customer behavior.

At EUR 27.55, Shurgard trades at the lower end of its historical range (52-week: EUR 24.35-38.00) and offers a forward dividend yield of approximately 4.2% based on the EUR 1.17 last dividend. The stock has underperformed its US peers by a wide margin over the past two years, primarily because (a) European interest rates rose sharply in 2022-2023, compressing REIT valuations, (b) European self-storage penetration is much lower than in the US, creating uncertainty about the growth trajectory, and (c) Shurgard lacks the scale and liquidity of US self-storage REITs, limiting institutional interest.

I believe this underperformance has created an opportunity. The self-storage business model is structurally advantaged in any economic environment: recessions drive demand (people downsize, move, divorce), and expansions drive demand (people accumulate more stuff). European penetration of self-storage is approximately 0.02 square meters per capita vs 0.75 in the US -- a 37x gap that represents decades of structural growth runway. The dividend of EUR 1.17 per share is well-covered by operating cash flow and represents a reasonable 4.2% yield. For a REIT with double-digit revenue growth and significant development pipeline, this is a balanced payout that allows for both income and reinvestment.

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Shurgard Self Storage S.A. is the largest owner-operator of self-storage facilities in Europe, operating under the Shurgard brand across seven countries: the Netherlands, France, Sweden, the United Kingdom, Belgium, Germany, and Denmark. The company was founded in 1995 as a European joint venture of Shurgard Storage Centers (later acquired by Public Storage in 2006). After Public Storage decided to focus on the US market, the European operations were spun off and listed on Euronext Brussels in October 2018.

The company is domiciled in Luxembourg (REIT-favorable tax regime), headquartered operationally in Brussels, and employs 836 full-time staff. CEO Marc F. Oursin has led the company since the 2018 IPO. The stock trades on Euronext Brussels under ticker SHUR and has a market cap of approximately EUR 2.78B.

The facilities are concentrated in major metropolitan areas (London, Paris, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Brussels, Berlin, Munich, Copenhagen) where population density and real estate costs are highest -- exactly where self-storage demand is strongest.

**Business Model:**

Self-storage is deceptively simple: build a temperature-controlled, secure building with individual lockable units, rent them to individuals and small businesses on month-to-month leases, and collect rent. The economics are extraordinary:

- **Very low variable costs:** Once built, a facility costs perhaps EUR 8-12/sqm/year to operate (security, cleaning, insurance, property tax). Rental rates of EUR 150-300/sqm/year give gross margins of 60-80%.
- **Customer stickiness:** The average self-storage customer stays 15-18 months, and the hassle of moving stored goods creates high switching costs. Price increases of 5-10% are absorbed because the effort of moving exceeds the incremental cost.
- **Recession resilience:** In downturns, people downsize and need storage. In expansions, people accumulate stuff. Either way, demand grows.
- **Limited competition risk:** Self-storage is hyperlocal (customers rarely travel more than 15-20 minutes), so each facility has a natural geographic moat. Zoning restrictions in European cities make new supply difficult to add.

## PART VI -- MANAGEMENT AND GOVERNANCE

**CEO: Marc F. Oursin** -- Has led Shurgard since before the 2018 IPO. Previously held executive roles at Public Storage's European operations. Oursin is an experienced self-storage operator who understands the European market dynamics and has executed well on the development pipeline.

**Public Storage Influence:**

Public Storage's ~35% ownership stake is both a positive and a negative:
- **Positive:** Provides operational expertise, technology transfer, and brand support. PS is the world's largest self-storage operator and its knowledge base is invaluable for a smaller European operator.
- **Negative:** Creates potential conflicts of interest (PS has governance influence but also operates competing self-storage facilities globally). PS's involvement may also deter other potential acquirers.
- **Take-private optionality:** If Shurgard's stock remains depressed, PS could theoretically offer to acquire the remaining 65% at a premium. This creates an implicit floor on the stock price, though PS has not historically shown interest in full consolidation.

**Board composition and governance.** Shurgard's board reflects the dual influence of Public Storage and European institutional investors. The board is chaired independently, with PSA's nominees holding minority representation — a structure that balances the strategic relationship with shareholder protection. Compensation disclosure follows European standards (less granular than SEC proxy requirements), but available data suggests executive compensation is reasonable for a EUR 2.8B market cap REIT. CEO Oursin's total compensation is estimated in the range of EUR 2-3M annually, including long-term incentive awards tied to FFO growth and total shareholder return.

**Management bench depth.** One legitimate concern is key-person risk. Oursin has been the face of Shurgard since the IPO and has personally overseen every major development decision and capital markets transaction. The CFO and COO roles are less prominent in public communications. If Oursin were to depart, the transition risk would be material given the relatively small management team (836 employees total, of whom a limited number are senior executives). The PSA relationship partially mitigates this — PSA could provide interim management support during a transition — but it remains a risk that is not priced into the stock.

**Insider transactions.** European disclosure rules require reporting of insider transactions by persons discharging managerial responsibilities. Based on available data, there have been no material insider sales during the FY2024-FY2025 period, which is a positive signal — management is not using the depressed stock price as an exit opportunity.

**The European Self-Storage Penetration Thesis -- Detailed Analysis:**

The core growth thesis for Shurgard rests on the European self-storage penetration gap vs the US. Here is the case, with honest caveats:

**Why European penetration should converge toward US levels:**
- Urbanization is accelerating across Europe (people live in smaller apartments, need external storage)
- Consumer accumulation behavior is converging globally (e-commerce, lifestyle goods)
- Small business growth (gig economy, online retailers) creates commercial storage demand
- Housing mobility is increasing (people move more often, need temporary storage)
- Awareness is growing through marketing and word-of-mouth (many Europeans have never heard of self-storage)

**Why European penetration may NOT reach US levels:**
- European homes typically have basements, attics, and garages (less true in cities, but true in suburbs)
- European consumers are culturally less acquisitive than Americans (smaller homes = less stuff)
- Planning permission for self-storage facilities is much harder in European cities (NIMBYism, zoning)
- European tax treatment of self-storage revenue may be less favorable than US REIT structures
- Population growth in Europe is near-zero or negative (vs US positive growth driving housing formation)

**My estimate:** European penetration will reach 0.10-0.15 sqm/capita over the next 20 years (5-7x current, but only 15-20% of US levels). This still implies a very large addressable market expansion for Shurgard, but investors expecting US-level saturation are likely to be disappointed.

At 0.10 sqm/capita and 300M population in Shurgard's seven countries, the total addressable market is approximately 30M sqm. Shurgard currently operates approximately 1.3M sqm, implying a 4.3% market share of the current addressable supply. If the market grows 5-7x and Shurgard maintains its share, its portfolio could grow to 7-9M sqm -- roughly 1,400-1,800 facilities, up from 267 today. This represents a 30+ year growth runway, which is extraordinary for a real estate company.

**Capital Allocation:**

Shurgard has pursued a growth-first strategy, investing EUR 84-152M annually in new facility development while growing the dividend 6-8% annually. The balance between development and shareholder returns has been reasonable, though the rising LTV (from 12% to 24%) suggests the easy phase of growth-without-leverage is ending. Future development will require either (a) more debt, (b) equity issuance, or (c) slower dividend growth.

**Deep dive on the top three risks.**

**Risk 1 — Interest rate sensitivity.** As a REIT with an active development pipeline financed by a combination of corporate debt and equity issuance, Shurgard's valuation is tightly correlated with European interest rates. The mechanism is twofold: (a) higher rates increase Shurgard's borrowing cost directly, compressing FFO growth, and (b) higher rates compress REIT multiples as investors demand higher yields from alternative investments. The 2022-2023 rate hiking cycle demonstrated this brutally: the stock fell from EUR 48 to EUR 24 — a 50% decline — driven almost entirely by rate expectations, not by any deterioration in operating fundamentals. Revenue grew double digits through the entire drawdown. The lesson: Shurgard's equity functions partly as a leveraged rate play, and no amount of operating excellence can fully offset a hostile rate environment. The countervailing factor is that the ECB began cutting rates in mid-2024 and market expectations point to a terminal rate of 2.0-2.5% — meaningfully below the peak. If these expectations are realized, Shurgard should benefit from both lower borrowing costs and multiple expansion.

**Risk 2 — Oversupply in mature markets.** The Netherlands and the United Kingdom are the most penetrated self-storage markets in Europe. New supply is being added by both existing operators (Safestore, Big Yellow) and new entrants (private equity-backed platforms, local developers). If supply growth outpaces demand growth in these markets, same-store revenue growth will decelerate. The Amsterdam and London metropolitan areas are particularly exposed. Shurgard's mitigation is its portfolio breadth across seven countries — even if the Netherlands saturates, Germany and France have decades of runway remaining.

**Risk 3 — Equity dilution from development financing.** Shurgard's share count has grown 13% from 89M to 101M over four years, primarily through equity issuance to fund new facility development. At the current stock price of EUR 27.55 (62% of book value), every share issued is deeply dilutive — existing shareholders are effectively subsidizing new development by accepting below-NAV issuance. Management has historically been judicious about issuance timing, but the structural conflict between growth-requires-capital and existing-shareholders-prefer-no-dilution is permanent. The most shareholder-friendly outcome would be for the stock to re-rate closer to NAV before additional equity is issued, but management cannot control market pricing.

**The biggest risk for Shurgard is interest rate sensitivity.** As a REIT with a development pipeline, Shurgard's valuation is highly correlated with European interest rates. The 2022-2023 rate hiking cycle compressed the stock from EUR 48 to EUR 24 -- a 50% decline driven almost entirely by rate expectations, not operating deterioration. If the ECB reverses course and raises rates again, Shurgard will be hit disproportionately.

The countervailing factor is that self-storage demand is genuinely counter-cyclical: the industry has historically seen occupancy stability through recessions as downsizing consumers create storage demand that offsets business demand weakness.

## PART X -- TAX CONSIDERATIONS

Shurgard is a Luxembourg-domiciled REIT (SIF/RAIF structure) listed on Euronext Brussels. Tax treatment is relatively favorable:

- **Luxembourg REIT structure:** Luxembourg investment fund vehicles are tax-transparent -- no corporate income tax at the REIT level, provided the vehicle distributes substantially all income. Shurgard is structured to be tax-efficient at the entity level.
- **Belgian withholding tax:** Since the stock is listed on Euronext Brussels, dividends may be subject to Belgian withholding tax of 30% for non-treaty investors. However, as a Luxembourg-domiciled entity, the actual withholding depends on the payment chain and the investor's tax residency.
- **Luxembourg-China tax treaty:** Luxembourg and China have a tax treaty that reduces dividend WHT to 5-10% (depending on ownership percentage). For portfolio investors (<10% ownership), the rate is typically 10%.
- **For US holders:** The Luxembourg-US treaty provides a reduced WHT of 15% (standard portfolio rate). The dividend should qualify as "qualified" for US tax purposes.
- **Scrip dividend alternative:** Shurgard has historically offered a stock dividend alternative, which may be tax-neutral in some jurisdictions (treated as a stock split rather than income).

**For a China-based tax resident:** The optimal path is claiming the Luxembourg-China treaty rate of 10% WHT. This requires appropriate documentation through the custodian chain (Euroclear Brussels). In practice, many investors receive the default 30% Belgian rate and must file for a refund -- a process that can take 12-24 months.
