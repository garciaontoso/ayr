## PART I — Executive Summary

Blue Owl Capital Corporation is a leading externally managed Business Development Company that provides predominantly first-lien senior secured loans to upper-middle-market U.S. companies. Managed by Blue Owl Capital Inc. (NYSE: OWL), OBDC has grown into the second-largest publicly traded BDC by total assets following successive mergers with OBDE (closed January 2025) and the aborted/terminated OBDC II merger. As of December 31, 2025, the portfolio comprised 234 companies across $16.5 billion of fair value, with 79% in senior secured investments and 96% in floating rate debt.

The dividend story at OBDC is one of a company that rode higher interest rates to record earnings in 2023, distributed generously through both base and supplemental dividends, and is now navigating a declining rate environment that compresses its core revenue stream. The base dividend stands at $0.37/quarter ($1.48 annualized), but Q4 2025 adjusted NII came in at just $0.36 per share — the first quarter in which OBDC under-earned its base dividend. CEO Craig Packer has explicitly warned that the board will "evaluate the dividend each quarter, particularly as the full effect of lower rates and spreads are now impacting the portfolio." This is the single most important development for income investors: the base dividend is no longer clearly sustainable at current rate expectations without a change in rates or spreads.

NAV per share has declined from $15.45 at the end of FY2023 to $14.81 at the end of FY2025, a 4.1% erosion that, while modest by BDC standards, signals that total return has been under pressure. However, $148 million in share repurchases at 86% of NAV in Q4 2025 were accretive, and a $1.4 billion asset sale at 99.7% of book value provides strong third-party validation of portfolio marks. For a dividend investor, OBDC offers a compelling current yield north of 11% on market price but is entering a period of likely dividend reduction, making it a hold rather than an accumulate.

## PART II — Company Snapshot

Blue Owl Capital Corporation (OBDC, formerly Owl Rock Capital Corporation / ORCC) was formed in 2016 and commenced operations as a BDC under the Investment Company Act of 1940. It completed its IPO in 2019. The company is externally managed by Blue Owl Credit Advisors LLC, a subsidiary of Blue Owl Capital Inc. (OWL), a publicly traded alternative asset manager with over $135 billion in credit AUM.

- **Sector / Industry:** Financial Services — Business Development Company (BDC) / Specialty Finance
- **Business model:** Originates and manages a portfolio of predominantly first-lien senior secured term loans to private-equity-backed upper-middle-market companies. Earns net investment income from the spread between asset yields (~10-11% all-in) and funding costs (~5-6%), distributing virtually all taxable income as dividends per RIC requirements.
- **Portfolio composition (12/31/2025):**
  - First lien senior secured: 79% (near record)
  - Joint ventures & specialty finance: ~12%
  - Second lien / subordinated: ~4%
  - Equity co-investments: ~5%
  - 96% floating rate, 4% fixed rate
- **Geographic exposure:** ~94% U.S.-based borrowers, limited international (UK, Europe, Australia)
- **Sector allocation:** Software (~18%), Insurance Brokerage (~10%), Healthcare (~10%), Business Services (~10%), Food & Beverage (~7%), with intentional avoidance of cyclicals (energy, chemicals, retail, autos)
- **Top customers / concentration:** Average position size ~40 bps of portfolio; top 10 investments ~22% of portfolio. 234 portfolio companies across 30+ industries.
- **Main competitors:** Ares Capital (ARCC, largest BDC), FS KKR Capital (FSK), Golub Capital BDC (GBDC), Blackstone Secured Lending (BXSL), Main Street Capital (MAIN)
- **Recent corporate actions:**
  - Jan 2025: Closed merger with Blue Owl Capital Corporation III (OBDE), adding $4B+ in portfolio assets
  - Nov 2025: Announced merger with OBDC II (Blue Owl Capital Corporation II), subsequently terminated in Q4 2025
  - Q4 2025: Terminated OBDC II merger; instead announced $1.4B asset sale at 99.7% of book value across Blue Owl BDCs ($400M from OBDC); $600M from OBDC II with plan to wind down via capital returns
  - Q4 2025: Repurchased $148M of stock (11.6M shares at ~86% of NAV) — largest buyback in OBDC history
  - Jan 2026: Moody's upgrade to Baa2
  - New $300M share repurchase authorization, replacing prior $200M plan
- **Market cap:** ~$6.3B / **Shares outstanding:** ~499M (post-repurchases) / **Avg daily volume:** [elevated post-merger, roughly 2x pre-merger levels]

**Inflection points:**

1. **2020 — COVID stress test:** Non-accruals spiked to ~2%, the dividend was maintained but not raised. NAV declined to $14.49 but recovered rapidly as portfolio companies proved resilient. Blue Owl took over one portfolio company (PLI) and managed it to near-full recovery.

2. **2022-2023 — Rate windfall:** The 525 bps of Fed rate hikes from March 2022 through July 2023 dramatically boosted NII on OBDC's 96% floating-rate book. NII per share went from $1.18 (FY2022) to a record $2.03 (FY2023). Management introduced the supplemental dividend framework in Q3 2022 and raised the base dividend three times ($0.31 -> $0.33 -> $0.35 -> $0.37).

3. **January 2025 — OBDE merger:** Added ~$4B in portfolio assets, ~130M new shares. Increased OBDC's total assets from ~$14B to ~$17B. Pro forma leverage dropped from ~1.2x to ~1.15x. The merger created $5M+ in annual OpEx synergies and additional financing cost savings still being realized.

4. **Q3-Q4 2025 — OBDC II merger saga:** Announced in November 2025, the OBDC II merger was intended to further consolidate Blue Owl's BDC platform. Market reaction was severely negative — OBDC stock sold off to the low-$11/high-$10 range (75-80% of NAV). Management terminated the merger and instead announced asset sales and a wind-down of OBDC II. The $1.4B asset sale at 99.7% of book value validated portfolio marks but the stock has not recovered.

5. **2025 rate environment — NII compression:** Fed rate cuts totaling 100 bps, combined with ~50 bps of spread compression, reduced NII/share from $0.47 (Q4 2024) to $0.36 (Q4 2025). The supplemental dividend fell from $0.05 (Q4 2024) to $0.00 (Q4 2025) and the base dividend is now under-earned by $0.01/share.

## PART IV — Dividend Deep Dive

- **Current yield on market price:** ~13.6% (base $1.48 annualized / ~$10.86 price) [approx]
- **Current yield on NAV:** 10.0% ($1.48 / $14.81)
- **Payout ratio (NII basis, Q4 2025):** 103% ($0.37 dividend vs $0.36 NII/share) — FIRST QUARTER OF UNDER-EARNING
- **Payout ratio (NII basis, FY2025):** ~119% total dividends / NII per share ($1.52 / $1.24 is misleading due to merger shares; actual coverage is closer to 97-100% when measured quarterly)
- **Dividend streak:** OBDC has paid uninterrupted quarterly dividends since IPO (2019), with base dividend increases from 2022-2023. However, supplemental dividends have been declining and the base dividend faces near-term risk of a cut. This is NOT a Dividend Aristocrat and never will be — BDCs by nature have variable distributions tied to rate cycles.
- **5y dividend CAGR:** Not cleanly calculable due to partial IPO year and merger-related share count changes. Base dividend grew from $0.31/quarter (2019 IPO level) to $0.37/quarter currently = ~3.6% annualized increase over ~6 years.
- **Last base hike:** Q4 2023 (from $0.35 to $0.37, the third consecutive $0.02 increase)
- **Last supplemental dividend:** Q2 2025 ($0.02/share). None declared in Q3 or Q4 2025.
- **Pattern of hikes:** Base increases during rate upcycles, supplementals as a buffer mechanism. Management has explicitly stated the supplemental framework was designed to adjust naturally with rates — and it has.
- **Dividend coverage by NII:** 0.97x in Q4 2025 (first under-earning quarter). Spillover income of $0.36/share provides roughly one additional quarter of coverage.
- **Currency of payment:** USD
- **Withholding tax for China tax residents:** 10% under US-China tax treaty (standard treatment for US-sourced dividends). BDC dividends are treated as ordinary income, not qualified dividends, making the effective WHT 10% on the full amount. Net yield after WHT: ~12.2% on market price.

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The critical question for OBDC dividend investors is whether the $0.37 base dividend can be maintained through a full rate-cutting cycle. The data suggests it cannot indefinitely.

Management has provided important guidance: Craig Packer stated on the Q3 2025 call that when rates were at a 3% SOFR environment (which was the case in 2021), OBDC's base dividend was approximately $0.33/share. The forward curve currently projects SOFR declining to approximately 3% by end-2026. If this materializes, a $0.33-$0.35 base dividend would be the sustainable level — implying a 5-11% reduction from the current $0.37.

However, there are offsetting factors. First, the OBDE merger added scale, generated $5M+ in OpEx savings, and creates another 50 bps of ROE accretion as financing is optimized and the portfolio is remixed. Second, specialty finance and JV investments (Wingspire, Fifth Season, Ambridge Inn, equipment leasing JV) contribute non-correlated income with low double-digit ROEs that are less sensitive to base rates. Third, if the M&A cycle recovers and/or syndicated loan spreads widen, OBDC's portfolio yield on new originations would improve. Fourth, the Moody's upgrade to Baa2 should lower future unsecured borrowing costs.

The bear case: if SOFR reaches 3% and spreads remain tight (475 over), sustainable NII/share would be roughly $0.33-$0.34/quarter. The $0.37 base would need to be cut. Management knows this — Craig Packer's language has progressively shifted from "comfortable through end of year" (Q3 2025) to "we're going to evaluate the dividend each quarter" (Q4 2025).

For a recession scenario, OBDC's COVID experience is instructive: the dividend was maintained but not raised, NAV declined ~5%, and non-accruals peaked at ~2%. The defensive portfolio (software, healthcare, insurance brokerage) proved resilient. However, in a recession combined with falling rates, the double hit to NII (from both lower rates and potential non-accruals) could accelerate a dividend cut.

**Bottom line on dividend safety: The current $0.37 base dividend has at most 2-3 quarters of coverage remaining before a likely reduction to the $0.33-$0.35 range. The spillover cushion of $0.36/share buys time but does not change the fundamental math of a floating-rate portfolio in a declining-rate environment. A 5-10% base dividend cut in 2026 is the most probable outcome.**

## PART V — Business Quality & Moat

- **Moat type:** Scale + platform incumbency + information advantage
- **Moat trajectory:** Widening (via mergers and platform expansion at Blue Owl)
- **Pricing power evidence:** Limited — OBDC is a price-taker in the direct lending market, competing with both syndicated loan markets and other large BDC/private credit platforms. Spreads have compressed 50 bps over the past 12 months. However, Blue Owl's position as a top-3 direct lending platform provides access to the largest, most attractive deals.

OBDC's competitive advantages are real but derive from its external manager (Blue Owl) rather than from OBDC itself. Blue Owl's $135B+ credit AUM makes it one of the largest direct lending platforms globally, providing three key advantages:

1. **Origination scale:** Blue Owl deployed $45B across its direct lending platform in 2025, with OBDC receiving $4B+. The platform's size allows participation in $2B-$5B deals that smaller BDCs cannot access. Average deal size has grown from $600M (2021) to $1.5B+ (2025), and Blue Owl leads or co-leads ~90% of its deals.

2. **Incumbency advantage:** ~50% of OBDC's originations come from existing borrowers (add-ons, refinancings). This is a genuine competitive moat — the sponsor/borrower relationship creates recurring deal flow even in muted M&A environments.

3. **Information advantage:** As administrative agent on ~65% of investments across the platform, Blue Owl has direct access to real-time borrower financial data. This information advantage supports better credit selection and earlier identification of problems.

4. **Diversified income streams:** OBDC's specialty finance and JV investments (Wingspire asset-based lending, Ambridge Inn railcar/aircraft, Fifth Season life settlements, equipment leasing, drug royalties) provide differentiated income less correlated to direct lending spreads and base rates. These generate 14%+ ROEs and represent ~12% of the portfolio with room to grow to 15%.

The key vulnerability is that OBDC is externally managed. The management fee structure (1.5% on gross assets, 17.5% incentive fee on income above a hurdle) is fixed and was not modified even as rate-driven earnings declined. CEO Craig Packer has explicitly refused to consider fee reductions, stating the structure has been consistent for 10 years. This creates a misalignment in a lower-rate environment: management fees consume a larger percentage of lower NII, compressing returns to equity holders. The incentive fee hurdle and cumulative loss protection provide some alignment, but the base management fee on gross assets does not.

## PART VI — Capital Allocation

1. **Dividends:** ~$770M in FY2025 dividends ($1.52/share x ~506M shares). This is the primary use of cash, as required by RIC tax status (must distribute 90%+ of taxable income).

2. **Buybacks:** $148M repurchased in Q4 2025 at average 86% of NAV. New $300M authorization in place. Management has signaled willingness to continue buying opportunistically. This is a genuinely shareholder-friendly action — buying at 86% of NAV is accretive to all remaining shareholders by ~$0.05/share in NAV.

3. **New originations / portfolio growth:** $4B+ deployed in OBDC during 2025. However, net leverage was managed down (1.19x from 1.22x) as management chose to reduce leverage and fund buybacks rather than maximize portfolio growth.

4. **Asset sales:** $400M sold from OBDC at 99.7% of book value in Q4 2025. This was tactical — reduced leverage by ~0.05x, trimmed some larger positions, and validated portfolio marks. Not a regular occurrence; management emphasized they are "buy-and-hold lenders."

5. **Debt management:** Extensive optimization of capital structure post-OBDE merger. Repriced CLOs, amended bilateral SPVs, refinanced higher-cost OBDE legacy debt. The Baa2 upgrade from Moody's should further reduce unsecured borrowing costs on future issuances.

**Capital allocation grade: B+** — Management has been disciplined across the cycle: introducing supplementals rather than unsustainably hiking the base in 2022-2023, buying back stock at 86% of NAV when the market panicked over the OBDC II merger, and selling assets at book value to validate marks and create deployment flexibility. The one negative is the refusal to consider management fee adjustments despite the structural shift in earning power, and the aborted OBDC II merger attempt that damaged shareholder confidence and stock price.

## PART X — Risks & Stress Tests

**Risk #1: Interest rate decline compressing NII beyond what dividend can absorb**
- Probability: HIGH (75%+ — the forward curve projects SOFR at ~3% by end-2026)
- Severity: MODERATE — a $0.02-$0.04/quarter reduction in base dividend (~5-11%) is likely, not a catastrophic cut
- Mitigant: Specialty finance/JV investments provide ~12% of portfolio at less rate-sensitive returns. OBDE merger synergies provide 50-75 bps of ROE improvement still being realized. Spillover income of $0.36/share provides bridge.

**Risk #2: Credit cycle deterioration in a recession**
- Probability: MODERATE (30-40% in 2026)
- Severity: MODERATE to HIGH — non-accruals could rise from 1.1% to 3-4%, requiring NAV write-downs and potential dividend impact
- Mitigant: 79% first lien senior secured, 42% average LTV, portfolio concentrated in defensive sectors (software, healthcare, insurance). COVID stress test showed resilience. Median borrower EBITDA of $133M with mid-to-high single-digit growth. Interest coverage of borrowers at 2.0x and improving.

**Risk #3: External management fee structure misalignment**
- Probability: HIGH (100% — it is the current structure)
- Severity: LOW but persistent — in a lower-rate environment, the 1.5% base management fee on $17B+ gross assets (~$255M/year) consumes a proportionally larger share of declining NII. Management has refused to modify fees.
- Mitigant: Incentive fee alignment is better (17.5% above hurdle with cumulative loss protection). Fee structure is industry-standard for externally managed BDCs.

**Risk #4: OBDC II wind-down reputational contagion**
- Probability: LOW to MODERATE — the wind-down is proceeding orderly, with 30% of capital returned at book value immediately
- Severity: LOW for OBDC directly (OBDC II is a separate entity), but the narrative of "halting redemptions" (management calls this a "complete mischaracterization") has created negative headlines
- Mitigant: Asset sale at 99.7% of book is the strongest possible counter-evidence. OBDC II wind-down proceeds on schedule with 5%/quarter capital returns expected.

**Risk #5: Tariff exposure / macro disruption**
- Probability: MODERATE
- Severity: LOW — management estimates tariff exposure at "mid-single digits" of portfolio (revised down from initial estimate). Key exposures: Conair (imports from China, CCC-rated first lien), Beauty Industry Group (tariff + operational issues). Both are already on the watch list and marked down. The defensive sector mix (services, software, healthcare) limits broader tariff impact.
- Mitigant: 94% US-based borrowers, primarily domestic-serving. Sponsors have significant equity and dry powder to support portfolio companies.

## PART XI — Thesis & Verdict

**Thesis (one paragraph):**

OBDC is a high-quality BDC managed by one of the premier direct lending platforms in the world, with a defensive portfolio of predominantly first-lien senior secured loans to upper-middle-market companies. The portfolio has been validated by independent third-party buyers at book value, non-accruals remain well below industry averages, and the balance sheet is conservatively leveraged with ample liquidity. However, the dividend is entering a period of likely reduction as lower SOFR and tighter spreads compress NII below the current $0.37/quarter base level. The stock trades at a 27% discount to NAV, providing substantial margin of safety, but the near-term catalysts are mixed: rate cuts will hurt NII, while an M&A recovery and spread widening would help. For a dividend investor already holding the position, this is a HOLD — the yield is attractive even after a likely modest cut, the portfolio quality is strong, and buying more at these levels requires conviction that the rate/spread cycle will turn. We would accumulate on further weakness below $10 (>32% discount to NAV) but not add at current levels given the near-certainty of a dividend reduction.

**Verdict:** HOLD

### Detailed NII Bridge Analysis (Q4 2024 to Q4 2025)

Understanding the NII decline from $0.47 (Q4 2024, pre-merger OBDC standalone) to $0.36 (Q4 2025, combined entity) requires decomposing the drivers:

- Pre-merger OBDC weighted average shares: ~390M
- Post-merger weighted average shares: ~506M (+30%)
- The OBDE merger added portfolio assets and NII in total dollar terms but also added ~130M shares
- On a pro forma combined basis, Q4 2024 NII/share would have been approximately $0.41-$0.42 (not $0.47)

**Step 2: Rate impact**
- SOFR declined approximately 100 bps from its peak in 2024 to end-2025 levels
- With ~96% floating rate portfolio, each 100 bps SOFR decline reduces annualized portfolio income by approximately $160-170M, or approximately $0.08/share/quarter
- Only about 70% of the rate cuts had flowed through by Q4 2024 (per CFO Lamm). Full flow-through completed by Q1-Q2 2025, with residual impact through Q4 2025

**Step 3: Spread compression**
- Weighted average portfolio spread compressed approximately 30-50 bps over 2025 (from ~620 bps to ~580 bps)
- This reflects both repricing of existing loans and new originations at tighter spreads
- Impact: approximately $0.02-$0.03/share/quarter decline

**Step 4: Non-recurring income variability**
- Q4 2024 had elevated one-time income (~$0.05/share from accelerated OID, dividends from portfolio company Bell Run)
- Q4 2025 also had some one-time income (~$0.02/share from call protection and merger OpEx true-up) but lower than Q4 2024

**Step 5: Operating expense and financing cost changes**
- OBDE merger synergies reduced OpEx by ~$5M annualized (~$0.003/share/quarter)
- Liability optimization lowered weighted average cost of debt modestly

### BDC-Specific Regulatory and Structural Considerations

Several structural features of the BDC vehicle are important for dividend investors to understand:

**RIC tax status:** OBDC must distribute at least 90% of its taxable income annually to maintain tax-exempt status at the corporate level. This means the dividend is not discretionary in the way that a traditional corporation's dividend is — it is structurally required. The risk is not that management chooses to cut the dividend; it is that NII physically declines to a level below the current payout.

**Spillover income:** When OBDC over-earns its dividend in a quarter, the excess accumulates as undistributed taxable income (spillover). This spillover can be used to support dividends in quarters where NII falls short, without triggering a special excise tax (4% excise on under-distribution). OBDC currently has $0.36/share of spillover — nearly one full quarter of base dividend. This is a genuine buffer but not an indefinite one.

**Leverage limits:** The Investment Company Act limits BDC leverage to 2.0x debt-to-equity (after the 2018 amendment). OBDC operates at 1.19x, well below the limit. This conservatism provides flexibility but also limits the ability to boost NII through leverage in a lower-spread environment. Management's target range of 0.9-1.25x is appropriate for the current portfolio composition.

**Incentive fee structure:** The 17.5% incentive fee on NII above a 7% annualized hurdle (1.75% quarterly) includes a cumulative loss protection feature — unrealized losses reduce the fee base. This provides meaningful alignment in a credit deterioration scenario. However, in a pure rate-decline scenario (no credit losses), the incentive fee declines naturally with NII, providing some buffer for shareholders. The base management fee of 1.5% on gross assets (~$255M/year on $17B+ gross assets) is fixed regardless of rate environment.

**Qualifying assets:** BDCs must invest at least 70% of assets in qualifying assets (primarily private U.S. companies). OBDC's specialty finance and JV investments count as qualifying. The non-qualifying basket (~30% capacity) provides flexibility for syndicated loan JV participations and other structures.

### The OBDC II Merger Saga — Full Timeline and Lessons

The OBDC II episode is the single most consequential event for OBDC's stock price in the past year and merits detailed analysis for dividend investors evaluating management quality and shareholder alignment.

**Background:** OBDC II (Blue Owl Capital Corporation II) was a private non-traded BDC launched approximately one year after OBDC. It was raised primarily from individual investors through the wealth channel and designed to provide access to the same direct lending strategy and portfolio. OBDC II had approximately $1.7B in total investments with 98% overlap with OBDC's portfolio — essentially a smaller clone fund.

**November 2025 — Merger announcement:** On the Q3 2025 earnings call, management announced a stock-for-stock merger whereby OBDC would acquire OBDC II. The rationale was sound: eliminate a duplicative structure, gain $1B in net assets, reduce leverage (OBDC II ran at 0.78x vs OBDC's 1.22x), and add ~$5M in annual cost savings. The exchange ratio would be struck on a NAV-for-NAV basis, protecting OBDC shareholders from dilution if OBDC traded below NAV.

**Market reaction:** Severely negative. OBDC's stock price declined from the mid-$13 range to the low-$11/high-$10 range over the weeks following the announcement. The market's concerns included: (a) another merger so soon after OBDE (merger fatigue), (b) OBDC II's slightly higher non-accrual rate, (c) fear of selling pressure from OBDC II retail investors receiving listed OBDC shares for the first time, and (d) broader skepticism about private credit marks being validated through friendly mergers rather than arms-length sales.

**Q4 2025 — Merger termination and pivot to asset sale:** Working with the board, management concluded the merger "no longer made sense" given the market reaction. They terminated the merger and instead announced a $1.4B asset sale: $600M from OBDC II (35% of fund assets), $400M from OBDC, and approximately $400M from another Blue Owl fund. The assets were sold at 99.7% of book value to four institutional buyers through a private process.

**OBDC II wind-down plan:** Rather than resuming the quarterly 5% tender offers, management announced a 30% pro rata capital distribution to all OBDC II shareholders from the asset sale proceeds. Going forward, an additional ~5% per quarter would be returned from repayments and earnings, with the possibility that half of OBDC II's capital could be returned by end-2026.

**Why this matters for OBDC dividend investors:**

1. The asset sale at 99.7% of book value is the strongest possible validation of OBDC's portfolio marks. As Packer emphasized: "Most industry private secondary sales are almost always executed at a discount to book value." Getting par from four independent institutional buyers after detailed due diligence on 128 positions is a genuine stamp of approval.

2. The $400M OBDC asset sale reduced leverage by 0.05x, trimmed three of the top five positions, and created capital for redeployment. Management described it as "scalpel-like precision" housekeeping. The sold assets had a weighted average spread of 500+ bps and 10-11% PIK exposure — consistent with the broader portfolio, not adversely selected.

3. The $148M share buyback at 86% of NAV was the largest in OBDC history and accretive to NAV by approximately $0.05/share. This demonstrated genuine shareholder alignment — management sold assets at 100% of book to buy stock at 86% of book, creating immediate value for remaining shareholders.

4. The risk: some headlines characterized the OBDC II situation as "halting redemptions," which management called a "complete mischaracterization." The reality is an accelerated return of capital versus the previous 5%/quarter tender framework. However, the narrative damage has been done, and OBDC's stock has not recovered. The OBDC II overhang will persist until the wind-down is substantially complete (potentially mid-to-late 2026).

5. For the dividend thesis specifically: the OBDC II episode has no direct impact on OBDC's NII generation or dividend sustainability. It is a market sentiment event, not a fundamental event. The dividend risk remains rate-driven, not OBDC-II-driven. However, the depressed stock price (0.73x NAV) means OBDC cannot issue equity (ATM program effectively shelved), limiting one tool for capital management.

### Software Portfolio — The AI Question

Software is OBDC's largest sector exposure at approximately 18% of the portfolio. As AI disruption concerns have risen across the technology sector, this warrants specific attention.

Management has addressed this directly on multiple calls. Logan Nicholson stated on the Q4 2025 call that OBDC's "40-person technology investment team reviewed our exposures again through an AI lens and confirm the fundamental health of our assets." Software portfolio companies showed LTM revenue growth of 10% and EBITDA growth of 16% in Q4 2025 — outpacing all other sectors.

Key characteristics of OBDC's software exposure:
- Primarily mission-critical, scaled enterprise software providers
- First lien, senior secured loans with LTVs of approximately 30%
- Only 4 of the top 25 OBDC investments are in software
- The broader Blue Owl platform has dedicated technology-focused BDCs (OBDC Tech I and II, merging) with specialist underwriting teams

The risk is not that OBDC's existing software loans default due to AI disruption — these are senior secured loans with 70% equity cushions. The risk is more subtle: if AI disruption slows growth or reduces margins at software portfolio companies, it could affect refinancing opportunities and spreads. However, management's tone suggests this is being actively monitored and the current portfolio is positioned in segments where AI is more complementary than disruptive.

Craig Packer's nuanced take (Q4 2025): "We're certainly not taking lightly the potential impact for AI. Having said that, we continue to see our best-in-class companies perform well and think they'll endure. And if we see opportunities, we'll do it. But I would say we're going to be very discriminating. And I don't think our software percentage will go up."

This is the right posture — maintaining discipline while not panicking out of a sector that has been OBDC's strongest performer.
