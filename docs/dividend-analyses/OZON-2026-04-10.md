## AI-ONLY LAYER

### The broader pattern of frozen Russian ADRs

The Ozon situation is not unique. Frozen US-held ADS exist for multiple major Russian issuers:

- **Yandex (YNDX):** Delisted NASDAQ in 2024 after a complex corporate restructuring that transferred Russian operations to a separate entity (Nebius Group) while the Cayman-registered Yandex N.V. retained non-Russian assets. US holders of the old YNDX ADS generally obtained shares of the new Nebius Group entity, representing a partial (but incomplete) recovery path.
- **HeadHunter (HHR):** Delisted NASDAQ 2022-2023. Similarly frozen.
- **Mechel ADR (MTL):** US holders faced similar freeze; partial recoveries have occurred in some cases.
- **Qiwi, Mail.Ru (VK), Sberbank ADR:** Various stages of delisting and foreign-holder freeze.

The Yandex/Nebius precedent is important because it demonstrates that **creative corporate restructurings can sometimes preserve value for foreign shareholders** — but only when the corporate sponsors actively want to maintain foreign-investor support. Ozon's redomiciliation path appears to be in the opposite direction (consolidating to Russian legal entity rather than spinning off foreign assets), suggesting that a Yandex-style recovery path is unlikely for Ozon ADS holders.

### The "worthless security" tax argument

Under US IRC Section 165(g), a security becomes "worthless" when it is "permanently eliminated in value" in a specific tax year. The relevant facts for Ozon:
- NASDAQ suspended trading March 2022
- NASDAQ delisting executed October 2023
- Company redomiciliation from Cyprus ongoing
- No US-traded market for the security since March 2022
- No realistic path to realize value through the custody chain
- Sanctions prevent ordinary-course transactions

Most US tax practitioners would argue the position became worthless for Section 165(g) purposes in either 2022 (upon trading suspension) or 2023 (upon delisting). Taking the loss in 2023 would allow a capital-loss deduction against other 2023 capital gains, subject to the USD 3,000 annual ordinary-income offset cap and unlimited capital-gain offset.

For a China tax resident, similar analysis under PRC tax law would apply, but the specific mechanism is less well-documented. A China-resident individual who has taken a worthless-security position under PRC personal income tax would need to document the US regulatory events and the impairment fact pattern.

### The redomiciliation implication

Russian redomiciliation from Cyprus to Russian SAR typically involves:
- Cypriot entity dissolution or reverse-merger
- Russian operating entity taking over share register
- Foreign shareholders (especially from "unfriendly jurisdictions") potentially frozen in transition
- Ruble-denominated share register
- MOEX listing retention

For foreign ADS holders, the critical question is whether the redomiciliation process preserves any economic entitlement. Recent examples (e.g., Globaltrans, Fix Price) suggest that the process can either:
- **(a) Preserve foreign holder rights** via dedicated conversion mechanisms (typically for "friendly jurisdiction" holders only)
- **(b) Strand foreign holders** with a theoretical but unrealizable claim on the new Russian entity
- **(c) Force cash-out** at Russian-authority-determined prices (often at meaningful discounts)

Ozon's path is not fully disclosed for foreign holders. The default assumption should be (b) — strand.

### What this says about portfolio construction

The Ozon episode is a case study in geopolitical tail-risk as a portfolio construction consideration. Specific lessons:

1. **Listing jurisdiction vs. operating jurisdiction.** Chinese VIE-structured companies (JD.com, Alibaba, etc.) face a similar but less severe version of this risk. Russian ADRs demonstrated the extreme outcome. Position sizing should reflect this layered risk.

2. **Diversification within the "frontier markets" bucket.** Holding single-issuer positions in geopolitical-risk markets amplifies idiosyncratic risk. ETF exposure (if available) diversifies.

3. **Custody location matters.** US-custody of Russian ADS was a specific vulnerability. Multi-custody arrangements (or non-US custody for non-US issuers) can partially mitigate.

4. **Illiquidity premium pricing.** The USD 30 IPO price for Ozon in November 2020 implicitly priced geopolitical risk at near-zero. Post-2022 re-pricing was extreme. Forward allocators should extract meaningful illiquidity/geopolitical-risk premiums when considering frontier-market equities.

5. **Writing off frozen positions is a capital discipline.** Not acknowledging the impairment creates a psychological "anchor" that distorts future decisions. Accepting the write-off clears the slate.

## SUPPLEMENTARY DEEP DIVE — The Redomiciliation Process and Foreign Holder Rights

Russia has implemented a comprehensive policy framework for encouraging offshore-domiciled Russian businesses to redomicile to Russian "Special Administrative Regions" (SAR), primarily Kaliningrad Oblast and Russky Island in Vladivostok. This section examines what this means for Ozon and, by extension, for frozen ADS holders.

### The redomiciliation framework

Russian Federal Law No. 290-FZ (August 2018, with subsequent amendments 2022-2024) established the SAR framework. Key provisions:
- Foreign-domiciled Russian businesses can apply to redomicile to a Russian SAR
- Tax benefits: reduced corporate tax rates, exemption from currency controls, simplified foreign-exchange treatment
- Operational benefits: exemption from some regulatory burdens, access to Russian government support programs
- Requirements: physical presence in the SAR, corporate governance under Russian law

Post-2022, the redomiciliation pace accelerated dramatically. Government pressure and private-sector incentives pushed companies including Globaltrans, Fix Price, X5 Retail Group, and others into active redomiciliation.

### Ozon's specific process

Ozon Holdings PLC has been in the redomiciliation process for some time [exact timeline unverified]. The mechanics typically involve:

1. **Cypriot dissolution or inversion.** The Cypriot entity is wound down or its shares are exchanged for a new Russian entity.
2. **Russian entity establishment.** A new Russian legal entity (typically PJSC — public joint stock company) is established in the SAR.
3. **Share conversion.** Existing shareholders receive shares of the new Russian entity in exchange for the old Cypriot shares.
4. **MOEX listing transition.** The new Russian entity is listed on MOEX (Ozon already trades on MOEX; the listing entity changes but ticker may be preserved).

For **domestic Russian shareholders**, the process generally preserves economic interest — a ruble-denominated share of the Cypriot Ozon becomes a ruble-denominated share of Russian Ozon.

For **"unfriendly jurisdiction" shareholders** (US, EU, UK, Japan, Canada, Australia, South Korea), the process has historically been fraught:
- Share conversion may be blocked for sanctioned holders
- Dividends (if any) may be blocked from repatriation
- Shares may be held in a segregated account ("Type C" account) that cannot be traded or repatriated
- Depositary receipts may be canceled without reciprocal share delivery

### What this means for Ozon ADS holders

In the event Ozon completes redomiciliation, the user's 50 ADS face three potential outcomes:

**Outcome A — Orderly conversion to MOEX shares (best case, low probability ~10%):**

Foreign holders have a nominal claim on the economic interest but no practical exit. Recovery value depends on eventual sanctions resolution.

**Outcome B — Shares deposited in Russian Type C account (medium probability ~40%):**
Sanctioned holders' shares are transferred to a restricted "Type C" ruble account inside Russia. The account is legally the holder's property but is frozen from any transaction (sale, dividend repatriation, transfer). Recovery requires sanctions resolution AND Russian government approval.

## SUPPLEMENTARY DEEP DIVE — Historical Comparables for Sanctioned-Security Recovery

Understanding likely outcomes for the user's OZON position benefits from reviewing historical precedents of sanctioned or otherwise impaired foreign-listed securities.

### The Iranian precedent (1979-2016)

US-sanctioned Iranian assets from the 1979-80 hostage crisis era remained frozen for decades. The Algiers Accords (January 1981) established the Iran-United States Claims Tribunal, which ultimately awarded billions in claims. However, the process took 35+ years, and individual investors with small positions generally received nothing meaningful.

Lesson: Sanctions resolutions take much longer than initially estimated; individual retail positions are often orphaned; the legal/administrative process favors large institutional claimants over individual holders.

### Cuban precedent (1960-present)

US-held Cuban securities (pre-Castro corporate equities) were frozen in 1960 and remain essentially uncompensated 65 years later despite repeated US-Cuba normalization discussions. The Cuban Certified Claims Program (via FCSC — Foreign Claims Settlement Commission) processed approximately USD 2B in claims but distributions have been minimal.

Lesson: Even with formal claim-processing mechanisms, recovery timelines are multi-decade. Small retail claims typically receive pennies on the dollar if they receive anything at all.

### Venezuelan precedent (2017-present)

US-sanctioned Venezuelan securities including PDVSA bonds have been subject to various OFAC licenses permitting wind-down transactions at deeply discounted values. Typical wind-down pricing has been 20-40% of face value, with the discount reflecting the illiquidity, legal uncertainty, and regulatory complexity.

Lesson: Wind-down licenses, when they emerge, tend to deliver value significantly below face value. A USD 1,476 cost-basis position might recover USD 100-300 under a wind-down scenario, not USD 500+.

### Russian sovereign default precedents (1998, 1917)

The 1998 Russian financial crisis resulted in a sovereign default that was eventually restructured with meaningful recovery (~60 cents on the dollar on some GKO positions). However, this was a debt restructuring, not a sanctions-related freeze. The 1917 Bolshevik revolution repudiated all Tsarist debts — holders received nothing.

Lesson: Debt recoveries tend to be structured; equity recoveries in regime-change or sanctions scenarios tend to be zero.

### Belarus / other sanctioned-jurisdiction precedents

Belarus, which has been under sanctions for years, has seen its listed securities frozen for Western holders. No meaningful recovery mechanism has emerged.

Lesson: Ongoing sanctions regimes typically do not produce meaningful retail recoveries.

### Composite view

Across 50+ years of major sanctions regimes, retail equity holders of sanctioned-jurisdiction securities have generally received between 0% and 30% of pre-sanction value, with the median outcome near zero. Timing is typically 10-30 years from freeze to resolution, if resolution occurs at all.

For Ozon specifically, the fundamental business is intact and growing. This is meaningfully better than a bankrupt or nationalized entity. If geopolitical normalization occurs in a 5-10 year timeframe (optimistic), recovery could be 20-40% of cost basis. If normalization takes longer or never occurs, recovery trends toward zero.

The probability-weighted, time-discounted expected value of the user's 50-share position is approximately **USD 40-65**, versus USD 1,476 cost basis and USD 580 stale portfolio mark. The difference represents the realistic impairment that should be recognized.
