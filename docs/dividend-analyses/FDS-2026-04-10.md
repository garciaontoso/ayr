## PART I — Executive Summary

FactSet Research Systems is the world's fourth-largest financial data and analytics provider by revenue, behind Bloomberg, LSEG (Refinitiv), and S&P Global Market Intelligence. Founded in 1978, publicly traded since 1996, and a member of the S&P 500 since December 2021, FDS sells a subscription-based, multi-asset-class data and workflow platform to approximately **9,000 clients and 237,000 professionals** worldwide (FY2025 10-K, Item 1 Business Overview). Revenue is overwhelmingly recurring — ASV retention >95%, client retention 91% — which is what makes the business a legitimate dividend compounder rather than a cyclical software reseller.

The dividend story is the cleanest in the data/analytics space. **Fiscal 2025 marked the 26th consecutive year of dividend increases on a split-adjusted basis** (FY2025 10-K, Dividends disclosure). The most recent hike — from $1.04 to $1.10 per share quarterly, approved May-2025 — is 6% and entirely unforced: payout ratio is only 26% of GAAP EPS ($4.40 annualized vs. $15.55 reported, or vs. $16.98 adjusted), and FCF coverage is 3.9x. This is a company that has *more* cash than it needs, and has consistently chosen a dual-return capital allocation mix: ~$300M/yr buybacks + ~$160M/yr dividends.

Why this report exists now: **the market has de-rated FDS from ~28x to ~22x forward earnings** over 2024–2025 on two fears that are, in my view, partly valid but fundamentally misread:

1. *"GenAI will commoditize financial data and kill FactSet."* No — it will kill the providers of low-margin undifferentiated data. FDS sells connected, normalized, cross-asset, workflow-integrated data with legal chain-of-custody. That is exactly what GenAI *needs* to be trustworthy in front of a fiduciary. The threat for FDS is not demand destruction; it is pricing power compression if the industry moves from "per-seat" to "per-query" pricing. That is a real risk, but it is at worst a 200–300 bps drag on Organic ASV growth, not a business-model rupture.

2. *"Sell-side weakness will keep ASV depressed."* Sell-side is ~18% of Organic ASV. Even if it grew at 0%, the other 82% (buy-side + Wealth + Market Infrastructure / CGS) is still compounding at 5–7%. And sell-side is already re-accelerating into Q2 FY26.

**Position recommendation:** full long position at current levels, with a hard stop on dividend safety only (i.e., I own this as long as the dividend keeps rising and FCF coverage stays >3x). Entry 3.0–4.0% of portfolio. This is a "hold forever if it keeps compounding" sleeve, not a tactical trade.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Annualized dividend: **$4.40/share** ($1.10 x 4) — yield estimate 1.0–1.1% depending on spot
- Payout ratio (GAAP EPS): **28.3%** ($4.40 / $15.55)

### Workforce and operational details (FY2025 10-K)

FactSet's operational model is distinctive among financial data providers in its reliance on global centers of excellence. As of August 31, 2025, the company had 12,800 employees across 35 offices in 19 countries, a 3.2% increase year-over-year. The geographic distribution is heavily skewed toward low-cost COEs: 8,854 employees (69%) in Asia Pacific (primarily India and the Philippines), 2,510 (20%) in the Americas, and 1,436 (11%) in EMEA. The COEs account for approximately 68% of total headcount, which is the structural foundation of the operating margin story -- 47% of employees work in Content Operations (the data normalization engine), 28% in Technology and Product Development, 21% in Sales and Client Solutions, and 4% in Corporate Support.

This workforce mix is both a margin enabler and a competitive moat. To replicate FactSet's data normalization capability, a competitor would need to hire and train approximately 6,000 content operations professionals in India and the Philippines -- a 3-5 year endeavor that no startup can shortcut. Bloomberg achieves similar scale through its ~20,000 employees globally, but at a significantly higher all-in cost per employee given its New York-heavy workforce.

The FY2025 10-K also discloses that 94% of employees participated in learning programs, completing 339,000 hours globally, with specific emphasis on AI capabilities (prompt engineering, coding, vector databases, data querying). The employee engagement survey achieved an 88% response rate, "significantly outpacing typical third-party benchmark response rates." These are small details that collectively signal a healthy organizational culture -- important for a business where the moat is human capital, not physical assets.

One detail worth noting for governance watchers: 434 employees are represented by mandatory works councils in France and Germany, and 25 by collective bargaining agreements in the US (via the P.A.N. Securities broker-dealer subsidiary). This is a minimal unionization footprint for a 12,800-person global company and does not represent a material cost or operational risk.

### Recent corporate actions and legal structure

The FY2025 10-K reveals several details that enrich the investment thesis:

- **New Credit Agreement (April 8, 2025):** $500M term loan + $1.0B revolving credit facility, replacing the 2022 facilities. PNC Bank as administrative agent. This refinancing was executed at presumably tighter spreads than the 2022 vintage given FDS's improved credit profile, and extends the maturity wall comfortably.

- **2025 Employee Stock Purchase Plan:** Filed as an S-8 on July 31, 2025 (Registration No. 333-289143). This is a standard ESPP offering 15% discount to market price -- a modest dilution source (~0.2% annually) but an important employee retention tool.

- **Equity compensation pool:** 1,977,727 shares issuable upon exercise of outstanding options/RSUs/PSUs (5.3% of shares outstanding), with 3,492,745 shares remaining available for future issuance. The weighted-average exercise price of outstanding options is $347.50 -- well below the current trading range, meaning virtually all options are in the money. This is a sign of a well-managed equity compensation program that has created real wealth for employees.

- **Material weakness in ITGCs:** Still in remediation as of the FY2025 10-K. The company has identified this as a "material weakness in internal control over financial reporting" related to IT general controls over revenue, accounts receivable, and deferred revenue. Remediation is expected to complete in FY2026. This is a controls *design* issue, not an earnings quality issue -- no restatement has been required, and E&Y has continued to issue unqualified opinions on the financial statements. However, it is the kind of overhang that weighs on institutional ownership until fully remediated.

- **New CLO from S&P Global MI:** Christopher McLoughlin joined as Chief Legal Officer in December 2024, previously serving as General Counsel of S&P Global Market Intelligence. This is a notable hire -- bringing in the former legal chief of a direct competitor signals confidence and suggests potential intelligence about competitive dynamics in the data/analytics market.

### Inflection points

**1. FY2019–2020: Organic growth slowdown to ~5%.** The Snow-era FactSet was quietly struggling. Organic ASV growth had drifted into the low-to-mid 5s. This was when FactSet started the multi-year shift toward "content refinery" / platform strategy — rebuilding the data spine into an open, connected, API-first architecture. This matters because without that 2019–2021 investment, FDS would not be positioned to monetize AI today.

**2. FY2022: CGS acquisition closes for $1.932B (March 2022).** This is the single most important capital-allocation decision of the past decade. CGS gives FDS the CUSIP issuance monopoly — a regulatory annuity with pricing power unconstrained by competition. It also doubled sell-side margin of safety overnight because banks *must* consume CUSIP/CINS data. Note the cosmetic damage to FY22 reported margins (25.8% GAAP) — that was driven by $62.2M in real-estate impairment charges from the hybrid-work footprint reduction *plus* acquisition costs, not business deterioration. GAAP EPS actually *decreased* 1.1% in FY22 — the stock reacted, the dividend did not stop.

**3. FY2022–2023: Senior Notes issued $1.0B + 2022 Credit Facilities.** FDS levered up to pay for CGS — 2.9% 2027 Notes + 3.45% 2032 Notes. Smart timing (locked in low long rates). This brought gross leverage to ~2.5x, meaningful for a formerly debt-free company, but manageable given recurring revenue.

**4. FY2024: Material weakness in IT general controls disclosed.** In the Aug-2024 10-K, FDS disclosed a material weakness in ITGCs over revenue, A/R, and deferred revenue. It is *still in remediation* as of the FY2025 10-K (p.1389, p.601) and expected to complete in FY2026. This is not a fraud flag — it is a controls design issue. But it is the kind of overhang that weighs on the multiple until fixed.

**5. FY2024–2025: Sales Tax Dispute with Massachusetts resolved.** $66.2M aggregate charge spread over FY23/FY24. Cleared in Nov-2024. This was ugly when it happened, but it's in the rearview mirror now. It artificially depressed FY24 op margin by ~220bps.

**6. July 2024: CFO musical chairs.** Linda Huber separated July 22, 2024 (with a signed severance agreement cited in the FY25 10-K exhibits). Helen Shan returned to CFO — she had been CFO 2018–2021 before rotating to CRO. Shan was on Marsh McLennan board, was Treasurer there, was CFO of Mercer. This is a seasoned finance hand returning because the company needed a safe pair of hands.

**7. Sept 2025: New CEO Sanoke Viswanathan starts.** Not a FactSet lifer. Not a product guy. Ex-JPMorgan, ex-McKinsey. This is a deliberate choice by the board: the next phase is *enterprise selling* to big buy-side and wealth platforms, not product innovation for its own sake. My interpretation: the board has concluded the product is good enough; execution against large enterprise deals is where the ASV unlock is.

**8. Q2 FY2026: Organic ASV accelerates to 6.7%.** This is the first real proof point that the buy-side front office push (backed by LiquidityBook) and wealth prospecting tools are generating incremental ASV above the steady-state ~5%.

**Ten-year CAGR observations:**
- Revenue: FY15 est ~$1.06B → FY25 $2.32B ≈ **8.1% CAGR**
- Net income: roughly $300M → $597M ≈ **7.1% CAGR**
- Diluted EPS: ~$6 → $15.55 ≈ **10.0% CAGR** (buybacks did work)
- Dividend/share: $0.43 (FY15 quarterly) → $1.10 (FY25 Q4) ≈ **9.8% CAGR**

The per-share compounding rate has been ~10%, driven by ~8% revenue growth + margin expansion + ~1%/yr buyback net accretion. That is exactly the profile a senior dividend investor should want: organic growth > dividend growth > inflation, with buybacks as the kicker.

## PART V — Dividend Deep Dive

### Dividend mechanics

- **Payment cadence:** Quarterly, paid ~18 days after the record date (record typically last business day of fiscal quarter).
- **Ex-date pattern:** Consistent — last business day of each fiscal quarter (Nov / Feb / May / Aug ends).
- **Currency:** USD.
- **Withholding for non-US holders:** US withholding applies per treaty — 30% default, 10–15% for treaty jurisdictions (e.g., 10% for China treaty residents, 15% for most EU).
- **Tax classification:** Qualified dividend for US holders (regular C-corp).

### Sustainability narrative

The dividend is effectively bulletproof. Three-way check:

1. **EPS payout:** $4.40 annualized vs. $15.55 GAAP EPS = **28.3%**. On adjusted EPS ($16.98) it's **25.9%**. Typical "sleep at night" zone is <50%; FDS is half of that.
2. **FCF payout:** $160M paid FY25 vs. $617.5M FCF = **25.9%**. FCF coverage ratio **3.86x**.
3. **Leverage vs. dividend:** Even if EBITDA fell 30% (it won't; ASV is 95% retained), leverage would still be ~1.6x — miles inside the 3.75x covenant.

### What would break the dividend?

Realistically, only three scenarios:

- **Catastrophic ASV collapse (−15% or worse).** This would require a broad industry disruption — Bloomberg + LSEG + MSCI + FactSet all losing share simultaneously to an unnamed insurgent. No current candidate is plausible.
- **M&A mega-deal financed with debt that consumes FCF for 3+ years.** FDS has never done this. CGS was the biggest deal in company history at $1.9B and it did not touch the dividend.
- **Material weakness in ITGCs escalates to restatement.** Currently disclosed as a controls *design* issue, not a restatement risk. Worth watching quarterly — the FY25 10-K explicitly says remediation is expected to complete in FY2026.

### Dividend growth runway

If FCF grows at the expected 5–7% rate (tracking Organic ASV + modest margin accretion), and the payout ratio creeps from 26% toward a still-conservative 35%, the math supports **8–10% annual dividend growth for the next 5–7 years** with high confidence. That is not in the price.

## PART VI — ASV / Organic Growth Deep-Dive

ASV is the only operating metric that matters for FactSet. Understanding it is the difference between modeling this company and guessing at it.

### Definition recap (from FY25 10-K, pp.807–811)

> "ASV at any point in time represents our forward-looking revenues for the next 12 months from all subscription services currently being supplied to clients. Organic ASV excludes ASV from acquisitions and dispositions completed within the last 12 months and the effects of foreign currency movements."

In FY2025, FactSet made a subtle but important definitional change: it stopped reporting "Organic ASV plus Professional Services" and now reports **Organic ASV** alone. The reason — explicitly stated — is a shift "toward providing more managed services and less project-based services." In plain English: FactSet wants investors to ignore one-time consulting revenue and focus on pure recurring. I approve.

### Segment geographies

Americas drives the bus — 66% of Organic ASV as of Feb-2026, up from 64% a year earlier. Asia Pacific is accelerating fastest (10.0% organic at Feb-26, vs. 7.2% at Aug-25). EMEA is the laggard at ~4% — no surprise, given European banking consolidation and MiFID-era cost pressure on buy-side research.

### The 7% question

**Can FDS re-accelerate Organic ASV to 7%+?**

My answer: **yes, within 12 months**, and here's why.

1. Q2 FY26 Organic ASV is already at 6.7% — the trend line is clearly up-and-to-the-right.
2. Asia Pacific is at 10.0% and accelerating. Every 100bps acceleration there = 10bps of total company.
3. LiquidityBook acquisition (Feb 2025) was not in Organic ASV in FY25 — it becomes organic starting Feb 2026. OMS/FIX connectivity is a high-demand area (hedge fund outsourced trading is one of the fastest-growing buy-side workflows).
4. Irwin (Nov 2024) — public-company IR platform — starts contributing to organic next quarter.
5. AI pricing monetization — FactSet has explicitly said in the FY25 10-K that AI is "generating usage, demand and commercial transactions" (p.98). This is a 0–100 bps tailwind as per-user AI fees start showing up in ASV.
6. The new CEO's enterprise-selling mandate. Viswanathan's track record at JPMorgan International Consumer & Wealth was building large-deal distribution. That skill is exactly what FactSet needs to convert ~9,000 clients into higher-ASV enterprise deployments.

The only thing holding 7% back is the EMEA headwind (buy-side consolidation) and the remaining sell-side sluggishness. Both should lift as the IPO/M&A cycle rebuilds in 2026–2027.

### Is FDS gaining share on Bloomberg?

**Directly on the trading floor: no.** Bloomberg is irreplaceable on a sell-side trading desk because of the IB chat network effect and fixed-income real-time data. FDS has never seriously competed there and does not pretend to.

**In the buy-side middle office and portfolio analytics: yes, FDS is winning.** This is where the CHI (client health index) numbers matter. FDS FY25 client count grew 9.5% (779 net adds) — meaningfully faster than the industry. User count grew 9.7% (20,943 net adds). Bloomberg terminal count has been roughly flat to slightly down for three years per trade-press reporting.

**In wealth management: FDS is winning aggressively.** The 2025 10-K specifically notes that "the user count increase was primarily driven by an increase in wealth management users" (p.842). This is the growth vector. Wealth has lower ARPU per seat than buy-side institutional, but the TAM is larger and less mature.

### The structural point

FDS and Bloomberg do not compete in the same workflow primarily. Bloomberg sells a terminal optimized for a trader who needs real-time market depth, IB chat, and news. FactSet sells a platform optimized for a portfolio manager or analyst who needs to build a fundamental research process, run portfolio attribution, and hand off to middle office. In the few overlap workflows (fundamental equity analyst at a long-only shop), FDS is typically the cheaper, more workflow-integrated choice. In the non-overlap workflows, Bloomberg wins by default.

**My view:** the Bloomberg-replacement thesis for FDS has never been the right thesis. The right thesis is that FDS is carving out a defensible, growing, high-margin position in the buy-side-plus-wealth segments where Bloomberg is overkill and Refinitiv is clunky. That's a ~$10B addressable revenue pool at maturity, of which FDS captures ~$2.3B today. There's substantial room to grow without ever taking a Bloomberg terminal from a trading desk.

## PART VIII — AI Disruption Thesis: Threat or Enabler?

This is the single most important conceptual question in the FactSet thesis, because the multiple compression of 2024–2025 is almost entirely AI-fear-driven.

### The bear case (articulated honestly)

1. GenAI models can ingest and summarize financial data, eliminating the need for structured, normalized feeds.
2. Clients will build their own in-house LLMs over open-source data.
3. Pricing moves from per-seat to per-query, compressing ARPU.
4. Content aggregation commoditizes; competitive differentiation collapses.
5. A well-funded startup (e.g., a Bloomberg-alternative using GenAI) could leapfrog FactSet's 40-year data collection moat.

This is internally consistent. It is what the market has partially priced in via multiple compression.

### Why the bear case is wrong (or at least substantially overstated)

**1. LLMs need trusted, linked, auditable data to be useful in regulated workflows.** A portfolio manager cannot act on "an LLM said so." The LLM needs a source, a time-stamp, a chain of custody, and a license. FactSet's proprietary Symbology (the linking layer connecting 50M+ securities across vendors) is the piece that makes GenAI outputs *trustable* in a fiduciary context. Without it, an LLM is a parlor trick.

**2. CGS / CUSIP is a regulatory monopoly.** No amount of GenAI removes the requirement for regulated entities to report using CUSIP. This revenue stream is literally immune to AI disruption. CGS alone is probably worth $2.5–$3B in any reasonable sum-of-parts — 10–15% of today's market cap is a regulatory annuity.

**3. FDS is not a commodity data reseller.** Only ~15–20% of ASV is single-source third-party data that could theoretically be replicated. The rest is proprietary normalization, workflow integration, and managed services. That stuff gets *more* valuable when GenAI makes every analyst 2x more productive — because now every analyst needs *more* data and *cleaner* integration.

**4. Enterprise selling matters more than per-seat selling now.** The industry trend is away from "you pay $12K per user, bye" and toward "we embed into your whole buy-side tech stack for $5M/yr." This is exactly where Viswanathan's enterprise-sales background fits. AI accelerates this trend — enterprise buyers want one vendor to support AI workflows end-to-end.

**5. FDS is monetizing AI directly.** From the FY25 10-K (p.98): *"FactSet's AI solutions are generating usage, demand and commercial transactions."* The AI Blueprint is commercialized. "Mercury" (the AI layer) is productized. Revenue is starting to show up. Not transformative yet, but material by FY27.

**6. FDS has the luxury of high margins and FCF to invest through a transition.** A cash-strapped competitor can't afford to rebuild a platform for AI. FDS can and is. Capex as a % of revenue rose from 3.9% (FY24) to 4.7% (FY25) — directly cited as "higher capitalized costs related to the development of our internal-use software" (p.1218). This is AI investment flowing through capex.

### My AI verdict

GenAI is not a threat to the business; it is a **2–3 year compression on ARPU growth** that FDS will offset with (a) volume growth (more users, more enterprise deals) and (b) new AI-native SKUs. Net effect on Organic ASV growth: probably −100 to −200 bps in a steady-state scenario, offset by enterprise-deal acceleration. The dividend is untouched.

The stock's implied bear case (22x forward) is pricing in a scenario more like −400 bps to Organic ASV growth over the medium term. That's too pessimistic, and it's the mispricing the report is leaning into.

## PART IX — Moat + Capital Allocation

### Moat assessment

I score FactSet's moat at **Wide–Narrow** (I'd call it "Wide minus," in Morningstar terms). The moat components:

**1. Switching costs (STRONG).** A portfolio manager trained on FactSet for 5+ years can't easily move. The platform is embedded in daily workflows, in Excel plug-ins, in report templates, in performance attribution models. Ripping out FactSet from a buy-side shop is a 12–18 month project with real operational risk. This is why client retention is >95% and has been for over a decade.

**2. Network effects (MODERATE).** Not as strong as Bloomberg (IB chat is a genuine network). FDS has a softer version via its Open:FactSet marketplace (100+ third-party data providers) and its shared analytics models. But this is Bloomberg-light, not Bloomberg-equivalent.

**3. Data scale + normalization (STRONG).** 68% of employees (~8,700 people) are in content operations, normalizing data in India and the Philippines. This is the *actual* moat — it is extraordinarily expensive to replicate. Real-time financial data is a commodity; *normalized, linked, cross-asset, point-in-time* financial data is not.

**4. Regulatory monopoly (STRONG, localized).** CGS / CUSIP is a genuine legal monopoly granted by the ABA under contract. Revenue-wise, it's maybe 10–12% of total, but margin-wise it's outsized.

**5. Brand + trust (MODERATE).** FactSet has a sterling reputation with buy-side fund administrators, auditors, and CFOs. That matters more than people think — when a compliance officer picks a data provider, brand trust is 50% of the decision.

### Capital allocation scorecard

| Decision | Grade | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| CGS acquisition 2022 ($1.9B) | **A** | Regulatory monopoly at a fair price; defensive and revenue-accretive |
| Truvalue/Cobalt acquisitions 2021 | B+ | ESG / private markets tuck-ins, both small and strategic |
| Irwin acquisition 2024 ($120M) | B | IR platform; small, expands public-company surface |
| LiquidityBook acquisition 2025 ($243M) | A− | Buy-side OMS — strategically critical to front-office push |

| R&D reinvestment | B+ | Rising capex into internal software is the right move for AI era |
| Portfolio pruning / divestiture FY25 | A | Sold non-core business for $17M pretax gain; focus is improving |

**Overall capital allocation grade: A−**. The CGS deal is one of the best information-services M&A transactions of the 2020s. Snow's tenure can be criticized on product pace but not on capital stewardship.

### New CEO implications

Viswanathan does not come from product. He comes from large-deal enterprise selling. The implicit board thesis: FactSet's product is *already good enough*. What it needs is a CEO who can close $50M enterprise deals with BlackRock, Vanguard, and the big wealth platforms. If that thesis is right, revenue acceleration follows naturally. If it's wrong, Viswanathan is a mismatch and the stock stays cheap for another 18 months. I lean toward "right" based on the board's access to private data we don't see.

### Scenarios

**Bear ($310–$350):** Organic ASV stalls at 4%, AI compresses ARPU, multiple re-rates to 18x forward. Yield would be 1.3%. Dividend still safe, just a capital-loss scenario on equity.

**Base ($460–$500):** Organic ASV sustains 6–7%, margins hold 32%, Adj EPS compounds at 8–10%, multiple stays at 22–24x forward. Total return ~10–12%/yr including dividend. This is my central case.

**Bull ($600+):** Organic ASV re-accelerates to 7%+, enterprise selling unlocks large deals, AI monetization adds 100 bps to growth, market re-rates FDS back toward 27–28x forward. Total return ~18%/yr.

### Fair value anchor

My fair value is **~$475–$500**, derived as follows:
- FY26E Adjusted EPS $17.80 (6% growth on $16.98)
- Fair multiple 26.5x (halfway between the 22x compressed trough and the 30x peak)
- $17.80 × 26.5 = **$472**
- Plus ~$10 of DCF model slippage on terminal value
- Rounded fair value **$475–$500**

Stock currently trades ~$420 ⇒ **10–20% upside to fair value**, plus a 1% dividend, plus the option value of AI monetization finally hitting the P&L. I'd call this a 12–15% annualized expected return over a 3-year hold, which is exactly the kind of return a compounder sleeve should target.

### The 22x vs. 25–28x debate

Is 22x the new normal? My answer: **no.** The narrative (AI fear + sell-side weakness) is cyclical and fades as (a) ASV reaccelerates (already happening, Q2 FY26 = 6.7%) and (b) AI monetization becomes visible in filings (FY27–FY28). 

Could FDS re-rate all the way back to 28x? Only with a clear inflection in Organic ASV above 7% — which is achievable but not my base case. 25–26x is the realistic multiple for a business that is definitionally *not* a pure software company (no negative working capital, no zero-marginal-cost scaling — content operations is a real cost center).

### Detailed risk quantification

**Material weakness timeline risk:** The ITGC material weakness was first disclosed in the FY2024 10-K (August 2024 filing). It has now been outstanding for one full fiscal year with remediation "expected to complete in FY2026." If remediation extends into FY2027, the market will reprice this from a nuisance to a governance concern. The probability of extension is low (~15%) based on the nature of the finding (controls design, not fraud), but it is the single highest-impact governance risk.

**CEO transition measurement:** Viswanathan has been in seat since September 8, 2025 -- approximately 7 months as of this report. The Q2 FY2026 results (February 2026 quarter-end) represent his first full quarter in charge. The fact that Organic ASV accelerated to 6.7% in that quarter is a positive data point, though it is too early to attribute this to any CEO-specific initiative. The true test will be the FY2026 full-year guidance (to be issued with Q4 FY2026 results in October 2026), which will be the first guidance cycle entirely owned by the new CEO. F. Philip Snow continued in an advisory capacity through December 2025 per the retirement agreement filed as an exhibit to the 10-K.

**Acquisition integration risk:** FDS completed three acquisitions in FY2024-FY2025: Irwin ($120.2M, Nov 2024), LiquidityBook ($243.2M, Feb 2025), and LogoIntern (small, undisclosed). Combined acquisition spend of ~$365M represents approximately 2.3% of market cap -- not a transformational bet, but enough to require execution. LiquidityBook is the strategically important one: a cloud-native OMS with a 200+ broker FIX network that directly addresses the buy-side front-office workflow gap. If LiquidityBook integration stalls, the "front-office expansion" narrative weakens. If it succeeds, it provides a new vector for ASV growth in the highest-value client workflows (hedge fund trading, outsourced execution).

### Why not higher conviction?

- Material weakness in ITGCs (not remediated yet) — deducts 1 point
- CEO transition execution risk — deducts 1 point
- Dealmakers/sell-side cyclical uncertainty — deducts 0.5 point
- Risk of AI-pricing model shift (per-query) disrupting ARPU trajectory — deducts 1 point

Total: 7.5/10 conviction, which I round to "Medium-High" for reporting.
