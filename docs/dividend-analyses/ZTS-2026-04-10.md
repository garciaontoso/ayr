## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: BUY below $130. ACCUMULATE at $126. -- Dividend Safety: 8/10 -- Confidence: High**

Let me be direct about what Zoetis is and is not.

It is not an income stock. At ~$126 and a $2.00 annualized dividend, the current yield is 1.59%. If you need 3%+ yield today, stop reading. ZTS will never satisfy you. But if you are building a 20-year dividend growth machine and understand that a stock yielding 1.5% today with a 15-year DGR of ~20% will be yielding 8-10% on cost within a decade, Zoetis belongs in the discussion for any serious compounder portfolio.

What the market is mispricing right now is the severity of the de-rating. ZTS has fallen from a peak of ~$244 (November 2021) to ~$126 -- a 48% drawdown from the highs. The trailing P/E has compressed from 57x to ~21x. The forward P/E on consensus 2026 EPS of ~$6.50 is approximately 19x. This is the cheapest Zoetis has traded on a P/E basis since the 2018 market correction. The de-rating has three causes: (1) Librela controversy and the resulting slowdown in U.S. Pain & Sedation growth, (2) post-COVID normalization of companion animal spending, and (3) the aggressive $2B convertible note issuance in December 2025 that expanded gross debt from $5.2B to $9.0B and diluted EPS optics. None of these three factors impairs the structural franchise. All three are being overweighted by the market.

The business underneath the noise is elite. FY2025 revenue of $9.467B (+2% reported, +3% operational ex-MFA divestiture and FX) set a new record. Operating margin expanded to 38.0% -- up 130bps YoY -- the seventh consecutive year above 32%. Net income reached $2.673B, EPS $6.02, adjusted EPS $6.41. Operating cash flow was $2.904B. Free cash flow was $2.283B, or $5.14 per share, covering the $2.00 dividend at 2.6x. ROIC of 25.8% is world-class for any healthcare company. This is not a broken business; it is a best-in-class franchise temporarily trading at mid-cap multiples.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**
1. **Valuation dislocation is extreme by ZTS standards.** At ~21x trailing EPS, the stock trades at a 42% discount to its 5-year average P/E of ~36x. The 10-year median P/E is ~35x. Even assigning a permanently lower terminal multiple of 28x (generous discount for the Librela uncertainty) to 2026E EPS of $6.50 yields a fair value of $182 -- 45% upside. You are getting the #1 animal health franchise in the world at the cheapest multiple since IPO-era multiples.
2. **Dividend growth trajectory is exceptional and accelerating.** The dividend has compounded at 20.4% annualized since initiation in 2013 ($0.195 to $2.00). The most recent raise (FY2025 to $2.00 from $1.728) was 15.7%. FCF payout ratio is under 40%. The company has headroom to deliver 12-15% annual raises for the next decade without stretching coverage.
3. **Animal health is the best structural growth market in healthcare.** No patent cliffs on biologics (mAbs are inherently hard to replicate in veterinary medicine -- no biosimilar pathway equivalent to human ANDA). No government pricing pressure. No PBM intermediation. Pet humanization is a multi-decade secular trend with 67% of U.S. households now owning pets. Companion animal products now represent 70% of ZTS revenue (up from 65% in 2023), and companion animal growth is structurally 6-8% per year.

**Top 3 reasons not to add at current price:**
1. **Librela U.S. trajectory is genuinely uncertain.** U.S. companion animal revenue for Pain & Sedation showed lower Librela sales in 2025 vs. 2024. The adverse event reports -- while statistically within expected ranges for a blockbuster veterinary drug -- have created real veterinarian hesitation. If Librela U.S. revenue stalls permanently below $500M, the pain franchise underperforms the bull case by ~$300-400M annually.
2. **Debt load just tripled.** The $2B convertible note issuance pushed gross debt to $9.0B from $5.2B. Net debt/EBITDA is now approximately 2.5x, up from ~1.2x a year ago. While the convertible was issued at an attractive 0.25% coupon and proceeds funded aggressive buybacks (23.9M shares retired in 2025 for $3.2B), this is the most levered ZTS balance sheet in history. If operating cash flow disappoints, the dividend growth rate will need to moderate.
3. **Livestock segment is declining.** Livestock revenue fell 5% YoY to $2.764B in 2025. The MFA divestiture explains roughly 3% of that decline, but even adjusting for it, livestock is flat to slightly negative. Draxxin has lost 66% of U.S. revenue since generic entry in 2021. The livestock segment is not impaired, but it is not contributing to growth.

**Next 12 months catalysts (high-probability):**
- **Lenivia and Portela launches** (EU/Canada approved 2025): 3-month dosing mAbs for dog and cat OA pain. These are the answer to Librela controversy -- less frequent injections, potentially better safety profile. If launched in the U.S. in 2026-2027, they re-ignite the pain franchise.
- **Q1 2026 earnings** with fiscal year alignment (December 2025 month for International subs will be reported for the first time -- creates a ~2.5-3.5% one-time international revenue uplift in the comparable quarter).
- **FY2026 dividend raise announcement** (likely February 2027, expect $2.25-2.30, representing 12-15% increase).
- **Share count reduction accelerates** -- with $2.4B remaining under the $6B buyback authorization, expect 15-20M shares retired in 2026, pushing EPS growth above underlying earnings growth.
- **Avian influenza vaccine demand** -- H5N2 conditional license for dairy cattle and poultry. If HPAI outbreaks continue, vaccine revenue could be a meaningful upside surprise.

## PART III -- THE 10-YEAR ARC (2015-2025)

The Zoetis story since the 2013 IPO is one of the most consistent value-creation narratives in healthcare.

**Phase 1: Post-Spinoff Optimization (2013-2016).** Revenue grew from $4.56B to $4.89B -- modest 1.8% CAGR -- as the company shed Pfizer overhead, built its independent commercial infrastructure, and initiated the first share repurchase program. Operating margins expanded from 18.0% to 28.6%. The dividend was initiated at $0.195/share (2013) and doubled to $0.38/share by 2016. The stock traded at $32-54 during this period.

**Phase 2: Innovation Inflection (2017-2020).** The launches of Simparica Trio (2019/2020), Apoquel Chewable (EU 2021), Cytopoint (2016 U.S.) and the early companion animal diagnostics buildout drove revenue from $5.3B to $6.7B -- 8.1% CAGR. Operating margins stabilized at 32-34%. The Abaxis acquisition (2018) established the diagnostics platform. The dividend grew from $0.42 to $0.80/share, a 17.5% CAGR. The stock reached $165 by end-2020.

**Phase 3: COVID Pet Boom and mAb Launches (2021-2023).** Pet adoption surged. Revenue accelerated from $7.8B to $8.5B. Librela launched in the EU (2020) and U.S. (2023). Solensia launched in the U.S. (2022). Operating margins expanded to 36%. The stock peaked at $244 in late 2021 on euphoric companion animal spending expectations. The dividend grew from $1.00 to $1.50/share.

**Phase 4: De-Rating and Reset (2024-2025).** Librela U.S. adverse event reports created veterinarian caution. Post-COVID pet spending normalized. The stock fell from $197 (end-2023) to $126. Revenue still grew -- $9.47B in 2025 is a record -- but growth decelerated to 2-3%. The company responded aggressively: operating margins hit 38%, the $2B convertible funded $3.2B in buybacks (23.9M shares retired), and the dividend was raised to $2.00/share. The share count dropped from 462M (2023) to 444M (2025). Management is buying back stock at the cheapest valuation in a decade -- this is exactly what good capital allocators do.

## PART V -- DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE: HIGH DGR, LOW YIELD

This is the section that matters most for the dividend growth investor, and it requires intellectual honesty about the tradeoff.

**The yield problem.** At $126 and $2.00 DPS, the current yield is 1.59%. The 10-year average yield has been approximately 0.7-1.0% (the yield has risen substantially from the ~0.5% lows of 2021 purely because the stock has been cut in half). Even at current depressed prices, you are getting less current income than a Treasury bill. The stock has never yielded more than ~1.6% since IPO.

**The growth answer.** The 12-year dividend CAGR of 21.5% is among the highest of any large-cap stock that has been paying dividends for over a decade. Importantly, the growth rate has not decelerated meaningfully: the 3-year CAGR is still 15.4%, the 5-year is 20.1%. This is not a front-loaded growth story that is exhausting itself.

**FCF coverage is robust.** The FCF payout ratio has ranged from 17-47% over the past decade, with the 2025 figure at 38.9%. There is significant room for continued double-digit dividend growth even if FCF growth moderates to mid-single digits. At a 50% FCF payout (which would still be conservative), the dividend could be $2.57/share -- a 28% increase from current levels -- without any underlying business growth.

**The compounding math.** If ZTS maintains a 12% annual dividend growth rate (well below the historical 15-21% range) from the current $2.00 base:
- Year 5 (2030): $3.52/share -- yield on $126 cost = 2.80%
- Year 10 (2035): $6.21/share -- yield on cost = 4.93%
- Year 15 (2040): $10.95/share -- yield on cost = 8.69%

Even at a more conservative 10% growth assumption:
- Year 10: $5.19/share -- yield on cost = 4.12%
- Year 15: $8.35/share -- yield on cost = 6.63%

This is the power of high-DGR compounding from a low base. A $10,000 investment today produces $159 in year-1 income. At 12% growth, that same investment produces $869 in year-15 income -- without reinvesting a single dividend.

**Management commitment.** The 2025 10-K states the quarterly dividend was $0.50/share and management expects to continue paying dividends. Given the 12-year unbroken streak, the sub-40% payout ratio, and the $2.9B operating cash flow base, I assign a near-zero probability to a dividend cut and an 85%+ probability of continued double-digit raises through 2028.

**Why the Safety rating is 8/10 not 9/10.** Two factors prevent a top score: (1) the debt load tripling to $9.0B gross creates interest coverage pressure if rates stay elevated (though the 0.25% convertible coupon mitigates this substantially), and (2) the business has zero dividend history through a severe recession -- ZTS has only existed as a public company since 2013, during one of the longest bull markets in history. We do not know how the dividend would fare in a deep economic downturn, though the defensive nature of pet healthcare spending provides comfort.

## PART VI -- LIBRELA CONTROVERSY + PIPELINE

This is the single most important section of this report because Librela is the fulcrum risk/opportunity for ZTS over the next 3-5 years.

**What Librela is.** Librela (bedinvetmab) is the first and only injectable monoclonal antibody for monthly alleviation of osteoarthritis pain in dogs. Approved EU 2020, U.S. 2023, China 2024. It targets nerve growth factor (NGF), a key pain mediator. The mechanism is genuinely innovative -- it is a biologic, not a small-molecule NSAID like Rimadyl, meaning it avoids the GI/renal/hepatic side effects that plague NSAIDs. The total addressable market for canine OA pain is enormous: an estimated 40% of dogs over age 8 have OA, and the majority are undertreated or untreated.

**The controversy.** Beginning in late 2023 and accelerating through 2024, veterinarians and pet owners reported adverse events including ataxia (loss of coordination), seizures, muscle atrophy, and in rare cases, death. The FDA's Center for Veterinary Medicine (CVM) received a significant number of adverse event reports. Social media amplified individual cases. Some veterinary practices stopped recommending Librela. Zoetis has maintained that the adverse event rate is within expected parameters for a widely prescribed new drug and that causation has not been established.

**The financial impact.** U.S. companion animal revenue for Pain & Sedation includes Librela, Solensia, and Rimadyl. The 2025 10-K specifically notes that U.S. companion animal revenue growth was "partially offset by lower sales of Librela" -- the first explicit acknowledgment of a U.S. sales decline for the product. Internationally, Librela continues to grow (the EU has had the product since 2020 with a longer track record and less controversy).

**My assessment.** The Librela controversy is real but manageable, for three reasons:

First, Zoetis has already developed the next generation. Lenivia (izenivetmab) was approved in the EU and Canada in 2025 as a 3-month dosing mAb for canine OA pain. Portela (relfovetmab) was similarly approved for cats with 3-month dosing. These longer-acting mAbs reduce injection frequency by 3x, potentially improving the risk/benefit perception. If approved in the U.S. (likely 2026-2027), they provide an on-ramp for veterinarians who pulled back from monthly Librela.

Second, the international experience remains positive. The EU has had 5+ years of post-marketing data. The CVMP has not taken restrictive action. Sales continue to grow in Europe and other international markets.

Third, the canine OA pain market is so severely underserved that even with controversy, Librela/Lenivia represent a step-change improvement over NSAIDs for many patients. Veterinarians are not abandoning the category -- they are being more selective about patient selection and monitoring.

**The broader pipeline.** Beyond the pain franchise, ZTS's pipeline includes:
- **Simparica franchise extensions** (new tick claims, flea tapeworm prevention approved 2025)
- **Vetscan Imagyst AI platform** (AI dermatology, AI fecal, AI urine sediment, AI blood smear, AI Masses added 2025)
- **H5N2 avian influenza vaccine** (conditionally licensed for dairy cattle and poultry, used for Hawaiian monk seals)
- **Dectomax-CA1** (first screwworm myiasis prevention in cattle, conditional U.S. approval 2025)
- **Blacksmith Medicines collaboration** (novel antibiotics for livestock, started 2024)
- **PetMedix and adivo acquisitions** (antibody-based therapeutics R&D platforms)

R&D spend of $698M (7.4% of revenue) is the highest in the animal health industry. The track record of converting R&D into revenue is strong -- nearly every major launch in the past decade has been a commercial success.

## PART VIII -- MOAT + CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### The Moat (Wide, Durable)

Zoetis possesses one of the widest moats in healthcare, built on five reinforcing advantages:

**1. Regulatory barriers.** Every animal health product requires market-by-market regulatory approval from agencies including the FDA/CVM, USDA/CVB, EMA/CVMP, and dozens of national authorities. ZTS has products approved in 100+ countries. The institutional knowledge, filing expertise, and relationships required to navigate this regulatory maze across all species and geographies cannot be replicated quickly.

**2. No veterinary biosimilar pathway.** This is the most underappreciated moat factor. In human healthcare, the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) created an abbreviated pathway for biosimilars that has eroded biologic exclusivity. No equivalent pathway exists in veterinary medicine. Librela, Cytopoint, Solensia, Lenivia and Portela are monoclonal antibodies that enjoy functional perpetual exclusivity -- competitors would need to run full clinical programs to gain approval.

**3. Salesforce scale and relationships.** With ~4,050 sales representatives and technical specialists, ZTS has the largest direct salesforce in animal health. These are not order-takers -- they provide clinical consulting, disease management training, and herd optimization advice. The relationships with individual veterinarians and livestock producers create high switching costs.

**4. Vaccine master seeds.** Vaccine manufacturing requires proprietary master seed stocks and adjuvant formulations. ZTS maintains one of the largest libraries of veterinary master seeds in the world. These cannot be duplicated and provide effectively permanent competitive protection for the vaccine portfolio.

**5. Diagnostics-Rx integration.** The Vetscan Imagyst AI platform creates an ecosystem lock-in: veterinarians using ZTS diagnostic instruments are more likely to prescribe ZTS pharmaceuticals. The addition of AI fecal, dermatology, urine sediment, blood smear, and Masses analysis to a single platform increases switching costs annually.

### Capital Allocation

Management has been an excellent allocator of capital, with one important caveat:

**Buybacks have been aggressive and well-timed.** In 2025 alone, ZTS repurchased 23.9M shares for $3.2B at an average price of ~$134. The share count has declined from 502M (2014) to 444M (2025) -- an 11.5% reduction. Importantly, the December 2025 convertible issuance funded $1.78B of the buyback at prices 40-50% below the 5-year average stock price. This is textbook capital allocation: issue cheap debt (0.25% coupon) to buy back stock at trough valuations.

**Dividends have been raised every year since initiation.** 12 consecutive annual raises at a 21.5% CAGR. The FCF payout ratio has remained below 40% in most years, providing a substantial buffer.

**M&A has been disciplined.** Major acquisitions include Abaxis ($1.9B, 2018), Jurox (~$0.3B, 2022), PetMedix, adivo, and Veterinary Pathology Group (2025). None of these were transformative bet-the-company deals -- they are bolt-on acquisitions that extend platform capabilities in diagnostics, mAb R&D, and reference lab services. Goodwill of $2.77B represents only 20% of total assets -- manageable and not indicative of acquisition overpayment.

**The caveat: leverage.** The $2B convertible issuance was financially astute (0.25% coupon, capped calls limit dilution up to ~$212/share) but it pushed gross debt to $9.0B. The debt maturity schedule is well-laddered (nothing due until 2027), but if ZTS needed to refinance in a higher-rate environment, the cost of capital could increase meaningfully. Current coverage ratios remain comfortable: interest coverage is approximately 10x+ EBITDA/interest.

## PART IX -- RISKS

**Risk 1: Librela regulatory action (Medium probability, High impact).** If the FDA CVM were to add a boxed warning, restrict Librela to specialist-only prescription, or (worst case) withdraw approval, the financial impact would be $400-600M in direct revenue loss plus downstream damage to the mAb platform narrative. I rate this as medium probability because: (a) the CVM has not taken action despite 2+ years of post-marketing data, (b) the adverse event rate appears within expected ranges for a widely prescribed new veterinary biologic, and (c) Lenivia provides a fallback product. However, the reputational damage from any formal regulatory action would be severe and long-lasting.

**Risk 2: Patent cliffs on legacy livestock products (High probability, Medium impact).** Draxxin has already lost 66% of U.S. revenue to generics since 2021. Excede/Naxcel formulation patents expire 2026-2027 in Japan and Brazil (the last remaining markets with protection). Cerenia injectable generics are entering the U.S. and EU markets. These are legacy livestock and companion animal products, and the revenue declines are real. The mitigant is that ZTS has been replacing these revenues with growth from newer franchises (Simparica Trio, Apoquel Chewable, diagnostics, mAbs).

**Risk 3: Companion animal spending normalization (Medium probability, Medium impact).** The COVID pet adoption boom created an unsustainable spike in veterinary spending growth. The normalization back to 4-6% annual growth (from 8-12% during 2020-2022) is already visible in ZTS's decelerating revenue growth. If a recession causes pet spending to decline (historically rare but not impossible), ZTS's premium valuation would be further pressured.

**Risk 4: Balance sheet leverage (Low probability, High impact).** At $9.0B gross debt and ~2.5x net debt/EBITDA, ZTS is more leveraged than at any point in its history. While the debt structure is favorable (the $2B convertible at 0.25% is essentially free money for 3.5 years), a severe cash flow shortfall could force a choice between dividend growth and debt reduction. I rate this as low probability because operating cash flow of $2.9B provides ample coverage, and the company maintains an undrawn $1.25B revolver expiring 2030.

**Risk 5: China macro weakness (Medium probability, Low impact).** China revenue declined from $320M (2023) to $227M (2025) -- a 29% decline over two years. Companion animal spending in China is highly sensitive to consumer confidence. While China is only 2.4% of total revenue, it was supposed to be the next major growth driver. Further deterioration would not be material to the overall business but would dampen the emerging markets growth narrative.

**Risk 6: Key-person risk -- CEO Kristin Peck.** Peck has been CEO since January 2020 and has overseen the mAb platform launches, the operating margin expansion from 34% to 38%, and the aggressive capital return program. Her departure or a strategic pivot under new leadership could alter the capital allocation philosophy that dividend growth investors depend on.

## PART XI -- VARIANT PERCEPTION

**What consensus believes:** ZTS is a premium compounder that has permanently de-rated due to the Librela controversy, slowing companion animal spending growth, and execution risk on the diagnostics build-out. Fair value is $140-160 (the current sell-side median is ~$165).

**What I believe differently:**

1. **The Librela controversy is a product-level issue, not a franchise issue.** Consensus is conflating Librela (one product) with the entire mAb platform (five products and counting). Lenivia and Portela -- the 3-month mAbs -- are specifically designed to address the dosing frequency concern that amplified the adverse event reports. If either product gains U.S. approval in 2026-2027, the pain franchise re-accelerates regardless of Librela's trajectory.

2. **The buyback at these levels is massively value-creative.** ZTS repurchased 23.9M shares in 2025 at an average price of ~$134. The shares have a conservatively estimated intrinsic value of $180+. Every dollar spent on buybacks at current prices generates $0.30+ of value destruction for sellers and $0.30+ of value accretion for holders. The $2.4B remaining under the buyback authorization at these prices is the equivalent of a hidden dividend increase.

3. **The convertible note is misunderstood.** The market is treating the $2B convertible as dilutive and leveraging. In reality: (a) the 0.25% coupon is essentially free capital for 3.5 years, (b) the capped calls limit dilution up to $212/share (68% above current), (c) the proceeds funded $1.78B in buybacks at trough valuations. Net-net, the transaction reduces the share count by more than the convertible would dilute it below $212. This is a net positive for shareholders.

4. **The yield is approaching actionable levels for DGI investors.** ZTS has never yielded 1.5%+ for an extended period. The combination of a high starting yield (by ZTS standards) and 15%+ DGR creates a rare entry point for compounders. The last time the forward yield was this high was never -- this is literally the highest-yielding entry point in ZTS history.
