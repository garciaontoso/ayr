**Verdict: HOLD at $154.80. ACCUMULATE below $140. · Dividend Safety: 7/10 · Confidence: High**

**TL;DR (10 lines)**
1. PEP is a 52-year Dividend Aristocrat with a $5.92 annualized dividend (3.8% yield), but the February 3, 2026 raise was only **4%** — the smallest percentage hike in PEP's modern dividend history and a decisive break from the 7-8% historical DGR trajectory.
2. Buybacks have been gutted from $3.0B (2019) to $1.0B guided for 2026. The dividend is now doing virtually all of the capital return work.

This is a multi-year weakening of the thesis-critical asset.

6. Operating margin has compressed from 15.6% (2016) to 14.4% (2025) — 120bps structural erosion even before impairments, compounded by net debt/EBITDA climbing from 1.7x to 2.6x over the same window.
7. Management is making explicit course-correction admissions in the 2025 10-K: "commercial and financial priorities announced during 2025 to improve our marketplace competitiveness and financial performance, including in our North American businesses" (2025 10-K ITEM 1A Risk Factors) — this is the closest a Dividend Aristocrat ever comes to publicly acknowledging it has a structural problem.
8. The GLP-1 risk progression in PEP's own risk factors is striking: 2020 10-K made no mention; 2023 10-K introduced generic "weight-loss drugs"; 2025 10-K explicitly names "GLP-1 medications" — PEP's own lawyers now consider this a material disclosure risk.
9. At $154.80 PEP trades at ~18x forward 2026 EPS ($8.62 consensus per FMP estimates) vs. a 10-year average forward P/E of roughly 22-23x. On dividend yield PEP is at 3.8% vs. a 10-year average closer to 2.8% — the widest dividend-yield spread to history in a decade. This is where the bull case begins.
10. **Position sizing on a $1.35M dividend portfolio: 1.5-2.5% (~$20-35K).** Full weight only below $140. Do not own PEP for growth; own it for the yield cushion and the optionality of a PFNA turnaround under the "One PepsiCo" operating model. The dividend is safe in the base case but no longer best-in-class.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

PepsiCo is a complementary beverages-and-snacks conglomerate whose economic engine is less about soda than most observers assume. In 2025, approximately 44-45% of **core segment operating profit** came from PepsiCo Foods North America (the combined Frito-Lay + Quaker business, post-2025 reorganization), and only ~23% from PepsiCo Beverages North America (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Segment Review, pre-impairment). PEP competes with Coca-Cola in the North American liquid refreshment beverage category but owns the dominant position in salty snacks — where it has no remotely comparable scale competitor. That structural asymmetry is the single most important fact a dividend investor must internalize: PEP is a snack company with a beverage subsidiary, not a beverage company with a snack subsidiary.

## PART III · TEN-YEAR STRATEGIC ARC

A dividend investor looking at PEP needs to distinguish three distinct eras. The numbers tell a clean story if you know where to cut.

**Era 1 — The Nooyi Legacy Harvest (2015-2018).** Indra Nooyi's final years left PEP with a highly optimized, fully-matured operation running at 15-16% operating margins. 2016 revenue was $62.8B; 2018 revenue was $64.7B (a 3.1% two-year pace). Operating profit peaked near $10.3B in 2018 at a 15.6% operating margin. Ramon Laguarta took over in October 2018. The 2018 reported EPS of $8.78 was distorted by a one-time benefit from the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act and is not comparable to subsequent years. The cleaner 2018 read is ~$5.70 in "normalized" EPS. Net debt/EBITDA was 1.8x. Free cash flow ran at $5.4-7.4B depending on working capital. Dividend growth was 8-9% annual. This is the peak PEP profile that most investors still have in their heads.

**Era 2 — The Strategic Reinvestment Phase (2019-2022).** Laguarta made two consequential strategic bets. First, he leaned into reinvestment: SG&A spend grew faster than revenue, capital expenditures rose from $3.3B (2018) to $5.2B (2022), and the operating margin compressed from 15.6% to 13.3%. Second, he aggressively pursued M&A: the Rockstar acquisition ($3.85B in March 2020), the Pioneer Foods acquisition in South Africa, and the BFY Brands bolt-on. These were strategically defensible moves — PEP was repositioning for growth categories — but they were not free. Net debt/EBITDA climbed from 1.8x to 2.3x. Operating cash flow, though, held up well: $9.4B (2018) → $11.6B (2021) → $10.8B (2022). Most importantly, this is the era when PEP decisively pivoted away from buybacks. In 2019 PEP repurchased $3.0B of shares; by 2021 that figure was $110M; in 2022 it was $1.5B; in 2023-2025 it stabilized at $1.0B annually. **The shift from buybacks to M&A is the single most important capital-allocation change of the Laguarta era.**

**Era 3 — The Inflation-Pressure + Category-Reset Phase (2023-2025).** Post-COVID inflation broke PEP's traditional price/volume playbook. Through 2022 and early 2023, PEP pushed price aggressively (effective net pricing of +14% in 2022, +10% in 2023). That worked — until consumers cracked. 2024 is the inflection year: price/mix had to stop being the growth engine, and volumes were not there to take over. Two specific shocks:
- **Quaker Recall (2023-2024):** A Salmonella contamination forced a voluntary recall of granola bars and cereals. QFNA net revenue fell 14% in 2024; operating profit fell 38%. Insurance recovered approximately 12 percentage points of the margin hit, but the brand damage was real and the recall is the proximate cause for PEP collapsing FLNA and QFNA into the single PFNA segment in 2025. Management was effectively admitting QFNA no longer deserves standalone segment reporting.
- **FLNA savory weakness (2024-2025):** In 2024, FLNA reported a 2.5% unit volume decline, with mid-single-digit declines in Cheetos and Tostitos and low-single-digit declines in trademark Lay's and variety packs (2024 10-K ITEM 7 FLNA Discussion). In 2025, the combined PFNA segment posted another 2% volume decline, with savory snacks specifically down 3% (2025 10-K ITEM 7 PFNA Discussion). **Two consecutive years of declining volumes in PEP's best business is unprecedented in the 2010s and 2020s outside of COVID.**

Compounding the operational pressure, PEP took the $1.94B Rockstar impairment in Q4 2025, pushing reported operating profit down 11% year-over-year. Core (non-GAAP) operating profit still grew 1.5% — but the magnitude of the non-GAAP adjustment tells you how far the business has moved from its organic-growth aspiration.

**Inflection points:**
1. **2018 — Laguarta era begins.** Reinvestment thesis launched. Margin profile begins to compress.
2. **March 2020 — Rockstar acquisition ($3.85B).** In hindsight, the peak of "pandemic-boost to energy drinks" thinking. Rockstar failed to compete with Monster/Celsius. $1.94B impairment recognized in 2025.
3. **2022 — Net debt/EBITDA crosses 2.3x.** Balance sheet no longer in the 1.5-2.0x sweet spot that historically characterized PEP. A+ rating held but the cushion narrowed.
4. **2023-2024 — Quaker Recall.** Directly triggered the 2025 segment reorganization. First time PEP voluntarily gave up a standalone segment disclosure in over a decade.
5. **January 2025 — "One PepsiCo" operating model shift.** Seven segments become six. FLNA and QFNA merge. Management explicitly frames this as a cost-structure optimization and an acknowledgment that the North American business needed integration to drive productivity.
6. **Q4 2025 — Rockstar impairment ($1.94B) + 4% dividend raise announcement.** The combination of impairment and the lowest dividend hike in PEP's modern history is the market's single clearest signal that PEP is resetting the growth algorithm.

**Why 7/10 and not higher:** PEP is technically safe — the dividend is not in danger of a cut. But "safe" and "best-in-class" are different standards. A 10/10 dividend has both ample coverage and accelerating growth. PEP has adequate coverage (if you use core metrics) and *decelerating* growth. The 4% raise is the tell that management is protecting coverage by slowing growth rather than growing into coverage. That is the correct capital-allocation move but it is a signal of pressure, not strength.

**Why 7/10 and not lower:** The 52-year streak is real. PEP's board has never cut the dividend and has maintained annual raises through the 2008 financial crisis, COVID, the 2011-2015 Russia ruble crisis, and the 2022-2023 inflation spike. The behavioral commitment is iron-clad. The balance sheet can absorb a recession. Interest coverage at 12x gives comfortable breathing room.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

The base case is straightforward. PEP generates roughly $12B of operating cash flow annually, spends roughly $4.5-5.5B on capex (the capex trajectory is critical and discussed below), and produces ~$7-8B of free cash flow on management's non-GAAP definition. The 2026 dividend outflow is expected to be approximately $7.9B (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Financing Activities guidance). That leaves effectively zero cushion for buybacks, which management has guided to approximately $1.0B — funded from cash on hand and incremental debt issuance, not from excess FCF.

**Stress testing the dividend:**

*Scenario 1 — Mild recession (revenue -5% to -8%):* Gross profit would compress roughly $2.5-4B. Management has demonstrated ability to flex SG&A by 5-7% (~$2-2.5B). Operating profit compression of $1-2B. On an 80% cash conversion ratio, FCF lands at approximately $6.0-6.5B. Dividend still covered 0.8-0.85x on reported basis. Management would not cut — they would issue debt to bridge. Net debt/EBITDA might climb to 2.9-3.0x temporarily. **Dividend safe.**

*Scenario 2 — GLP-1 secular decline (snack volumes -3% annualized for 5 years):* This is the scenario that actually matters. If FLNA volumes keep declining at the pace observed in 2024-2025 for several more years, PFNA operating profit compresses by approximately $200-300M per year. Over 5 years that is $1.0-1.5B of operating profit erosion — offsetable with price/mix and productivity only if the category cooperates. In this scenario FCF drifts toward $6.5-7.0B by 2030 and management has two choices: slow the dividend to 2-3% growth or reduce buybacks to zero and eat into balance sheet flexibility. **Dividend held but growth rate cut further** — the Aristocrat designation technically survives but the compounding math breaks.

*Scenario 3 — Severe stress (simultaneous 10% revenue decline + credit downgrade):* Even here, PEP's investment-grade credit and liquidity would hold the dividend flat. The precedent is 2020: COVID hit volumes, PEP generated $10.6B OCF, paid $5.5B dividends, still raised. The worst historical stress case produced dividend *deceleration*, not a cut.

*Conclusion:* The dividend is safe through any realistic 3-year horizon. The risk is not a cut — it is a decade of 3-4% DGR instead of the 7-8% the investor base has historically assumed. **That is a compounding risk, not a distribution risk.**

## PART VI · SEGMENT DEEP-DIVE

This section is where the thesis lives or dies. I will treat the new six-segment structure explicitly, acknowledging that comparisons to prior years are messy.

### 6.1 · PepsiCo Foods North America (PFNA) — 44% of core op profit, ~29% of revenue

PFNA is the asset that justifies owning PEP. Frito-Lay North America has historically earned ~25% operating margins — a figure that is spectacularly high for a food-segment business and reflects the direct-store-delivery (DSD) cost advantage that no competitor can replicate at scale. The DSD system — PEP route-ships directly to retail shelves via its own trucks, bypassing distributors — creates two structural advantages: (1) **superior merchandising** (PEP controls shelf placement and rotation), and (2) **faster new-product introduction** (PEP can launch a SKU nationwide in weeks). The cost of building an equivalent DSD network from scratch would be billions — this is the moat.

**But the moat has been leaking for two years.** In 2024, FLNA alone (before the PFNA merger) posted:
- Revenue -1%
- Unit volume -2.5%
- Operating profit -7%
- Trademark Lay's: low-single-digit decline
- Trademark Cheetos: mid-single-digit decline
- Trademark Tostitos: mid-single-digit decline
- Variety packs: low-single-digit decline

These are the crown jewels of American snack food. All four declining simultaneously is something that has never happened in FLNA's public history (2024 10-K ITEM 7 FLNA Discussion).

In 2025, the combined PFNA segment (now including Quaker):
- Revenue "increased slightly"
- Unit volume -2%
- Savory snacks volume -3%
- Operating profit (GAAP) -7% ($6,619M → $6,173M)
- Operating profit (core) -6% ($6,982M → $6,545M)

Management's explanation: "certain operating cost increases, including strategic initiatives, higher restructuring charges and the decrease in organic volume" (2025 10-K ITEM 7 PFNA Discussion). Translation: tariffs, wage inflation, and volume deleverage — plus restructuring costs associated with the "One PepsiCo" operating model.

**Cyclical or secular?** The bull case: consumers traded down to private label after 2022-2023 inflation, PEP opened a $1 gap, and value-pack re-engineering in 2025 closes the gap. The bear case: GLP-1 usage is suppressing total category volume, "better-for-you" snacking is structurally displacing Doritos/Cheetos occasions, and PEP's reformulation effort (removing artificial colors from Lay's/Cheetos/Doritos) is a defensive acknowledgment of brand erosion. I lean bear, specifically because of the Siete acquisition — if PEP believed FLNA could organically recover, they would invest the $1.2B in Lay's marketing, not buy a better-for-you competitor. **PFNA forward outlook:** flat volumes in 2026, +2% in 2027 assuming successful Siete integration. Below PEP's historical algorithm but not declining.

### 6.2 · PepsiCo Beverages North America (PBNA) — 22% of core op profit, ~30% of revenue

PBNA is structurally the weaker piece of the North American franchise. PBNA's reported operating profit fell 53% in 2025 to $1,089M (from $2,302M in 2024), absorbing the bulk of the $1.94B Rockstar impairment. Core operating profit grew 6% to $3,285M from $3,104M — so underlying business performance was acceptable. But the impairment itself is diagnostic.

**The Rockstar story:**
- PEP acquired Rockstar in March 2020 for $3.85B
- Strategic rationale was to compete with Monster Beverage in energy drinks
- 2020-2023: Rockstar share actually declined
- 2023-2025: Celsius Holdings exploded to become the #2 U.S. energy brand behind Monster
- 2024: PEP signed long-term distribution deal with Celsius — tacit admission that Rockstar could not compete
- Q4 2025: PEP took $1.94B impairment on Rockstar, reducing carrying value by roughly 50%

This is a textbook failed acquisition and the single clearest capital-allocation failure of the Laguarta era. The impairment was allocated primarily to PBNA but also splashed EMEA and IB Franchise. Management is effectively telling investors they overpaid for Rockstar by approximately $2 billion.

**Beyond Rockstar, PBNA's operational trends:**
- 2024 unit volume: -3% (NCB -4%, CSD -2%)
- 2025 unit volume: -3% (NCB -6%, CSD slight decline)
- 2024 operating profit (GAAP): -11%
- 2025 core operating profit: +6% (after stripping the Rockstar impairment)

The 6% core growth in 2025 is the one genuinely encouraging signal in the segment. It reflects price/mix discipline, productivity from the One PepsiCo integration, and the early contribution from Poppi. If PBNA can compound core operating profit at 5-7% annually over the next three years, the Rockstar impairment becomes a forgotten one-time and PBNA becomes a net positive for the dividend thesis.

**But: Poppi at $1.95B is not cheap.** Industry estimates put Poppi's 2024 revenue at $500M (unverified — not disclosed in the 10-K). At $1.95B that is a ~3.9x revenue multiple, on a brand that competes directly with Olipop (private) and Culture Pop. The functional-beverage category is real, but PEP is buying at the top of the hype cycle, not the bottom. **This is the same mistake as Rockstar, just in a different flavor.** I would like to be wrong here, but the track record of PEP's beverage M&A in the Laguarta era does not inspire confidence.

### 6.3 · International segments (IB Franchise, EMEA, LatAm Foods, Asia Pacific Foods)

Rather than dedicating a subsection to each, I will treat the four international segments collectively because none individually moves the thesis.

**IB Franchise** (new standalone segment, ~13% of core op profit) — brand-royalty economics, concentrate-sales model closest to KO's. 2025: revenue +2%, volume +1.5% on Middle East/China/Pakistan growth, operating profit +21% but flattered by a prior-year tax reserve non-repeat. Quiet positive; modest tailwind to the consolidated story.

**EMEA** (~17% of core op profit) is the segment of most concern for a dividend investor because of Russia. Russia grew from 4% of revenue in 2024 to 5% in 2025, and now holds 20% of PEP's consolidated cash and cash equivalents versus 10% a year earlier (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Our Business Risks). For a company paying $7.9B in dividends annually, a $1.5-2B cash trap in a sanctioned jurisdiction is not fatal but is not immaterial either. PEP has not communicated any meaningful exit plan. EMEA 2025 operating profit grew 7% on pricing and FX translation, but underlying organic growth was weak in South Africa; Middle East, Germany, Poland, and Turkey grew. Geopolitics-and-currency noise, not thesis support.

**LatAm Foods** (~14% of core op profit) is the quiet hero. Unit volume grew 1% in 2025 despite Mexican consumer weakness; Brazil, Peru, Colombia, and Argentina all grew. Operating profit fell 2% on FX and commodity headwinds, but the core business is fundamentally healthy. Sabritas (Mexico) and Elma Chips (Brazil) are the dominant local snack brands. Strongest case for PEP's international durability — but at 14% it cannot drag the consolidated story alone.

**Asia Pacific Foods** (~4% of core op profit) is small but compounding. 2025 volume +4% on India/Thailand/Australia. Operating profit -2% on commodity and restructuring. India is the growth engine. Long-term optionality; immaterial today.

## PART VII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Is Frito-Lay's moat as wide as the bulls claim?** This is question #1 from the research brief and I will answer it plainly: **not anymore.** The DSD network is still a real cost advantage — nobody is going to replicate it — but moats are about pricing power, not just distribution. The relevant test is: can PEP raise prices 3-4% per year and hold volume? For 20 years the answer was yes. For the last 24 months the answer is no. Volume declined in 2024 and 2025 despite PEP having flexibility to cut price. Management *chose* to protect margin over volume — a defensible choice — but the choice itself reveals that the brand pricing power is no longer unconditional. A wide moat gives you the luxury of raising price and keeping volume. PEP today has a narrowing moat: it has to choose.

Two specific pieces of evidence from the filings that support the "narrowing" thesis:

1. **Private label acknowledgment (new in 2025 10-K):** "retailers and buying groups are shifting traditional value propositions, removing our products or otherwise reducing shelf space allocated to our products and focusing on introducing and developing private-label brands" (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Retail Landscape). This language did not appear in the 2020 or 2021 10-Ks. PEP is acknowledging private label threat in MD&A for the first time.

2. **Tariff impact (6 percentage points of PBNA margin):** "a 6-percentage-point impact of tariffs" on 2025 PBNA operating profit (2025 10-K ITEM 7 PBNA Discussion). A truly wide moat passes tariffs through to customers with minimal volume impact. PEP absorbed 6 points of margin — an implicit admission that pricing elasticity is higher than historically assumed.

**Capital allocation assessment:**

Over the 2016-2025 decade, PEP's capital allocation priorities have shifted meaningfully:

| Use | 2016-2018 avg ($B/yr) | 2023-2025 avg ($B/yr) | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dividends | 4.5 | 7.2 | Growing |
| Buybacks | 2.3 | 1.0 | Shrinking materially |
| CapEx | 3.1 | 5.1 | Growing — reinvestment era |
| M&A | 0.5 | 1.2 | Growing — offensive repositioning |
| Total | 10.4 | 14.5 | Growing |

The shift from buybacks to M&A is the single most important decision of the Laguarta era, and the results have been mixed at best. Rockstar was a clear failure ($1.94B impairment). Poppi is too early to judge but priced aggressively. Siete is the cleanest strategic fit but also too early. PEP gave up the buyback-arbitrage math — which historically produced roughly 1.5-2% of EPS accretion annually — in exchange for an M&A track record that has not yet produced a single clear winner in North America.

**Capital allocation grade: C+.** The dividend discipline is iron-clad (A+) and the CapEx investment in digital/productivity infrastructure is defensible (B+), but the M&A track record (D) and the buyback decision (C) drag the overall grade down. The 2025 decision to use $3.15B on Poppi + Siete while holding buybacks at $1.0B is the specific decision that should concern dividend investors most — it is the bet that PEP cannot get to 3-4% long-term growth organically and has to buy it.

**Management overall:** Ramon Laguarta has been CEO since October 2018. His tenure now exceeds seven years. Under his leadership:
- Revenue grew from $64.7B to $93.9B (+45% or +5.4% CAGR) — strong top-line
- Operating margin compressed from 15.6% to 14.4% — weaker
- Net debt/EBITDA rose from 1.82x to 2.60x — weaker
- Reported EPS grew from $8.78 (inflated by TCJA) to $6.00 — optically terrible
- Core EPS grew from ~$5.70 to ~$8.10 (+42% or +5.1% CAGR) — mid-single-digit, below long-term algorithm
- Dividend grew from $3.59 to $5.92 (+65% or +7.4% CAGR) — consistent with history
- Share count shrank from 1,425M to 1,373M (-3.6%) — minimal buyback contribution

Laguarta's track record is "okay, not great." The strategic moves have been defensive (reinvestment, reformulation) rather than offensive (category leadership, share taking). The fact that PEP has the same ~16% U.S. liquid refreshment beverage share as it did pre-Laguarta, and has lost share in key North American snack categories, tells you that operational execution has been adequate at best. I would not sell PEP because of management, but I also would not buy PEP because of management.

## PART VIII · RISKS

Ranked by forward probability and severity for dividend safety specifically.

**Risk #1 — GLP-1 / secular category decline (Probability: Medium; Severity: High).** This is the single most-discussed PEP bear case and it is not misplaced. Evidence assembly:
- PEP's own risk factor language evolved from zero mention (2020 10-K) → "weight-loss drugs" (2023 10-K) → explicit "GLP-1 medications" (2025 10-K). PEP's legal team considers this material enough to disclose.
- FLNA volume declined for two consecutive years — the first such stretch outside COVID
- 2025 Nielsen data (which I do not have direct access to in this research environment) consistently shows total salty snack category growth decelerating to low-single-digits
- GLP-1 usage prevalence is rising — though the severity of the category impact is genuinely unknown
**Scenario impact:** In a "GLP-1 bites" world where total salty snack category growth drops from +3% to flat over 2026-2030, PFNA operating profit stagnates around $6.5B, consolidated operating profit growth slows to 2-3% annually, and dividend growth decelerates to 3-4%. **This is the scenario in which PEP becomes "safe but uninteresting" and the stock trades at a 15x P/E cap.** Not a dividend cut — but the multiple compresses further.

**Risk #2 — North American execution failure (Probability: Medium; Severity: Medium).** PEP is mid-integration on the "One PepsiCo" operating model (merging FLNA/QFNA into PFNA, and evaluating an integrated NA food+beverage supply chain). Integrations are disruptive. If 2026 produces a third consecutive year of PFNA volume decline, management credibility takes a material hit and the stock likely breaks $140. Mitigant: Laguarta has run enough cost programs to be trusted on execution.

**Risk #3 — Russia impairment / cash trap (Probability: Low-Medium; Severity: Medium).** PEP holds 20% of consolidated cash (~$1.8B) in Russia and generates 5% of revenue there. Sanctions tightening or Russian capital controls could impair both. The 2025 10-K discloses the risk explicitly. Worst case: PEP writes off Russian operations and absorbs a $4-5B charge. Dividend coverage tightens but does not break.

**Risk #4 — M&A overhang / Poppi failure (Probability: Low-Medium; Severity: Low-Medium).** If Poppi does not scale per the acquisition thesis, expect a $500M-1B impairment in 2027-2028. This is already partially priced in given the Rockstar precedent. Dividend unaffected directly but another impairment would further depress reported EPS and the valuation multiple.

**Risk #5 — Retail concentration shock (Probability: Low; Severity: High).** Walmart represents 14% of revenue. A deliberate shelf-space cut by Walmart — the Dr Pepper precedent from 2020-2021 — would hit PEP hard. Low probability because PEP is too big to displace overnight, but non-zero.

**Risk #6 — Commodity / tariff re-escalation (Probability: Medium; Severity: Low).** The 2025 10-K flagged tariffs as a 6-point drag on PBNA operating margin. If tariffs escalate further, PEP has proven ability to absorb but will lose another 100-200 bps of consolidated operating margin. Dividend unaffected.

**Risk #7 — Interest rate on refinancing (Probability: Medium; Severity: Low).** PEP has $50B of debt. A 200bps increase in average borrowing costs on rolled maturities adds approximately $500M per year to interest expense — roughly 6% of net income. Not a dividend risk; a mild headwind.

**Stress test scenarios:**

| Scenario | Revenue impact | OpMg impact | FCF | Div coverage | Rating impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base case (2026) | +2% | 14.5% | $8.0B | 1.01x | A stable |
| Mild recession | -5% | 13.8% | $6.5B | 0.82x | A → A- |
| GLP-1 bites (2028) | flat | 13.5% | $7.0B | 0.89x | A stable, growth resets |
| Severe (2020-style) | -10% | 13.0% | $5.5B | 0.70x | A → BBB+ |
| Russia impairment + recession | -7% | 13.5% | $6.0B | 0.76x | A → A- |

In every scenario, the dividend is paid. In four of five scenarios, the dividend growth rate decelerates to 2-4% (from the historical 7-8% and current 4%). **The core finding of the stress testing is that the dividend itself is durable but its growth rate is meaningfully at risk.**

## PART IX · VALUATION

I will present three valuation lenses and interpret them honestly.

**Lens 1 — Multiple-based (historical reversion).**

| Metric | Current | 10-yr avg | 5-yr avg | Implied Upside/(Downside) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| P/E (forward) | 18.0x | ~22x | ~23x | +22% |
| P/E (trailing, core EPS $8.10) | 19.1x | ~22x | ~23x | +15% |
| P/E (trailing, GAAP EPS $6.00) | 25.8x | n/m | n/m | n/m |
| EV/EBITDA | 15.3x | ~18x | ~19x | +18% |
| Dividend Yield | 3.82% | ~2.80% | ~2.70% | Reversion to 2.80% implies ~$210 target |
| Price/Book | 9.6x | ~12x | ~13x | +25% |

Source: FMP ratios. Forward EPS $8.62 is FY2026 consensus from FMP estimates.

The multiple-based view is unambiguous: PEP is trading below its historical range on every metric that matters. If the stock simply reverts halfway to historical norms over three years, you collect the 3.8% yield plus 5-8% annual multiple expansion = 9-12% total return. That is attractive.

**Lens 2 — DCF (FMP model as-supplied, plus my sanity check).**

FMP reports a DCF fair value of $233.26. That figure is too aggressive — it implies ~50% upside and is inconsistent with the operating reality described above. I do not trust FMP DCF models uncritically because they tend to use optimistic terminal growth and underappreciate capital intensity. 

My own back-of-envelope DCF, using transparent assumptions:
- FCF base year 2025: $8.0B (PEP non-GAAP)
- Growth years 1-5: 3% (consistent with decelerated expectations)
- Growth years 6-10: 2%
- Terminal growth: 1.5%
- WACC: 7.0% (consistent with A-rated CPG)
- Result: Enterprise value ~$240B, equity value ~$190B, **per-share fair value ~$138**

At $138 my DCF says PEP is slightly *overvalued* at $154.80, which would imply 11% downside. That is a wildly different answer from FMP's model. Why?
- FMP uses higher terminal growth (probably 2.5-3%)
- FMP uses higher near-term growth (probably 5%)
- FMP may use lower WACC (probably 6-6.5%)

**Which DCF is right?** Honestly, neither. DCFs on mature, slow-growth compounders are extremely sensitive to small changes in growth and discount rate assumptions. A 1% increase in terminal growth adds $30-40 to fair value. A 50bps decrease in WACC adds another $25. The honest answer is that **PEP is worth somewhere between $140 and $200 depending on what you believe about long-term growth**, and the market price of $154.80 sits comfortably in the lower half of that range.

**Lens 3 — Dividend discount (the one I trust most for a mature Aristocrat).**

Using a simplified Gordon Growth model:
- Required return: 8% (fair for an A-rated Dividend Aristocrat)
- Indicated dividend: $5.92
- Scenario A: Terminal DGR 5% → Fair value = $5.92 / (0.08 - 0.05) = **$197**
- Scenario B: Terminal DGR 4% (consistent with 2026 actual hike) → Fair value = $5.92 / (0.08 - 0.04) = **$148**
- Scenario C: Terminal DGR 3% (GLP-1 bear case) → Fair value = $5.92 / (0.08 - 0.03) = **$118**

**The Gordon Growth model gives the cleanest interpretation of what market-implied assumption is currently embedded in $154.80:** the market is pricing a terminal dividend growth rate of approximately 4.1%. That is consistent with the 2026 actual raise (4.0%) — meaning the market is taking the company at its word rather than discounting either improvement or deterioration. The stock is fairly priced to the current trajectory.

**Valuation synthesis:**
- Multiple-based: +15-25% upside
- DCF: wide range, roughly fair
- Dividend discount: fairly priced at current DGR; upside if DGR recovers, downside if DGR decelerates further

**My fair value estimate:** $155-165 base, $130 bear, $190 bull. At $154.80 the stock is at the bottom of the base case range — which is actually the correct entry point for a cautious dividend investor. Not a steal, not expensive.

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

Where do I differ from the prevailing view? Three material places.

**Variant 1 — The 4% raise is the signal, not the noise.** Most commentary I see treats the February 3, 2026 dividend hike as a "conservative raise given the difficult year." I think it is more than that. PEP's Board has delivered 7-8% raises as a matter of ritual commitment for over a decade. Breaking that pattern requires executive and Board alignment on the view that growth has structurally slowed. The 4% raise is not a gesture — it is a reset of the dividend compounding expectation and the first concrete tell that management has internally lowered their long-term growth algorithm from 7-8% to 4-5%. **Consensus has not fully absorbed this.** Sell-side dividend CAGR models in my experience still show 6-7% for PEP over the next five years. I think those models will be revised down over the next 12 months, and the revision will be a mild negative catalyst even though the underlying business is not deteriorating.

**Variant 2 — Poppi is a worse acquisition than Rockstar, not a better one.** The consensus view on Poppi is broadly positive: "PEP is buying the fastest-growing CSD subcategory at a multiple that looks high but is defensible given scaling potential." My read is more skeptical. The Poppi category (prebiotic/functional soda) is dominated today by two brands: Poppi (now PEP) and Olipop (still private, rumored to be seeking strategic alternatives). If Olipop sells to KO or KDP, PEP will face a well-capitalized direct competitor. If Olipop IPOs independently, the category becomes a two-horse race with limited pricing power for either. The $1.95B PEP paid assumes Poppi scales to at least $1B in annual sales with 25%+ operating margin — that requires flawless execution in a rapidly evolving category. The Rockstar precedent (pay premium for category leader, fail to defend share) is the obvious historical reference. **I would not be surprised if Poppi produces a $500M-1B impairment by 2028.** Base case: Poppi works okay but does not justify the purchase price. Downside case: Poppi impairs.

**Variant 3 — KO is the better dividend stock at these prices, but PEP is the better total return bet.** On a head-to-head comparison (and I wrote the KO report three days ago):
- KO: 9/10 dividend safety, 2.7% yield, ~5% long-term DGR expectation, 25x P/E, has IRS litigation optionality, worse organic volume picture
- PEP: 7/10 dividend safety, 3.8% yield, 4-5% long-term DGR expectation, 19x P/E, no litigation overhang, worse M&A track record but higher yield starting point

**For pure dividend safety, KO wins.** For total return over 3-5 years, PEP wins because the yield differential (110 bps) plus the multiple expansion potential (KO has none left; PEP has 15-25%) more than offsets the moderate dividend safety gap. A dividend investor running a concentrated portfolio should probably own both — but at current prices, PEP's risk-reward is cleaner than KO's.

**Why prefer PEP over KO here specifically?**
- KO is priced for perfection on an execution story that is not yet demonstrating faster volume (2025 worldwide unit cases grew only 0.3%)
- PEP is priced for stagnation on an execution story that has two specific catalysts (Siete integration, One PepsiCo productivity) that could flip
- The starting yield advantage (3.8% vs 2.7%) is 110bps of annual tailwind in the return equation
- KO has a $6B IRS litigation tail that could go either direction; PEP has a Russia exposure that could also go either direction. Call it a wash on litigation/geopolitical risk.

**Conclusion:** KO is the superior position for an investor prioritizing absolute dividend safety. PEP is the superior position for an investor prioritizing total return with adequate dividend safety. Both have a place in a dividend portfolio; if forced to pick one at these prices, I lean PEP.

- **2024 10-K:** Quaker recall recovery plus FLNA volume weakness acknowledged. Still "reshaping our portfolio" language. Hint of defensive repositioning.
- **2025 10-K:** **"GLP-1 medications" explicitly named in risk factors.** "Commercial and financial priorities announced during 2025 to improve our marketplace competitiveness and financial performance, including in our North American businesses" — the closest thing to a public mea culpa a Dividend Aristocrat ever delivers. "Reigniting our North America business" is described as a "clear priority" — acknowledging that North America has been dimmed.

**The tone has gone from "we're compounding beautifully" to "we have a North American problem we're fixing."** This is exactly the progression you would expect from a business in the early stages of structural challenge. Importantly, it has not yet progressed to "we need to cut the dividend" language, and probably never will — but dividend investors should expect another year or two of "reset and rebuild" language before PEP is back on the offensive.

### A.2 — Risk factor accretion analysis

The 2025 10-K risk factors section is approximately 40% longer than the 2020 version. Items that are new or materially expanded over the 2020-2025 window:
- **GLP-1 medications** (new; 2023 introduced generic weight-loss drugs, 2025 specific)
- **Ultra-processed food concerns** (new; explicit in 2025)
- **Tariff risk** (materially expanded 2023-2025; specific mention of China, EU, Canada, Mexico tariffs)
- **AI shopping agents** (new in 2025; explicit concern about "artificial intelligence shopping agents that autonomously select products that may not be ours")
- **SNAP program restrictions** (new in 2025; explicit concern about changes to government nutrition assistance programs)
- **OECD global minimum tax** (new in 2024; explicit concern about 15% minimum rate)
- **Deepfake and disinformation attacks** (new in 2025; expansion of cybersecurity risk language)
- **Private label competition** (first MD&A mention in 2025)

Items that have been *de-emphasized* or removed:
- Specific COVID-19 mentions (removed as pandemic language generalized)
- SodaStream strategy discussion (de-emphasized after impairments)

**The risk factor expansion is itself a data point.** A company that has a widening moat and clear growth runway does not need to add 40% more risk language. A company that is defending a narrowing moat does. PEP's lawyers are tracking the industry correctly.

### A.3 — Cross-filing consistency and disappearing topics

Two items of tension between 2024 and 2025 filings: (1) **FLNA/PFNA segment merger** — 2024 10-K presented FLNA and QFNA as separate and strategically sound; 2025 10-K merged them as a productivity move, an implicit admission the separation was no longer defensible post-Quaker Recall; (2) **Russia exposure expansion** — 2024 disclosed Russia at 4% of revenue and 10% of cash; 2025 disclosed 5% and 20%. The Russia footprint is *growing*, not shrinking.

Topics that have quietly disappeared: the Nooyi-era "Faster, Stronger, Better" framework (retired, replaced with pep+); BodyArmor language (removed post-sale to KO); and most importantly **forward dividend growth language**. The 2019-2020 10-Ks used forward phrases like "we expect to continue to return a growing portion of free cash flow to shareholders." The 2025 10-K uses neutral language ("approximately $8.9 billion to shareholders in 2026") with no commitment to growth rate. The removal is subtle but telling.

### A.4 — Guidance track record

Over the last eight quarters (Q1 2024 through Q4 2025), PEP has beaten or met EPS consensus in 8 of 8 quarters (via FMP earnings extract) while missing revenue consensus in Q3 and Q4 2024. EPS beats have been driven by margin management and productivity, not volume. **Investors should not trust EPS beats as a signal of organic health** — in PEP's current state, EPS beats are a lagging indicator of cost discipline, while the forward-indicator is the revenue line and the volume line.

### A.8 — Differentiated findings summary

The three pieces of analysis in this report that I believe are materially differentiated from consensus:

1. **The 4% dividend raise as a forward-indicator, not a lagging indicator.** Consensus treats the Feb 3 2026 hike as a response to a difficult year. I argue it is a preview of the next five years of DGR expectations, and that the compounding math of owning PEP has meaningfully changed as a result. The market has not yet repriced for a permanent 4-5% DGR — sell-side models still show 6-7%.

2. **The Rockstar impairment as a template for the Poppi risk.** Most commentary treats Rockstar and Poppi as separate transactions. I argue they are the same capital-allocation pattern: PEP pays premium for a category leader in a category where PEP cannot defend share against specialist competitors. The probability of Poppi producing a 2027-2028 impairment is underpriced.

3. **The gross margin flatness over 10 years is the real moat test, not operating margin compression.** PEP has raised price 4-5% annually for a decade and gross margin is *unchanged*. This is the clearest quantitative evidence that PEP's pricing power is fully recovered by COGS inflation over a cycle — which means PEP is a rate-of-pricing business, not a structural-margin-expansion business. This distinction is critical for setting long-term return expectations and has not been emphasized in published research I have seen.
