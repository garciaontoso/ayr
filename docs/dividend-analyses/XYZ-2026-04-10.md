## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE. Capital Return Program Safety: 7/10. Conviction: Medium.**

Block, Inc. is the most intellectually challenging position in the fintech landscape. At $61.35 the market is valuing a business generating $10.4B in gross profit (up 17% YoY), $1.7B in GAAP operating income, $1.3B in net income, and $2.42B in free cash flow at just 3.6x trailing gross profit and 15.4x trailing FCF. For context, PayPal trades at roughly 7.1x FCF and Shopify at 10x+ revenue. The disconnect exists because the market cannot decide whether Block is a high-growth fintech platform or a Jack Dorsey vanity project with a bitcoin fetish and too many distractions (TIDAL, Proto mining rigs, Bitkey wallets).

Our reading of the six years of SEC filings is that the market is materially undervaluing the Cash App franchise and meaningfully over-penalizing the bitcoin exposure and Afterpay integration drag. Cash App produced $6.3B in gross profit in 2025 on $15.7B in segment revenue, growing GP 21% YoY. That segment alone, valued at even 8x gross profit (conservative for a 59M-user neobank growing 20%+), is worth ~$50B — more than the entire current market cap. Square produced $3.9B in gross profit on $8.5B in revenue, growing 9%. Even at a mature 5x GP multiple, Square is worth ~$20B. Sum-of-parts math delivers $70B+ before assigning any value to Proto, Bitkey, or TIDAL. The stock trades at $37B.

The problem, and the reason for only "medium" conviction, is threefold. First, Dorsey's decision to cut 40%+ of the workforce is either visionary (AI replaces human overhead) or catastrophic (product innovation dies). We will not know for 12-18 months. Second, $1.2B in annual stock-based compensation against $1.3B in net income means GAAP EPS is almost entirely SBC-funded — real economic earnings are much lower than the headline suggests. Third, transaction/loan/consumer receivable losses surged 68% to $1.34B in 2025, driven by Cash App Borrow's aggressive expansion (loan losses up 154% to $821M). If credit losses accelerate beyond management's models, Cash App's most profitable growth engine becomes a liability.

**Top 3 reasons to own at $61.35:**

1. **Cash App at scale is dramatically undervalued.** 59M monthly transacting actives, $316B in annual inflows, $6.3B gross profit growing 21% — this is a top-5 US consumer financial app priced as if it is in decline. It is not.
2. **FCF inflection creates buyback capacity that compounds.** $2.4B in FCF vs $2.3B in buybacks. Share count declining ~3-4% annually at current prices. At $61 this is an extremely efficient use of capital — management is retiring shares at roughly 15x FCF.
3. **Operating leverage is still in early innings.** Adjusted Operating Income of $2.1B (20% of GP) leaves enormous room for margin expansion, especially post-restructuring. PYPL runs at 18% op margin on revenue; Block's GP margin expansion from 33% (2020) to 43% (2025) is structural.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own at $61.35:**

1. **The 40% headcount cut could break product velocity.** Reducing from 10,205 to ~6,000 employees is not optimization — it is amputation. If engineering output suffers, Cash App loses its edge against Venmo, Chime, Apple Pay, and dozens of neobank challengers.
2. **SBC of $1.2B is nearly equal to GAAP net income of $1.3B.** Real economic earnings — net income minus SBC — are roughly $100M. You are paying 370x real economic earnings. The buyback is funded by free cash flow, not real earnings, because SBC is a non-cash charge — but eventually those shares vest and dilute.
3. **Jack Dorsey is an ideological CEO, not a disciplined capital allocator.** Bitcoin mining rigs (Proto), music streaming (TIDAL, impaired $60.3M in Q4 2024), self-custody wallets (Bitkey), and the company name change to "Block" (2021) then ticker to "XYZ" (2025) are all signals of a CEO who prioritizes vision over shareholder returns. The $9B buyback authorization is a welcome counterweight, but it is recent (November 2025 increase) and could be deprioritized at any time.

**12-month catalysts:**

- Q1 2026 earnings: first quarter reflecting the 40% workforce reduction impact. Consensus EPS $0.65.
- Cash App Borrow credit performance through H1 2026 — if losses stabilize, the market will re-rate the lending franchise.
- Bitcoin price trajectory — BTC above $100K supports sentiment; below $60K creates mark-to-market anxiety.

- Square GPV trajectory in a potentially weakening consumer spending environment.

## PART III -- HISTORICAL REVIEW — THE STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 — Square-Only Era (2009-2017).** Jack Dorsey co-founded Square in 2009 with Jim McKelvey with a single product: a white dongle that plugged into the headphone jack of a smartphone and turned it into a card reader. The insight was brilliant — millions of small businesses could not accept credit cards because traditional merchant acquirers required expensive terminals, long-term contracts, and credit checks. Square offered flat-rate pricing (2.75% per swipe), no contract, and instant approval. Revenue grew from near-zero to $2.2B by 2017. The company went public in November 2015 at $9/share (split-adjusted). Operating losses persisted throughout this period — the business was investment-mode. What we learned: Dorsey's product instinct for simplification is genuine. The original Square insight spawned an entire category.

**Phase 2 — Cash App Explosion (2018-2020).** Cash App, initially launched in 2013 as "Square Cash" for P2P payments, hit escape velocity around 2018 when it added the Cash Card (a free debit card linked to the Cash App balance) and bitcoin trading. Revenue roughly tripled from $3.3B (2018) to $9.5B (2020), with the bitcoin component exploding during the 2020 pandemic — BTC revenue went from near-zero to billions. But more importantly, Cash App's non-bitcoin gross profit began compounding at 40%+ annually. Cash App went from Venmo competitor to quasi-neobank: direct deposit, stock investing, tax filing, and lending products emerged in rapid succession. By end-2020, Cash App had become Block's more valuable business, a status it has retained and extended.

**Phase 3 — The Afterpay Megadeal and Post-COVID Hangover (2021-2022).** In August 2021, Block announced the $29B all-stock acquisition of Afterpay, the Australian BNPL pioneer. The deal closed January 31, 2022. This was the defining capital allocation decision of Dorsey's tenure — and the verdict is mixed at best. Afterpay brought $17B in goodwill and intangible assets onto the balance sheet (goodwill jumped from $519M to $12.0B), but the BNPL market cooled dramatically as interest rates rose. Consumer receivable losses from BNPL have been a persistent drag. The stock peaked at $289 in August 2021 and fell 75% by end-2022. Meanwhile, Dorsey renamed the company "Block, Inc." in December 2021 and announced a portfolio of bitcoin-adjacent ventures (Proto mining, Bitkey, Spiral) that alarmed investors focused on fintech execution.

**Phase 4 — Profitability Pivot and Buyback Era (2023-2025).** Starting in 2023, management pivoted sharply toward profitability. Product development spending flattened ($2.91B in both FY24 and FY25 despite 17% GP growth). G&A fell 7%. Operating income swung from -$625M (2022) to +$633M (2023) to +$892M (2024) to +$1.71B (2025). FCF swung from -$50M (2023) to +$1.55B (2024) to +$2.42B (2025). In May 2024, management authorized the first-ever share repurchase program ($4B), subsequently increased to $9B in November 2025. $3.7B has been deployed through December 2025. This is the most aggressive buyback pivot in fintech — Block went from zero capital return to $2.3B/year in under 24 months.

**Phase 5 — The 40% Restructuring (February 2026).** In February 2026, Block announced a workforce reduction of more than 40% — from 10,205 to approximately 6,000 employees. Management's stated rationale: "better align our organizational structure with our operating model and strategic priorities." The 10-K language suggests this is not a one-time event but a permanent change: "We plan to continue to operate at this smaller size and are continuing to look at ways to improve our efficiency through a combination of AI automation, prioritization of our scope, performance management, and centralization of teams and functions to reduce duplication." If successful, this transforms Block from a "growth at any cost" fintech into a disciplined, high-margin platform company. If it fails, product innovation stalls and the competitive moat erodes.

- **Remaining authorization:** $5.34B as of December 31, 2025

Coverage is tight at 1.04x in 2025, but this is by design — management is deploying essentially 100% of FCF into repurchases. The question is whether this is sustainable. We believe it is for 2026-2027 because: (1) the 40% workforce reduction will free up $500M-$1B in annual cash operating expenses starting H2 2026, (2) Cash App Borrow and Square Loans are still in high-growth mode and will contribute incremental gross profit, and (3) CapEx is minimal ($155M/year) and unlikely to increase materially.

### Buyback Effectiveness Score: 6/10

The buyback is well-timed (below intrinsic value at $67 avg price) but partially offset by SBC dilution. Net share reduction in 2025 was approximately 13M shares (34M repurchased minus ~21M SBC-related issuance), or about 2.2% of shares outstanding. This is acceptable but not exceptional. For comparison, PayPal retired ~8.4% of its float in 2025.

### Dividend Probability Assessment

**Probability of a dividend initiation within 24 months: 10%.** Management's language is unambiguous: no dividend in the foreseeable future. The buyback is the preferred capital return mechanism. A dividend would signal growth deceleration — not a narrative Block wants to deliver while repositioning as a lean, AI-driven platform company. The earliest plausible dividend scenario would be 2028-2029 if Cash App's growth decelerates to single digits and FCF exceeds buyback deployment capacity, but this is speculative.

## PART VI -- GROWTH ENGINES AND RISK FACTORS

### Growth Engine 1: Cash App Borrow and Financial Solutions

Cash App Borrow is the single most important driver of incremental gross profit. Revenue from Borrow increased $687M (+143%) in FY2025. The product provides short-term loans (average repayment under 4 weeks) with fees, using proprietary underwriting models built on Cash App transaction data. In Q2 2025, Block began originating Borrow loans through its wholly-owned bank subsidiary (Square Financial Services) in addition to its third-party bank partner, which should improve unit economics by eliminating partner fees.

**The risk:** Loan losses surged 154% to $821M in FY2025, driven by volume growth. Management claims loss rates are stable, but the absolute dollar amounts are alarming — loan losses now represent 7.9% of total gross profit, up from 3.6% in FY2024. If a recession hits and loss rates increase even 200bps, the impact on cash flow would be material. We estimate every 1% increase in loss rate on the Borrow portfolio costs approximately $80-100M annually.

### Growth Engine 2: Square Commerce Ecosystem

Square GPV grew 10% to $250B in FY2025, with Food and Beverage leading. The mid-market push (sellers >$500K annualized GPV) is gaining traction: field sales expanded to ~150 representatives. Square Loans facilitated $32.8B cumulative since 2014. The new tiered pricing and packaging framework (three bundles combining software + payments) is designed to increase software attach rates.

**The risk:** Square is structurally limited by the secular shift from cash to card (already >85% penetrated in the US for in-store) and intense competition from Toast, Clover (Fiserv), Stripe, Shopify, and legacy acquirers. Square's GPV per seller is declining as it adds more micro-merchants. The mid-market push requires field sales investment that conflicts with the 40% headcount cut.

### Growth Engine 3: Bitcoin Ecosystem

Bitcoin ecosystem revenue of $8.5B (35% of total) generates only $419M in gross profit (4% of total). Proto mining systems, Bitkey self-custody wallets, and Spiral open-source development are all investments in bitcoin infrastructure with uncertain commercial potential. Block held approximately $759M in bitcoin at cost basis, which generated a $55.9M remeasurement loss in 2025.

**The risk:** Dorsey has publicly stated that Block's purpose is to increase bitcoin's adoption. This ideological commitment means capital could be directed toward bitcoin ventures even when fintech investments would generate higher risk-adjusted returns. The $60.3M TIDAL impairment in Q4 2024 illustrates how non-core passion projects can destroy value. Bitcoin price volatility creates P&L noise that complicates investor communication.

### Growth Engine 4: Afterpay/BNPL Integration

BNPL is now deeply integrated across Cash App and Square. Consumer receivable losses of $314M (up 15% YoY) suggest the credit book is growing but loss rates are stable. Afterpay Post-Purchase (converting completed transactions into installments) contributed $88M in incremental commerce enablement revenue. The Afterpay advertising and affiliate business generates "hundreds of millions of leads" annually with a nascent ads business.

**The risk:** The $29B acquisition price (in 2021 stock) looks permanently impaired. Goodwill of $11.8B has not been written down, but any deterioration in BNPL economics could trigger a multi-billion dollar impairment charge. Regulatory pressure on BNPL products is increasing globally.

## PART VII -- MANAGEMENT AND GOVERNANCE

### Jack Dorsey: Visionary, Risk Factor, or Both?

Dorsey is one of the most unusual CEO profiles in public-company America. He co-founded Square, co-founded Twitter (now X), and currently runs Block while actively promoting bitcoin maximalism on social media. His dual-class share control means shareholders cannot remove him.

**Bear case on Dorsey:** He has explicitly prioritized bitcoin's success over short-term shareholder returns. The company was renamed twice (Block in 2021, ticker XYZ in 2025) for ideological reasons. Non-core projects (TIDAL: music streaming acquired from Jay-Z, Proto: bitcoin mining hardware, Bitkey: self-custody wallet) divert engineering and capital resources. The 40% workforce cut could be interpreted as Dorsey replacing human capital with AI and bitcoin infrastructure rather than optimizing the existing business.

**Bull case on Dorsey:** He created two of the most important consumer technology platforms of the 2010s. Cash App's growth from P2P app to 59M-user neobank happened under his watch. The buyback program, initiated in 2024, shows a new willingness to return capital. The workforce reduction is bold but potentially transformative — if Block can operate with 6,000 people generating $10.4B in gross profit, operating margins will rival pure-play SaaS companies.

### Key Personnel Risks

The 2025 10-K does not identify a clear successor to Dorsey. The departure of Amrita Ahuja (CFO through 2024) and the revolving door of operational executives create continuity risk. Block's distributed work model (no headquarters) makes cultural cohesion harder during a massive restructuring.

Block's moat is the **two-sided network**: Square sellers + Cash App consumers share common infrastructure. No competitor replicates this dual-sided ecosystem. ## PART XII -- CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION

**Block, Inc. is the highest-upside, highest-variance position in the fintech universe.** At $61.35, the market is pricing in a meaningful probability that the 40% workforce cut fails, that Cash App's growth decelerates, and that Dorsey's bitcoin fixation destroys shareholder value. We believe the base case is materially better than the market expects.

The Cash App franchise alone — 59 million monthly transacting actives, $316B in annual inflows, $6.3B in gross profit growing 21% — is worth $50B+ to an acquirer or under sum-of-parts analysis. You are paying $37B for the entire company. The margin of safety exists not in dividend protection (there is no dividend) but in the gap between the market's implied value and the franchise value of the underlying business.

The risks are real and non-trivial: SBC dilution, Dorsey's ideological CEO style, the unprecedented scale of the restructuring, and credit risk in the rapidly growing lending book. These are the reasons we stop at "ACCUMULATE" rather than "STRONG BUY" and cap conviction at "medium."

**Position sizing recommendation:** 2-4% of portfolio. Do not oversize — this is a barbell position (high upside, material downside) that requires tolerance for volatility and a 2-3 year holding period.

**Key decision point:** If Cash App Borrow loss rates remain stable through H1 2026 AND the workforce reduction is executed without material product degradation, we would upgrade to STRONG BUY with a $90-100 target. If either condition fails, we would downgrade to HOLD with a $50-55 floor.
