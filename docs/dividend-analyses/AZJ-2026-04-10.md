## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD / ACCUMULATE only below AUD 3.50. Dividend Safety: 5/10. Confidence: Medium.**

Aurizon Holdings is Australia's largest rail-based freight company, transporting approximately 250 million tonnes of commodities annually across a four-segment business: a regulated Network monopoly around the 2,670 km Central Queensland Coal Network (CQCN), a Coal haulage above-rail business working the same Queensland corridor plus NSW Hunter Valley, a Bulk commodities segment (iron ore, grain, minerals, containerised freight), and Containerised Freight acquired through the 2022 One Rail Australia deal. The stock trades at AUD 4.11 after a +34.9% twelve-month return, delivering a 3.83% dividend yield on a ~90% payout ratio against sharply compressed FY2025 earnings.

The central question for a dividend investor is whether Aurizon is a durable infrastructure compounder with franked dividend support, or whether the structural decline of coal and the regulatory reset embedded in the UT6 Network Undertaking (effective from 1 July 2025) have permanently impaired the earnings base underpinning the distribution. The evidence points to something between these two poles and closer to the latter: a business whose Network cash engine faces a 15-20% revenue reset under UT6, whose Coal haulage volumes are at cyclical rather than structural peaks, whose Bulk diversification is real but immature, and whose balance sheet (AUD 5.5B net debt, net debt/EBITDA ~3.6x) leaves limited cushion if coal volumes decline faster than the base case.

Three facts anchor the analysis. First, FY2025 financial results revealed the margin compression the market had been debating. Revenue grew +2.8% to AUD 3,952M -- acceptable given commodity price normalization -- but underlying EBIT fell and statutory NPAT declined -25.4% to AUD 303M. EPS of AUD 0.17 is the second-lowest pre-COVID print of the last decade. Interest expense tripled over five years to AUD 353M, reflecting the debt-funded One Rail Australia acquisition in 2022 and higher floating-rate costs in 2023-2025. Dividend per share fell -7.7% in FY2025 to AUD 0.157, but the underlying signal from the board is a commitment to a 60-70% payout band on underlying NPAT -- so DPS tracks earnings with a one-year lag.

Second, the UT6 Queensland Competition Authority Network Undertaking (the regulatory tariff framework governing the CQCN for 2025-2029) represents the largest identified headwind. The QCA's draft determination in late 2024 proposed WACC reductions and lower regulatory asset base revaluations that Aurizon disputed in arbitration. The final UT6, approved in mid-2025, reduced network-regulated revenue relative to UT5 but less severely than the draft suggested. Network EBITDA is nevertheless expected to run at AUD 550-620M in FY2026 vs the UT5 peak of ~AUD 730M -- a -15% to -20% reset in what was the single most dependable cash engine in the portfolio. [unverified: precise UT6 headline revenue cap for FY2026 not consistently disclosed in English press coverage; management guidance refers to "regulated Network earnings growth rate approximately flat to +2% under UT6, resetting from a lower base".]

Third, the strategic pivot to Bulk and Containerised Freight (the One Rail thesis) is proceeding but is earnings-dilutive in the near term. Bulk contract wins in FY2025-26 include Yilgarn Iron (contract through December 2029), Alcoa technology trial (October 2025), BHP Copper South Australia (June 2025), CBH grain (2021 extension), Tronox mineral sands (2022), and the Team Global Express AUD 1.8B containerised freight contract (2023). These diversify revenue but at EBITDA margins of ~20-25%, well below the ~55% margin of the regulated Network. The bulk mix shift is a quality trade-down in near-term dividend coverage even if it reduces long-term coal beta.

**Should a portfolio hold AZJ as a core dividend position?** Not as a core. As a high-yield income sleeve with optionality on franking credits for Australian-resident investors, yes -- at the right price. For a non-Australian dividend investor (including the portfolio's China-resident holder, who loses franking credit benefits entirely and faces a 15% Australian non-resident WHT on unfranked portions), the risk/reward is more balanced toward HOLD. The 6,000-share position represents approximately AUD 24,660 at reference price -- a modest tracking position.

**Top 3 reasons to OWN:**

1. **Regulated Network moat and franking.** The CQCN is an irreplaceable asset. The QCA framework, while pricing down in UT6, guarantees a regulated rate of return on an RAB of ~AUD 6B for the next 25+ years of the 99-year lease. The Network segment generates ~AUD 500-600M of EBITDA with near-zero volume sensitivity within a wide band. For Australian-resident holders, dividends are fully franked at 100% (FY2024, FY2025), providing a 1.43x gross-up multiplier (yield ~5.47% gross including franking credit).

2. **Coal is not going to zero in the next decade.** Metallurgical coal (60-70% of Aurizon's haulage tonnage) is required for steelmaking and has no economic substitute at scale before 2035-2040. Indonesia, India, and Southeast Asia are net coal importers from Australia. Even thermal coal volumes to Japan, Korea, and Taiwan are rising in the 2024-2026 Russia-export-disruption environment. The "stranded asset" narrative for Aurizon's coal exposure is real but applies to the 2035-2045 horizon, not 2026-2030.

3. **One Rail / Bulk diversification reduces structural coal beta from ~70% to ~55% of EBITDA by FY2028.** Container freight (Team Global Express contract) plus bulk minerals/grain provides genuine optionality on Australian GDP growth rather than pure coal export volumes. If management executes at 25%+ EBITDA margins on Bulk by FY2028 (current ~22%), the group earnings multiple should re-rate.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own (or to size small):**

1. **Coal transition risk is structural and non-linear.** Even if metallurgical coal volumes hold through 2035, the regulatory and political risk of Queensland imposing a carbon tariff, volume cap, or accelerated depreciation on coal infrastructure rises each year. Shareholder activist pressure on index-weight-allocated assets (CQCN) is increasing. Aurizon's own net-zero-by-2050 roadmap implicitly signals coal revenue attrition.

2. **Leverage + high payout ratio + declining earnings = narrow dividend margin of safety.** Net debt/EBITDA at 3.6x (Reuters calc) with rising interest expense and a 90% trailing payout ratio means any further EBITDA compression forces a dividend cut or new equity. The Altman Z-Score of 1.07 (distress threshold <1.8) flags this explicitly. Aurizon cut the dividend in FY2020-21 and again in FY2024-25; a third cut is within the envelope.

3. **Non-Australian holders forfeit franking -- material yield haircut.** A fully-franked AUD 0.157 dividend is worth AUD 0.224 to an Australian-resident investor (yield 5.47% gross) but only AUD 0.157 to an offshore investor, reduced further by 15% Australian WHT on the unfranked portion (FY2025 was 100% franked so WHT was zero on cash dividend but franking credit value is foregone). For a China-resident dividend investor, the effective yield is ~3.83% cash, lower than pure infrastructure alternatives (Transurban, Atlas Arteria, Yuexiu Transport) offering similar real yield without the franking dependency.

**12-month catalysts:** FY2026 results (August 2026) with first full year of UT6 tariff regime. Clarity on FY2027 UT7 consultation timeline. Additional Bulk contract wins or losses. Any emerging announcements on coal volume normalization (Queensland budget, Chinese coal tariffs). Potential M&A in containerised freight or energy transition logistics (critical minerals).

## PART IV -- DIVIDEND DEEP DIVE

Aurizon's dividend policy has been formalized since 2018 as a payout band of 60-70% of underlying NPAT, with semi-annual distribution (interim in February, final in August). In practice, the payout has often exceeded the upper end of the band in years of earnings compression because the board has signaled willingness to sustain DPS in the face of near-term volatility. The FY2025 payout ratio of ~92% on underlying NPAT (or ~90% on reported NPAT) is materially above policy and reflects board intent to cushion shareholders from the UT6 transition year.

- **Current (FY2025) yield:** 3.83% at AUD 4.11 share price
- **Grossed-up yield (Australian residents, including franking credit):** 5.47% [approx, assuming 100% franking, 30% corporate tax rate, 1.43x gross-up multiplier]
- **Yield to non-Australian residents (after 15% Australian WHT on any unfranked portion, NZ/China/HK treaty):** 3.83% cash (no WHT on 100%-franked dividends for non-Australian tax residents under ATO rules on fully-franked distributions paid to non-residents; franking credits have no economic value for non-residents but the cash dividend is exempt from Australian WHT when franked)
- **Payout ratio (EPS basis):** 92% [approx -- EPS AUD 0.17, DPS AUD 0.157]
- **Payout ratio (FCF basis):** 34% [approx -- DPS payments ~AUD 264M on FCF AUD 777M]. Apparent low FCF ratio is misleading because FCF is cyclically elevated by capex timing; through-cycle FCF payout runs closer to 50-55%.
- **Dividend streak:** 14 years of uninterrupted payments since IPO (2011-2025), with 10 years of DPS in the range AUD 0.10-0.26. Not a streak of consecutive growth: DPS has declined in FY2021, FY2022, FY2024, and FY2025 on a year-on-year basis.
- **5y DPS CAGR (FY2020-FY2025):** -7.7% annualized (AUD 0.235 → AUD 0.157)
- **10y DPS CAGR (FY2016-FY2025):** +4.4% annualized (AUD 0.102 → AUD 0.157)
- **Last full-year DPS decision:** FY2025 final dividend AUD 0.07 + interim AUD 0.085 = AUD 0.155 total, +AUD 0.002 from FY2024 interim/final totaling AUD 0.157. [approx]
- **Pattern of hikes:** Variable. Aurizon does not practice progressive (always-up) dividend policy. DPS tracks underlying NPAT with a 0-1 year lag.
- **Currency of payment:** AUD. Hong Kong residents and other offshore holders receive AUD proceeds; FX managed by broker/custodian. The ADR program (AZKOY, OTC Pink) is illiquid and not a reliable vehicle for larger positions.
- **Franking status:** 100% franked since FY2024 (was 70% franked FY2015-FY2023). Franking credits have economic value only for Australian-resident taxpayers and Australian-registered superfunds.

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The FY2025 dividend was sustainable but not comfortably so. At AUD 0.157 DPS and ~1.68B shares outstanding, total cash dividend payments were ~AUD 264M. Operating cash flow of AUD 1,467M covered dividends plus capex (AUD 687M) plus interest (AUD 353M) plus buybacks (AUD 190M) with ~AUD -27M to -40M shortfall requiring net new debt. This is the third consecutive year where Aurizon has funded its total shareholder return program from internal cash flow plus modest incremental leverage -- sustainable at current leverage ratios but deteriorating quality.

The key risk scenario for a dividend cut is:
- A severe China steel demand shock reducing metallurgical coal exports (e.g., -15% volume shock for 2+ years) combined with:
- Thermal coal price reversion to <AUD 70/tonne, reducing above-rail Coal EBIT by ~AUD 200M
- UT6 network revenue reset fully materializing in FY2026 (-AUD 120M EBITDA vs FY2025)
- Team Global Express contract disruption (low probability but material: AUD 1.8B is ~12-14% of forward revenue)

Combined stress: ~AUD 420M EBITDA hit. This would force payout ratio above 100% on underlying NPAT and likely trigger a -20% to -30% DPS cut to ~AUD 0.11-0.13 -- a return to FY2016 nominal levels.

Aurizon cut the dividend materially in FY2022 (-20%) and again marginally in FY2024 and FY2025. A third cut (the fourth since IPO if one counts the FY2017 rebasing) would not be unprecedented but would further erode the "stable dividend" investment thesis. For yield-focused investors, Aurizon is a B-grade dividend stock: yield of 3.83% is attractive but reliability is B/C rated against A-grade infrastructure peers (Transurban TCL, Atlas Arteria ALX).

**Stress test comparison (2008/2020):** Aurizon was not a listed company during 2008 GFC. In 2020 COVID dislocation, Aurizon held DPS flat at AUD 0.237 even as NPAT declined modestly (FY2020 had June fiscal year-end, so the direct hit was limited). The 2022 BHP-China coal tariff dispute and La Nina flooding caused AUD 100M+ EBITDA impact but did not force dividend suspension. The question is whether a compound stress (UT6 + coal transition + leverage) could do what COVID could not.

## PART V -- BUSINESS QUALITY & MOAT

Aurizon has three distinct moats, of materially different quality, that together define the business:

**Moat #1 -- The Network Monopoly (wide, regulated).** The CQCN represents an essentially irreplaceable asset: 2,670 km of heavy-haul rail infrastructure connecting Queensland's Bowen Basin coal deposits to deepwater ports at Abbot Point, Dalrymple Bay, Hay Point, and Gladstone. The 99-year state-granted lease (expiring 2109) has no competing parallel route. Any alternative would require decades of environmental approvals, billions in capex, and cooperation from coal producers who have no incentive to fund redundancy. The moat is "wide" in the Porter sense but regulated in economic rent: the QCA caps Network's revenue at the regulated RAB times WACC. This limits upside but also guarantees downside within the regulatory cycle. UT6 (2025-2029) reset revenue lower; UT7 (2029-2033) will be another consultation. Management has signaled they expect a more favourable political environment for UT7 given Queensland's declining public fiscal position and rising dependence on coal royalties.

**Moat #2 -- Coal haulage (medium, declining).** Above-rail coal haulage has two-and-a-half competitors: Aurizon, Pacific National, and Genesee & Wyoming Australia. In Queensland's Bowen Basin specifically, it is effectively a duopoly with Pacific National. Customers (the coal miners) award take-or-pay contracts of 5-10 year duration with volume commitments and CPI escalators; they do not switch providers annually. Switching costs are moderate (terminal infrastructure compatibility, rolling stock, crew familiarity). This moat is real but narrowing structurally as coal transition reduces the addressable market by ~2-3% per year through 2035 and then accelerates.

**Moat #3 -- Bulk commodities (narrow, emergent).** The 2022 One Rail acquisition added bulk grain, mineral sands, iron ore, and intermodal freight to Aurizon's footprint. Here, competition includes Pacific National, Qube Holdings (QUB), SCT Logistics, and regional/specialist operators. Aurizon's advantage is scale and network effects with rolling stock maintenance + crew depth + Queensland/NSW network proximity. Individual contracts (e.g., Team Global Express AUD 1.8B, Yilgarn Iron, Alcoa trial, CBH grain) are won on pricing + reliability + logistics integration. There are no structural barriers to entry beyond capital intensity. Aurizon's Bulk segment EBITDA margins of 20-25% are consistent with a reasonably competitive market; they will not expand to Network-level margins without segment consolidation (unlikely).

**Pricing power evidence:** Limited on Bulk/Coal (volume-based, CPI-escalated contracts with little scope for real price increases). Strong on Network (regulated: prices reset every 4 years upward historically, though UT6 is the exception). Aurizon does not have the kind of consumer-facing pricing power that defines branded businesses.

**Moat trajectory:** Narrowing overall. Network moat is the widest but UT6 signals regulatory willingness to compress returns during transition. Coal moat is narrowing with the commodity's own decline. Bulk moat is emergent -- net positive direction but from a small base.

**Return on invested capital trend:** ROIC declined from 8-9% in FY2020-FY2021 (UT5 peak + COVID freight tailwind) to 5.9% in FY2025 (StockAnalysis.com TTM). This is a 300+ bps compression driven primarily by (a) higher RAB / capital base post-One Rail and (b) UT6 revenue reset + interest expense rising.

**Pricing decision reference: FY2023 Container Freight contract.** Team Global Express AUD 1.8B 10-year contract signed at negotiated rates -- Aurizon took a share but management acknowledged margins are at Bulk-segment levels (20-22% EBITDA) vs Coal segment (30%+ EBITDA). The willingness to accept lower margin reflects strategic diversification priority over near-term profitability.

## PART VI -- CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**FY2025 capital allocation breakdown (AUD M):**

1. **Growth + maintenance capex:** 687 (17.4% of revenue; ~9% of asset base)
2. **Dividends:** 264 (35% of FCF)
3. **Buybacks:** 190 (24% of FCF; 2.8% of shares at ~AUD 4 average price)
4. **Interest expense:** 353 (nearly tripled in 5 years from 134 to 353)
5. **Net debt paydown:** -40 (slight increase in net debt during FY2025)

**Capital allocation grade: C+**

Justification:
- Growth capex discipline is acceptable -- maintenance capex runs at AUD 400-500M annually; growth capex on Bulk diversification and Network upgrades is AUD 200-300M. Not excessive given the asset base of AUD 10.2B PP&E.
- Dividend policy (60-70% underlying NPAT) is standard infrastructure-yield discipline but has been stretched to 90%+ in FY2024-FY2025 to cushion shareholders -- a policy flexibility that preserves optionality but signals management prioritizing shareholder return over balance sheet repair.
- Buybacks at AUD 4 average during FY2025 when the stock trades at ~12x EPS and ~5% free cash flow yield are at best neutral-to-accretive, but arguably imprudent given rising leverage. AUD 190M returned via buyback could have funded 70bps of net debt reduction or 1.0x additional dividend coverage. Buying back shares at 23x trailing P/E (peak cycle earnings) rather than retaining capital reflects management's preference for signalling over strict value maximization.
- M&A: The 2022 One Rail acquisition was priced at ~7-8x EBITDA which is at the high end of railroad comparables but justified by strategic diversification. Execution has been fair -- revenue synergies emerging but margin uplift from integration has been slower than planned. The Magnetic Rail divestment at AUD 425M was value-accretive relative to imputed standalone value.
- Debt management is a concern. Net debt rose from AUD 3.3B (FY2022 pre-One Rail) to AUD 5.5B (FY2025). Interest expense tripled. Net debt/EBITDA at 3.6x is in the upper half of regulated infrastructure comparables (TCL ~5x, ALX ~5x, but these have longer asset lives + higher margins). Management has targeted flat-to-modest deleveraging but FY2025 executed slight releveraging -- inconsistent with guidance.

### Supplementary analysis -- Franking credits and the non-Australian investor

A detailed understanding of the Australian imputation system is essential to evaluate AZJ for any non-Australian portfolio. Australia's dividend imputation system, introduced in 1987, allows corporate income tax paid at the company level to be credited against a shareholder's personal tax liability. The company pays 30% corporate tax; when it distributes after-tax profits, an Australian-resident shareholder receives a franking credit equal to the corporate tax already paid. A fully franked AUD 0.157 dividend implies an AUD 0.067 franking credit, delivering an AUD 0.224 grossed-up value (AUD 0.157 / 0.70).

For Australian-resident investors:
- A retiree in pension phase (0% personal tax rate) can claim the full AUD 0.067 franking credit as a cash refund, delivering an economic yield of 5.47% on AUD 4.11 share price.
- A retiree in accumulation phase (15% personal tax rate) can offset the franking credit partially, delivering ~5.20% yield.
- A top-marginal-tax-rate individual (47% including Medicare) still benefits because they avoid paying tax on the first AUD 0.067 of income -- effective yield ~4.60%.

For non-Australian residents (including the portfolio's China tax resident):
- The cash dividend of AUD 0.157 is received without Australian WHT on 100%-franked distributions (ATO exemption for fully-franked distributions to non-residents).
- Franking credits have no economic value to non-residents. They cannot be used as a tax credit in the investor's home jurisdiction (China does not recognize Australian franking credits).
- The economic yield is purely the cash: 3.83%.
- If Aurizon reduces franking below 100% (a risk if a material portion of income becomes non-Australian, e.g., through offshore acquisitions), the unfranked portion would be subject to 15% Australian WHT under the Australia-China DTA (Article 10). This would reduce cash yield proportionally.

**Implication for the portfolio:** For a China-resident holder, AZJ yields 3.83% cash. Compared to Yuexiu Transport Infrastructure (6.22% cash yield, 0% WHT) or a domestic Hong Kong infrastructure/REIT (>5% cash yield, often 0% WHT), Aurizon is a structurally lower-yielding alternative with comparable cyclical risk. This reinforces the HOLD / small-position-size recommendation.

### Supplementary analysis -- ESG positioning and coal transition realism

Aurizon's 2050 net-zero commitment, published in the FY2022 Annual Report and reaffirmed FY2024-FY2025, defines:

### Supplementary analysis -- UT6 Network Undertaking mechanics

The UT6 regulatory framework governs Aurizon Network's monopoly revenue from the Central Queensland Coal Network for the 2025-2029 regulatory period. Key parameters as finalized by the Queensland Competition Authority:

- **Regulatory Asset Base (RAB):** Approximately AUD 6.1B (starting RAB for UT6), rolled forward from UT5
