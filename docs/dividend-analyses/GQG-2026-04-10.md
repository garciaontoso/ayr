## PART II — Company Snapshot

GQG Partners Inc. is a boutique investment management firm specializing in actively-managed, concentrated equity portfolios. The company was co-founded in March 2016 by **Rajiv Jain** (Chairman and Chief Investment Officer) and **Tim Carver** (Chief Executive Officer) in Fort Lauderdale, Florida. Despite being US-based operationally, GQG chose to IPO on the Australian Securities Exchange in October 2021 at A$2.00 per share — raising US$893M, Australia's largest IPO of the year. The Australian listing was driven by (i) the deep Australian institutional investor appetite for asset management companies, (ii) favorable ASX listing rules for founder-controlled firms, and (iii) Carver's established Australian connections.

The firm is structured as a Delaware corporation (GQG Partners Inc.) with a US-based operating subsidiary (GQG Partners LLC), plus regulated entities in the UK, Australia, and Abu Dhabi Global Markets. Total headcount: 236 full-time staff — extraordinarily lean for a USD 150B+ AUM manager. Revenue per employee runs approximately USD 3.3M, among the highest in the global asset management industry. This lean structure is a competitive advantage: it supports the 81% operating margin.

**Sector / Industry:** Financial services — asset management (active equity boutique)

GQG operates in the global active equity management industry, competing against traditional asset managers and the relentless growth of passive/index investing. Its competitive positioning is defined by three elements: (i) Jain's investment performance and brand, (ii) the unusually lean and capital-light operating structure, and (iii) the 90% payout that creates a direct shareholder claim on the cash flows.

GQG's 81% operating margin is roughly double the industry average and reflects extreme capital efficiency: no proprietary trading, no alternatives platform, no complex multi-affiliate structure, no excess headcount. Revenue is pure management fees — no performance-fee upside or downside.

**The Magellan Cautionary Tale:**

Magellan Financial Group (ASX: MFG) provides the direct cautionary comparison and the single most important historical precedent for any GQG investor to understand. Before its 2022 crisis:
- Magellan was the darling of the ASX — founder-led (Hamish Douglass), concentrated quality-growth, high margins, generous dividend
- AUM peaked at A$115B in 2021
- Stock peaked at A$50+
- Dividend yield typical 5-8%

The crisis trajectory:
- **February 2022:** Hamish Douglass took personal medical leave
- **March-December 2022:** AUM collapsed from A$115B to A$70B as institutional clients redeemed
- **2023:** AUM further fell to ~A$55B
- **2024-2025:** AUM stabilized around A$35-40B but never recovered
- **Stock:** Fell from A$50+ peak to A$8 in 2022 — an 80%+ drawdown from peak

Magellan's collapse is a template for how quickly a founder-dependent asset manager can lose AUM when the founder becomes unavailable. The pattern: large institutional redemptions first (pension funds and insurance companies with formal manager-change triggers), then wealth management platform redemptions, then retail redemptions. The cascade can complete within 18 months.

**GQG's Differentiators vs Magellan:**

1. **Deeper team and explicit succession planning.** Jain has consistently invested in growing the analyst and PM team. At 236 employees managing USD 150B, GQG is deeper per dollar of AUM than Magellan was at its peak.

2. **Four distinct strategies, not one.** Magellan's AUM was concentrated in Global Equity (one strategy). GQG has four (International, Emerging Markets, Global, US) — each with distinct team and process documentation. This provides some succession flexibility.

3. **Globally diversified client base.** Magellan was disproportionately reliant on Australian super funds and retail platforms. GQG has US pension/institutional, sovereign wealth, global private banks, and Australian allocators — more diversified and potentially stickier.

4. **Younger founder.** Jain (~56-58) has potentially 7-12 more productive years of active management; Douglass was ~55 when he took leave.

5. **No personal crisis signaling.** Jain has continued to write commentary, attend investor meetings, and publicly represent GQG throughout the 2023-2025 governance events.

**Despite these mitigants, the Magellan precedent means the market will always apply a key-person discount to GQG. This is rational. Treat it as priced in and size positions accordingly.**

**The Adani Episode Deep Dive — Updated with November 2024 Indictment:**

The Adani saga has unfolded in three distinct phases:

*Phase 1 — January 2023 Hindenburg Report.* Hindenburg Research published a short report alleging Adani Group accounting fraud and stock manipulation. GQG held approximately USD 1.8-2.5B across multiple Adani entities. Adani stocks fell 30-60%. GQG stock fell ~25%. Rajiv Jain defended the positions publicly, arguing the allegations were overblown and that Adani's core infrastructure assets were genuinely valuable.

*Phase 2 — 2023-2024 Recovery and Position Addition.* Adani stocks recovered most of their losses. GQG actually *added* to the positions during the crisis — the Adani stake reportedly grew to ~USD 4B cumulative. This was a contrarian play that validated Jain's conviction framework but raised concentration concerns. By mid-2024, Adani stocks had largely recovered and GQG's direct financial loss was minimal.

*Phase 3 — November 2024 US Bribery Indictment.* The US Department of Justice indicted Gautam Adani and associates on bribery charges related to US-backed renewable energy projects. This was separate from the Hindenburg allegations. GQG stock fell up to 25% in sympathy. Adani Group entities faced renewed scrutiny from ratings agencies and lenders. Rajiv Jain again publicly defended the investment case, but the issue raised fresh concerns about the concentration and the due diligence that supported the position sizing.

**Updated lessons from the Adani saga:**
- Jain's willingness to hold and add to controversial positions is a feature of his process — validated over 20+ years of track record — but creates episodic blow-up risk
- GQG's AUM proved remarkably resilient to both Adani episodes: net inflows persisted through both events

## PART XI — Tax Considerations

GQG Partners is a Delaware corporation listed on the ASX. The tax treatment is unusual and depends heavily on holder's residency:

- **Australian withholding tax on dividends:** 30% default for non-resident holders, reducible under tax treaties to 10-15% range. Since GQG's underlying income is US-sourced (management fees on USD AUM), franking credits (Australian tax credits) are unlikely to attach to the dividend in most years. Confirm with GQG tax disclosure for each dividend.
- **US-source income considerations:** Some commentators argue GQG's dividends are effectively US-sourced for tax purposes, potentially invoking US WHT (15-30% depending on treaty status). Others view them as Australian-sourced because GQG Partners Inc. is the ASX-listed legal entity. Tax advice required.
- **For a China-based tax resident (the primary user of this report):** The US-China tax treaty provides 10% WHT on US-source dividends. The Australia-China treaty provides 15% on Australian-source dividends. Since GQG's tax characterization is unclear, actual WHT may be 10-15%. Double-check with tax advisor; treaty benefits may require specific documentation.
- **No Australian capital gains tax on ASX shares for non-Australian residents** unless the shares constitute "taxable Australian property" (TAP). GQG shares are unlikely to qualify as TAP since the underlying assets are primarily US-based.

**Practical implication:** For a China-based investor, expect approximately 10-15% WHT on dividends, yielding a net yield of 10-11% at current prices (versus the 11.8% gross yield).
