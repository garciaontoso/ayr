GQG's operating margin of 81% is roughly double the industry average and reflects the firm's extreme capital efficiency: no proprietary trading, no alternatives platform, no complex multi-affiliate structure. Revenue is pure management fees.

**GQG vs Magellan (closest ASX peer):**

Magellan Financial Group (MFG.AX) provides the most direct comparison and cautionary tale. Magellan was the darling of the ASX -- founder-led (Hamish Douglass), concentrated quality growth, high margins, generous dividend. Then Douglass had a personal crisis, took medical leave in February 2022, and AUM collapsed from A$115B to A$35B as institutional clients redeemed. The stock fell from A$50+ to A$8. This is exactly the scenario GQG investors must price in: key-person departure risk in a founder-led boutique.

**GQG's Differentiators vs Magellan:**
1. GQG has built a deeper team (236 employees vs Magellan's leaner structure at its peak)
2. Jain has been explicit about succession planning and has promoted multiple portfolio managers
3. GQG's client base is more globally diversified (US pension funds, sovereign wealth, Australian super funds)
4. GQG's four strategies provide more diversification than Magellan's concentrated global equity focus

**Still,** the Magellan precedent means the market will always apply a key-person discount to GQG. This is rational.

**The Adani Episode -- A Deep Dive:**

In January 2023, Hindenburg Research published a short report on Adani Group alleging accounting fraud and stock manipulation. At the time, GQG held approximately $1.8-2.5B across multiple Adani entities (Adani Enterprises, Adani Ports, Adani Green Energy, Adani Total Gas). The positions represented a significant concentration within GQG's international and EM equity strategies.

The market reaction was brutal: Adani stocks fell 30-60%, and GQG's stock price dropped approximately 25% in sympathy as investors feared (a) direct mark-to-market losses on the Adani holdings, and (b) reputational damage leading to institutional redemptions. Rajiv Jain took the unusual step of publicly defending the positions, arguing that Hindenburg's allegations were overblown and that Adani's core infrastructure assets (ports, power, airports) were genuinely valuable franchises with durable cash flows.

**The aftermath validated Jain's thesis -- partially.** Adani stocks recovered most of their losses over the following 18 months. GQG's direct financial loss was temporary. However, the episode had lasting consequences:

1. **Some institutional allocators added GQG to their "watch list."** Endowments and pension funds with ESG mandates cannot hold managers with governance controversy exposure. This may have cost GQG mandate wins that are invisible in reported AUM.

2. **The concentration question became front-and-center.** A single conglomerate representing 5-8% of a strategy's AUM raises legitimate risk management concerns. Jain argued this reflected conviction, not recklessness, but the line between the two is always debatable.

3. **The episode demonstrated Jain's temperament.** Most asset managers would have quietly reduced their Adani positions to manage reputation risk. Jain added to them. This is the behavior of a true contrarian investor -- and also the behavior that creates blow-up risk. Whether you view this as a positive or negative depends on your assessment of Jain's judgment.

4. **Net flows remained positive through the episode.** This is the most important data point: GQG continued to receive net inflows even during the Adani controversy, suggesting the institutional investor base has deep confidence in Jain's process. Money talks louder than headlines.

**Fee Structure and Economics:**

GQG charges management fees of approximately 50-65 basis points (weighted average across strategies), with no performance fees on the vast majority of AUM. This fee level is moderate for an active equity boutique (hedge funds charge 1.5-2.0%, large active managers charge 40-80bps, passive/index charges 3-10bps). The absence of performance fees means revenue is stable and predictable, but it also means GQG does not participate in the upside of exceptional years the way a hedge fund would.

The fee rate has been stable over time, which is somewhat unusual in an industry experiencing relentless fee compression. GQG's pricing power comes from performance: as long as Jain delivers top-quartile returns, clients will pay the management fee without complaint. If performance deteriorates to median or below, fee pressure will emerge rapidly.

## PART X -- TAX CONSIDERATIONS

GQG Partners is an Australian-listed company (ASX) domiciled in Delaware, USA. Tax treatment is unusual:

- **Australian withholding tax on dividends:** Australia generally withholds 30% on unfranked dividends paid to non-residents (reducible under tax treaties). GQG dividends may be partially or fully unfranked since the company earns income in the US, not Australia, meaning Australian franking credits may not apply.
- **US-source income:** GQG's operating entity is US-based. Dividends from a Delaware entity to Australian shareholders may be subject to US withholding (15-30% depending on treaty status).
- **For a China-based tax resident:** The US-China tax treaty provides 10% WHT on US-source dividends. The Australia-China treaty provides 15% WHT on Australian-source dividends. Since GQG is effectively a US income earner listed in Australia, the tax treatment may involve either or both treaty rates depending on the dividend's classification. Professional tax advice is essential.
- **No capital gains tax on ASX shares for non-Australian residents** unless the shares are TAP (taxable Australian property), which GQG shares are not since the underlying assets are primarily US-based.
