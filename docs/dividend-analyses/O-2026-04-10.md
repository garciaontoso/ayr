## TL;DR (10 lines)

1. **AFFO per share growth has decelerated to ~2% per year** ($4.19 → $4.28 in 2025) despite $6.3B of acquisitions at a headline 7.3% cash yield — the size-to-growth drag is now visible in the numbers, not just in theory.
2. **Dividend growth has also collapsed** to 2.9% in 2025 vs ~3-5% historical — the 31-year Aristocrat streak is being preserved via increments of *$0.0005 per share per quarter*, which is closer to a token than a meaningful raise.
3. **The 75.2% AFFO payout ratio is near the ceiling**; the math for ongoing raises now depends entirely on AFFO per share growth — and the ATM dilution channel (42M shares issued in 2025 alone, ~4.7% of outstanding) is the bottleneck.
4. **The stock has been rerated to ~13.2x AFFO** (14.0x 2024 AFFO at the Jan 2024 peak → ~13.2x at year-end 2025), compressing the historical 18-20x multiple — the market is accurately pricing the slower growth, not overreacting.
5. **Europe is 19% of ABR and 60% of 2025 acquisition volume** — the European platform is the only live growth vector with real scale, and it works because local cap rates are wider than the U.S. and because EUR/GBP debt provides a natural FX hedge.
6. **Spirit Realty integration is substantially done**: $24M of merger/transaction costs in 2025 vs $96M in 2024; the Spirit rental revenue comparability gap has closed.
7. **The balance sheet is conservative by any measure** — 5.4x net debt/pro forma EBITDAre, A3/A- credit, 93% fixed rate, 5.5-year weighted-average maturity, 3.9% weighted-average cost of debt — but the *cost of equity capital* (stock issued at $57.14 weighted average in 2025 for a 5.7% yield) is the real constraint.
8. **Monthly dividend is secure**; the 3-year cut probability is <3% — net debt/EBITDAre is well inside investment grade, AFFO coverage is 1.33x, and management is preserving the streak as an identity asset.
9. **Data centers remain aspirational** — 3 properties with $24.6M of ABR vs $5.31B total portfolio ABR. The Digital Realty JV and the December 2025 $800M Aria/Vdara preferred equity investment are marquee deals, not a business line.
10. **Verdict: HOLD at $56.37. ACCUMULATE below $52.** The thesis is a 5.7% starting yield plus 2-3% growth for ~8% long-term total return. That is fine if you believe rates are structurally lower and the cost of capital will normalize; it is boring if you don't.

## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD at $56.37 · Accumulate below $52 · Trim above $68 · Dividend Safety: 8/10 · Confidence: High**

Realty Income in 2026 is the clearest case study in the income-REIT universe of what happens when a successful net-lease compounder gets too big for its own playbook. For two decades the formula worked: issue stock at a premium to NAV, use the proceeds to buy $6B-$10B of freestanding retail properties at cap rates 150-250 bps above cost of capital, fund 93% of the balance sheet at fixed rates, let 1-2% same-store rent growth and a 98%+ occupancy compound over time, and — most importantly — pay a monthly dividend that rises in approximately $0.005-per-share increments so that the "133 increases since the 1994 IPO" headline never gets interrupted (O 2025 10-K ITEM 7 Recent Developments). The model delivered an AFFO per share CAGR of approximately 5.5% from 2016 through 2021, monthly dividend income that senior investors treated as a near-annuity, and a 15-20x AFFO multiple that compensated management for the discipline.

Then in 2022-2025 three things happened simultaneously. **First**, the balance sheet got big enough that incremental growth stopped mattering. Realty Income now owns 15,511 properties at ~$62.2B of gross real estate held for investment (2025 10-K Balance Sheet), has $5.31B of total portfolio annualized base rent (2025 10-K ITEM 7 General), and a total market capitalization of $82.5B including equity and debt (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Capitalization). At that scale, even $6.3B of annual acquisitions at a headline 7.3% cash yield produces only ~$460M of incremental NOI — barely more than 2% of pro forma EBITDAre. Against 907 million weighted-average diluted shares (2025 10-K FFO reconciliation) and assuming marginal equity issued at ~$57 and debt at ~4%, the AFFO per share accretion is literally 4-6 cents per share. That is not a growth business; that is an annuity with a small organic escalator. **Second**, the stock itself — issued at a weighted-average price of $57.14 per share on the 2025 ATM program (2025 10-K ITEM 7 ATM Program) — went from being a premium-to-NAV funding engine to trading below most reasonable NAV estimates, closing traps shut on the "issue equity, buy properties, accrete" loop that defined the pre-2022 era. **Third**, the Spirit Realty merger (closed January 23, 2024) and the VEREIT merger (closed November 1, 2021) pulled forward the growth curve but added 2,018 properties from Spirit and ~3,800 from VEREIT — so the 2024 and 2025 AFFO per share prints ($4.19 and $4.28) had to overcome meaningful integration drag, higher debt service (the VEREIT mortgages and Spirit notes), and equity dilution from the exchange ratios. The 2.1% AFFO per share growth in 2025 (2025 10-K AFFO Reconciliation) is the honest signal of the underlying run rate, not an integration anomaly.

**The dividend growth number is the most revealing data point in the filing.** The five dividend increases during 2025 totaled one quarter of one cent per share per month ($0.2640 → $0.2695, a $0.0055 cumulative increase; 2025 10-K ITEM 7). The 2026 first increase took it to $0.27 flat — another $0.0005 move. The dividends paid per share rose from $3.1255 in 2024 to $3.2170 in 2025, a 2.9% increase. To put this in context: Realty Income's historical dividend growth averaged ~4-5% in the 2015-2021 window and ~1-3% during 2020-2022 (COVID + post-merger digestion). The current cadence is closer to the Colgate-Palmolive ceremonial Dividend King increment than to what a growth-oriented REIT should be paying. Management is protecting the streak by dialing each raise to the minimum preservable increment.

**Variant perception:** The market is probably underpricing two things and overpricing one. *Underpriced:* (a) the European platform — at 19% of ABR and ~60% of current-year acquisition volume, with local cap rates 50-100 bps wider than U.S. and with a natural currency hedge through GBP/EUR-denominated debt (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Debt Financing Activities), Europe is the only place where spread investing still works at scale; and (b) the Fund — the $1.5B private capital vehicle that closed in late 2025 (2025 10-K ITEM 7 U.S. Private Fund Business) gives Realty Income a capital-light fee stream that does not dilute common equity. *Overpriced:* the data center narrative. Three properties, $24.6M of ABR (0.46% of portfolio), one Northern Virginia JV with Digital Realty, and one Aria/Vdara Las Vegas preferred equity investment do not constitute a data center business. Management namechecks data centers in every investor presentation but the cumulative capital deployed is immaterial to AFFO per share.

**Top 3 reasons to own at $56.37:**
1. **The monthly dividend is as safe as any in the S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats index**. AFFO payout ratio 75.2%, net debt/EBITDAre 5.4x, $2.7B of revolver availability, A3/A- credit, 93% fixed-rate debt at 3.9% weighted-average cost, debt service coverage 4.7x (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Covenants). A 3-year dividend cut probability is materially below 3%, and the 6-year cut probability is not meaningfully higher as long as same-store rent growth stays positive and occupancy stays north of 98%.
2. **The 5.7% starting yield is being paid monthly and increments regularly**, which is exactly the cash flow profile a $1.35M retirement portfolio needs. At current prices, a 1% portfolio position in Realty Income delivers ~$770 of gross annual income and about $720 of after-withholding income (assuming the 30% China-U.S. treaty rate applied to REIT distributions; see notes on fiscal residency). Monthly compounding via DRIP meaningfully improves total return vs quarterly payers.
3. **Europe is a real, scalable growth vector** — not a spin story. 19% of ABR, 60% of 2025 acquisition volume, €1.3B of senior unsecured notes issued in 2025 at 3.375% and 3.875% (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Note Issuances) funding Euro-denominated assets at materially positive spreads. The UK/Europe platform is the single most important capital allocation decision Realty Income has made since the VEREIT merger.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add:**
1. **AFFO per share growth is 2-3% and probably not accelerating**. The math is brutal: at $56 equity price, issuing shares at a 7-8% implied cost of equity (yield + expected growth) to buy properties at 7.3% cash yield leaves essentially zero spread after G&A and integration drag. The size has eaten the spread. Unless cost of capital compresses materially (a 300-400 bps 10-year rally), the growth rate stays where it is. See Part VI.
2. **The $0.0005 dividend increments are a warning sign**, not a stability signal. They suggest management is operating at the ceiling of its organic AFFO growth and cannot afford a meaningful raise without either cutting buybacks to zero (already happening — 0 shares repurchased in 2025) or taking the payout ratio above 80%. The 2026 payout trajectory points to another 2-3% dividend year, not 4-5%.
3. **The valuation has already rerated and the stock has not meaningfully responded**. At $56.37 / $4.28 AFFO = ~13.2x, O now trades below NNN (typically ~15x), ADC (~18x), and EPRT (~18x). The compression from 20x to 13x is not a mispricing; it is the market accurately pricing the slower growth and the higher-for-longer rate environment. Re-rating to 15x requires either clear evidence that growth has reaccelerated or a 150+ bps Treasury rally.

**Next 12-18 months catalysts:**
- **Cost of capital normalization**: any 50-bps Treasury rally tightens Realty Income's implied cost of equity and should mechanically widen the investment spread on new deals.
- **Fund closing** (on or before March 31, 2026 per 2025 10-K ITEM 7 U.S. Private Fund Business): $1.7B cap on cornerstone round, with recurring asset management fees flowing to the TRS. If the Fund scales to $5-10B of AUM over three years, it is 3-5% AFFO accretion without common equity dilution.
- **Data center JV progression**: Phase 1 of the Northern Virginia development with Digital Realty began leasing in 2024; Phase 2 and additional sites could shift the narrative.
- **FY2026 AFFO guidance**: watch for $4.35-$4.45 range. Above $4.45 means growth reacceleration; $4.35 and below means 2% is the new normal.
- **Rate cycle**: every 50 bps of Fed cuts compresses 10Y yields and should lift the relative attractiveness of the 5.7% yield.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Realty Income Corporation (NYSE: O) is an S&P 500 real estate investment trust headquartered in San Diego. Founded in 1969 and listed on the NYSE in 1994 at an initial share price of ~$8, the Company is the largest net-lease REIT globally and "real estate partner to the world's leading companies" (O 2025 10-K ITEM 1 The Company). As of December 31, 2025, Realty Income owned or held interests in 15,511 properties, 97.8% of which are single-tenant freestanding commercial buildings leased to 1,761 clients across 92 separate industries, with approximately 355.0 million square feet of leasable space (O 2025 10-K ITEM 7 General). The total portfolio annualized base rent was $5.31 billion with a weighted-average remaining lease term of 8.8 years and a property-level occupancy rate of 98.9% (2025 10-K ITEM 1 Property Portfolio Information).

The core business model is the triple-net lease ("NNN"): Realty Income owns land and buildings; the tenant pays rent plus real estate taxes, insurance, and maintenance; Realty Income's rental revenue stream is therefore structurally insulated from operating expense inflation and capital expenditures. Of 17,204 in-place leases, 80.6% provide for contractual rent escalations through fixed increases, CPI/RPI indexation, or percentage rent (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Rental Revenue). The remaining 19.4% are flat leases — largely VEREIT and Spirit inherited leases that will be repriced on renewal. The average rent recapture rate on re-leases in 2025 was 103.9% — meaning tenants who re-lease expiring space generally agree to 4% higher rents than the expiring rent, which is a core measure of portfolio rent roll durability (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Leasing Results).

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — 10-YEAR STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 — Late Ed Meyer / Early Sumit Roy Era (2014-2019).** The pre-merger Realty Income was a quiet compounder. CEO Ed Meyer (since 1997) had built the playbook: buy freestanding single-tenant retail at 6.5-7.5% cap rates, finance ~55% with equity and 45% with fixed-rate unsecured debt, pay a rising monthly dividend. Sumit Roy — who had joined in 2013 as head of acquisitions — was promoted to COO in 2015 and CEO in October 2018. The 2019 acquisition run-rate was $3.66B at a 6.4% initial weighted-average cash yield (2019 10-K Acquisitions During 2019), and the portfolio consisted of 5,860 properties, 98.6% occupied, with AFFO per share of $3.32. Key strategic moves during this phase were (a) entry into the U.K. in 2019 (the first European deal was the Sainsbury's sale-leaseback), and (b) the approximately $14B ATM equity raise capacity that allowed the Company to access the equity market on demand. The 2014-2019 AFFO per share CAGR was approximately 4.5-5.0%.

**Phase 2 — COVID Shock (2020).** March-June 2020 was the only genuine stress test Realty Income had seen since the 2008-2009 Great Recession. Theater tenants (Cineworld/Regal, AMC) went fully in arrears; LA Fitness and other gym operators suspended rent payments; certain restaurant concepts stopped paying. Rent collection dropped to ~82% in April 2020 (O 2020 10-K ITEM 7 Recent Developments, discussion of COVID impact). The Company pivoted by:
- Granting lease concessions to distressed tenants totaling $24.5M of deferred rent (2020 10-K ITEM 7 AFFO Reconciliation footnotes).
- Reserving $74.0M against theater receivables; eventually recording $147.2M of impairment provisions (2020 10-K ITEM 7).
- Slowing acquisitions dramatically: 2020 total acquisitions were $2.3B at 5.9% cash yield vs $3.7B at 6.4% in 2019 (2020 10-K ITEM 7 Acquisitions During 2020).
- Maintaining the dividend and extending the streak.

Despite the pandemic, AFFO per share actually grew to $3.39 in 2020 (+2.1%) — a remarkable demonstration that the net-lease model works because tenants under long-term leases generally pay their rent rather than default and lose the location. The dividend per share increased a token 1.4% (from $2.794 to $2.833; 2021 10-K ITEM 7). Rent collection recovered to above 95% by December 2020.

**Phase 3 — The VEREIT Merger (2021).** On April 29, 2021 Realty Income announced its agreement to merge with VEREIT, the #3 net-lease REIT, at an exchange ratio of 0.705 Realty Income shares for each VEREIT share — an implied $11B enterprise value transaction (2021 10-K ITEM 1 Recent Developments — Merger with VEREIT). The merger closed November 1, 2021. Immediately after closing, Realty Income spun off 92 office properties acquired from VEREIT into Orion Office REIT Inc., distributed at 1 Orion share per 10 Realty Income shares. The combined entity added ~3,800 properties overnight, pushing the count to 11,136 and the property-type mix toward industrial (~11%) and away from pure retail.

The strategic logic was coherent: (1) scale advantages in capital markets, (2) diversification benefits, (3) absorption of VEREIT's industrial/office exposure while spinning out the riskiest office assets, (4) cost synergies of ~$45-55M/year. The execution was technically clean: transaction costs of $167.4M in 2021 and $13.9M in 2022 (2022 10-K ITEM 7 Merger and Integration-Related Costs) — on a $11B deal, that's under 1.5% in friction costs, which is excellent.

The strategic cost, however, was durable: **pre-merger Realty Income was ~$40B market cap and could grow AFFO per share at 5-6% per year from a $4-5B annual acquisition run-rate**. Post-VEREIT, the base was too big for the same rate of growth to move the needle. In 2021, excluding VEREIT-assumed properties, the Company invested $6.2B at a 5.5% initial weighted-average cash yield (2021 10-K ITEM 7 Acquisitions During 2021) — a big number in absolute terms, but diluted into a $50B+ pro forma portfolio, the marginal impact was small. AFFO per share rose from $3.39 to $3.59 (+5.9%), but the go-forward growth rate had quietly stepped down.

**Phase 4 — Rate Shock (2022-2023).** The Fed's hiking cycle broke the cheap-capital model. The 10-year Treasury ran from ~1.5% in late 2021 to 4.0%+ by late 2022 and again in late 2023. Cost of equity for a 5%-yielding REIT with 3% expected growth repriced from ~8% to ~11%. The stock fell from $78 in April 2022 to the low-$50s by October 2023 — a ~35% drawdown peak-to-trough.

Management's response was disciplined but mechanical:
- **Acquisition cap rates widened** from 5.5% (2021) to 6.0% (2022) to 7.1% (2023) (respective 10-K ITEM 7 Acquisitions During sections). That widening was *not* because the Company had suddenly become a better buyer — it was because the cost of capital had increased, forcing sellers to accept higher cap rates across the market. Nominal spreads looked attractive but real spreads (cap rate minus cost of capital) held approximately constant.
- **Acquisition volume accelerated**: $6.2B in 2021, $8.2B in 2022, $9.5B in 2023 — record run rates as management tried to overwhelm the cost-of-capital headwind with volume. This was the right playbook but the math was running against them.
- **Debt issuance stayed steady**: 2023 saw notes issued at 5.05% to 6.00% (2023 10-K ITEM 7 Note Issuances) — higher coupons than the 2020-2021 2-3% issues but still investment grade and manageable.
- **Dividend raises compressed**: 2022 raise was the last one with a $0.0100 monthly step in November 2021; thereafter increments collapsed to $0.0005-$0.0060 per month (2023 and 2024 10-K Dividend Increases tables). The Aristocrat streak was preserved but at minimum viable increments.

AFFO per share: $3.92 (2022) → $4.00 (2023) = 2.0% growth. Starting to look like the new run-rate.

**Phase 5 — Spirit Realty Merger (January 2024).** On January 23, 2024 Realty Income closed the all-stock acquisition of Spirit Realty Capital Inc. Pre-merger Spirit owned 2,018 U.S. retail, industrial, and other properties across 49 states. The deal was structured as an all-stock exchange and was intended to be accretive from day one via (a) G&A synergies, (b) cost-of-capital advantages (Realty Income's A3/A- vs Spirit's BBB- rating), and (c) portfolio diversification (2023 10-K Recent Developments — Closing of Spirit Realty Capital Merger).

The integration added $47M of Spirit rental revenue in the 2024 year-over-year comparison but came with meaningful transaction costs: $96.3M of merger, transaction, and other costs in 2024 (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Merger, Transaction and Other Costs). The 2024 AFFO per share print of $4.19 beat 2023's $4.00 by 4.8%, but approximately half of that was the Spirit accretion math; the organic underlying growth was approximately 2%.

**Phase 6 — Current Positioning (2025).** The filing year is notable for what did *not* happen as much as what did. The Company invested $6.3B at a 7.3% cash yield (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Investments), raised $2.4B of equity via the ATM program at a weighted-average price of $57.14, issued $800M of USD senior unsecured notes and €1.3B of Euro notes, and closed the Spirit integration (transaction costs collapsed to $24.2M). The private capital Fund raised $1.5B in commitments, closing on or before March 31, 2026 with a $1.7B cap. A strategic relationship was formed with GIC in January 2026 for a $1.5B+ build-to-suit development JV. The Company repurchased no shares under the $2.0B repurchase authorization — zero buybacks all year.

**AFFO per share grew to $4.28 from $4.19 — exactly 2.1%** (2025 10-K AFFO Reconciliation). The dividend per share rose 2.9% (from $3.1255 to $3.2170). Net debt/EBITDAre stayed at 5.4x. Occupancy ticked up from 98.7% to 98.9%. In other words: **the 2025 report is the clearest statement management has ever made that the business is now a 2-3% grower, not a 5%+ grower**. That is not a failure — it is a phase transition into a different asset class: a high-quality, geographically diversified, conservatively financed, durable-rent annuity with a 5.7% starting yield and ~2-3% dividend growth.

**The 30-year Aristocrat streak.** Realty Income has had 133 dividend increases since its NYSE listing in October 1994 (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Recent Developments). ## PART VI · SIZE-TO-GROWTH TRADEOFF — THE CORE STRUCTURAL QUESTION

This section attempts to quantify the single most important challenge facing Realty Income: **the mathematical relationship between the Company's current market capitalization and the cost-of-capital-adjusted acquisition volume required to move AFFO per share meaningfully.** The argument is counterintuitive but central to any realistic forward view.

**The base math.** As of December 31, 2025, Realty Income had approximately 934 million shares of common stock outstanding (2025 10-K Balance Sheet Common stock outstanding) and a closing share price of $56.37, for a common equity market cap of approximately $52.6B. Total capitalization (including debt) was $82.5B per the 2025 10-K ITEM 7 Capitalization. Trailing AFFO was $3,886M and AFFO per share was $4.28 (diluted).

To grow AFFO per share by 4% (i.e., from $4.28 to $4.45), Realty Income must generate an incremental ~$155M of AFFO attributable to the current share count — before accounting for any new share issuance. If management funds acquisitions with an equal mix of debt and equity, and issues new stock at, say, $57 per share (roughly current levels), each $1 of new equity costs roughly $0.07 of cash per year (5.7% dividend yield + 1.5% share buyback-equivalent retention) and each $1 of new debt costs approximately $0.043 (the 2025 10-K weighted-average incremental cost of debt on recent issuances was 3.8-4.7%). The blended marginal cost of capital is approximately 5.6% pre-tax at a 50/50 mix.

Realty Income reports 2025 acquisitions at a 7.3% initial weighted-average cash yield. The implied nominal spread is 7.3% − 5.6% = 170 basis points — which looks attractive until you account for (a) G&A overhead of ~3.5% of rental revenue (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Results of Operations), (b) recurring capex and leasing costs (50-80 bps of asset value annually; 2025 10-K AFFO Reconciliation), (c) property-level expenses (1.6% of revenue per ITEM 7), and (d) the lag between capital deployment and stabilized cash flow. The true economic spread after all adjustments is approximately 80-100 bps of the gross asset value.

**So — to grow AFFO by $155M, Realty Income must acquire:**

$155M / 0.009 (90 bps net spread on gross asset value) = approximately **$17.2 billion of gross real estate acquisitions per year**.

That is 2.7x the 2025 actual acquisition run-rate ($6.3B) and 1.8x the 2023 peak run-rate ($9.5B). Realistically, $17B of annual acquisitions is not achievable for Realty Income — not because the pipeline doesn't exist (the net-lease transaction market globally is ~$70-100B per year), but because (a) funding $17B of annual capital deployment would require $8.5B of new equity per year at the current price (a ~15% dilution rate, which destroys the accretion math), and (b) debt capacity is already constrained at 5.4x net debt/EBITDAre.

**At the actual $6.3B run-rate, the AFFO per share growth the math implies is:**

$6.3B × 0.009 = $56.7M of incremental AFFO, or ~1.5% AFFO per share growth *before* same-store rent growth and re-leasing spreads.

Add ~1.0% from same-store contractual escalators and another 10-15 bps from re-leasing spreads, and the organic plus acquired growth rate lines up at approximately 2.5-2.8% AFFO per share growth. **Which is exactly what Realty Income is delivering.**

**This is the size-drag in one equation.** A $50B+ equity base cannot be materially moved by $5-10B of annual acquisitions at a 7% cash yield when the cost of capital is 5-6% and G&A is 3-4% of rents. The Company is not doing anything wrong — management is executing the net-lease playbook correctly. The playbook itself has a ceiling, and Realty Income has reached it.

**Historical comparison — what scale of acquisitions was needed to drive 5% AFFO growth in earlier years?**

| Year | AFFO ($B) | 5% AFFO growth would require | Actual acquisitions | Realized AFFO/sh growth |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2016 | ~1.0 | +$50M → ~$5B acq | ~$1.6B | 4-5% |
| 2019 | ~1.05 | +$52M → ~$5B acq | $3.66B | 4.5% |
| 2022 | 2.40 | +$120M → ~$12B acq | $8.19B | 9.2%* |
| 2025 | 3.89 | +$195M → ~$22B acq | $6.30B | **2.1%** |

*2022 growth boosted by the full-year inclusion of VEREIT.

The dilemma is clear: as the base has grown, the acquisition volume required to grow AFFO per share at a given rate has grown proportionally. But the capital markets and transaction market have not scaled with Realty Income's needs. The 2023 run-rate of $9.5B was the realistic capacity ceiling; pushing beyond $10-12B annually starts to force the Company into suboptimal deals (lower cap rates, lower-credit tenants, longer holding periods) that damage spread economics.

**What can break the size drag?**
1. **Cost of capital compression**: a 150-200 bps rally in the 10-year Treasury, combined with the stock recovering to $70-75, would reduce the implied cost of capital to ~4.5% and widen the net spread on new deals from 90 bps to ~170 bps. At that point, the same $6.3B run-rate produces ~2.7% acquisition-driven AFFO growth. Combined with same-store growth, you're back at ~4% AFFO per share growth and a dividend grower. This is entirely possible over a 2-3 year cycle.
2. **European expansion at scale**: European acquisitions in 2025 were approximately 60% of total ($3.8B) at cap rates 50-100 bps wider than U.S. (implied; Realty Income does not separately disclose European cap rates but the Decathlon sale-leaseback and Sainsbury's-type deals suggest 6.5-7.5% GBP/EUR cap rates vs 7.0-7.5% U.S.). Europe is already contributing meaningfully to spread; scaling it to 30-40% of ABR would add ~30-50 bps of blended portfolio spread.
3. **Fund growth**: the private capital Fund operates at fee income to Realty Income without consuming common equity. If the Fund grows from $1.5B to $10B of AUM over 3-5 years, management fees (~0.75-1.00% of committed capital) contribute $75-100M of pre-tax profit annually — approximately 2-3% of consolidated AFFO without any dilution. This is the highest-marginal-return capital allocation opportunity Realty Income currently has.
4. **Data center scale**: purely aspirational at current size. 3 properties with $24.6M of ABR is 0.46% of the portfolio. For data centers to matter, Realty Income would need to deploy $3-5B of capital over 2-3 years — which the current $349M of real estate under development (Q3 2025 10-Q Section A) suggests is not the pace.

**My base case for 2026-2028 AFFO per share growth: 2.5-3.5%.** Management's target (unstated but implied) is probably 3.5-4.5%. The gap between management's aspiration and the math-implied reality is the investor debate for 2026-2028. I am skeptical that the aspiration can be achieved without a meaningful cost-of-capital tailwind.

Regulatory risks include Brexit aftereffects, EU sustainable finance regulations, and potential cross-border tax changes.

The downside is low (any impairment is well contained) but the upside requires multi-billion-dollar deployment that has not happened.

**Risk 7 — Dividend cut.** <3% probability over 3 years. See Part V.

**Risk 8 — M&A overreach.** After VEREIT ($11B) and Spirit ($9B+), management has a track record of large transformational M&A. The next logical target would be a European competitor (LXI REIT, Supermarket Income REIT) or a U.S. peer (EPRT, ADC, STAG). A poorly-timed next M&A could compound the size drag. Mitigants: Realty Income's 91% service/non-discretionary/low-price-point tenant mix is more resilient than pure merchandise retail.

**Risk 10 — Impairments.** 2025 impairments were $471M (up from $426M in 2024), split between real estate ($434M) and credit losses ($37M). The real estate impairment is material but appears to be primarily driven by specific property dispositions and distressed tenants rather than a portfolio-wide value correction. A portfolio-wide 5% write-down on the $62B real estate at cost base would be $3.1B — a theoretical maximum — but is not signaled in any disclosure.

**Risk 11 — REIT status.** Virtually zero probability. Realty Income meets the 90% distribution test at 159% (i.e., way above the floor), the asset and income tests are met mechanically via the net-lease structure, and the organization has a multi-decade track record of compliance.

**Risks I'm not flagging:** data concentration (Dollar General and Family Dollar combined at ~6%; if both failed simultaneously, the recapture rate would likely be 60-70% of current rents, containing the damage), cybersecurity (adequate disclosure but no specific incidents), and gaming regulation (the Wynn Resorts and MGM Bellagio exposures are contained and both tenants are investment grade).

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

**The variant view I am most willing to defend: "The size drag is real, structural, and not a temporary phenomenon — but the market has correctly priced it, and the dividend + slow growth + European optionality combination is a reasonable income vehicle at $56.37. The bear case (that O is a value trap) and the bull case (that a cost-of-capital tailwind drives reacceleration) are both over-played."**

Most sell-side and REIT-focused buy-side commentary on Realty Income falls into one of two camps: (a) the "rate cut will fix it" bull thesis, which implicitly assumes that a return to 2.5-3.0% 10-year Treasury yields is the base case and that such a scenario re-inflates the AFFO multiple to 18-20x; or (b) the "permanent size drag" bear thesis, which argues that the Company has become so large that incremental capital allocation destroys value and that the dividend is at risk within 3-5 years. Both camps are wrong on details.

**The bulls are wrong because rate cuts do not reverse the structural issue.** Even in a scenario where the 10-year Treasury drops to 3.5% (a 75 bps rally), the Company's cost of equity compresses to approximately 8.0% and the blended cost of capital becomes approximately 5.2%. The implied spread on new deals widens from 80 bps to ~130 bps — still well below the 2019 level of 150-180 bps. AFFO per share growth accelerates to perhaps 3.5-4.0% from the current 2.5% — an improvement, but not a return to the 5-6% pre-merger pace. The AFFO multiple re-expands to perhaps 15-17x — again, an improvement, but not back to the 20x+ peak. The fair value in a "moderate rate rally" scenario is ~$72-78, not the $90-100 implied by the bulls.

**The bears are wrong because they underestimate the durability of 98% occupancy and 75% payout ratio.** Even if AFFO per share growth stays permanently stuck at 2-3%, the dividend is covered 1.33x, the balance sheet is A-rated, and the monthly cash flow to shareholders is reliable. The "permanent size drag" is a real constraint but it does not threaten the dividend. It threatens the *growth rate of the dividend*, which is a different problem with different implications. A 5.7% yield with 2.5% growth is not a value trap; it is a low-growth annuity with a modest inflation escalator — precisely what a senior income investor often wants as a portfolio component.

**My variant perception is that the most overlooked variable is the European platform.** At 19% of ABR and growing at 60% of marginal acquisition volume, Europe is the one area where Realty Income's scale is an advantage rather than a liability. The Company has built relationships with Sainsbury's, Tesco, Asda, B&Q, Decathlon, Carrefour and others that require a fully-integrated European platform to support — a barrier to entry that smaller U.S. peers cannot easily replicate. Cap rates in the UK and continental Europe have structurally tracked 50-150 bps wider than U.S. net-lease cap rates for decades, and the EUR/GBP debt markets offer natural currency hedging through local-currency issuance. The Company's ability to issue €650M of 3.375% notes in June 2025 (2025 10-K ITEM 7 Note Issuances) — at a coupon materially below what they could achieve in USD — is a direct result of ECB policy and Eurozone investment-grade credit spreads. Every euro-denominated bond that funds a euro-denominated property at a wider cap rate is value-accretive in a way that U.S. domestic deals increasingly are not.

**The variant view implies:** European exposure should grow to 30-35% of ABR over 2026-2028. Every 200 bps of European ABR growth adds approximately 5-8 bps of blended portfolio spread (European cap rates ~80 bps wider, split across the 200 bps of incremental exposure). Over a three-year period, this alone could add 20-25 bps of annual AFFO per share growth — meaningful but not transformative.

**Secondary variant perception: the Fund.** The $1.5B Fund (capping at $1.7B in 2026) generates asset management fees to Realty Income's TRS without consuming common equity. If the Fund scales to $5-10B of AUM by 2030, the fee income contribution is $50-100M annually — approximately 1.5-3.0% of consolidated AFFO without any dilution. This is a high-marginal-return capital allocation channel that the market is not yet pricing.

**What I am NOT saying:**
- I am not saying the data center narrative is real. It isn't, at current scale.
- I am not saying the dividend streak should be broken in favor of buybacks. The streak has cultural value that management has calibrated to preserve at the minimum cost.
- I am not saying that Realty Income will outperform the S&P 500 over the next 5 years. It probably will not.
- I am not saying Realty Income is undervalued relative to its NNN REIT peers. It is fairly valued, and the multiple accurately reflects the growth deceleration.

**The variant perception in one sentence:** *Realty Income is a well-managed, conservatively-financed, low-growth dividend annuity with a real European growth vector, and the market is pricing it approximately correctly — which is exactly what a senior dividend investor wants from a 3-5% portfolio position*.
