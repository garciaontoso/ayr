## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: SPECULATIVE BUY below $25. Safety: 4/10. Confidence: Moderate-Low.**

Robert Half is not a broken business. It is a brutally cyclical business sitting in what is either the bottom of a multi-year downturn or the early stages of a secular decline driven by AI. These two scenarios produce radically different outcomes for the dividend, and the market is currently pricing the stock as if both are equally likely -- which creates opportunity if you have a view.

Here is my view: **staffing is cyclical, not dying.** The data supports this. In every prior downturn (2001-2003, 2008-2010, 2015-2016, 2020), RHI's revenues contracted 15-35%, EPS fell 50-80%, and the stock dropped 40-60%. Each time, the recovery took 2-3 years and produced new highs in revenue, EPS, and dividends. The current downturn follows the same pattern -- contract talent revenues are down 31% from the 2022 peak, permanent placement is down 40%, and total company EPS has fallen 78% from peak. These are depression-level numbers that are consistent with late-cycle trough behavior, not a permanent industry impairment.

The critical difference this cycle is Protiviti, which has grown from a $0.5B afterthought in 2010 to a $2.0B consulting powerhouse that now accounts for 36% of total revenue. Protiviti is structurally less cyclical -- it grew revenue 1% in 2024 while staffing revenues fell 14%. It generates consistent segment income margins of 8-10%. And it is a direct beneficiary of AI compliance, technology risk, and regulatory complexity. Protiviti is the hedge that did not exist in 2009.

**The dividend, however, is genuinely at risk.** At $2.36/share on TTM EPS of $1.33, the payout ratio on earnings is 177%. On TTM FCF of $2.68/share, it is 88%. The cash balance has declined from $732M (end of 2023) to $365M (September 2025) as the company has funded the dividend and buybacks in excess of earnings. At the current burn rate (~$60M/quarter in dividends vs ~$25-35M in quarterly net income), the cash cushion buys approximately 18-24 months before a cut becomes necessary if earnings do not recover. Management has never cut the dividend in 22 years, including during 2009 when they paid $0.48 on $0.24 EPS. That precedent is meaningful but not infinitely extendable.

**Top 3 reasons to buy (specific):**
1. **Trough valuation on a proven cyclical compounder.** RHI trades at 18.9x TTM earnings, but on normalized mid-cycle EPS of ~$3.50, the forward P/E is 7.2x. In 2022, RHI earned $6.03/share. The stock traded at $85+ at peak. From $25, a return to mid-cycle economics produces a double or better within 3 years.
2. **Net cash balance sheet with zero leverage.** Zero long-term debt, $365M cash, $100M undrawn credit facility, and no pension obligations. This balance sheet has survived every downturn since the 1990s without a single penny of borrowing. Altman Z-Score of 3.4 (still above the safe threshold) despite trough earnings.
3. **Protiviti is a $2B consulting business growing within a cyclical wrapper.** Protiviti alone generates ~$78M of segment income over 9 months ended September 2025. Annualized, that is ~$100-110M of consulting income -- enough to cover approximately 40-45% of the annual dividend cost by itself.

**Top 3 reasons to sell / avoid:**
1. **AI is not a theoretical risk -- it is happening now.** Generative AI is automating bookkeeping, data entry, basic legal research, and junior code development -- all areas where RHI places contract workers. Management acknowledges "the development, proliferation and adoption of artificial intelligence" as a risk factor. If 20-30% of contract talent hours are permanently displaced, the revenue base does not fully recover.
2. **The dividend payout ratio is indefensible on current earnings.** 177% payout on earnings, 88% on FCF. The company is literally paying dividends from its cash reserves. That is not income investing -- that is capital return from liquidating the balance sheet.
3. **Revenue has declined for three consecutive years.** $7.24B (2022) to $6.39B (2023) to $5.80B (2024) to ~$5.38B (TTM 2025). Four consecutive years of decline is unusual even for a cyclical business. RHI has never had a revenue decline streak this long in its modern history.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- **Q4 2025 / FY2025 earnings** (expected February 2026) -- will confirm whether the sequential revenue deceleration in Q3 2025 (-7.5% YoY) is stabilizing or accelerating
- **Dividend declaration** (typically late January / early February 2026) -- freeze or cut is the pivotal moment. A freeze preserves the streak. A cut breaks it.
- **U.S. employment data / Fed rate cuts** -- multiple rate cuts (consensus 2-3 in 2026) should ease hiring conditions and stimulate staffing demand within 2-3 quarters
- **Protiviti growth trajectory** -- if Protiviti can reaccelerate to 5%+ growth, it partially offsets continued staffing weakness
- **Share buyback cadence** -- 1.7M shares repurchased in 9M2025 at avg ~$47. At $25, management has more firepower; watch for acceleration

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Robert Half Inc. (formerly Robert Half International Inc.) is the world's largest specialized staffing and consulting firm, founded in 1948 and headquartered in Menlo Park, California. The company operates through three reportable segments:

**1. Contract Talent Solutions (~55% of revenue):** Provides temporary/contract professionals in finance & accounting (F&A), technology, marketing & creative, legal, and administrative & customer support. This is the core cyclical staffing business -- the segment most directly exposed to hiring cycles and AI disruption. Revenue has fallen from $4.53B (2022) to $3.36B (2024), a 26% decline.

**2. Permanent Placement Talent Solutions (~9% of revenue):** Full-time placement services. Revenue recognized when candidates accept employment offers. The most volatile segment -- permanent placement revenues fell 14% in 2024 and a further 11% in Q3 2025 YoY. This segment is a high-margin, high-beta play on employer hiring confidence.

**3. Protiviti (~36% of revenue):** A wholly-owned global consulting subsidiary providing internal audit, risk management, technology consulting, and business performance improvement services. Founded in 2002 from the ashes of Arthur Andersen's consulting practice. Revenue has grown from $1.93B (2023) to $1.95B (2024) -- up 1.1% against a backdrop of staffing collapse. Protiviti is the structural anchor.

## PART III -- THE 10-YEAR ARC: FROM STAFFING KING TO TROUGH SURVIVOR

The story of RHI over the past decade is a parable about cyclicality, Protiviti's rise, and the painful math of shareholder returns during a deep downturn.

**Phase 1: Steady Growth (2015-2019).** Revenue grew from $5.09B to $6.07B, compounding at 4.5% annually. EPS climbed from $2.69 to $3.90. The dividend increased annually from $0.80 to $1.24 -- a 55% cumulative raise. Share count declined from 131M to 119M via consistent buybacks averaging $250M/year. Protiviti grew from $850M to $1.35B in revenue. The model worked: staffing provided the earnings base, Protiviti provided growth diversification, and capital return kept per-share metrics compounding.

**Phase 2: COVID Shock and V-Recovery (2020-2022).** Revenue fell 16% in 2020 to $5.11B as COVID froze hiring. EPS dropped to $2.70. But the V-shaped recovery was spectacular -- 2021 revenue surged 26% to $6.46B, and 2022 set an all-time record of $7.24B. EPS peaked at $6.03 in 2022. The dividend accelerated: $1.36 (2020) to $1.72 (2022). The stock hit an all-time high above $125 in late 2021.

**Phase 3: The Long Downturn (2023-Present).** Beginning in mid-2023, a combination of factors crushed staffing demand: economic uncertainty, corporate cost-cutting, work-from-home normalization, and the emergence of generative AI as a substitute for certain contract roles. Revenue declined 12% in 2023, another 9% in 2024, and is tracking down ~7% in 2025. EPS has collapsed from $6.03 (2022) to $1.33 (TTM). The stock has fallen from $125+ to $25 -- an 80% drawdown.

**The critical insight:** This downturn is longer but not deeper than 2009 in percentage terms. In 2009, EPS fell from $1.81 (2007) to $0.24 -- an 87% decline. Revenue fell from $4.65B to $3.04B -- a 35% decline. The current EPS decline is 78%, and the revenue decline from peak is 26%. The difference is duration -- this downturn is now in its third year, whereas 2009 was a single-year trough followed by immediate recovery. The prolonged nature is what makes this cycle uniquely dangerous for the dividend.

## PART V -- DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE

### The Streak: 22 Consecutive Years of Dividend Growth

Robert Half began paying dividends in Q2 2004 at $0.06/quarter ($0.24 annualized). Every year since, the Board has raised the dividend -- including through:

- **2008-2009 Great Financial Crisis** (EPS fell from $1.59 to $0.24; dividend raised from $0.44 to $0.48)
- **2020 COVID shutdown** (EPS fell 31%; dividend raised from $1.24 to $1.36)
- **2023-2025 staffing downturn** (EPS fell 78% from peak; dividend raised from $1.72 to $2.36)

The consistency is remarkable -- an 11.5% CAGR over 21 years with no interruption. But the sustainability is now in question.

The crossover happened in mid-2024. Since then, RHI has been paying dividends in excess of earnings. The cash balance decline tells the story: $732M (Dec 2023) to $538M (Dec 2024) to $365M (Sep 2025). At the current quarterly dividend cost of ~$60M and quarterly earnings of ~$25-35M, the net cash drain is ~$25-35M per quarter, implying a theoretical cash exhaustion (from dividends alone) in approximately 10-14 quarters if nothing changes.

### What Management Is Signaling

The October 2025 dividend declaration maintained $0.59/quarter -- no increase, no cut. This is the first time in the company's dividend history that the quarterly rate has been held flat for more than two consecutive quarters. Management is clearly signaling: "We believe this is a trough, we will defend the dividend, but we are not raising it into a declining earnings environment."

The 10-K language is telling: the Company "expects that internally generated cash will be sufficient to support the working capital needs of the Company, the Company's fixed payments, dividends, and other obligations on both a short-term and long-term basis" (2024 10-K, Liquidity and Capital Resources). They are explicitly including dividends in this confidence statement. Management has also meaningfully slowed buybacks -- $92M in 9M2025 vs $196M in 9M2024 -- to conserve cash for the dividend.

### Bear Case for the Dividend

If TTM EPS does not recover above $2.00 by mid-2027, a dividend cut becomes inevitable. The math:
- Annual dividend cost: ~$239M (101M shares x $2.36)
- Annual net income at current run rate: ~$133M
- Annual FCF at current run rate: ~$267M
- Annual deficit funded from cash: ~$133M (dividends) - $133M (NI) = effectively zero after non-cash items; but FCF barely covers dividends + minimal buybacks
- If cash falls below $200M, the Board will act. At the current trajectory, that is approximately Q3-Q4 2027.

### Bull Case for the Dividend

A return to mid-cycle EPS of $3.00-3.50 (which requires only a modest staffing recovery) immediately restores the payout ratio to 67-79%, well within manageable range. FCF coverage at mid-cycle would be 1.3-1.5x. The dividend survives and the streak hits 25 years. The stock re-rates from 9% yield to 4-5% yield, which implies a price of $47-59 -- a double or better from here.

## PART VI -- THE STAFFING CYCLE AND PROTIVITI: TWO BUSINESSES IN ONE WRAPPER

### Staffing is Cyclical -- The Historical Evidence

Robert Half's staffing business (contract + permanent placement) has experienced five distinct downturns in the past 25 years:

| Downturn | Revenue Decline (Peak-Trough) | EPS Decline | Duration | Recovery Time to New Peak |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2001-2003 (Dot-com bust) | -36% | -85% | 2 years | 4 years |
| 2008-2010 (GFC) | -35% | -87% | 2 years | 4 years |
| 2015-2016 (Mild cycle) | -3% | -31% | 1 year | 2 years |
| 2020 (COVID) | -16% | -31% | 1 year | 1 year |
| 2022-2025 (Current) | -26%+ | -78% | 3+ years | TBD |

The pattern is clear: staffing revenues are highly correlated with GDP growth, unemployment rates, and business confidence. Every prior decline has been followed by recovery. However, the current cycle is uniquely long -- three consecutive years of decline is unprecedented in RHI's modern history.

### Why This Cycle Is Different (Maybe)

Two structural factors differentiate the current downturn:

**1. AI Displacement.** Generative AI is automating tasks that previously required temporary workers: bookkeeping, data entry, basic programming, legal document review, marketing copy, and customer service. RHI's three largest contract talent categories -- finance & accounting, technology, and administrative/customer support -- are all directly in AI's crosshairs. The Q3 2025 data shows contract talent hours down 13.4% YoY while bill rates rose 3.6% -- this mix (fewer hours, higher rates) is consistent with AI displacing lower-value roles while demand persists for higher-skilled positions.

**2. Remote Work Normalization.** The shift to remote/hybrid work has expanded the candidate pool geographically, reducing the premium that local staffing agencies command. Companies can now hire contract workers from lower-cost regions without going through traditional staffing intermediaries.

However, there are counterarguments:

- **Job openings remain well above historical averages.** As management notes in the Q3 2025 10-Q: U.S. unemployment for college-educated professionals is 2.7% with "even lower rates prevailing among specialized accounting, finance and technology roles." This suggests pent-up demand, not permanent destruction.
- **AI creates new staffing needs.** AI implementation requires change management consultants, compliance specialists, data engineers, and project managers -- all areas where RHI and Protiviti operate.
- **The staffing industry has survived every prior technological disruption** -- the internet (1990s), offshoring (2000s), cloud computing (2010s), and the gig economy (2010s-2020s).

## PART VII -- MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### Competitive Position

Robert Half's moat is narrow but durable. It consists of:

1. **Brand and scale.** RHI is the world's largest specialized staffing firm. The "Robert Half" brand has 75+ years of recognition in finance and accounting staffing. This matters because hiring managers default to known brands when filling specialized contract positions.

2. **Database and matching technology.** Decades of placement data across millions of candidates create a proprietary matching engine. Management is investing in AI-augmented tools for recruiters -- the AI replaces the manual matching process, not the need for the service.

3. **Protiviti's consulting relationships.** Internal audit and risk consulting engagements create sticky, recurring relationships with corporate CFOs and CAOs. These same relationships generate cross-sell into contract staffing when clients need project-based talent.

4. **No debt, no distress risk.** In an industry where competitors carry debt and face periodic liquidity crises, RHI's fortress balance sheet is itself a competitive advantage. It allows the company to maintain recruiter headcount during downturns (investing for recovery) while leveraged competitors fire staff.

The moat is NOT wide because:
- Staffing is fundamentally a commoditized service with low switching costs
- Entry barriers are minimal -- anyone can open a staffing agency
- Clients are increasingly using vendor management systems (VMS) and managed service providers (MSPs) that pit staffing firms against each other on price

### Capital Allocation: Excellent Until 2024

| Period | Buybacks | Dividends | Total Return | % of FCF |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2015-2019 | $1,311M | $617M | $1,928M | 89% |
| 2020-2022 | $767M | $513M | $1,280M | 73% |
| 2023-2025 (est) | $623M | $666M | $1,289M | 106%+ |

The company has returned virtually all free cash flow to shareholders over the past decade. From 2015-2022, this was prudent -- FCF comfortably covered both dividends and buybacks. Since 2023, capital return has exceeded FCF, funded by drawing down the cash balance. This is unsustainable beyond 18-24 months but not irrational if management genuinely believes the cycle will turn.

Buyback effectiveness has been mixed. The company repurchased 3.5M shares in 2024 at an average price of ~$71 -- those shares are now worth $25. From 2022-2024, RHI spent ~$800M buying stock at an average price of ~$70-80 that is now trading at $25. This represents ~$550M of shareholder value destruction from poor buyback timing. To management's credit, they sharply reduced buybacks in 9M2025 ($92M) and are buying at much lower prices (~$47 average).

## PART VIII -- RISKS

### Risk #1: AI-Driven Permanent Volume Destruction (HIGH)
If generative AI permanently displaces 20-30% of contract talent hours in F&A, technology, and admin support, RHI's normalized revenue base falls from ~$6.5B to ~$5.0-5.5B, normalized EPS falls to $2.00-2.50, and the current dividend of $2.36 becomes a permanent stretch rather than a cyclical anomaly. This is the existential risk.

### Risk #2: Prolonged Trough Exhausts Cash Reserves (MODERATE-HIGH)
Cash declined from $732M to $365M over 7 quarters. Another 7 quarters at the same rate brings cash below $100M, forcing a dividend cut. The revolver is only $100M and exists primarily for letters of credit, not working capital.

### Risk #3: Litigation Overhang (MODERATE)
The Gentry class action (California temp workers denied interview compensation, class certified March 2024, trial expected November 2025) and the Dorff case (recruiter misclassification) present non-trivial liability exposure. No amounts have been accrued because the outcomes are not estimable, but a large adverse judgment could consume cash needed for dividends.

### Risk #4: Pricing Pressure from VMS/MSP Intermediation (MODERATE)
Vendor management systems increasingly commoditize temporary staffing procurement. As more corporate clients route staffing purchases through VMS platforms, RHI faces gross margin compression on contract talent solutions. The data already shows this: contract talent gross margins declined from 39.8% (2023) to 39.2% (2024) to 38.9% (Q3 2025).

### Risk #5: Protiviti Deceleration (MODERATE)
Protiviti's segment income margin has declined from 9.7% (2022-2023) to 8.2% (2024) to 5.3% (9M2025). If consulting deceleration is structural rather than cyclical, the hedge thesis weakens. The Q3 2025 data shows Protiviti gross margin dropping to 20.9% from 24.6% a year earlier -- the steepest decline in Protiviti's history.

### Risk #6: Management Credibility Gap (LOW-MODERATE)
Management has been saying "pent-up demand" and "early signs of improvement" for six consecutive quarters while revenues have continued declining. At some point, persistent optimism without revenue inflection damages credibility with investors.

## PART X -- VARIANT PERCEPTION: WHAT THE MARKET IS MISSING

### What consensus gets right:
- The staffing industry faces genuine AI disruption risk
- The dividend payout ratio is unsustainable at current earnings levels
- Revenue has declined for three consecutive years with no clear inflection

### What consensus is missing:

**1. Protiviti is being valued at zero.** At an EV of $2.17B, the market is implicitly valuing the entire company (including a $2.0B revenue, $100-160M income consulting business) at roughly 1x Protiviti's revenue. Protiviti peers (Booz Allen, CACI, Leidos, ManpowerGroup's consulting units) trade at 1.5-3x revenue. If Protiviti alone is worth $3-5B, the staffing business is being assigned a negative enterprise value. That is mathematically incoherent unless you believe Protiviti will deteriorate to zero earnings, which contradicts 20 years of growth.

**2. The labor market tightness is being ignored.** U.S. unemployment for college-educated professionals is 2.7%. Job openings remain well above the 2015-2019 average. These are not conditions consistent with a permanent staffing impairment -- they are conditions consistent with delayed hiring that will eventually be released. When hiring urgency returns, it returns violently (see 2021).

**3. The 2009 playbook exists.** RHI maintained its dividend through the Great Recession when EPS was $0.24 and the payout ratio was 200%. The stock recovered from $15 to $55 within three years. The company had less cash ($366M) and Protiviti was much smaller ($500M revenue). If RHI could survive 2009, it can survive this -- unless you believe AI is a more permanent disruptor than the worst financial crisis in 80 years.

**4. Bill rate increases matter.** While hours worked are declining 13%, bill rates are rising 3.6%. This is the opposite of what you would see in a permanently impaired industry. Rising bill rates indicate that the professionals RHI places are still in high demand -- there are just fewer engagements. When engagement volume recovers, the higher bill rates produce immediate operating leverage.

**5. The buyback at $25 is extremely accretive.** RHI has 5.6M shares still authorized for repurchase. At $25/share, that is $140M of buyback capacity that would retire 5.5% of outstanding shares. If management shifts the capital allocation mix entirely to buybacks (pausing the dividend raise), the per-share recovery is amplified significantly.
