## PART I -- Executive Summary

Robert Half Inc. is the world's largest specialized staffing and business consulting firm, operating through three segments: Contract Talent Solutions (56% of FY2025 revenue), Permanent Placement Talent Solutions (8%), and Protiviti consulting (36%). The company sits at or near the deepest earnings trough since the 2008-2009 Great Recession. Full-year 2025 revenue of $5.38 billion declined 7.2% from 2024 and 26% from the 2022 peak. Diluted EPS collapsed to $1.33 versus $6.03 at peak -- a 78% decline. The annual dividend of $2.36 per share now exceeds trailing earnings by a wide margin, producing a 177% earnings payout ratio that is the most stressed level in the company's 22-year dividend-paying history.

The critical development from Q4 2025 is the first positive sequential revenue growth on a same-day, constant-currency basis in 13 quarters. CEO Keith Waddell stated on the January 29, 2026 earnings call that weekly revenue trends were "very encouraging and better than they had been even for the first 3 weeks, which were good themselves." Management projected a return to positive year-over-year revenue growth by Q3 2026 if current trends continue. Q4 operating cash flow of $183 million was the highest quarter of 2025 and 18% above Q4 2024, and for the full year, free cash flow covered the dividend. The cash balance ended 2025 at $464 million, better than the $365 million reported at Q3 end, because strong Q4 working capital management added $100 million to cash after paying the dividend.

The verdict is HOLD with a Medium conviction. The 9.4% yield at the current ~$25 price is either a generational opportunity in a proven cyclical compounder or a value trap if AI permanently destroys the temp staffing addressable market. I weight the cyclical-recovery scenario at 60%, the slow-grind scenario at 25%, and the secular-decline scenario at 15%. The probability-weighted fair value is approximately $37, implying 48% upside. However, the extreme width of the outcome distribution -- from potential doubling to a 40-50% further decline with a dividend cut -- means this is not a position to aggressively accumulate until there is at least one quarter of confirmed year-over-year revenue growth, which management expects in Q3 2026.

- Contract Talent is further divided into Finance & Accounting (largest), Technology, Administrative & Customer Support, Marketing & Creative, and Legal. All functional specializations are reported under the unified Robert Half brand following the 2022 brand consolidation.
- Permanent Placement is the original 1948 business -- fee-based full-time professional recruitment. Revenue is recognized when candidates accept employment. This is the highest-margin but most volatile segment.
- Protiviti is a wholly owned consulting subsidiary founded in 2002 from the former Arthur Andersen internal audit practice. It provides internal audit, risk management, technology consulting, data analytics, and business performance improvement. Protiviti operates through blended teams of full-time Protiviti consultants and Robert Half contract professionals, creating a "configure-to-fit" model unique in the industry.

**Geographic exposure:** U.S. 77.6% of revenue, International 22.4%. Talent Solutions operates in the U.S. and 18 other countries. Protiviti operates in the U.S. and 13 other countries, with independently owned member firms extending reach to 28+ countries. Key international markets: Germany, U.K., Canada, Brazil, Australia.

Approximately 70% of Talent Solutions revenue comes from small-and-medium businesses (SMBs), with 30% from mid-cap enterprises. Protiviti serves a broader mix including large enterprises and public sector.

**Main competitors:** Talent Solutions competes with ManpowerGroup, Kelly Services, Kforce, Heidrick & Struggles, and hundreds of regional/local staffing firms. Protiviti competes with the Big Four accounting firms, Booz Allen Hamilton, and mid-tier consulting firms including FTI Consulting and Navigant (now Guidehouse).

**Recent corporate actions:** In FY2025, the company repurchased 1.7 million shares at a cost of $80 million (average ~$47/share), plus 0.2 million shares for tax withholding. The company entered a new $100 million credit agreement in May 2025 (replacing the 2020 facility), maturing in May 2030, with zero borrowings outstanding. A small acquisition of $11 million was completed in 2025. The company declared $2.36/share in dividends ($240 million total) and as of December 31, 2025 has 5.6 million shares remaining under its Board-authorized repurchase plan.

## PART IV -- Dividend Deep Dive

### The Streak: 22 Consecutive Years of Dividend Growth

Robert Half began paying dividends in Q2 2004 at $0.06 per quarter ($0.24 annualized). Every year since, the Board has raised the dividend -- through:

- **2008-2009 Great Financial Crisis:** EPS fell from $1.59 to $0.24; dividend raised from $0.44 to $0.48. Payout ratio hit 200%.
- **2020 COVID shutdown:** EPS fell 31%; dividend raised from $1.24 to $1.36.
- **2023-2025 staffing downturn:** EPS fell 78% from peak; dividend raised from $1.72 to $2.36.

The February 2026 declaration maintained $0.59 per quarter -- no increase, no cut. This is the first time the quarterly rate has been held flat for more than two consecutive quarters in the company's dividend history. Management is clearly defending the streak at the current level while declining to raise into a trough.

The crossover happened in mid-2024. Since then, RHI has been paying dividends that equal or exceed earnings. However, the critical update from Q4 2025 is that management explicitly addressed this on the earnings call. CEO Waddell stated: "For all of 2025 for the full year, our free cash flow covered the dividend, and we reached into the balance sheet for about $100 million to buy stock." This means the dividend was covered by FCF and the cash drawdown was attributable to buybacks, not dividend payments. This is a meaningful distinction -- the dividend remains within FCF coverage, and the balance sheet draw was a deliberate capital allocation choice to repurchase shares.

### What Management Is Signaling

Management's Q4 2025 guidance implies continued improvement: "Given those trends, if you extrapolate them...we would have enough free cash flow in 2026 to cover the dividend. And then we could look to our balance sheet to the extent we wanted to buy stock." This is the clearest statement of dividend sustainability management has made in six quarters.

The cash balance recovery from $365M (Q3 end) to $464M (Q4 end) provides additional runway. At the current quarterly dividend cost of ~$60M, the cash balance alone covers 7-8 quarters of dividends even with zero free cash flow -- a scenario that is not remotely likely.

### Bear Case for the Dividend

If revenue continues declining into 2027 and FCF drops below $200M, the dividend becomes untenable without further balance sheet draw. The math: annual dividend cost is $240M. If FCF falls to $180M, the annual deficit of $60M would exhaust the cash cushion to dangerous levels ($464M - $60M x 3 years = $284M) within three years. A dividend cut becomes likely if the cash balance falls below $200M, which I estimate occurs in late 2028 under a bearish scenario. Under the secular-decline scenario, where AI permanently impairs staffing volume by 25-30%, the dividend is cut within 18-24 months.

### Bull Case for the Dividend

A return to mid-cycle EPS of $3.00-3.50 (requiring only a modest staffing recovery to 2019 revenue levels) immediately restores the payout ratio to 67-79%, well within the historical range. FCF coverage at mid-cycle would be 1.3-1.5x. The dividend streak hits 25 years (Dividend Aristocrat status) by 2029. The stock re-rates from a 9% yield to a 3-4% yield, implying a price of $59-79 -- a potential triple from here.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

Robert Half's moat is narrow but durable. It consists of four pillars:

**1. Brand and scale (75+ years).** RHI is the world's largest specialized staffing firm and has been named one of Fortune's World's Most Admired Companies for 29 consecutive years -- the only staffing firm to receive this distinction. The brand carries particular weight in finance & accounting staffing, where "Robert Half" is synonymous with professional temporary placement. For hiring managers at SMBs who need a contract CFO, controller, or senior accountant, the brand provides instant credibility.

**2. Proprietary data and AI-augmented matching.** Decades of placement data across millions of candidates create a matching engine that improves with scale. More critically, RHI has actual performance ratings on candidate work history -- data that competitors and platforms like LinkedIn do not possess. Waddell emphasized on the Q4 2025 call that AI-generated resume fabrication is making RHI's vetting services more valuable: "Over half of job seekers today are using AI to tailor their resume to the job requirement...one of the LLMs frequently fabricated and created fictitious work history...our performance ratings for how candidates actually performed on prior assignments [are] the gold standard for vetting."

**3. Protiviti's consulting relationships.** Internal audit and risk consulting engagements create sticky, recurring relationships with corporate CFOs and CAOs. These same relationships generate cross-sell into contract staffing. In FY2025, $484M of Protiviti revenue was delivered using Robert Half contract professionals (intersegment eliminations), demonstrating the integrated model's value.

**4. Fortress balance sheet.** Zero debt, $464M cash, $100M undrawn credit facility, and no pension obligations. In a cyclical industry where competitors carry leverage, the debt-free balance sheet is a structural competitive advantage that allows the company to retain its best recruiters during downturns while leveraged competitors conduct layoffs.

**Moat trajectory:** Stable to narrowing for talent solutions, widening for Protiviti. The staffing moat faces genuine pressure from AI-driven matching platforms, vendor management systems (VMS), and remote work expanding candidate pools. However, Protiviti's moat is widening as AI compliance, technology risk consulting, and data governance create new recurring demand streams. Technology consulting is now Protiviti's largest solution area and growing.

## PART VI -- The Staffing Cycle and Protiviti: Two Businesses in One Wrapper

### Staffing is Cyclical -- The Historical Evidence

Robert Half's staffing business (contract + permanent placement) has experienced five distinct downturns in the past 25 years. Understanding this cyclical pattern is essential for valuing the company and assessing dividend sustainability.

| Downturn | Revenue Decline (Peak-Trough) | EPS Decline | Duration | Recovery Time to New Peak |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2001-2003 (Dot-com bust) | -36% | -85% | 2 years | 4 years |
| 2008-2010 (GFC) | -35% | -87% | 2 years | 4 years |
| 2015-2016 (Mild cycle) | -3% | -31% | 1 year | 2 years |
| 2020 (COVID) | -16% | -31% | 1 year | 1 year |
| 2022-2025 (Current) | -26%+ | -78% | 3+ years | TBD |

The pattern is clear: staffing revenues are highly correlated with GDP growth, unemployment rates, and business confidence. Every prior decline has been followed by recovery. However, the current cycle is uniquely long -- three consecutive years of revenue decline is unprecedented in RHI's modern history. Even the 2008-2010 downturn, which was deeper in percentage terms (87% EPS decline vs. 78% currently), lasted only two years before recovery began.

### Why This Cycle May Be Different

Two structural factors differentiate the current downturn from prior cycles:

**1. AI Displacement.** Generative AI is automating tasks that previously required temporary workers: bookkeeping, data entry, basic programming, legal document review, marketing copy, and customer service. The FY2025 10-K data is telling: contract talent hours declined 14.1% year-over-year while bill rates rose 3.5%. This pattern -- fewer hours at higher rates -- is consistent with AI displacing lower-value roles while demand persists for higher-skilled positions. The mix shift is structural, not merely cyclical.

However, the counterarguments are substantive. Waddell made several points on the Q4 2025 call that deserve serious consideration:

- **Accounting is already automated.** Even the smallest businesses use QuickBooks, NetSuite, and tax software. The incremental AI displacement opportunity for RHI's finance & accounting staffing may be smaller than headlines suggest because the low-hanging fruit was automated a decade ago.
- **GenAI accuracy is insufficient for accounting.** Accounting demands precision and auditability. Current LLMs hallucinate too frequently to be trusted with financial reporting, particularly for regulated entities where errors carry legal liability.
- **AI creates new staffing complexity.** AI-generated resumes and fabricated work histories make it harder for clients to hire on their own. Over half of job seekers use AI to tailor resumes, and RHI's internal testing found one major LLM "frequently fabricated and created fictitious work history." This makes RHI's verified performance data more valuable, not less.
- **Admin/Customer Support is resilient.** Counter to the narrative that call centers are most AI-vulnerable, Waddell noted that RHI's customer service line actually outperformed the rest of the Admin & Customer Support category in Q4 2025.

**2. Remote Work Normalization.** The shift to remote/hybrid work has expanded the candidate pool geographically, reducing the premium local staffing agencies command. Companies can now hire contract workers from lower-cost regions without traditional intermediaries. However, RHI's 10-K frames this as an advantage for a firm with global scale: "The expanded acceptance of remote work has created a significant opportunity for the Company. It brings together the Company's numerous strengths, including its global brand, global office network, global candidate database, and AI-driven technologies and data analytics at the scale needed to excel at out-of-market recruitment."

### The SMB Focus: Why RHI Lags but May Recover Faster

RHI is approximately 70% SMB-exposed, versus most public staffing peers who serve mid-to-large enterprises. This explains why RHI has underperformed the staffing industry during this downturn -- enterprise clients were the first to resume hiring as economic confidence improved, while SMBs have been in cost-containment mode for four consecutive years. ADP data cited by management shows companies with fewer than 500 employees grew headcount by only 1.1% annually from January 2022 to December 2025, versus 2.8% for larger companies.

The bullish interpretation: SMBs are now operating with skeleton crews after four years of underinvestment. When hiring confidence returns -- driven by rate cuts, easing inflation, and normalization of trade policy -- the snapback in SMB staffing demand should be violent, as it was in 2021 when a V-shaped recovery drove 26% revenue growth. The mid-cap clients (30% of talent solutions) are already performing better, suggesting the enterprise-to-SMB recovery cascade is underway.

### Protiviti: The Structural Hedge That Justifies the Premium

Protiviti has transformed from a post-Andersen start-up into a $2.0 billion consulting powerhouse that fundamentally changes the risk profile of the Robert Half dividend.

Key Protiviti growth drivers for 2026 and beyond:

- **Technology consulting is now the largest solution area**, led by platform modernization, data optimization, and AI implementation. This positions Protiviti at the intersection of the biggest corporate spending priority.
- **International Protiviti grew 10.9% in FY2025** while U.S. Protiviti declined 2.5%, driven by stronger regulatory environments in Europe and Asia.
- **Protiviti headcount grew 1.5% in 2025** to 11,200, indicating management is investing in capacity ahead of expected demand recovery.
- **The configure-to-fit model** (blending Protiviti full-time consultants with Robert Half contract professionals) creates cross-sell: $484M of intersegment revenue in FY2025 demonstrates the integrated flywheel.
- **Management's 2026 margin target:** 100-200 basis points of gross and operating margin improvement, driven by better utilization, staff mix normalization, and pricing recovery.

If Protiviti can return to 8-10% adjusted operating margins on $2.0-2.2B revenue, it would generate $160-220M of segment income -- enough to cover 67-92% of the annual dividend cost independently, without any contribution from the staffing segments.

## PART VII -- Capital Allocation

Robert Half has historically been one of the most shareholder-friendly capital allocators in the S&P 500, returning virtually 100% of free cash flow to shareholders through a combination of dividends and buybacks.

### Capital Deployment ($M)

| Period | Dividends | Buybacks | Total Return | FCF | Payout % of FCF |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2019-2022 | 657 | 1,045 | 1,702 | 2,060 | 83% |

| **7-year total** | **1,321** | **1,665** | **2,986** | **3,179** | **94%** |

From 2023-2025, total capital return exceeded FCF by approximately $165M, funded by drawing down the cash balance. This is the period of concern for dividend investors.

### Buyback Effectiveness: Mixed

| Year | Shares Repurchased (M) | Cost ($M) | Avg Price | Price Today |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2021 | 2.5 | 288 | ~$95 | $25 |
| 2022 | 3.0 | 320 | ~$84 | $25 |
| 2023 | 3.4 | 257 | ~$76 | $25 |
| 2024 | 3.8 | 272 | ~$71 | $25 |
| 2025 | 1.9 | 91 | ~$47 | $25 |

From 2021-2024, the company spent approximately $1.14B buying back shares at an average price of ~$80. Those shares are now worth ~$25 each, representing approximately $800M of destroyed economic value from poor buyback timing. To management's credit, they sharply reduced buybacks in 2025 (from $272M to $91M) as the stock declined, and the remaining 5.6M authorized shares at $25 represent $140M of highly accretive potential repurchases.

In Q4 2025, no open-market shares were repurchased at all -- only withholding-tax shares. This was the right decision: conserve cash for the dividend. If the stock remains depressed and earnings recover, the accumulated buyback capacity becomes a powerful tool for per-share accretion.

### Capital Allocation Grade: B-

The grade reflects excellent dividend stewardship (22-year streak maintained through multiple crises) offset by poor buyback timing (buying heavily at $70-95 and stopping near $25-47). The decision to prioritize the dividend over buybacks in 2025 was correct, and the slowing of buybacks as cash reserves declined was prudent. However, the aggregate shareholder return from 2021-2024 significantly overspent relative to intrinsic value.

## PART VIII -- Management Assessment

**CEO:** M. Keith Waddell. Has served as President since 2006 and CEO since 2019 (replacing Max Messmer, who led the company for 30+ years). Previously CFO from 1993-2006. Waddell is a career Robert Half executive with 30+ years at the company. He is the prototypical "operator-CEO" -- deeply familiar with unit economics, weekly revenue trends, and staffing cycle dynamics. His communication on earnings calls is detailed, data-driven, and consistently optimistic-but-honest.

**CFO:** Michael C. Buckley. Promoted to CFO in 2024, replacing Waddell's dual CEO/CFO role. Previously Senior Vice President of Finance. Provides detailed operational metrics on calls and has improved the granularity of guidance disclosure.

**Insider ownership:** As of June 30, 2025, directors and officers held approximately 3.35 million shares (3.3% of outstanding). This is a moderate level -- meaningful enough to align incentives but not so concentrated that it raises governance concerns.

**Compensation structure:** Executive compensation includes base salary, annual cash incentives tied to revenue and operating income targets, and long-term stock-based awards. The annual incentive plan uses a formulaic approach based on financial metrics. Stock compensation expense was $59M in FY2025 -- approximately 1.1% of revenue, which is reasonable for a professional services firm.

**Guidance track record:** Management provides quarterly guidance for the next quarter only, which is appropriate for a cyclical business with limited forward visibility. Over the past 8 quarters (Q1 2024 - Q4 2025), actual results have consistently come in at or slightly above the midpoint of guidance, suggesting a pattern of modest conservatism.

**Communication credibility concern:** Management has referenced "pent-up demand" and "early signs of improvement" in every earnings call since Q4 2023 -- six consecutive quarters before the Q4 2025 call finally showed tangible sequential improvement. This extended period of optimistic language without revenue inflection created a credibility gap that is only now beginning to close. Investors should monitor whether Q1 2026 results confirm the improvement or represent another false dawn.

## PART XI -- Variant Perception: What the Market Is Missing

### What consensus gets right:
- The staffing industry faces genuine AI disruption risk
- The dividend payout ratio is unsustainable at current earnings levels
- Revenue has declined for three consecutive years with no confirmed inflection

### What consensus is underweighting:

**1. Protiviti is being valued at zero.** At an enterprise value of approximately $2.1 billion, the market is implicitly valuing the entire company (including a $2.0B revenue, $112M adjusted income consulting business) at roughly 1x Protiviti's revenue. Protiviti peers (Booz Allen, CACI, Leidos, Guidehouse) trade at 1.5-3x revenue. If Protiviti alone is worth $3-5B, the staffing business is being assigned negative enterprise value. That is mathematically incoherent unless you believe Protiviti will deteriorate to zero earnings, which contradicts 20 years of uninterrupted revenue growth.

**2. The labor market tightness is being ignored.** U.S. unemployment for college-educated professionals is 2.8% (FY2025 10-K). Job openings remain well above the 2015-2019 average. These are not conditions consistent with permanent staffing impairment -- they are conditions consistent with delayed hiring that will eventually release. When hiring urgency returns, it returns violently. The 2021 experience (26% revenue growth in a single year) demonstrates the operating leverage in the model.

**3. The 2009 playbook exists and the balance sheet is stronger.** RHI maintained its dividend through the Great Recession when EPS was $0.24 and the payout ratio was 200%. The stock recovered from $15 to $55 within three years. At that time, the company had roughly $366M in cash and Protiviti was a $500M revenue business. Today, the cash balance is $464M and Protiviti generates $2.0B in revenue. The structural position for surviving a deep trough is materially stronger than in 2009.

**4. Bill rate increases indicate pricing power, not demand destruction.** While contract talent hours are declining 14%, bill rates are rising 3.5%. This is the opposite of what you would see in a permanently impaired industry experiencing commoditization. Rising bill rates indicate that the professionals RHI places remain in high demand -- there are simply fewer engagements. When volume recovers, the higher rates produce immediate operating leverage.

**5. The buyback at $25 is among the most accretive in the S&P 500.** RHI has 5.6 million shares authorized for repurchase. At $25 per share, that is $140M of buyback capacity that would retire 5.5% of outstanding shares in a single authorization cycle. If management shifts capital allocation entirely to buybacks (freezing the dividend), the per-share recovery is amplified significantly. Even if they do not execute the full authorization, the option value is meaningful.

**6. Q4 2025 cash flow was a positive inflection the market has not fully processed.** Operating cash flow of $183M in Q4 -- the highest quarter of 2025 -- is a 18% increase over Q4 2024. The cash balance recovered by $99M from Q3 end. And management stated full-year FCF covered the dividend. These facts are inconsistent with a company on the verge of a dividend cut.

## PART XII -- Risks & Stress Tests

### Risk #1: AI-Driven Permanent Volume Destruction (Probability: Medium-High; Severity: Existential)

Generative AI is automating tasks that previously required temporary workers: bookkeeping, data entry, basic programming, legal document review, marketing copy, and customer service. RHI's three largest contract talent categories -- finance & accounting, technology, and administrative/customer support -- are all directly in AI's crosshairs.

The FY2025 data is consistent with this risk: contract talent hours declined 14.1% while bill rates rose 3.5%. Fewer hours at higher rates means the remaining work is higher-skilled and harder to automate, but the volume destruction is real. The Q4 2025 transcript provides some counterbalance -- Waddell cited an Oxford Economics study concluding that "firms don't appear to be replacing workers with AI on a significant scale" and that SMB clients are not citing AI as a factor in headcount decisions. However, this is a lagging indicator; AI adoption accelerates non-linearly.

**Mitigant:** Protiviti benefits from AI (compliance, implementation, data governance). Protiviti's technology consulting is now its largest solution area. RHI's vetting advantage grows as AI-generated resume fabrication makes candidate assessment harder.

### Risk #2: Prolonged Trough Exhausts Cash Reserves (Probability: Medium; Severity: High)

Cash declined from $732M (Dec 2023) to $464M (Dec 2025) -- a $268M drawdown over two years. At the current run rate, the dividend is barely covered by FCF, with buybacks funded from the balance sheet. If revenue declines continue into 2027, FCF could fall below the dividend.

**Mitigant:** Q4 2025 cash flow was strong ($183M operating CF), and the cash balance recovered $99M from Q3 end. Management has effectively zeroed out open-market buybacks, dedicating all available FCF to the dividend. The $464M cash balance at zero debt provides substantial runway.

### Risk #3: Gentry Class Action Litigation (Probability: Low-Medium; Severity: Medium-High)

The Gentry case (California temp workers denied interview compensation, class period from 2010 to present) had its liability phase trial in November 2025 with closing arguments in January 2026. A court ruling is expected after the March 2026 case management conference. If liability is found for the plaintiff, a separate damages phase follows. No amounts have been accrued because outcomes are "not estimable." Given that the class covers 15+ years of California-based temporary employees, a large adverse judgment could consume significant cash.

**Mitigant:** The company believes it has "meritorious defenses" and is vigorously defending. A motion for class decertification is being pursued.

### Risk #4: Protiviti Margin Compression (Probability: Medium; Severity: Medium)

Protiviti's adjusted gross margin fell from 23.7% (2024) to 21.6% (2025), and adjusted operating income margin fell from 8.1% to 5.8%. The decline reflects lower utilization, unfavorable staff mix (more junior staff at lower bill rates), and competitive pricing from the Big Four. If Protiviti cannot expand margins back toward 8-10% as it scales, the segment income contribution to dividend coverage weakens.

**Mitigant:** Waddell stated on Q4 2025 that "Protiviti would be disappointed if, for 2026, they didn't add 100 to 200 basis points to their gross and operating margins for the year." There is hidden capacity in underutilized full-time staff currently reassigned to lower-rate roles.

### Risk #5: Management Credibility Gap (Probability: Low; Severity: Low-Medium)

Six consecutive quarters of "pent-up demand" commentary before tangible improvement materialized. Investors discount management optimism after repeated false starts, which delays multiple re-rating even as fundamentals improve.

## PART XIII -- Thesis & Verdict

**Thesis:** Robert Half is a proven cyclical compounder sitting at or near the deepest earnings trough in its modern history. The company's zero-debt balance sheet, $464M cash reserve, and Protiviti's structural hedge provide sufficient runway for the dividend to survive 18-24 months of continued weakness. The Q4 2025 sequential inflection -- the first in 13 quarters -- provides the earliest tangible evidence that the trough may be forming. However, the 177% earnings payout ratio, the genuine AI disruption risk to contract talent, and the three-year revenue decline streak create a risk profile that demands position-size discipline.
