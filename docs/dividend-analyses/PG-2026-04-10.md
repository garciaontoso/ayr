## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD at $145. ACCUMULATE below $137. · Dividend Safety: 8/10 · Confidence: High**

The consensus narrative on Procter & Gamble is comfortable and lazy: greatest consumer staples franchise ever built, 69 consecutive years of dividend increases, buy it and forget it. That narrative is half right and half dangerously complacent. The half it gets right is the moat — P&G's brand portfolio generates $14-17B of annual free cash flow from a cost structure that is getting leaner every year, and there is no credible scenario in which Tide, Pampers, Gillette and Crest simultaneously lose relevance. The half it gets wrong is the price you are paying for that moat and the trajectory of organic growth underneath it.

Here is the problem that almost nobody on the sell side is writing about candidly: **P&G's organic sales growth has decelerated from 7% in FY2021 to 2% in FY2025 and flatlined to 0% in Q2 FY2026** (the quarter ended December 31, 2025). Unit volume declined 1% in the most recent quarter. The company is executing a $1.5-2.0B restructuring plan that will eliminate up to 7,000 non-manufacturing jobs by mid-2027, which is the kind of thing management does when the top line stops cooperating. You are paying ~25x trailing earnings for a business that is currently printing zero organic growth and absorbing 60bps of tariff drag per quarter.

Meanwhile, the dividend — while supremely safe — is growing at a pace that barely outruns inflation. The DPS CAGR over the past five years is approximately 5%, and the most recent increase (April 2025) was a modest 5% hike to $4.0268 per share annualized (based on $1.0065 quarterly from the prior year stepping up to $1.0568 per Q2 FY2026 10-Q shareholder equity statement). At a ~2.5% yield, you need the stock to appreciate 5-7% annually just to match the S&P 500's historical total return. That is possible but not guaranteed at current multiples.

My view is opinionated and specific: P&G is a **HOLD** at $145 for existing shareholders because the dividend is rock-solid and the restructuring should deliver margin expansion into FY2027. It is an **ACCUMULATE** only below $137, which represents roughly 22x forward earnings and a ~2.7% yield — a level that gives you a genuine margin of safety against further organic growth deceleration.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**
1. **Free cash flow machine with fortress-grade consistency.** FY2025 OCF of $17.8B, FCF of $14.0B, adjusted FCF of $14.6B. The company has generated $14-18B of OCF every single year for a decade. This is the highest-quality cash-flow stream in consumer staples, period. Dividend coverage (FCF/dividends paid) was 1.42x in FY2025 — comfortable with room for continued buybacks.
2. **The restructuring is real and will show up in margins.** The June 2025 restructuring announcement ($1.5-2.0B over two years, up to 7,000 job cuts) is the most aggressive overhead restructuring P&G has undertaken since the Lafley-era portfolio pruning. H1 FY2026 already shows 60bps of tariff headwind being partially offset by 170bps of manufacturing productivity savings. When the restructuring charges roll off in FY2028, operating margins should sustainably exceed 25%.
3. **Share count reduction is disciplined and meaningful.** P&G repurchased $6.5B of stock in FY2025 and $3.5B in H1 FY2026. Shares outstanding have declined from ~2,765M diluted in FY2016 to ~2,424M diluted in Q2 FY2026 — a ~12% cumulative reduction that directly compounds EPS and DPS growth beyond what operating earnings alone deliver.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add at current price:**
1. **Organic growth has gone to zero and volume is declining.** Q2 FY2026 organic sales were flat, with volume down 1%. Greater China — once P&G's growth engine — is contracting across hair care, oral care, fabric care and skin care. North America volume is flat to down in several categories. You are paying a premium multiple for a business whose top line is entirely dependent on pricing, with no volume kicker.
2. **Tariff exposure is non-trivial and not yet fully absorbed.** H1 FY2026 shows 60bps of tariff-related gross margin drag, and management has flagged tariffs as a continuing headwind. With U.S.-China trade friction intensifying and P&G sourcing commodities globally, the tariff trajectory is unlikely to improve in the near term. This is a direct hit to the FCF that funds the dividend.
3. **Valuation offers no margin of safety.** At ~25x trailing EPS ($6.51 FY2025 GAAP, $6.83 Core), the stock trades at the top of its 10-year historical range. FMP DCF of $230.71 and consensus price target of $166.43 both imply upside, but neither accounts for a scenario where organic growth stays at zero for another four quarters. At $145, the dividend yield is ~2.5%, which is at or below the 10-year average of ~2.5-2.7%.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- **Q3 FY2026 earnings** (expected late April 2026) — first full quarter reflecting new tariff regime; organic growth trajectory is the key variable
- **Glad joint venture termination** (January 2026) — ~$500M cash proceeds, $250-300M after-tax gain expected in Q3 FY2026
- **Restructuring execution** — tracking to incur half of $1.5-2.0B by June 2026; job-cut progress will signal margin inflection timing
- **FY2027 dividend increase announcement** (expected April 2027) — 69th consecutive increase virtually certain
- **Greater China stabilization** — any signs of market recovery in SK-II or hair care would be meaningfully positive for the Beauty segment

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

The Procter & Gamble Company is the world's largest consumer packaged goods company by market capitalization (~$340-390B depending on the day), founded in Cincinnati in 1837 and incorporated in Ohio. The company sells products in approximately 180 countries through mass merchandisers, e-commerce, grocery stores, membership clubs, drug stores, specialty beauty stores and direct-to-consumer channels. Walmart alone represents approximately 16% of total sales (PG FY2025 10-K ITEM 1). The top 10 customers account for 43% of net sales.

The operating model is organized into five Sector Business Units (SBUs), which are also the five reportable segments: Beauty, Grooming, Health Care, Fabric & Home Care, and Baby, Feminine & Family Care. Enterprise Markets handle in-country execution for smaller geographies. Corporate Functions and Global Business Services provide centralized overhead.

**Key leadership:** Jon Moeller served as Chairman, President and CEO through FY2025. The FMP profile lists Shailesh G. Jejurikar as CEO — he was elevated from COO to President and CEO effective October 2025 (PG 2025 Proxy cycle), with Moeller remaining as Executive Chairman. This is a classic internal succession from a 30-year P&G lifer who ran Fabric & Home Care and served as Chief Operating Officer.

## PART III · THE 10-YEAR ARC (FY2016-FY2025)

P&G's last decade is a story of deliberate portfolio pruning followed by margin expansion, not top-line acceleration. Revenue has grown from $65.3B (FY2016) to $84.3B (FY2025) — a 2.9% CAGR that masks the fact that the company divested dozens of brands (including most of the beauty portfolio to Coty in 2016) and then rebuilt organic growth through pricing power and premiumization. The more honest measure is the four-year stretch from FY2021 to FY2025: revenue grew from $76.1B to $84.3B (2.6% CAGR), while operating income grew from $18.0B to $20.5B (3.3% CAGR).

The margin story is compelling. Gross margin expanded from 49.6% (FY2016) to 51.2% (FY2025), with a notable dip to 47.4% in FY2022 during the commodity inflation spike — a V-shaped recovery that demonstrated P&G's pricing power. Operating margin expanded from 20.6% to 24.3% over the same period, with FY2019 being an outlier (8.1%) due to a massive Gillette goodwill writedown and Shave Club competitive pressure.

The most important chart in P&G's history is the one nobody draws: **net income progression when you strip out impairments and restructuring.** Adjusted for the FY2019 Gillette writedown ($8.0B pre-tax), the FY2024 Gillette intangible impairment ($1.3B), and the FY2025 Argentina liquidation charges ($801M after tax), normalized net income has compounded at approximately 6-7% annually since FY2016. That is a respectable number for a consumer staples behemoth, and it is entirely achievable going forward if operating margins continue to expand through the current restructuring.

The concern is that margin expansion is doing all of the work because volume growth has evaporated. P&G's organic sales growth cadence has been: FY2021 +7%, FY2022 +7%, FY2023 +6%, FY2024 +4%, FY2025 +2%, H1 FY2026 +1%, Q2 FY2026 +0%. That is a textbook deceleration curve, and the question is whether it stabilizes at 2-3% (manageable) or continues toward negative territory (problematic). Greater China market contraction is the single biggest driver of the deceleration.

## PART VI · SEGMENT DEEP-DIVE

### Beauty (18% of sales, 16% of net earnings — FY2025)

**Brands:** Head & Shoulders, Pantene, Herbal Essences, Rejoice (hair care); Old Spice, Safeguard, Secret, Native (personal care); Olay, SK-II (skin care).

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales decreased 2% to $15.0B. Net earnings declined 8% to $2.7B. The culprit is unmistakable: SK-II. The super-premium Japanese skin care brand — which has higher-than-segment-average selling prices — suffered volume and distribution losses, dragging unfavorable mix of 200bps on segment revenue and 130bps on gross margin (PG FY2025 10-K ITEM 7). Greater China hair care declined high single digits organically. Skin care organic sales decreased high single digits with mid-teens declines in both North America and Asia Pacific.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Beauty net sales recovered to +6% ($8.2B), driven by a 4% volume increase and 2% pricing. Organic sales +5%. Personal Care was the star — high single-digit growth led by innovation in Greater China, North America and Europe. Hair Care grew mid-single digits. Skin Care remains the laggard with ongoing softness (Q2 FY2026 10-Q). Market share of the Beauty segment decreased 0.4 points despite the revenue recovery, indicating the growth is coming from lower-share-weight categories.

**Opinion:** SK-II is a brand in structural decline. Chinese consumers have shifted to domestic alternatives (Proya, Winona) and the post-COVID travel retail recovery has disappointed. P&G's response has been to lean into Personal Care (Native, Old Spice innovation) where it is gaining share, but SK-II's drag on mix and margins makes Beauty the weakest segment. I would not be surprised to see P&G explore strategic options for SK-II within 2-3 years.

### Grooming (8% of sales, 10% of net earnings — FY2025)

**Brands:** Gillette, Venus, Braun.

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales flat at $6.7B. Net earnings increased 7% to $1.6B with 150bps of margin improvement. Volume grew 2% but was entirely offset by FX (-2%), geographic mix (-1%), and divestitures (-1%). North America volume declined due to retail inventory reduction. Market share decreased 0.1 points.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Grooming net sales increased 4% ($3.6B) driven by 4% volume growth and 2% pricing, offset by 2% FX drag. The Gillette indefinite-lived intangible asset ($12.8B carrying value as of December 2025) passed its annual impairment test with fair value exceeding carrying value by greater than 10% — but sensitivity analysis shows that a 25bps increase in discount rate or decrease in growth rate would each reduce fair value by 5% (Q2 FY2026 10-Q Note 4). This is a watched asset.

**Opinion:** Grooming is a cash cow with limited growth potential. The blades-and-razors market has been structurally disrupted by subscription services and the cultural shift toward facial hair. P&G has responded with premiumization (GilletteLabs, Venus premium) and geographic expansion (IMEA), which is the right strategy for a mature franchise. Margin expansion is the story, not top-line growth.

### Health Care (14% of sales, 15% of net earnings — FY2025)

**Brands:** Crest, Oral-B (oral care); Vicks, Metamucil, Pepto-Bismol, Neurobion (personal health care).

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales increased 2% to $12.0B. Net earnings increased 8% to $2.4B with 120bps of margin improvement. Organic sales grew 3%, the best of any segment. Market share increased 0.2 points globally. Oral care grew on premiumization (power brushes, premium paste), while personal health care benefited from share gains in North America and market growth in Latin America. Greater China oral care declined mid-teens.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Health Care net sales increased 4% ($6.6B) with organic growth of 5%. This is P&G's fastest-growing segment and the one where premiumization is working best — consumers are trading up to power toothbrushes and premium respiratory treatments.

**Opinion:** This is the quiet gem in P&G's portfolio. Oral care has sustainable premiumization tailwinds and Oral-B's power brush ecosystem creates recurring revenue dynamics. Personal health care (Vicks, Metamucil) benefits from aging demographics. If P&G ever did a spin-off or portfolio transaction, Health Care would be the most valuable segment on a standalone basis.

### Fabric & Home Care (36% of sales, 35% of net earnings — FY2025)

**Brands:** Tide, Ariel, Downy, Gain (fabric care); Cascade, Dawn, Fairy, Febreze, Mr. Clean, Swiffer (home care).

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales flat at $29.6B. Net earnings increased 3% to $5.8B. Organic sales grew 2%. Market share increased 0.1 points. The global fabric care market share exceeds 35%; home care exceeds 30%. Innovation (concentrated formulations, Tide Evo) and premiumization are driving mix improvement even as volumes are flat.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Fabric & Home Care net sales increased 1% ($15.5B), with organic sales +1%. This is the largest segment and operates as a steady-state cash generator. Greater China fabric care continues to contract, offset by innovation-driven growth in North America and Europe.

**Opinion:** This is the segment that pays the dividend. $29.6B of revenue at a ~20% net margin generates ~$5.8B of net earnings — more than enough to fund the common dividend alone ($9.87B total, of which F&HC's share is roughly 35%). Tide's brand power in North America is among the strongest of any consumer brand globally. The risk is private-label encroachment: in a tariff-driven cost inflation environment, retailers are promoting their own brands more aggressively. P&G's response (Tide Evo, concentrated pods, sustainability positioning) is the right playbook.

### Baby, Feminine & Family Care (24% of sales, 24% of net earnings — FY2025)

**Brands:** Pampers, Luvs (baby care); Always, Always Discreet, Tampax (feminine care); Bounty, Charmin, Puffs (family care).

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales flat at $20.2B. Net earnings flat at $4.0B. Organic sales grew 1%. Baby care declined mid-single digits with volume down across all regions except Greater China (where Pampers is gaining share). Feminine care flat. Family Care grew low single digits on North America volume. Market share decreased 0.2 points.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Baby, Feminine & Family Care net sales declined 1% ($10.3B). Baby care specifically was the worst performer with a 5% net sales decline and 3% decline for the overall segment in Q2 FY2026. Organic sales were -1% for the segment in H1. This is the only segment posting organic declines.

**Opinion:** Baby care is the structural weak spot. Global birth rates are declining, and Pampers faces intensifying competition from local players in every major market outside North America. Feminine care has premiumization potential (Always Discreet for incontinence is a $2B+ addressable opportunity), but baby care drags the segment. Family Care is a North American monopoly (40%+ share for Bounty, 25%+ for Charmin) that prints cash but has no international growth lever.

## PART VII · MOAT & CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### Moat Assessment: Wide (High Confidence)

P&G possesses a textbook wide moat built on three reinforcing pillars:

**1. Brand intangibles.** P&G owns 20+ brands that each generate over $1B in annual sales. The institutional knowledge embedded in 189 years of consumer marketing — how to position a detergent in Lagos, a diaper in Shanghai, a toothbrush in Sao Paulo — is irreplicable. Retail shelf space allocation reflects decades of category captain relationships. The ~$42B of goodwill and ~$22B of intangible assets on the balance sheet represent the capitalized value of these relationships, and they understate the true economic value because many of P&G's strongest brands (Tide, Crest, Bounty) were internally developed, not acquired.

**2. Scale-driven cost advantages.** P&G spends approximately $2B annually on R&D and $7-8B on advertising. No single competitor can match both simultaneously. The combination of global manufacturing scale (operations in ~70 countries), procurement leverage, and technology transfer across categories creates a cost advantage that is self-reinforcing: higher margins fund more R&D, which produces better products, which gain share, which further improves scale.

**3. Switching costs (soft but real).** Consumers do not sign contracts to use Tide, but habitual purchasing behavior creates meaningful retention. P&G's innovation model is specifically designed to raise switching barriers through ecosystem effects: Oral-B power brush refills, Swiffer refill pads, Gillette blade cartridges. The subscription model potential in razors and oral care adds a transactional switching cost layer.

### Capital Allocation

P&G's capital allocation framework is explicit and hierarchical (PG FY2025 10-K MD&A):

1. **Fund the business** — CapEx of $3-4B annually (~4-5% of revenue), R&D ~$2B
2. **Pay the dividend** — first priority for excess cash (~$10B annually)
3. **Opportunistic M&A** — historically disciplined; last major acquisition was Merck's consumer health business in 2018
4. **Share repurchases** — residual cash returned; $5-11B annually

The framework is sound but conservative. My critique is that P&G has been insufficiently aggressive on bolt-on acquisitions in faster-growing categories (supplements, dermatological skin care, premium home care) where its distribution network would create immediate synergies. The Glad joint venture termination (~$500M proceeds) is a minor portfolio simplification but does not address the growth deficit.

## PART VIII · RISK MATRIX

### Tier 1: High-Impact, Medium-Probability

**1. Tariff escalation permanently compresses margins.**
H1 FY2026 already shows 60bps of tariff drag on gross margin. P&G sources raw materials (resins, pulp, fragrances) globally and manufactures in ~70 countries. A scenario where U.S.-China tariffs intensify, EU retaliatory tariffs expand, and commodity import costs rise simultaneously could consume 100-150bps of gross margin — roughly $800M-$1.2B of pre-tax income. P&G's pricing power provides a partial offset, but in a zero-volume-growth environment, price increases risk accelerating private-label switching. Management specifically flagged tariffs as a continuing risk in the Q2 FY2026 10-Q.

**2. Greater China market contraction becomes structural.**
China appears across every segment discussion in the 10-K as a source of volume decline: hair care (high single-digit organic decline), oral care (mid-teens decline), fabric care (contraction), skin care (low single-digit decline), feminine care (mid-single-digit decline). Only baby care and personal care grew in Greater China in FY2025. The question is whether this is cyclical (post-COVID demand normalization, real estate wealth effect) or structural (domestic brand preference, demographic decline). If structural, P&G's single-largest international market could be a revenue headwind for years.

**3. Private-label share gains in staples categories.**
In North America and Europe, private-label penetration has been increasing since the 2022-2023 inflation cycle. P&G's pricing power protects it better than most, but in family care (paper towels, toilet paper) and fabric care (laundry detergent), the quality gap between private label and branded is narrowing. Aldi, Lidl, and Costco's Kirkland brand are all gaining share in P&G's core categories.

### Tier 2: Medium-Impact, Medium-Probability

**4. Gillette intangible impairment.**
The $12.8B Gillette indefinite-lived intangible asset passed its December 2025 impairment test with fair value exceeding carrying value by greater than 10%. But sensitivity analysis shows that modest changes in assumptions (25bps on discount rate or growth rate, 50bps on royalty rate) each reduce fair value by 4-5% (Q2 FY2026 10-Q Note 4). Another FX-driven weakening or grooming market contraction could trigger a second writedown. A $1-2B impairment would be non-cash and non-core but would generate negative headlines.

**5. Restructuring execution risk.**
The $1.5-2.0B restructuring program with up to 7,000 job cuts by June 2027 is the most aggressive organizational change since the 2014-2017 portfolio pruning. Executing at this scale while simultaneously defending market share in a decelerating growth environment requires exceptional management attention. If key talent departs or market execution suffers during the transition, the margin benefits could arrive later and smaller than projected.

### Tier 3: Lower-Probability, High-Impact

**6. Category disruption (DTC/digital-native brands).**
The 2015-2020 period saw Dollar Shave Club, Harry's, Native, and others disrupt P&G's fortress categories. P&G responded by acquiring Native and creating GilletteLabs, but the structural risk of digital-native brands with zero distribution cost persists. The next wave could come in skin care (already happening), oral care, or baby care.

### DCF Crosscheck

FMP's DCF model outputs $230.71 per share, implying ~59% upside from the current $145.37. This strikes me as too aggressive — it likely assumes a perpetuity growth rate and discount rate combination that reflects P&G's historical growth, not its current deceleration. A more conservative DCF using:
- Base FCF: $14.5B (normalized FY2025-FY2026)
- Growth rate years 1-5: 4% (margin expansion + modest organic growth + buyback benefit)
- Growth rate years 6-10: 3%
- Terminal growth rate: 2.5%
- WACC: 7.5%

...yields a fair value in the $155-165 range, which aligns with the sell-side consensus price target of $166.43 and the median of $170.

### My Fair Value Estimate: $155-160

At $145, PG offers roughly 7-10% upside to fair value plus a ~2.5% dividend yield, implying a 12-month total return potential of ~10-13% in a base case. That is adequate but not compelling for new capital. Below $137 (~22x forward), the risk/reward shifts meaningfully in the buyer's favor.

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

**What the consensus thinks:** P&G is an all-weather compounder. Buy it for the 2.5% yield, collect 5-6% annual dividend growth, and ride 4-5% EPS growth from margin expansion and buybacks. Price target $166.

**Where I differ:**

1. **Organic growth will stay below 2% for longer than consensus expects.** The deceleration from 7% to 0% over five years is not a blip — it reflects real structural headwinds (Greater China decline, birth rate impact on baby care, grooming market maturation). Consensus FY2027 revenue of $89B implies ~3% growth; I think $87-88B is more realistic unless China inflects.

2. **The restructuring savings will be partially consumed by tariff costs.** Consensus models the $1.5-2.0B restructuring as pure margin accretion. But 60bps of quarterly tariff drag (annualized ~$500M+) eats into those savings. Net margin benefit may be 50-70% of the headline number.

3. **Dividend growth is converging toward 3-4%, not 5-6%.** The Board's recent raises have clearly moderated. With FCF payout already at 70% and organic growth near zero, the mathematical ceiling on sustainable DGR is approximately earnings growth minus CapEx inflation — call it 4-5% at best, with risk to the downside if FCF slips below $14B. Income investors pricing in 6%+ DGR are going to be disappointed.

4. **The Gillette impairment risk is underpriced.** An indefinite-lived intangible at $12.8B that passes its impairment test by "greater than 10%" is an asset within one bad year of another writedown. The market has already absorbed two Gillette impairments (FY2019 goodwill, FY2024 intangible); a third would not be catastrophic but would further erode the brand's halo.

**Where the consensus might be right and I might be wrong:** P&G's innovation pipeline may be stronger than the current results suggest. The Tide Evo launch, GilletteLabs expansion, and Oral-B smart brush ecosystem represent genuine category-expanding innovations. If these products gain traction in 2026-2027, organic growth could re-accelerate to 3-4%, and my below-consensus revenue estimate would be too bearish.

*KO FY2025 FCF payout is distorted by fairlife payment; normalized ~75%.

P&G's dividend is safer than KMB's (which has higher leverage and weaker organic growth), comparable to CL's, and slightly below KO's (which has lower leverage and the IRS refund optionality). However, P&G's organic growth profile is currently the weakest in this peer set, which is unusual for the industry leader. The valuation is mid-pack — cheaper than KO and CL on forward P/E but more expensive than KMB.

### Final Rating

**Dividend Safety: 8/10** — Essentially no risk of a dividend cut in the next 5 years. FCF coverage is adequate, balance sheet is conservative, and the cultural commitment to the streak is absolute.

**Dividend Growth: 6/10** — DGR is decelerating and likely to settle at 3-5% absent an organic growth re-acceleration. Not bad, but not what you want at a 2.5% starting yield.

**Total Return: 6/10** — At $145, you are paying fair value for a low-growth compounder. The math works out to ~8-10% annualized total return (2.5% yield + 3-4% DGR + 2-3% multiple expansion if organic growth recovers), which is below the S&P 500 long-run average. Compelling only below $137.

**Overall: HOLD / ACCUMULATE BELOW $137**

P&G is the consumer staples franchise. It will exist in 50 years, it will still pay a dividend, and it will still own the #1 or #2 position in every category it competes in. But at $145, the stock is priced for perfection in a business that is currently delivering imperfection. The restructuring provides a path back to mid-single-digit earnings growth, but tariffs and Greater China are real headwinds that the consensus is not adequately discounting. Wait for a better entry point, and if you own it, sleep well — the dividend is safe.
