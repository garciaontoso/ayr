## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Procter & Gamble Company is the highest-quality consumer staples franchise in the world. It has increased its dividend for 69 consecutive years, maintains an Aa3/AA- credit rating, and generates $14-18 billion of operating cash flow annually with metronomic reliability. For a dividend investor, PG represents the closest thing to a guaranteed income stream that exists in public equities. That is not a question. The question is what you are paying for that guarantee and what rate of growth you can expect on top of it.

The answer, as of April 2026, is honest but uncomfortable: you are paying approximately 22-25x trailing earnings for a business whose organic sales growth has decelerated from 7% in FY2021 to 2% in FY2025 and flatlined to 0% in Q2 FY2026 (the quarter ended December 31, 2025). Unit volume declined 1% in Q2 FY2026. For the full H1 FY2026, organic sales grew just 1%, with total company volume flat. The company is simultaneously executing a $1.5-2.0 billion restructuring program that will eliminate up to 7,000 non-manufacturing overhead positions by June 2027 -- the most aggressive organizational overhaul since the Lafley-era portfolio pruning. Management does not undertake restructurings of this magnitude when the top line is cooperating.

Meanwhile, the dividend itself grows at a pace that barely outruns inflation. The 5-year DPS CAGR is approximately 5%, the 10-year CAGR is approximately 4%, and the most recent increase (April 2025) was a modest step-up to $1.0568 per share quarterly ($4.23 annualized), representing roughly 5% growth. At a starting yield of approximately 2.5-2.9%, you need 5-7% annual price appreciation just to match the S&P 500's historical total return. That is possible but not guaranteed when organic growth is zero.

My view: PG is a HOLD at current prices for existing shareholders because the dividend is rock-solid and the restructuring should deliver margin expansion into FY2027-FY2028. It is an ACCUMULATE only below $137, where you get roughly 22x forward earnings and a yield approaching 3.1% -- a genuine margin of safety against further organic growth deceleration. For new capital at current levels, there are better risk-adjusted opportunities elsewhere in consumer staples.

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

The Procter & Gamble Company is the world's largest consumer packaged goods company by market capitalization (approximately $340-390 billion), founded in Cincinnati in 1837 and incorporated in Ohio. Products are sold in approximately 180 countries through mass merchandisers, e-commerce, grocery stores, membership clubs, drug stores, specialty beauty stores and direct-to-consumer channels. Walmart alone represents approximately 16% of total sales. The top 10 customers account for 43% of net sales (FY2025 10-K Item 1), a concentration that has increased from 40% in FY2023.

The operating model is organized into five Sector Business Units, which are also the five reportable segments. Enterprise Markets handle in-country execution for smaller geographies. Corporate Functions and Global Business Services provide centralized overhead. On-the-ground operations span approximately 70 countries across 99 manufacturing sites (24 in the U.S., 75 internationally).

- **Sector / Industry:** Consumer Staples -- Household & Personal Products
- **Business segments:** Beauty (18% of revenue, 16% of net earnings), Grooming (8%, 10%), Health Care (14%, 15%), Fabric & Home Care (36%, 35%), Baby, Feminine & Family Care (24%, 24%). All percentages FY2025 ex-Corporate.
- **Geographic exposure:** United States ~49% of FY2025 revenue (up from 44% in FY2020), International ~51%. Largest international markets: Greater China, United Kingdom, Canada, Japan, Germany -- collectively ~21% of net sales.
- **Customer concentration:** Walmart ~16%; top 10 customers ~43% of total sales.
- **Main competitors:** Unilever, Colgate-Palmolive, Kimberly-Clark, Henkel, Church & Dwight, Reckitt Benckiser, private-label brands (Kirkland, retailer own brands).
- **Recent corporate actions:** $1.5-2.0B focused restructuring (announced June 2025), Argentina substantial liquidation (completed September 2024), Glad JV termination with Clorox (January 2026, ~$500M proceeds), Gillette intangible impairment ($1.3B in FY2024).

**Key leadership:** Jon Moeller served as Chairman, President and CEO through FY2025 and signed the 10-K on August 4, 2025. Shailesh G. Jejurikar, previously Chief Operating Officer and a 30-year P&G lifer who ran Fabric & Home Care, was elevated to President and CEO effective October 2025 with Moeller remaining as Executive Chairman. Andre Schulten remains CFO. This is a classic internal succession from within P&G's develop-from-within culture. On the Q2 FY2026 earnings call, both Jejurikar and Schulten were present and actively engaged -- the transition appears seamless.

## PART III -- HISTORICAL REVIEW (FY2016-FY2025)

P&G's last decade is a story of deliberate portfolio pruning followed by margin expansion, not top-line acceleration. Revenue grew from $65.3 billion (FY2016) to $84.3 billion (FY2025) -- a 2.9% CAGR that conceals the divestiture of dozens of brands (most of the beauty portfolio to Coty in FY2016-FY2017) and the subsequent rebuild through pricing power and premiumization. The more honest measure is the five-year stretch from FY2020 to FY2025: revenue grew from $71.0B to $84.3B (3.5% CAGR), while operating income grew from $15.7B to $20.5B (5.5% CAGR).

The margin story is the dominant narrative. Gross margin expanded from 49.6% (FY2016) to 51.2% (FY2025), with a notable V-shaped dip to 47.4% in FY2022 during the commodity inflation spike -- proof that P&G's pricing power absorbs input cost shocks with a one-year lag. Operating margin expanded from 20.6% to 24.3% over the same period, with FY2019 an outlier (8.1%) due to the $8.0B Gillette goodwill writedown. When you strip out impairments and restructuring charges, normalized net income has compounded at approximately 6-7% annually since FY2016.

The concern is that the margin expansion engine is now doing all the work because volume growth has evaporated. The organic sales growth cadence tells the story: FY2021 +7%, FY2022 +7%, FY2023 +6%, FY2024 +4%, FY2025 +2%, H1 FY2026 +1%, Q2 FY2026 +0%. That is a textbook deceleration curve. Greater China market contraction is the single biggest driver, but North American volume is also flat to negative in several categories.

**Inflection points:**

## PART IV -- DIVIDEND DEEP DIVE

### The Streak

P&G has increased its dividend for 69 consecutive years, making it one of the longest-tenured Dividend Kings in existence. The streak began in 1956. P&G has paid a dividend continuously since its incorporation in 1890 -- 135 consecutive years of uninterrupted payments (10-K shareholder return section). Only a handful of companies (American States Water, Dover Corporation, Northwest Natural) have longer increase streaks, and none approach P&G's market capitalization or brand portfolio breadth.

The 10-K explicitly states: "P&G has been paying a dividend for 135 consecutive years since its incorporation in 1890 and has increased its dividend for 69 consecutive years since 1956. Over the past ten years, the dividend has increased at an annual compound average rate of 5%."

The Q2 FY2026 10-Q confirms the current quarterly dividend rate is $1.0568 per share ($4.23 annualized), up from $1.0065 per share in the year-ago period. The FY2025 raise was the smallest in the dataset at approximately 2.4% (per FMP data), while the step-up to the current rate represents approximately 5%. The five-year DPS CAGR (FY2020-FY2025) is approximately 5.1%. The ten-year DPS CAGR is approximately 4.0%.

The trend direction is worth watching. P&G's Board is calibrating raises to sustainable levels, which is prudent. But income investors pricing in 7-8% DGR should look elsewhere. The mathematical ceiling on sustainable DGR is approximately earnings growth minus CapEx inflation: call it 4-5% at best, with downside risk if FCF stays below $15 billion.

The FY2025 FCF payout of 70% is elevated versus FY2024's 56%, driven by higher CapEx ($3.77B vs $3.32B) and lower OCF from working capital and pension impacts. The 10-K explains the OCF decline explicitly: $806M in postretirement benefit impacts, $562M in 2017 Tax Act transitional payments, plus inventory and payable headwinds. This is a yellow flag, not a red flag. A return to $16B+ FCF (demonstrated in FY2024) would push the payout back to the low 60s.

H1 FY2026 operating cash flow was $10.4B (vs $9.1B prior year), representing a healthy improvement. Capital expenditures were $2.37B, yielding H1 adjusted FCF of approximately $8.7B. Annualized, that trajectory suggests FY2026 full-year FCF in the $15-16B range, which would bring dividend coverage back above 1.5x.

## PART V -- SEGMENT DEEP DIVE

### Beauty (18% of sales, 16% of net earnings -- FY2025)

**Brands:** Head & Shoulders, Pantene, Herbal Essences, Rejoice (hair care); Old Spice, Safeguard, Secret, Native (personal care); Olay, SK-II (skin care). Effective July 1, 2024, Beauty separated Skin and Personal Care into distinct operating segments with independent management, financial targets, and reporting (10-K Note).

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales decreased 2% to $15.0B. Net earnings declined 8% to $2.7B. The culprit is unmistakable: SK-II. The super-premium Japanese skin care brand suffered volume and distribution losses, dragging unfavorable mix of 200bps on segment revenue and 130bps on gross margin (10-K MD&A). Greater China hair care declined high single digits organically. Skin care organic sales decreased high single digits with mid-teens declines in both North America and Asia Pacific. Personal care was the bright spot at high single-digit growth, led by innovation in Old Spice and Safeguard.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Beauty staged a significant recovery, with net sales up 6% to $8.2B driven by 4% volume growth and 2% pricing. Organic sales +5%. On the Q2 FY2026 call, Schulten specifically noted that SK-II grew mid-teens or more in Greater China, and Pampers in Greater China was up double digits. Personal care led with high single-digit growth across Greater China, North America, and Europe. However, market share decreased 0.4 points despite revenue recovery, indicating growth is coming from lower-share-weight categories.

**Assessment:** SK-II's turnaround in H1 FY2026 is notable after FY2025's sharp decline, but I am cautious about declaring structural recovery. Chinese consumers have shifted toward domestic alternatives (Proya, Winona), and the super-premium segment is structurally different post-COVID. Hair care market share continued declining 0.8 points in H1 FY2026. The separation of Skin Care and Personal Care into distinct operating segments is a signal that management recognizes these businesses require fundamentally different strategies.

### Grooming (8% of sales, 10% of net earnings -- FY2025)

**Brands:** Gillette, Venus, Braun. P&G holds over 45% global grooming share, over 60% in blades and razors, over 25% in male electric shavers.

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales flat at $6.7B. Net earnings increased 7% to $1.6B with 150bps of margin improvement. Volume grew 2% but was entirely offset by FX (-2%), geographic mix (-1%), and divestitures (-1%).

**H1 FY2026 update:** Net sales increased 4% to $3.6B. But Q2 FY2026 individually showed a 9% decline in net earnings with 290bps margin compression, driven by unfavorable product mix and tariff costs. Grooming market share declined 0.6 points in H1, the largest decline of any segment. On the Q2 call, Schulten described Grooming improvement as dependent on "initiative timing" and "quality of execution in US stores."

The Gillette indefinite-lived intangible asset ($12.8B carrying value as of December 2025, per Q2 10-Q Note 4) passed its annual impairment test with fair value exceeding carrying value by greater than 10%. Sensitivity analysis: a 25bps increase in discount rate reduces fair value by 5%; a 25bps decrease in growth rate reduces fair value by 5%; a 50bps decrease in royalty rate reduces fair value by 4%. This is an asset within one bad year of another writedown.

**Assessment:** Grooming is a high-margin cash cow with limited growth potential. The global blades-and-razors market has been structurally disrupted. P&G's strategy of premiumization and female grooming expansion (Venus) is correct but will not deliver top-line acceleration. Jejurikar specifically called out Venus as having "upside in pretty much every region" on the Q2 call. The Gillette intangible risk is the key monitoring item.

### Health Care (14% of sales, 15% of net earnings -- FY2025)

**Brands:** Crest, Oral-B (oral care); Vicks, Metamucil, Pepto-Bismol, Neurobion (personal health care). P&G holds nearly 30% global oral care market share.

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales increased 2% to $12.0B. Net earnings increased 8% to $2.4B with 120bps of margin improvement. Organic sales grew 3% -- the best of any segment. Market share increased 0.2 points globally.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Net sales increased 4% to $6.6B with organic growth of 5% in Q1 and 3% in Q2. This is P&G's fastest-growing and most consistently profitable segment. The Q1 FY2026 call highlighted personal health care in Latin America growing 15% organically, with Brazil up nearly 30%. Greater China oral care continued declining (double-digit decrease in H1 FY2026), but global share still increased 0.3 points.

**Assessment:** This is the quiet gem in P&G's portfolio. Oral care has sustainable premiumization tailwinds from power toothbrushes and premium paste (Oral-B's ecosystem creates recurring revenue dynamics). Personal health care benefits from aging demographics (Metamucil, Vicks). If P&G ever did a portfolio transaction, Health Care would be the most valuable segment on a standalone basis. The Latin America execution under the new organizational structure is particularly impressive.

### Fabric & Home Care (36% of sales, 35% of net earnings -- FY2025)

**Brands:** Tide, Ariel, Downy, Gain (fabric care); Cascade, Dawn, Fairy, Febreze, Mr. Clean, Swiffer (home care). P&G holds over 35% global fabric care market share and over 30% in home care.

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales flat at $29.6B. Net earnings increased 3% to $5.8B. Organic sales grew 2%. Market share increased 0.1 points. Innovation (Tide Evo, concentrated formulations) and premiumization are driving mix improvement on flat volumes.

**H1 FY2026 update:** Net sales increased 1% to $15.5B, with organic sales flat in both Q1 and Q2. Market share declined 0.3 points in H1, with fabric care specifically losing 0.7 points -- the largest share decline of any individual category. The Q1 call extensively discussed Tide's biggest upgrade to liquid detergent in 20 years (boosted formula) and the national expansion of Tide Evo (functional fibers platform). In the Q2 call, Schulten acknowledged that category growth in the U.S. and Europe is running at approximately 1.5-2% value growth, with volume "essentially flat."

**Assessment:** This is the segment that pays the dividend. $29.6B at approximately 20% net margin generates $5.8B of net earnings. The Tide Evo platform is the most important innovation in P&G's pipeline -- if it achieves national scale, it could reaccelerate category growth. But the fabric care share losses in H1 FY2026 (-0.7 points) are a warning signal. Private-label encroachment is real, particularly in concentrated detergents and paper products. Europe is specifically problematic, with fabric care organic sales declining low single digits in H1 FY2026 due to competitive activity.

### Baby, Feminine & Family Care (24% of sales, 24% of net earnings -- FY2025)

**Brands:** Pampers, Luvs (baby care); Always, Always Discreet, Tampax (feminine care); Bounty, Charmin, Puffs (family care). P&G holds over 30% global baby care share, over 30% in feminine care.

**FY2025 performance:** Net sales flat at $20.2B. Net earnings flat at $4.0B. Organic sales grew 1%. Baby care declined mid-single digits with volume down across all regions except Greater China (where Pampers is gaining share via the Prestige line).

**H1 FY2026 update:** Net sales declined 1% to $10.3B. Baby care specifically was the worst performer with -5% net sales decline in Q2. Organic sales for the segment were -4% in Q2 and -2% for H1. Family Care was down double digits in Q2 due to prior-year pantry loading from port strike fears (Q2 FY2025 had elevated retail inventory build). This base effect was the single largest distortion in Q2 FY2026 results -- Schulten made this clear on the call: "Organic sales excluding Family Care were up 1% for the quarter."

The Q2 call featured an extended discussion of Pampers Prestige in Greater China, which has real silk ingredients and has driven double-digit organic growth and nearly 3 points of share gain over 18 months. This is one of the most successful regional innovations P&G has executed in years.

**Assessment:** Baby care is the structural weak spot globally due to declining birth rates. Pampers China is the exception that proves the rule -- super-premium innovation in a high-spending-per-baby market can offset unit volume declines. Family Care is a North American cash machine (40%+ share for Bounty, 25%+ for Charmin) with no international growth lever. Feminine care has premiumization potential through Always Discreet (incontinence, a $2B+ addressable market).

## PART VI -- MOAT & CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### Moat Assessment: Wide (High Confidence)

P&G possesses a textbook wide moat built on three reinforcing pillars:

**1. Brand intangibles.** P&G owns 20+ brands that each generate over $1 billion in annual sales. The institutional knowledge embedded in 189 years of consumer marketing is irreplicable. Retail shelf space allocation reflects decades of category captain relationships. The approximately $42B of goodwill and $22B of intangible assets on the balance sheet understate the true economic value because many of P&G's strongest brands (Tide, Crest, Bounty) were internally developed, not acquired.

**2. Scale-driven cost advantages.** P&G spends approximately $2B annually on R&D and $7-8B on advertising. No single competitor can match both simultaneously. The combination of global manufacturing scale (99 manufacturing sites in 32 countries), procurement leverage, and technology transfer across categories creates a cost advantage that is self-reinforcing. The 10-K explicitly describes this: "productivity improvement enables investments to strengthen the superiority of our brands via product and packaging innovation, more efficient and effective supply chains, equity and awareness-building brand advertising." P&G delivered 170bps of manufacturing productivity savings against gross margin in both Q1 and Q2 FY2026 -- this is a structural advantage.

**3. Switching costs (soft but real).** Consumers do not sign contracts, but habitual purchasing behavior creates meaningful retention. P&G's innovation model specifically raises switching barriers through ecosystem effects: Oral-B power brush refills, Swiffer refill pads, Gillette blade cartridges. The Tide Evo platform adds a new dimension -- a fundamentally different form factor that creates its own switching cost once consumers adopt.

**Moat trajectory:** Stable, with the caveat that private-label encroachment in North America and Europe is a slow but real narrowing force. Market share decreased 20 basis points in FY2025 and continued declining in H1 FY2026. Only 25 of the top 50 category-country combinations held or grew share in Q2 FY2026.

### Capital Allocation

P&G's framework is explicit and hierarchical (10-K MD&A):

1. **Fund the business** -- CapEx of $3-4B annually (~4-5% of revenue), R&D ~$2B
2. **Pay the dividend** -- first priority for excess cash (~$10B annually)
3. **Opportunistic M&A** -- historically disciplined; last major acquisition was Merck's consumer health business in 2018
4. **Share repurchases** -- residual cash returned; $5-11B annually depending on conditions

H1 FY2026 buybacks totaled $3.53B (annualizing to ~$7B), consistent with the $6-7B annual target. The 10-K confirmed FY2025 buybacks of $6.5B at an average price of $162.14 per share. Shares outstanding declined from approximately 2,342M (July 2025) to 2,324M (December 2025) -- an 18M reduction in six months.

Over the past decade, diluted shares have declined from approximately 2,765M (FY2016) to 2,424M (Q2 FY2026 diluted) -- a cumulative approximately 12% reduction that directly compounds EPS and DPS growth.

## PART X -- RISKS & STRESS TESTS

### Tier 1: High-Impact, Medium-Probability

**1. Organic growth remains at or below zero for 4+ quarters.** The deceleration from 7% to 0% over five years is not mean-reverting noise -- it reflects structural headwinds: Greater China market contraction, declining global birth rates (baby care), grooming market maturation, and post-inflation consumer belt-tightening. Schulten on the Q1 call: "Usage volume growth is slow. Honestly flat... both in the U.S. and in Europe." If organic growth does not recover to at least 2% by H2 FY2026, the consensus revenue estimates ($87-89B for FY2027) will need to come down, and the stock will de-rate.

**2. Tariff escalation permanently compresses margins.** H1 FY2026 shows 60bps of quarterly tariff drag on gross margin. P&G sources raw materials globally and manufactures in ~70 countries. The Q2 10-Q explicitly quantified it: 60bps of tariff costs as a gross margin headwind in both Q1 and Q2. Schulten on the Q1 call discussed tariff mitigation: "The biggest help on tariffs has been exclusion of materials, natural materials and ingredients that cannot be grown in the U.S." But this is policy-dependent and reversible. A sustained 100-150bps of tariff drag would consume $800M-$1.2B of pre-tax income.

**3. Greater China market contraction becomes structural.** Greater China appears across every segment as a source of decline: hair care (high single-digit organic decline in FY2025), oral care (mid-teens decline), fabric care (contraction). The H1 FY2026 improvement (Greater China organic sales +3% in Q2, +5% in Q1) is encouraging but heavily concentrated in Pampers Prestige and SK-II. Schulten described China market growth as "still negative" on the Q1 call. If the recovery stalls, P&G's single-largest international market will be a multi-year headwind.

### Tier 2: Medium-Impact, Medium-Probability

**4. Private-label share gains accelerate.** Market share declined in 25 of the top 50 category-country combinations in Q2 FY2026. Fabric care share losses of 0.7 points in H1 are the most concerning data point. Private-label penetration has been increasing since the 2022-2023 inflation cycle. Aldi, Lidl, and Costco's Kirkland brand are all gaining share in P&G's core categories. Robert Moskow of TD Cowen specifically pressed management on promotion intensity on the Q2 call; Schulten acknowledged that promotion levels will "move back to pre-COVID levels of around 30%."

**5. Gillette intangible impairment.** Fair value exceeds carrying value by greater than 10% as of the December 2025 test. But sensitivity shows that modest assumption changes (25bps) each reduce fair value by 4-5%. A third impairment (after FY2019 goodwill, FY2024 intangible) would be non-cash but would generate negative headlines.

**6. Restructuring execution risk.** The $1.5-2.0B program with up to 7,000 job cuts is complex organizational surgery executed in a decelerating growth environment. The 10-Q already shows elevated restructuring costs flowing through: 50-70bps of restructuring cost headwind in H1 FY2026 gross margin. If key talent departs or execution suffers during the transition, margin benefits arrive later and smaller than projected.

### Stress Test Scenarios

- **Recession (-15% FCF):** FCF drops to ~$12B, dividend still covered at 1.2x. No dividend cut risk, but buybacks suspended entirely.
- **Margin compression (-200 bps operating margin):** Operating income drops from ~$20.5B to ~$18.8B, net income to ~$14B. FCF payout rises to ~75% but remains sustainable.
- **Refinancing at +200 bps:** Interest expense rises from ~$900M to ~$1.6B (on ~$35B total debt). Interest coverage falls from ~23x to ~13x. Still extremely comfortable.
