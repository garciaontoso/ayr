## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD at GBP 38.10. ACCUMULATE below GBP 35. · Dividend Safety: 8/10 · Confidence: Medium-High**

Intertek is the purest play on a structural megatrend that never makes headlines: the relentless, secular growth of testing, inspection, and certification (TIC). Every consumer product sold in a regulated market must be tested. Every building material must be certified. Every pharmaceutical must be inspected. Every food product must meet safety standards. This testing infrastructure is non-discretionary, embedded in global supply chains by regulation, and grows at GDP+ regardless of economic cycles. Intertek is the third-largest TIC company globally behind SGS (Swiss) and Bureau Veritas (French), with GBP 3.4B revenue across 100+ countries through a network of 1,000+ laboratories and offices.

The stock trades at 3,810p against an FMP DCF of 7,280p — a 48% discount. CEO Andre Lacroix has driven a consistent margin-expansion strategy since 2015, taking operating margins from approximately 14% to 17%. The dividend has been raised for 20+ consecutive years. FCF generation is stable and predictable — GBP 396-480M annually over the past six years. The dividend yield of approximately 4.3% (GBP 1.65 / GBP 38.10) is the highest it has been in a decade, because the stock has de-rated from GBP 51.50 (52-week high) to GBP 38.10 — a 26% decline driven by concerns about organic growth deceleration and competitive pressure from SGS and Bureau Veritas.

My view: the de-rating is overdone. TIC is a structurally growing, regulation-driven, non-cyclical industry. Intertek's business model — asset-light, labor-intensive, locally-delivered, globally-networked — is a near-perfect dividend compounder. The 4.3% yield with 5-8% DGR gives a total return profile of 9-12% with low drawdown risk.

**Top 3 reasons to own (specific):**
1. **TIC is a structural growth industry with regulatory tailwinds.** Every new regulation — EU REACH chemicals, PFAS restrictions, sustainability reporting (CSRD), food safety modernization, medical device recertification — creates testing demand. Regulatory complexity is increasing globally, and it never decreases. Intertek's addressable market grows at approximately 4-5% annually.
2. **Capital-light, high-ROIC business model.** CapEx is 4-5% of revenue. The business is labor-intensive (25,000+ employees are the primary asset), locally delivered (clients send products to the nearest Intertek lab), and generates 30%+ ROIC. Minimal inventory, no major fixed-asset requirements, and predictable recurring revenue from long-term testing contracts.
3. **20+ year dividend growth streak at 4.3% current yield.** This is a rare combination in UK equities: long track record of growth + elevated current yield due to share price compression. The 25% DPS growth shown in FMP finGrowth for 2025 signals management confidence in the business trajectory.

**Top 3 reasons not to own:**
1. **Organic growth has decelerated.** Revenue grew only 1.1% in 2025 (GBP 3,394M to GBP 3,432M). Even allowing for FX headwinds, organic growth appears to have slowed from the 5-6% rates seen in 2022-2023. If organic growth settles at 2-3%, the growth premium in the stock evaporates.
2. **Competition is intensifying.** SGS (CHF 6.9B revenue, recently merged with Bureau Veritas) creates a TIC giant with significant scale advantages. The SGS-BV combination may pressure Intertek on pricing in commoditized testing services.
3. **Margin expansion runway is narrowing.** Operating margin has improved from 14% (2016) to 17% (2025), but further expansion requires either revenue growth acceleration or deeper cost restructuring. The easy gains (lab utilization, pricing) may be largely captured.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- H1 2026 results (July 2026) — organic growth trajectory and margin trends
- Response to SGS-Bureau Veritas merger — strategic positioning update
- Potential bolt-on M&A to strengthen positions in sustainability and ESG testing
- 2025 dividend announcement — expect ~GBP 1.70-1.80 (3-9% increase)

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — STRATEGIC ARC

**Phase 1 — Pre-Lacroix Growth (2008-2015).** Under previous management, Intertek grew primarily through acquisitions and geographic expansion. Revenue grew from ~GBP 1.5B to ~GBP 2.5B through bolt-on deals in Asia, Middle East, and Latin America. Margins hovered at 14-15%. The TIC industry was consolidating globally, with SGS, Bureau Veritas, and Intertek emerging as the "Big Three."

**Phase 2 — Lacroix Total Quality Assurance (2015-2020).** Andre Lacroix became CEO in 2015 and introduced the "Total Quality Assurance" strategy — a shift from testing individual products to providing end-to-end quality assurance across entire supply chains. This meant cross-selling (a client who tests a product in one lab now gets supply chain audit, factory inspection, and regulatory certification), pricing optimization (moving from cost-plus to value-based pricing), and operational efficiency (lab utilization targets, shared services consolidation). Margins improved from ~14% to ~16.5% by 2019. COVID (2020) disrupted physical inspection work temporarily, compressing margins to 14.6%.

**Phase 3 — Post-COVID Recovery and Margin Focus (2021-2025).** The recovery was slow but steady. Revenue returned to pre-COVID levels by 2022 and has grown modestly since. Lacroix's margin expansion continued: operating margins crossed 17% in 2025. The strategy has shifted subtly: less emphasis on acquisitive growth and more on organic margin improvement, portfolio optimization (exiting low-margin government services contracts), and new high-growth verticals (sustainability testing, ESG assurance, battery and EV testing).

## PART VI · SGS-BUREAU VERITAS MERGER — COMPETITIVE IMPLICATIONS

The announced merger between SGS and Bureau Veritas (confirmed late 2025/early 2026) creates a TIC giant with approximately CHF 14B (~GBP 12B) of combined revenue — roughly 3.5x Intertek's size. This is the most significant competitive development in the TIC industry's history.

**Implications for Intertek:**
1. **Pricing pressure in commoditized services.** The merged SGS-BV entity will have pricing power in high-volume, standardized testing services (food safety, consumer products, cargo inspection). Intertek may face margin compression in these segments.
2. **Talent competition.** The combined entity will offer broader career paths and potentially higher compensation. Intertek risks losing key technical staff, particularly in high-demand areas like sustainability and EV battery testing.
3. **M&A landscape shifts.** Bolt-on acquisition targets that previously considered Intertek as a buyer may now prefer the larger SGS-BV platform. Intertek's acquisition currency (lower market cap) becomes relatively less attractive.

**Mitigants:**
- Intertek's Total Quality Assurance strategy differentiates on service quality, not scale. For premium clients who value end-to-end supply chain assurance, Intertek's focused approach may be preferable to a mega-conglomerate.
- The merger will distract SGS and BV management for 2-3 years — creating an opportunity for Intertek to gain share through superior service while competitors focus internally.
- Regulatory antitrust review may require SGS-BV divestitures that Intertek could acquire.

## PART VII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

**Moat pillars:**
1. **Regulatory mandates.** Testing and certification are required by law in virtually every industry. This is non-discretionary spending — clients cannot avoid it.
2. **Accreditation barriers.** Each lab requires individual accreditation from national bodies (UKAS, A2LA, CNAS). Building a new accredited lab network takes years and millions of investment.
3. **Customer relationships and trust.** Switching TIC providers involves re-validating years of test data, re-certifying products, and risking supply chain disruption. Clients don't switch for a 5% price reduction.
4. **Global network.** 1,000+ labs in 100+ countries enables Intertek to serve multinational supply chains end-to-end. Few competitors can match this geographic breadth.

**Capital allocation scorecard:**
- **Dividends: A-.** 20+ year growth streak, but the 2020 cut (albeit minor) and the 2025 step-up to 76% payout raise questions about sustainability.
- **Buybacks: B+.** GBP 368M in 2025 is aggressive and well-timed (below intrinsic value). Historically modest.
- **M&A: B.** Bolt-on deals have been sensible but uninspiring. No transformative acquisition.
- **Organic investment: B+.** Lab expansion in sustainability, EV testing, and digital testing is strategically sound.

**Detailed risk analysis — SGS-Bureau Veritas merger:**
The SGS-BV combination creates an entity with approximately 180,000 employees, 2,600+ offices, and CHF 14B+ revenue. For context, Intertek has 25,000 employees and GBP 3.4B revenue. The scale differential is approximately 4:1. This matters in three ways: (1) the merged entity can cross-sell across a broader service portfolio, (2) it can bid for larger global contracts that require multi-country lab coverage, and (3) it can invest more in technology (AI-powered testing, digital certificates, IoT sensors). Intertek's response will likely be to double down on its premium positioning — "Total Quality Assurance" as a differentiated service model versus commoditized volume testing — and to pursue bolt-on acquisitions in high-growth niches (sustainability, EV batteries, PFAS testing) where scale matters less than expertise.

The Products segment is the growth engine and highest-margin division. Trade and Resources are mature, lower-margin businesses that contribute stability but limited growth. ## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

**Consensus view:** Intertek is a high-quality TIC company facing competitive pressure from the SGS-BV merger, with decelerating organic growth and a fully-valued dividend. Hold.

**My view:** The 4.3% yield at 17.6x P/E is a fair entry point for a non-cyclical, regulation-driven, 20+ year dividend grower. The SGS-BV merger concern is valid but overblown — merger integration will distract the combined entity for 2-3 years, and Intertek's premium positioning (Total Quality Assurance) targets a different client segment than high-volume commoditized testing. The 25% DPS increase in 2025 signals management confidence. The organic growth deceleration to 1.1% in 2025 needs monitoring, but FX headwinds and portfolio rationalization explain much of it. At GBP 38.10, you are paid 4.3% to wait for growth to re-accelerate.
