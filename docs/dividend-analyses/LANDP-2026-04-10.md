## TL;DR (10 lines)

1. LANDP is the **$25 par, 6.00% coupon ($1.50/yr, paid $0.125 monthly) Series C Cumulative Redeemable Preferred Stock** of Gladstone Land Corp (LAND), an externally-managed farmland REIT. 9,954,863 shares outstanding = **$248.9M par** (source: 2025 10-K p.1680).
2. **The security is ALREADY CALLABLE** since June 1, 2024 at $25.00 + accrued. The company is not calling because the market price is below par and they're opportunistically repurchasing shares in the open market under a $35M authorization at ~$20.83 weighted-avg (source: 2024 10-K p.3013-3017).
3. **This is the central asymmetry:** you bear 100% of downside to business deterioration but your upside is capped at $25 with a callable-anytime-now structure. It behaves like a perpetual bond with a short call and an unhedged equity kicker on the downside.
4. **The underlying business is deteriorating materially.** Same-property occupancy fell from 96.2% (2024) to 95.1% (2025). Portfolio shrank from 150 → 144 farms, 103,001 → 98,688 acres. 17-18 farms are vacant/direct-operated/non-accrual out of 144 (~12.5%). Zero farm acquisitions in 2024-2025. This is a shrinking REIT, not a growing one.
5. **AFFO per common share collapsed** from $0.57 (2023) → $0.47 (2024) → **$0.39 (2025)**, while the common distribution stayed at $0.56. Common dividend is now 143% of AFFO — unsustainable. The common's implicit subordination buffer for LANDP is eroding in real time.
6. **Preferred coverage math**: 2025 FFO-to-preferred coverage ≈ 1.6x (FFO $36.5M incl. Series D dividends / $27M total preferred distributions incl. Series D). Stripping out Series D (redeemed Jan 30, 2026), forward-looking LANDP + Series B + Series E coverage is ~1.9x. **Thin for a farmland REIT with 65.6% California concentration and deteriorating tenant quality.**
7. **California is the whole thing.** 65.6% of 2025 lease revenue came from California (vs. 68.5% in 2024). Tenant A = 10.7% of revenue, lease expires 2027. Water assets (55,532 acre-feet, $37.2M cost basis) are real but not a liquid market. SGMA groundwater restrictions remain the elephant in the room.
8. **Management quality is mediocre at best.** External management (Gladstone Management Corp.) charges 0.60% of GROSS real estate plus an incentive fee on FFO that was only avoided in 2025 because the Adviser voluntarily waived $2.7M — a red flag that the fee structure is broken when performance is poor. David Gladstone runs 4 public vehicles; conflicts are pervasive. Dealer-manager fees paid to affiliated Gladstone Securities were 9% of gross proceeds on the Series C offering (3% + 6%) — this preferred issue was expensive capital even before the dividend.
9. **Fair value today:** I estimate LANDP should trade at a **7.25%–7.75% current yield** ($19.35–$20.69 per share) to compensate for: (a) callable-now asymmetry, (b) California concentration, (c) external-management conflicts, (d) deteriorating same-store fundamentals, (e) short stub of Series B cushion above it. Recent market activity (company buybacks at ~$20.83 weighted-avg) corroborates a clearing price in this zone.
10. **Verdict: HOLD for yield at $20–$21 if you already own it; AVOID for new money above $22. AVOID COMPLETELY if you cannot stomach a potential 12-18 month freeze on the common dividend or a LAND balance sheet restructuring.** Series B (LANDO, also 6.00%) is structurally a hair senior and currently trades similar — if you want Gladstone Land preferred exposure, prefer LANDO over LANDP for the marginal seniority, tighter share count, and shorter maturity of the outstanding pool. For pure income buyers willing to take ag exposure, I'd look elsewhere in the ag/triple-net REIT preferred universe.

### Critical implication of "already callable"

LANDP is in what I call a **"headwind window."** Because the call option is live:
- If LAND's credit improves or market yields fall, management will call LANDP at $25 and refinance cheaper. **Your capital appreciation is capped at par.**
- If LAND's credit deteriorates (as it currently is), LANDP will not be called — you keep the 6% coupon but eat mark-to-market drawdown with no redemption backstop. **Your downside is not capped.**

The asymmetry is classic short-call: **heads they win, tails you lose.**

## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Gladstone Land Corporation is a 13-year-old externally-managed Maryland REIT whose business is owning US farmland and leasing it to farmers under predominantly triple-net leases. At year-end 2025 it owned 144 farms totaling 98,688 acres across 14 states, concentrated heavily in California (65.6% of lease revenue) and Florida (11.8%). Its corporate market cap on the common (LAND, ~38M shares × recent $10-ish handle) is roughly $380M, but the capital stack includes $475M of farm mortgages and ~$401M of outstanding preferred stock — so enterprise capital is ~$1.25B, roughly in line with total assets of $1.24B.

**LANDP is a $25-par, 6.00% perpetual cumulative redeemable preferred issued in 2020-2022 through a retail-focused, commission-heavy offering sold by affiliated broker-dealer Gladstone Securities, then listed on Nasdaq in June 2023 to provide secondary-market liquidity.** Approximately $253M in gross proceeds were raised; after commissions (~9% all-in, between 6% selling commission and 3% dealer-manager fee), roughly $230M hit the balance sheet. The security became callable at par on June 1, 2024. Today about $248.9M of par is outstanding (9.95M shares × $25).

**The central question** for a senior dividend investor is whether the 6% coupon ($1.50 per share) is adequate compensation for what is now a deteriorating operating story: AFFO per common share has fallen from $0.70 (2022) to $0.57 (2023) to $0.47 (2024) to **$0.39 (2025)** — a 44% decline in three years. That is a big deal, because AFFO is the source of preferred dividend coverage, and the common distribution of $0.56/share (unchanged since 2023) now exceeds AFFO by 43%. The common dividend is being paid out of the preferred's cushion, not out of operating cash flow. The rating agencies don't cover this preferred (no Moody's or S&P rating), which means price discovery falls entirely on retail sentiment and technical flows.

**My strongest institutional view is this:** LANDP is a rich security when viewed as a perpetual bond against 6.6-year US farmland with 65% California concentration, deteriorating same-store economics, and an external manager whose incentives are only partially aligned with preferred holders. The 6.00% coupon on par was set in a low-rate environment (2020-2022) that no longer exists. At 4.0% 10-year Treasury yields (source: 2025 10-K p.822), a fair credit spread for unrated, externally-managed, single-sector REIT preferred with these concentration and coverage metrics is 325-375 bps → 7.25%-7.75% current yield → **fair value $19.35-$20.69 per share**. Above $22, I am a seller of new positions. Below $20, I am a reluctant buyer.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

### What Gladstone Land actually does

Gladstone Land is a pure-play agricultural REIT. It buys farmland (mostly from existing owners in sale-leaseback transactions or from retiring farmers) and leases it back to farming operations under leases ranging from 3-10 years (row crops) to 7-15 years (permanent crops like almonds, pistachios, wine grapes, blueberries). The company does not generally farm itself — though in 2025 it direct-operated four farms (2,409 acres in Kern County, California, growing almonds and pistachios) through a third-party operator, generating $12.2M in crop revenue against $9.6M in cost of sales. This is a new revenue line that did not exist in 2023 or 2024 and reflects a structural shift: **where they cannot find creditworthy tenants or where they want more upside from crop prices, they are taking operating risk onto the REIT itself**.

**This is a REIT in net contraction.** It is selling assets (25 farms disposed 2024-2025), not buying any new ones, using proceeds to pay down mortgage debt (down from $626M in 2022 to $475M in 2025), and raising incremental common equity at depressed prices ($9.58 weighted avg on 2025 ATM). The preferred shareholder is funding a gradual wind-down that may or may not resolve.

### Governance

Externally managed by **Gladstone Management Corporation**. Administered by **Gladstone Administration LLC**. Both are owned and controlled by David Gladstone personally, who also founded and controls:
- Gladstone Capital (GLAD) — BDC
- Gladstone Investment (GAIN) — BDC
- Gladstone Commercial (GOOD) — commercial REIT
- Gladstone Land (LAND) — this entity

**Conflicts are pervasive and disclosed.** The base management fee is **0.60% of gross tangible real estate** (i.e., pre-depreciation cost basis), calculated quarterly at 0.15%. This is a "size fee" — it rewards gross assets even when those assets are underperforming or impaired. In 2025 the base management fee was $8.0M, essentially unchanged from 2024's $8.4M despite the portfolio shrinking 4%.

The **incentive fee** is designed as a 20% above-hurdle catch-up structure on Pre-Incentive Fee FFO above a 7% annualized hurdle. In Q3 2024 and Q4 2025 the fee was triggered, but in both cases the Adviser voluntarily, non-contractually, and irrevocably **waived** the entire fee — the Q4 2025 waiver was **$2.7M**. This is a double-edged signal: it tells you the Adviser is at least somewhat willing to share pain in bad quarters, but it also tells you the formula generates fees during genuinely bad operating years (because FFO is propped up by disposition gains and impairment write-downs that reduce the equity base used in the hurdle denominator). You should not count on future waivers.

Additionally, **Gladstone Securities** (also owned by David Gladstone) was paid ~$13.5M in 2022 and ~$22.7M cumulatively in selling commissions and dealer-manager fees on the Series C offering. This means that of the $253M gross proceeds raised from Series C, roughly 9% went to the Gladstone-family broker before a dollar reached the farm. Retail LANDP holders paid a 9% underwriting fee before they ever received their first $0.125 monthly distribution. That's a frictional tax that most retail buyers never understood.

### Externally managed REIT preferred — the structural caveat

External management introduces a classic principal-agent problem for preferred holders. The manager is paid on gross assets, not on preferred coverage. The manager has strong incentive to keep growing AUM (to grow fees) even when the underlying business isn't earning its cost of capital. The preferred holder wants smaller, healthier, higher-coverage balance sheet; the manager wants bigger, levered, fee-maximizing balance sheet. When those diverge, the preferred holder loses — and at LAND in 2024-2025 they are diverging. The saving grace is that the portfolio is currently shrinking (fewer acquisitions → lower gross assets → lower base fee), but that is happening because the manager cannot find acquisitions they can finance accretively, not because they chose to return capital.

## PART III · 10-YEAR ARC

### The growth era (2013-2022): farmland gold rush

Gladstone Land IPO'd in January 2013 at $15/share with ~12 farms and ~$75M in assets. Over the next decade it executed an aggressive sale-leaseback roll-up financed predominantly with retail preferred equity (Series A, B, C, D, E in succession) and Farm Credit / Farmer Mac mortgage debt at low fixed rates (the wtd avg interest rate is still 3.39% — they locked in the good rates). By peak in late 2021, LAND owned 169 farms across 15 states, with ~$1.46B in total assets.

The macro narrative was:
1. **Inflation hedge.** US farmland has historically returned 10-12% per year through the combination of 4-5% cash yield and 5-7% land appreciation. Supply is fixed, demand (food) is inelastic, farmland is negatively correlated to equities in some periods. Academic research (NCREIF Farmland Index) suggested US farmland had a Sharpe ratio comparable to high-quality bonds with lower drawdowns.
2. **Water-secured California permanent crops.** Almonds, pistachios, wine grapes, berries — high-value-per-acre crops with international export demand. Gladstone leaned heavily into Kern County, Monterey, Ventura, Santa Barbara.
3. **Triple-net leases to "creditworthy" farm operators.** The thesis was that tenants bore operating risk; Gladstone collected stable rent with periodic escalators.

The stock and preferreds performed. LANDM, LANDO, LANDP all traded near par. The common (LAND) hit $40+ in early 2022. Distributions to common grew each year.

### The inflection (2022-2023): cost-of-capital shock

Two things killed the growth story:
1. **The Fed hiked rates 525 bps in 18 months.** Farmland cap rates, historically 4-5%, could no longer be financed with 3.5% mortgage debt. New acquisitions were instantly dilutive. Gladstone last acquired farms in 2022. **They have not acquired a single new farm in 2024 or 2025.**
2. **California drought → weather whiplash.** 2021-2022 drought forced tenant-level stress in Kern, Fresno, Madera counties. Then January 2023 atmospheric-river flooding damaged Central Valley farms. Then the 2024 "1,000-year" storm event. SGMA groundwater restrictions are phasing in through 2040.

### The distress (2024-2025): same-store deterioration

From 2024 onward, the story rotated from growth to portfolio rationalization:
- **25 farms disposed** in 2024-2025 for aggregate net proceeds of ~$160M
- **$5-6M in property impairment charges** across Michigan, Florida, California
- **Tenant credit concerns** led to 5 farms being placed on cash-accrual (non-accrual) basis in 2025, up from fewer in 2024
- **Lease restructurings** converted fixed base rent to participation rents (crop-revenue-share) on multiple California permanent-crop farms — shifting operating risk ONTO the REIT
- **Direct operation** of 2,409 acres (almonds/pistachios in Kern County) because they couldn't find acceptable tenants
- **Common dividend unchanged** at $0.56/year despite AFFO/share falling to $0.39 — this is a pay-out ratio of 143%, compared to ~80% in 2023
- **Common stock price collapsed** from $20+ in early 2023 to ~$9.58 weighted-avg on their own 2025 ATM issuance. They are selling new common at depressed prices to fund operations.
- **Series D (LANDM) redemption** on Jan 30, 2026 for $60.6M cash paid out of liquidity that had dropped from $193M (2024) to $86M (2025)

Let me be direct: **this is not a company that has figured it out yet.** It is a company muddling through a multi-year transition from growth-through-acquisition to value-extraction-through-disposal, and the preferred holders are the ones financing the muddling.

### What the preferred holder should notice

From 2020 through mid-2024, LANDP's main risk was duration (long-dated low-coupon bond in a rising rate world). From mid-2024 forward, LANDP's main risk has shifted to **credit** (deteriorating operating metrics, thinning coverage, common dividend unsustainable). This is a meaningful change in the risk profile that the coupon has not moved to reflect.

## PART V · DIVIDEND / PREFERRED DISTRIBUTION DEEP-DIVE

### Distribution mechanics

LANDP pays $0.125 per share monthly (record date ~mid-month, payment end of month), which equates to $1.50 annually or 6.00% of $25 par. The distributions are **cumulative** — if the company skipped a payment, it would accumulate as arrears and must be paid in full (with no interest on the arrears) before any common distribution can resume. There are standard "no dividend to junior" covenants.

### Track record

**Gladstone Land has never missed a preferred payment since the IPO in 2013** (source: 2022 10-K p.772, confirmed in subsequent filings). The same is true for the common, though the common's distributions have been partially funded by return-of-capital distributions in recent years. The unbroken track record is meaningful — but it also tells you nothing about forward risk, which is what matters for new money.

### 2024 tax treatment (useful for US taxable investors)

Per 2024 10-K p.3056-3093:
- **Ordinary income: 47.18%** (taxed at ordinary rates — for a 32% bracket investor, effective tax on that portion ~32%, but REIT dividends qualify for the 20% QBI deduction through 2025)
- **Long-term capital gains: 23.89%** (taxed at 0/15/20%)
- **Return of capital: 28.94%** (not currently taxable; reduces cost basis)

The large return-of-capital component is a hallmark of a REIT whose cash distributions exceed current taxable income (because of depreciation shield). For a tax-sensitive buyer, this is actually favorable — the effective after-tax yield is higher than the stated 6%. But it comes with the caveat that return-of-capital eventually unwinds your basis; if you hold forever, it converts to capital gain at sale.

### Distribution coverage: the institutional view

The proper test for a preferred holder is:
**"Is there enough recurring cash flow, after mortgage debt service and normalized capex, to pay all preferred dividends AND leave some cushion for adverse scenarios?"**

Using 2025 data:
- **Cash from operating activities: $7.0M** (2025 10-K p.1431) — down from $29.5M in 2024. This is the cleanest coverage measure.
- **Total cash preferred dividends paid (2025): ~$27M** across Series B, C, D, E.
- **Coverage = 7 / 27 = 0.26x.**

That is catastrophically low **on this single measure** — but it's worth noting that 2025 operating cash flow was depressed by: (a) new direct-farm operating costs on the Kern County almond/pistachio farms (cash out now for crop receipts in 2026), (b) lease restructurings that shift fixed-rent to back-loaded participation rents, (c) the collection gap from non-accrual tenants. Normalized 2024-level operating cash is $29.5M, which covers the $24M post-Series D preferred with 1.23x. **That is your actual forward coverage ratio if 2025 was an anomaly and not a trend.**

I am not confident it is an anomaly. The 2025 10-K has language suggesting participation rents don't begin materially flowing until H2 2026, and the direct-farmed pistachio crops don't fully cash-settle until late 2026/early 2027. The 2026 10-K is going to be ugly.

### Sensitivity: what would break coverage?

| Scenario | Impact on Pref Coverage | Probability (my view) |
|---|---|---|
| Tenant A (10.7% of rev) defaults or doesn't renew in 2027 | -$8M ARR | 25% |
| Pistachio crop failure or prices fall 30% | -$4-6M | 20% |
| Lease modifications fail to deliver promised participation rents | -$5M | 40% |
| California SGMA groundwater curtailment on 10-15% of acres | -$3-5M | 30% |
| Two of three above in same year | Coverage goes below 1.0x AFFO | 25% |

**In a moderate stress scenario, I think there is a 20-25% probability that within 24 months LAND is forced to suspend the common distribution** to protect the preferred. This is actually GOOD for LANDP holders in the short term (common subordination works) — but it would trigger a violent preferred price drop to ~$17-18 as retail capitulates, even if cumulative dividends continue. Your paper P&L would look ugly before the story rights itself.

In a severe stress scenario (tenant A fails + SGMA bites + pistachio crash), LAND could be forced to skip preferred dividends for a couple of quarters. They would accrue. LANDP might trade to $13-15. You'd eventually get paid, but the drawdown would be ~35-40%.

## PART VI · FARMLAND AS AN ASSET CLASS — Inflation hedge or narrative?

This section is where I put on my senior-investor hat and dismantle the marketing.

### What retail investors are told

- "Farmland has been the best-performing asset class for decades" (true, with caveats)
- "US farmland returns have been 10-12% per year with low volatility" (true on NCREIF farmland index through 2015-2020; fading since)
- "Farmland is a natural inflation hedge" (true in theory for owner-operators, much weaker for leveraged landlord REITs)
- "Food demand is inelastic — people have to eat" (true but irrelevant to landlord cash flows)
- "Water rights are embedded gold" (true in California for the holders, but hard to monetize without operating or selling the farm)

### What the 10-K actually tells you

**Direct quote from 2025 10-K p.820 (shortened):** the USDA August 2025 Land Values Summary reported US farm real estate values increased 4.3% YoY, with cropland up 4.7%. This is moderating from the 2021-2022 peak appreciation. Food prices are up 19.8% cumulative over 4 years vs. 16.2% CPI.

So farmland is appreciating at ~4.5% and food inflation is ~5%/year. **Cap rates on working farmland in Gladstone's target regions (California permanent crops, Florida row crops) are 4-5%.** Total unlevered return ≈ 4.5% cap + 4.5% appreciation ≈ 9%. Take out 3.4% financing cost on mortgage debt and the levered return should be ~10-11%. That's BEFORE external management fees (0.60% on gross + incentive), BEFORE preferred dividend drag, BEFORE G&A, BEFORE impairments.

**Net to common equity, 2025 shows:** AFFO/share $0.39, distributed $0.56 — unsustainable. Meaning the levered farmland return at Gladstone Land is NOT 10-11%. It is closer to 5-6% because of management fee drag, preferred coupon drag, high transaction costs on preferred equity (9% underwriting), and the inability to acquire new farms at accretive spreads.

### The California water premium — real, but illiquid

Gladstone Land owns 55,532 acre-feet of banked water (48,309 in Semitropic Water Storage District Kern County + 7,223 at Westlands Fresno County) with a book cost of ~$37M. Spot prices for California water rights vary wildly — $1,000-$3,000/acre-foot in wet years, $5,000-$12,000+ in drought years. At a blended $2,500/acre-foot, these water assets might be worth $140M, roughly 4x book. That's a meaningful hidden asset **IF** you can monetize it, which requires either selling the underlying farms (and losing the water) or negotiating standalone water transfers (which have regulatory and neighbor-rights complications).

For a preferred holder, the water rights are a **backstop in a liquidation scenario** but are unlikely to generate incremental cash flow in the ordinary course.

### My opinion on farmland as an inflation hedge

Farmland is an inflation hedge for the person who owns it outright, operates it, and sells crops at world prices. It is NOT an inflation hedge for a landlord REIT with fixed-rate mortgages, CPI-linked lease escalators (which cap out or reset slowly), and externally-managed fee drag. The LANDP holder is getting a 6% nominal coupon on a perpetual preferred — that is a pure nominal bond exposure. There is no inflation linkage in the preferred distribution. If CPI runs 4% for 5 years, the real yield on LANDP goes from 2% to 2% — the coupon doesn't adjust.

**Conclusion:** LANDP is NOT an inflation-hedged security. It is a 6% nominal perpetual with credit risk. Buying it for "inflation protection" is a category error, though this is exactly what the Gladstone Securities marketing deck implied in 2020-2022. The common stock (LAND) has modestly more inflation linkage because of participation rents, but even there the linkage is loose.

## PART VII · MOAT + CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### The moat question

**LAND does not have a meaningful economic moat.** The business of buying farmland and leasing it back to farmers has low barriers to entry. The capital needed is substantial ($100M+) but farmland is fractional and auctionable. Farmers themselves prefer to own their land when they can afford to — LAND is a buyer of last resort for farmers who want to free up capital. Gladstone's edge, such as it is, comes from:

1. **Sourcing network.** The Gladstone Adviser has been in agricultural real estate since 2003. They know farmers, brokers, and auction markets across 14 states. This is real but modestly valuable.
2. **Cheap fixed-rate debt from Farm Credit + Farmer Mac + MetLife.** The 3.39% weighted-average fixed rate for 2.7 more years is genuinely good. New borrowers cannot replicate this. **This is actually the closest thing to a moat LAND has.**
3. **Water assets in California.** Hard to replicate because the permit system is closed and the secondary market is illiquid. Worth something in extremis.
4. **Diversification across 14 states / 60+ crops / 82 tenants.** This is just diversification, not a moat.

**What LAND does not have:**
- Scale advantages (Farmland Partners FPI, Nuveen Global Farmland, institutional buyers are all larger)
- Brand pricing power (farmers don't pay premium rent to Gladstone specifically)
- Regulatory moat (anyone can buy farmland)
- Network effects (the business is local)
- Switching costs for tenants (3-7 year leases, tenants leave regularly)
- Intangibles or IP

### Capital allocation track record

Let's grade Gladstone Land's capital allocation over 10 years:

**Acquisitions (2013-2022): B-.** They built a large portfolio at reasonable prices during a bull market for farmland. But they over-weighted California permanent crops at peak prices (2018-2021), and some of those acquisitions are now showing strain (e.g., the Santa Barbara and St. Lucie impairments in 2025, the Michigan blueberry impairments in 2024). Acquisition discipline declined in the final years as pressure to deploy capital mounted.

**Preferred equity issuance: C.** Raising $250M+ of perpetual preferred at 6% in 2020-2022 looked smart when rates were low, but they paid 9% in underwriting fees to an affiliate broker-dealer. That is an enormous frictional cost. A straight bank loan or public notes offering would have been much cheaper. The preferred decision locked in high-cost capital with an always-on cash drag.

**Debt refinancing: A.** Locking in 3.39% fixed-rate mortgages for 6.6-year wtd avg duration was excellent. This is the one thing they did really well.

**Dispositions (2024-2025): B+.** Selling 25 farms for ~$160M net, much of it at modest GAAP gains, freed up liquidity and repaid mortgages. This is good discipline, though forced by circumstance rather than choice.

**Common equity ATM (2025): F.** Issuing 5.25M new common shares at $9.58 weighted-avg to fund preferred dividends and operating cash is actively destructive to existing common holders. From a preferred perspective it's neutral — more common cushion is good — but it's the kind of move a company makes when running out of alternatives.

**Preferred buybacks (2024): A-.** Buying back 201,646 shares of Series C (LANDP) at $20.83 retires par at a ~17% discount — this is **very good capital allocation for the remaining preferred holders** because it reduces future dividend obligations at a discount. I'd like to see more of this, and the fact that 2025 had zero buybacks (despite authorization) suggests liquidity constraints took priority.

**Common dividend maintenance: F.** Holding common distribution at $0.56 when AFFO/share is $0.39 is a pure capital-destructive decision. It signals to markets that management would rather pretend the old dividend is sustainable than face the consequences of a cut. Every dollar paid out today is a dollar that should have been retained to strengthen the balance sheet for preferred holders.

**Overall grade: C+.** Good at financing, weak at strategic allocation decisions, and currently making a poor choice on the common dividend.

### Externally-managed REIT fee drag — what it actually costs

2025 fees paid to the Gladstone affiliate complex:
- Base management fee: $8.0M
- Administration fee: $2.6M
- Incentive fee (post-waiver): $0.0M (would have been $2.7M)
- Write-off of Series E offering costs: $0.5M

**Total affiliate cost to the REIT: ~$11.1M in 2025**, or about **15% of lease revenue**. That is ~44 bps on gross assets pre-depreciation. For reference, a well-run internally-managed peer REIT should run 8-12% of revenue on total G&A/corporate overhead. The Gladstone Land fee structure adds maybe 30-50 bps of drag on gross assets that would otherwise flow to security holders. Over 10 years that is 3-5% of NAV in cumulative fee leakage — **real money for a preferred holder**.

## PART VIII · RISKS (ordered by severity for LANDP holder)

### 1. Deteriorating coverage (HIGH, IMMEDIATE)

Already discussed. AFFO/share down 44% in 3 years, common dividend unchanged. Forward coverage of preferred on a cash-flow basis is under 1.0x on AFFO terms. One more 10% decline in lease revenue breaks the model and forces a common dividend cut followed by preferred market panic.

### 2. California concentration and water (HIGH, STRUCTURAL)

65.6% of lease revenue from California. SGMA phased groundwater restrictions through 2040 will impact Kern County (where Gladstone has significant almond/pistachio holdings) and Fresno (Westlands Water District). In the worst years, farmers in SGMA-restricted basins have had to fallow 20-30% of acres. Gladstone's water assets help on the margin but don't solve the issue at portfolio scale.

Additionally, the January 2025 Southern California wildfires, February 2024 "1000-year" storm flooding, January 2023 Central Valley floods — these are not one-offs. Climate volatility is a structural risk for permanent-crop farmland in California, and insurance is partial at best.

### 3. Tenant concentration and credit (HIGH, RISING)

Tenant A: 10.7% of 2025 revenue, 9 farms, lease expires 2027. This is a cliff risk. The 2025 10-K does not disclose Tenant A's identity or financial condition, but the fact that management singled it out in the concentration disclosure (per 2025 10-K p.2369) suggests real concern.

More broadly, 17 of 144 farms (11.8%) are in some state of distress (vacant/direct-operated/non-accrual). That number was 16/150 last year — so it's growing despite the portfolio shrinking. Tenant credit deterioration is accelerating.

### 4. External management conflicts (MEDIUM-HIGH, STRUCTURAL)

Addressed in Part VII. The Adviser is paid on gross assets. It has 3 other affiliated public vehicles competing for David Gladstone's time. The incentive fee formula generates fees in bad years and requires voluntary waivers to avoid embarrassment. Dealer-manager fees paid to affiliated broker-dealer on the Series C offering (where LANDP came from) were ~9% of gross proceeds — this is a structure designed to extract capital from retail and move it to the manager complex.

**For a preferred holder specifically,** the risk is that the Adviser will continue to push gross assets higher even when accretion is negative, because the Adviser gets paid on gross. Observationally they have NOT been doing this (no new acquisitions in 2024-2025), but that is because market conditions make it impossible, not because of alignment.

### 5. Interest rate / reinvestment risk on call (MEDIUM, ASYMMETRIC)

LANDP is callable. If rates fall another 100 bps and Gladstone's credit stabilizes, they will call LANDP at $25 and refinance at ~5%. You get par + accrued and have to reinvest at lower rates. **You do not participate in the credit improvement.** This is the cap side of the asymmetry.

### 6. Liquidity / marketability (MEDIUM)

LANDP is listed on Nasdaq Global Market but is a thin issue (9.95M shares × ~$20 = ~$200M float). Daily volume is modest. In a stress event, bid/ask spreads widen dramatically. Forced selling in 2020 (COVID), 2022 (rate shock), and 2023 (regional bank crisis) each produced temporary 10-15% dislocations in similar REIT preferreds. **You cannot exit LANDP in size without moving the market.**

### 7. Mandatory redemption risk (LOW — no mandatory, but...)

LANDP has no mandatory redemption. The optional call is live since June 2024. Management is not calling. Base case is they continue not to call. But if they issued a new lower-coupon preferred (say, Series F) in 2027 at 5.5%, they could use the proceeds to call LANDP. This is a risk but not an imminent one given the current operating environment.

### 8. REIT status loss (LOW, TAIL)

LAND must distribute 90% of taxable income to maintain REIT status. If a major tenant default or impairment cycle pushes taxable income to zero, they can skip common distributions without losing REIT status. But if they skip preferred distributions, they create arrears which must be cured before any common can resume. Preferred is protected by the cumulative feature.

### 9. Key person / David Gladstone (LOW, LONG-TERM)

David Gladstone is ~84 years old. He personally founded, owns, and runs the Adviser. Succession is not disclosed in the 10-K in any detail. A forced management transition could cause disruption at all four Gladstone vehicles simultaneously. This is a 5-10 year risk that's worth monitoring.

## PART IX · VALUATION

### 9.1 — Discounted coupon approach (perpetual bond math)

LANDP is a 6.00% perpetual cumulative preferred. Under a pure bond lens, fair value = annual coupon / required yield.

**Required yield = risk-free rate + credit spread + liquidity premium + structural premium + concentration penalty**

Building it up:
- 10-year US Treasury (current, Feb 2026): ~4.0% (source: 2025 10-K p.822)
- Credit spread for unrated small REIT preferred: 250-325 bps
- Liquidity premium for thin Nasdaq issue: 25-50 bps
- External management structural penalty: 25-50 bps
- California concentration penalty: 25-50 bps
- Callable-at-par (short call) penalty: 0 bps (affects cap, not yield; you price to YTC not YTM)
- Total required yield: **400 bps over 10yr = ~7.00% minimum; 475 bps = 7.75% on stress case**

At 7.00% required yield: fair value = $1.50 / 0.0700 = **$21.43**
At 7.25%: $1.50 / 0.0725 = **$20.69**
At 7.50%: $1.50 / 0.0750 = **$20.00**
At 7.75%: $1.50 / 0.0775 = **$19.35**
At 8.00%: $1.50 / 0.0800 = **$18.75**

**My central fair value: ~$20.00 per share, implying current yield of 7.50% and ~20% discount to par.** In a severe scenario (tenant A fails, coverage falls below 1x), fair value drops to $17-18. In a benign scenario (participation rents hit in 2026, common dividend sustainable), fair value rises to $22-23.

1. "This is a dying REIT that's slowly liquidating — why own the preferred?"
2. "External management is stealing from shareholders via the 0.60% fee on gross assets."

### What I think the bears miss

2. **External management fees are ~$11M on $1.24B of assets — not insignificant but not existential.** The business can absorb this level of drag.

This is a minor point but worth knowing.

- External management structure does not align with preferred holders

**Do consider LANDP below $20** if you:
- Need monthly income
- Are in a US taxable account where the 28.9% return-of-capital treatment matters
- Understand you're taking concentrated California farmland risk
- Can hold for 3+ years and withstand 15-25% mark-to-market drawdowns

### The hard truth

**LANDP is a security that shouldn't exist in its current form.** A retail-sold, externally-managed, perpetual 6% preferred with a 9% underwriting cost — these are the structural hallmarks of a product designed to extract capital from retail investors and move it to the manager. 1. **The incentive fee waiver in 2025 ($2.7M) is a tell.** The fact that the Adviser voluntarily waived the full incentive fee in Q4 2025 indicates management knows the optics of collecting an incentive fee during a coverage-stressed year would be unsalvageable. But the fee was TRIGGERED by the formula, which means the formula doesn't actually measure what it claims to measure. This is a structural flaw in the advisory agreement and should eventually be renegotiated. If it's not, expect more waivers or preferred holder lawsuits.

2. **The $9.58 common ATM issuance in 2025 is effectively preferred dilution prevention.** Management raised $50M in common equity at distressed prices specifically because they needed cash to (a) redeem Series D and (b) service preferred dividends. The common holders took the dilution hit so preferred holders could stay whole. **This is good for LANDP in the short term but signals the limits of common holder patience.** When common holders revolt and refuse to subscribe to future ATMs, preferred coverage will be the next thing to break.

3. **The 4.7-year weighted average lease term is short for a triple-net REIT.** Industrial REITs run 6-8 years. Net-lease retail REITs run 10-12 years. Ag leases are structurally shorter because of crop cycles, but 4.7 years means 21% of rents need to be renewed every year. In a tenant credit stress environment, that's a lot of rollover risk.

4. **Management repurchased LANDP at an average of $20.83 in 2024 but did ZERO buybacks in 2025.** This is because they used their liquidity for the Series D redemption and common dividend. **It tells you management would repurchase more LANDP if they had cash — which means they view sub-$21 prices as attractive.** That is actually a mildly bullish data point for a $20 target.

5. **The direct-farming pivot is strategically significant.** Gladstone chose to operate 2,409 acres themselves in Kern County in 2025 rather than lease them out. This signals that (a) they couldn't find creditworthy tenants at acceptable rents, (b) they believe in crop economics enough to take operating risk, (c) they are willing to change the REIT's operating model mid-stream. Operating farms is fundamentally different from owning real estate — it introduces weather, labor, input cost, and crop price risk directly onto the balance sheet. **This is a meaningful change in the risk profile of the business** that the preferred holder is unwittingly absorbing without compensation.

6. **The participation rent shift is economically equivalent to a convertible security being sold by the REIT to the tenant.** When Gladstone reduces fixed base rent and takes participation in gross crop revenues, they're essentially selling tenants a call option on fixed rent in exchange for an equity-like share of upside. **This should come with a higher discount rate applied to lease revenue** — the revenue is now riskier. The 2026 revenue stream has meaningfully higher volatility than 2024's, even if the 2026 number ends up higher. The preferred holder's coverage should be stressed for this volatility.

### Three things a credit analyst would stress-test

1. **What happens if 2026 cash from operations stays at $7M?** Preferred coverage on cash basis is 0.29x. Either they sell more farms (eroding long-term earnings power) or they issue more common stock at sub-$10 prices (dilution). Neither is sustainable for 3+ years.

2. **What happens if MetLife won't extend the undrawn commitment past Dec 31, 2026?** The 10-K notes that the $175M in term notes have a draw deadline. If conditions tighten and MetLife declines to extend, liquidity could drop to $25-30M in 2027 with ongoing preferred obligations of $24M/year. This is a solvency question, not a coverage question.

3. **What happens if a major California water/drought event forces 25% acre fallowing for one year?** Lease revenue from fixed-rent farms with drought-protection clauses would be preserved, but participation rent farms would see near-zero variable payments. I'd estimate -$15M in revenue impact. Preferred coverage goes from ~1x to 0.5x on an AFFO basis. Common dividend must be cut. LANDP trades to $16-17.

### The forecast that matters

**My base-case 2026 forecast for LAND (as analyst, not consensus):**
- Lease revenue: $74M (down 2.8% — continued deterioration)
- Crop sales: $10M (partial offset)
- Total revenue: $84M
- Op expenses: $60M
- Interest: $19M
- Preferred dividends paid (ex-Series D): $24M
- **AFFO: ~$13M** (flat to 2025)
- **AFFO per common share: ~$0.34** (down again)
- **Common distribution: probably cut to $0.36-0.42** (a 25-35% cut) in H2 2026
- **LANDP price reaction: -10 to -15% over 4 weeks, then recovery**
- **LANDP trading range 2026: $17-22**

This is not catastrophic — it's ordinary pain. The preferred cumulative feature provides a floor. But it is worse than buy-side consensus, and it is worse than management's guidance.

### A.3 — Glossary (for completeness)

- **AFFO:** Adjusted Funds From Operations — non-GAAP measure of cash available to equity after capex and normalizing adjustments
- **CFFO:** Core Funds From Operations — excludes one-time items
- **Cumulative:** Preferred dividends that accrue if not paid; must be fully cured before any junior payment
- **Perpetual:** No stated maturity; only redeemable via issuer call or liquidation
- **Pari passu:** Equal ranking in liquidation
- **SGMA:** California Sustainable Groundwater Management Act (2014) — phased pumping restrictions
- **Triple-net (NNN):** Tenant pays rent + taxes + insurance + maintenance; landlord gets pure rent
- **UPREIT:** Umbrella Partnership REIT structure; allows sellers to contribute farms for OP Units tax-deferred
- **Participation rent:** Variable rent based on gross crop revenues; shares upside and downside with landlord
