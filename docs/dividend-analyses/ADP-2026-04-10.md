## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

ADP is the highest-quality compounder in the portfolio and it is not close. The company has compounded revenue at ~6.5% per year, net income at ~11%, and free cash flow at ~12% across the last decade; it has expanded operating margin by ~700 basis points; shrunk the share count by ~11%; raised the dividend for **50 consecutive years** (crossing the Dividend King threshold per the Q3 FY25 10-Q); carries more cash than debt; and does all of this with capital intensity of less than 1% of revenue (ADP FY2025 10-K ITEM 7; Q3 FY25 10-Q commentary).

The reason ADP trades at $203.61 today versus the low-$300s high last year is a mix of three things, in descending order: (1) **the market is pricing in a rate-cut drag on client funds interest revenue** — the most important swing factor for ADP's near-term EBIT and the piece retail investors misread; (2) **multiple compression on all 20x+ quality compounders** as money rotates to cyclicals and small-caps; (3) **cloud-native HCM fear** (Rippling, Gusto, Paycom, Workday). Of those, only #1 is quantifiable — and it is not large. The 10-K ITEM 7A discloses ~$24M of EBT per 25bps parallel curve shift, which on a $5.3B EBIT base is less than 50bps of margin exposure per 25bps. The other two are narrative risks that will prove right or wrong based on FY2027–2028 bookings and retention — both currently at record highs (FY25 ES retention 92.1% per the Executive Overview).

The most underappreciated positive is Maria Black's execution. Two years into her CEO tenure (she succeeded Carlos Rodriguez in January 2023), she has launched ADP Lyric HCM (new global enterprise platform, September 2024), completed the WorkForce Software acquisition ($1.16B net cash disbursement per FY25 cashflow), acquired PEI in Mexico, deployed ADP Assist across all three strategic platforms, and delivered three consecutive "trifecta" quarters of revenue + margin + EPS growth above the long-term algorithm. Q2 FY26 hit 6% organic constant currency revenue, 80 bps EBIT margin expansion, and 11% adjusted diluted EPS growth to $2.62 (ADP Q2 FY26 10-Q Executive Overview). This is the cleanest execution environment in the FY2016–FY2026 arc.

ADP deserves a quality premium. At $203.61, the stock trades at ~20x trailing GAAP EPS, ~19.5x FY26 consensus (~$10.45), and a ~5.8% FCF yield against $4.77B FY25 FCF on an ~$82.5B market cap. The 10-year P/E range has been 17–28x with a median ~23x, so the stock sits at the low end of its modern multiple range despite fundamentals better than the median year. Sell-side median target $272 (+34%) looks reasonable. FMP's DCF of $374.88 is mechanical and too aggressive (it extrapolates the rate tailwind) but even haircut to $280–$310 the risk/reward is skewed meaningfully to the upside.

**Verdict.** HOLD the full position. ACCUMULATE on weakness below ~$195. This is a position you size up on drawdowns, not one you trade around.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. Recurring-revenue compounder with 92.1% revenue retention, ~13-year ES client life, $10.6B of undrawn committed credit, and a net cash balance sheet — one of maybe a dozen public companies that cannot plausibly cut the dividend in the next 10 years.
2. Structural operating leverage — margin expanded 700bps (19.3% → 26.3%) over a decade and is still expanding (+80bps most recent quarter). Platform consolidation is ongoing.
3. Capital return discipline — FY25 returned $3.7B ($2.4B dividends + $1.3B buybacks at $289 avg, 42% above today's $203).

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add (above ~$200):**
1. The rate-cut cycle is a real headwind. 100bps of blended yield compression = ~$376M of revenue at ~100% contribution margin, ~5% of EBIT. Consensus models this but not every investor does.
2. Multiple compression risk — 20x is below the 10y median of ~23x, but quality compounders can go to 17x in a risk-off cycle. ADP could trade to $175 on unchanged fundamentals.
3. Cloud-native disruption risk below 50,000 employees — Paycom, Rippling, Gusto are real threats in the sub-1,000 employee segment.

**12-month catalysts:** Fed rate path (25bps = $24M EBT per the 10-K); Q3/Q4 FY26 bookings re-acceleration above +3%; ADP Lyric HCM enterprise logo wins against Workday; Maria Black's first Investor Day under her own banner (likely fall 2026).

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — 10 YEAR ARC

ADP's last decade splits cleanly into four phases. Understanding the phase framework is the difference between reading this stock as a boring old dividend compounder versus understanding why the margin structure keeps inflecting upward.

### Phase 1: Carlos Rodriguez Transformation Era (FY2016–FY2022)

Rodriguez ran what ADP called the "transformation initiative" — consolidating data centers, migrating legacy clients onto cloud platforms, outsourcing service work, and most importantly **spinning off the CDK Global dealer-services business in September 2014**, which refocused ADP on HCM pure-play.

The defining moment of the era was the **Pershing Square activist campaign in 2017** — Bill Ackman argued that ADP's margins were hundreds of basis points below where they should be, citing Paychex as a more efficient comparable. ADP won the proxy fight, but in hindsight **Pershing's thesis was directionally correct**: EBIT margin went from 19.3% in FY16 to 25.4% in FY24 — precisely the ~600bps expansion Pershing projected. The difference is ADP delivered it on its own timeline without Pershing's aggressive cut-first playbook.

- **FY2016:** Revenue $11.67B, op margin 19.3%, dividend $0.90B, 456M shares.
- **FY2019:** Revenue $14.18B, margin ~22%, adjusted diluted EPS $5.45 (+20%). (FY2019 10-K confirmed revenue figure.)
- **FY2020 (COVID):** Revenue $14.59B — barely dented by the pandemic because of the recurring per-employee-per-month model. Pays per control went to -1.2% but rebounded within 12 months. EPS fell only ~6% peak to trough. Dividend was raised anyway.
- **FY2021–FY2022:** Recovery to $15.0B → $16.5B, margin back above 23%. Rodriguez's final years laid the groundwork for the NextGen platforms.

### Phase 2: Maria Black Handoff (FY2023)

Black was named president in April 2022 and CEO in January 2023. She brought a sales/international operator background (PEO → national accounts → international → CRO → president) and a willingness to replace platforms rather than patch them. Where Rodriguez was a finance operator squeezing legacy platforms, Black launched **Workforce Now Next Generation** (shipped FY24) and **ADP Lyric HCM** (shipped September 2024) — ground-up rewrites.

FY23 numbers were strong: revenue $18.0B, adjusted diluted EPS $8.21. The dividend was raised 20% in November 2022 — the largest raise in a decade, management telegraphing confidence in the new CEO.

### Phase 3: Rate Tailwind + AI Investment (FY2024–FY2025)

Two forces compounded. First, the Fed hiking cycle became a tailwind: client funds float income went from ~$550M in FY22 to **$1,024.7M FY24 and $1,189.1M FY25** as the average yield rose from ~1.0% to 3.2% and the average balance grew ~6% per year. That ~$640M of incremental near-100%-contribution-margin revenue is ~3% of total revenue and arguably 5%+ of marginal EBIT. Second, Black reinvested the rate-cycle windfall into product — launching ADP Assist (the GenAI cross-platform agent), Workforce Now NextGen, and Lyric HCM. Most management teams would have dropped the savings to the bottom line. Black spent it on the future.

**Key FY25 numbers (audited, FY2025 10-K ITEM 7):** Revenue $20,560.9M (+7% organic); EBIT $5,310.1M (25.8% margin, +50bps); adjusted EBIT margin 26.0% (+50bps); diluted EPS $9.98 (+10%); net earnings $4,079.7M; cash returned $3.7B ($2.4B dividends + $1.3B buybacks); ES bookings +3%, retention 92.1%, pays per control +1%, PEO WSEs +3%.

### Phase 4: Current — "Trifecta" Quarters (FY2026 YTD)

ADP has delivered the "trifecta" — revenue growth, margin expansion, and EPS growth above long-term algorithm — for three consecutive quarters. Q2 FY26 (Dec 2025, most recent disclosed) was particularly strong:

## PART VI · CLIENT FUNDS INTEREST & RATE SENSITIVITY

Most generalist investors misunderstand this line item. It is the single biggest source of near-term earnings volatility and the main reason the stock is at $203 instead of $280.

### How it works

ADP collects payroll funds from clients ~2 days before disbursing to employees and ~3 days before remitting payroll taxes. The aggregate average float balance was **$37.6B in FY25** (FY25 10-K ITEM 7A). ADP invests this money in investment-grade corporate bonds, agency securities, Treasuries, and money market funds with maturities up to 10 years. Interest earned is recorded as "Interest on funds held for clients" — operating revenue, not other income.

Key facts from the FY25 10-K ITEM 7A: portfolio laddered to 5 years (extended) and 10 years (long); strategy designed to "average our way through an interest rate cycle"; minimum BBB for corporates; FY25 yield 3.2% (up from 2.9% FY24 and ~1.1% FY22); net unrealized pre-tax losses on AFS improved from ($1,515.8M) to ($425.7M) YoY as the curve came down.

### Rate sensitivity (the quantified view)

From the FY25 10-K ITEM 7A market risk section, the disclosed parallel-shift sensitivity is approximately **$24M of EBT impact per 25bps shift** across the full portfolio over 12 months (and ~$8M per 25bps on the short-term portion only). That is roughly $0.05 of GAAP EPS per 25bps — about 0.5% of annual EPS.

**Fed cycle scenarios:**

| Scenario | Cumulative cuts | EBT impact | % of FY26 EBT | EPS impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mild easing | 75 bps | ($72M) | ~1.3% | ($0.14) |
| Base consensus | 150 bps | ($144M) | ~2.6% | ($0.28) |
| Aggressive | 250 bps | ($240M) | ~4.3% | ($0.47) |
| Extreme | 400 bps | ($384M) | ~7.0% | ($0.76) |

The 10-K disclosure measures instantaneous parallel shifts, so the actual multi-quarter impact is **smaller** because only ~20–30% of the book re-prices per year. Full impact shows up over 2–3 years.

### Why the market is overreacting

Consensus FY27 EPS is ~$11.00–$11.30, already embedding 100–150bps of Fed cuts. If the Fed cuts less than consensus, ADP beats. If more, ADP misses by maybe $0.30–$0.50 against an $11 base — a 3–5% miss. The stock is priced for a 10–15% miss. Asymmetry.

Three offsets that bear models underweight:
1. **Balance growth** — +6.5% YoY. Every 1% balance growth offsets ~4bps of yield compression.
2. **Pricing** — 2–3% per year, pure margin flow-through. Over a 3-year cycle ~$1.6B of cumulative recurring revenue.
3. **Interest expense declines in parallel** — $4.8B of commercial paper at the FY25 peak. A 150bps Fed cut saves ~$50M of CP interest.

**Bottom line: the rate cut drag is real but bounded at ~$100–$250M of EBT over a normal cycle against $5.3B of FY25 EBT.** It is a headwind. It is not existential.

## PART VII · COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

ADP's moat comes from **scale**, **switching costs**, and **compliance infrastructure**. The question is whether those three moats are being eroded by cloud-native entrants.

### The competitive set, ranked by threat level

**Tier 1 — Real strategic threat:**
- **Workday (WDAY)** — Enterprise HCM for Fortune 1000. Owns the largest deals. ADP's Lyric HCM is explicitly positioned to compete here; before Lyric, ADP had no credible answer above ~10,000 employees.
- **Paychex (PAYX)** — Direct competitor in small/mid-market. Similar margin profile (~36–37% EBIT). Effectively a duopoly with ADP in US small business payroll.

**Tier 2 — Cloud natives gaining share in small/mid-market:**
- **Paycom (PAYC)** — focuses on 500–10,000 employees. Growing at 10–15% but decelerating. Biggest incremental competitor in the sub-5,000 segment.
- **Paylocity (PCTY)** — mid-market focused. Similar profile to Paycom.
- **Gusto** (private) — SMB focused, strong UX. Major threat to RUN in the 1–50 employee segment.
- **Rippling** (private) — Started as unified IT/HRIS/payroll, now pursuing global enterprise. Raised $1.4B+ at ~$13B valuation. The name ADP's strategy team worries most about.

**Tier 3 — Adjacent:** BambooHR, SAP SuccessFactors, Oracle HCM Cloud, TriNet (PEO competitor).

### Market share evidence

From the FY25 10-K ITEM 1: ADP pays **1 in every 6 US workers** (26M+ of 167M employed); 940,000 small business clients on RUN; 90,000 mid-market/large clients on Workforce Now; 140,000+ employers on Workforce Management; 190,000+ on Retirement Services; 260,000+ on Insurance Services; PEO has 18,000+ clients and 750,000+ worksite employees (largest IRS-Certified PEO).

For context: Paychex ~745,000 clients, Paycom ~37,000, Rippling ~40,000+. ADP is ~25x the size of either Paycom or Rippling in client count. The question is whether that scale is a moat or a liability when cloud-native tools can be delivered faster and cheaper.

### ADP's defense: three moves

1. **Platform consolidation** — migrating older Autopay/Enterprise clients onto Workforce Now NextGen and Lyric. Cost-save + retention tool.
2. **AI integration (ADP Assist)** — betting that the data advantage (42M workers, 140+ countries, decades of compliance data) lets ADP train AI models proprietary competitors cannot match. My view: matters less than ADP thinks in the short term (SMBs buy on UX), more in the long run.
3. **WorkForce Software acquisition (Oct 2024)** — closes the workforce management gap that let Workday beat ADP in large-enterprise RFPs. The single most important M&A move of Black's tenure.

### Switching costs (the most underrated moat)

The FY2024 10-K ITEM 1 states ES client retention is ~13 years, PEO ~6 years. Thirteen years. At 92.1% revenue retention, implied annual churn is ~7.9% and average client lifetime is ~12.6 years — consistent with the 13-year disclosure. Switching costs are real because payroll is mission-critical, compliance errors create legal exposure, data migration is expensive, and paycards/benefits integrations are sticky.

**Verdict on moat trajectory: STABLE, possibly widening.** The +3% bookings number is concerning but 92.1% retention is a record high. If clients were defecting to Rippling/Paycom en masse, retention would be declining. It's not.

### Risk #1: Rate cut cycle drag on float income (MEDIUM)

Covered in Part VI. Math: $24M EBT per 25bps parallel shift, partially offset by balance growth (+6.5% YoY), pricing (+2–3%), and interest expense reduction. Over a 150bps Fed cycle the cumulative drag is ~$144M EBT, ~2.6% of FY26 base. Meaningful but not existential. The 5-year risk I am most worried about. Rippling, Gusto, and Paycom offer legitimately better UX than ADP's older platforms in certain segments. Evidence for the bear case: ES new business bookings decelerated from +10% FY23 to +6% FY24 to +3% FY25. Evidence for the bull case: retention at 92.1% is a record high — if share loss were rampant, retention would decline. Mitigants: Lyric HCM, Workforce Now NextGen, RUN NextGen, WorkForce Software acquisition — the playbook is right. The open question is execution.

**This is the single metric to monitor quarterly.**

### Risk #3: Valuation multiple compression (MEDIUM)

At 20x trailing, ADP is already below its 5-year median ~24x. But quality compounders can go to 16–17x in a risk-off cycle. 20x → 17x on unchanged EPS = ~15% drawdown to ~$173. This is pure sentiment risk with no fundamental trigger. Mitigant: at 3.0% yield + 9%+ DGR + 2% buyback = ~14% expected total return even at flat multiple. That is the floor.

### Risk #4: Pays per control deceleration (MEDIUM)

FY25 +1%, Q2 FY26 +1%. If this rolls over to 0 or negative in H2 FY26 it would signal the labor market is actually softening. Every 1% of pays per control = roughly $60–80M ES revenue impact. Mitigant: In FY20 (COVID) this went to -1.2% and FCF still exceeded $2.4B.

### Risk #5: Cybersecurity breach (LOW PROBABILITY, VERY HIGH MAGNITUDE)

ADP holds payroll data for 42M workers and moves ~$3.3T of client funds annually. A breach could trigger direct losses, client defection, GDPR fines (4% of global revenue = ~$800M), and reputational damage. Mitigant: NIST-aligned cyber program, OCC-supervised ADP Trust Bank, and the FY25 10-K ITEM 1C discloses no material cybersecurity incidents identified to date. This is accepted risk of the business.

### Risks #6–#10

Regulatory burden (GDPR, EU AI Act, state privacy), credit rating (currently AA-), ADP Indemnity workers' comp liability, activist return, and AI adoption speed. None are individually material per the risk matrix above.

## PART XI · VARIANT PERCEPTION

ADP is the most heavily covered name in HCM. Sell-side consensus is generally correct on the setup. My variant view is not about direction (consensus is right that ADP is a hold/buy around $200) but about the **magnitude of the Lyric HCM platform optionality** that is not currently in the model.

### The Lyric HCM thesis (AI platform transition)

Most sell-side models treat Lyric as "another NextGen platform, eventually migrates existing customers, marginal value creation." The cautious base case. But this misses a more interesting possibility: **Lyric is ADP's first credible enterprise (>10,000 employee) offering since the old Enterprise platform was discontinued, and it is launching into a window where Workday is approaching $100B of market cap and is arguably priced for perfection.**

From the FY2025 10-K ITEM 1, management describes Lyric as a unified global HCM solution specifically targeting large enterprises needing flexibility across regions. And from the Q2 FY26 10-Q, management explicitly calls out "continued new business momentum for our Next Gen solutions, including Workforce Now Next Gen and ADP Lyric HCM." Two things in that language: this is the first time ADP has called out Lyric in the MD&A executive overview (management is comfortable claiming traction publicly), and "for large, global enterprises" is a direct shot at Workday's core market.

If Lyric wins even a handful of flagship Fortune 500 accounts over the next 12–18 months, the narrative shifts from "mature payroll company" to "AI-enabled global HCM platform taking share from an overvalued competitor." That is a multiple-expansion event, not an EPS event.

**Supporting evidence:** WorkForce Software acquisition closed the workforce management gap Lyric needed to be credible. Maria Black's international background is perfectly suited to a global-first product. R&D is flat (~$988M) but heavily concentrated on NextGen platforms. Fortune 500 Workday replacement deals are worth $15–30M ARR each at ~90% gross margin.

**Why it is not in consensus:** Lyric is still being rolled out. First reference customers are live but case studies are not broadly published. Wall Street will not give credit until there is a quantifiable bookings acceleration. That lag is the opportunity.

**Monitoring:** (1) Q3 and Q4 FY26 bookings commentary calling out "Lyric momentum" or "enterprise wins" explicitly; (2) Workday customer retention misses; (3) ES gross bookings growth above +5%.

**Variant view bottom line:** The market prices ADP as a mature rate-sensitive payroll processor. The correct framing is ADP is halfway through a platform transition that, if executed, positions it as the only global-scale HCM provider with competitive cloud architecture, 100% float-income optionality, and 1.1M clients to cross-sell into. That is a more valuable business than the market currently thinks.
