## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The market has overcorrected on Lamb Weston. The stock is down 63% from its September 2023 peak of $114.89, trading at $42.25 -- a level that implies permanent margin destruction and zero recovery in restaurant traffic. Neither is true.

Three things broke simultaneously in FY2024-FY2025 that created a perfect storm: (1) restaurant traffic in North America and Europe slowed meaningfully as consumers pulled back on eating out, pressuring volume; (2) the company was mid-cycle on a $2.5B+ multi-year capacity expansion program (Idaho, Netherlands, China, Argentina) that inflated depreciation and interest expense precisely when revenues softened; and (3) CEO Tom Werner was replaced by Mike Smith in January 2025 under pressure from activist Jana Partners, which took a board seat. The result was a FY2025 earnings collapse -- EPS fell from $6.98 (FY2023) to $5.01 (FY2024) to $2.51 (FY2025), with gross margin compressing from 26.8% to 21.7%.

But the trajectory is improving. In Q3 FY2026 (thirteen weeks ended February 22, 2026), net sales rose 2.9% year-over-year to $1,565M. Gross margin at 21.2% is still depressed, but operating cash flow for the thirty-nine-week period hit $596M vs $485M prior year -- a 23% improvement. Capex is plummeting: $257M YTD vs $550M prior year. Free cash flow has swung from negative ($131M) in FY2024 to positive $230M in FY2025, and the Q3 FY2026 run-rate points to $400M+ for the full fiscal year.

The company's Cost Savings Program and restructuring (announced July 2025 and October 2024, respectively) have already generated $102M in charges through Q3 FY2026, with another $20M expected in Q4 FY2026 and $25-50M in FY2027. These are real actions: permanent closure of production facilities in the International segment to improve asset utilization, headcount reductions, and potato contract terminations. When the restructuring is complete by mid-FY2027, annual run-rate savings should be $85-100M.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **Global frozen potato monopoly.** LW is the #1 supplier of frozen potato products in North America and a leading supplier internationally, operating 26 production facilities across 8 countries. McDonald's alone represents ~15% of net sales. This is an entrenched, mission-critical supply chain position that competitors cannot easily replicate.
2. **Capex cliff is here.** Peak capex was $930M in FY2024. FY2025 was $638M. The Q3 FY2026 run-rate suggests ~$340M annualized. Every dollar of capex reduction flows directly to FCF and dividend coverage.
3. **Valuation is historically cheap.** At $42.25 with FMP DCF of $68.79, the stock trades at a 39% discount to intrinsic value. Consensus estimates call for $3.30 EPS by FY2028, implying 12.8x forward P/E. The 5-year average was north of 20x.

**Top 3 risks:**
1. **Restaurant traffic is not recovering.** If GLP-1 adoption and consumer austerity permanently reduce french fry consumption by even 3-5%, the capacity built over 2022-2025 becomes a stranded asset problem. Volume declined in FY2025 and the company's own 10-K acknowledges inventory levels exceeded typical ranges for two consecutive years.
2. **Leverage is elevated.** Total debt of $4.16B against equity of $1.74B gives a debt-to-equity ratio of 2.4x. Net debt/EBITDA is approximately 4.5-5.0x on trailing numbers. The revolving credit facility rate dropped from 5.94% to 3.83%, which helps, but the balance sheet needs deleveraging.
3. **Margin recovery is not guaranteed.** Gross margin at 21.7% (FY2025) vs 26.8% (FY2023) reflects not just cyclical softness but structural input cost inflation in potatoes, edible oils, and energy. The company faces $500M in environmental compliance spending over the next six years. Cost absorption on new capacity requires volume that has not materialized.

**Next 12 months catalysts (high-probability):**
- **Q4 FY2026 earnings (July 2026):** Historically the strongest quarter for sales and cash flow. Consensus expects $0.55-0.60 EPS. A beat here confirms the trough.
- **FY2027 guidance (July 2026):** First full-year guide under Mike Smith without restructuring noise. The market needs to see a $2.50+ EPS target to re-engage.
- **Cost Savings Program exit rate (mid-FY2027):** Annual savings target of $85-100M should be fully in the run rate. Watch the Q2 FY2027 call for confirmation.
- **September 2026 dividend declaration:** Any increase -- even $0.01 -- signals management's conviction. A flat declaration would not be alarming but would delay re-rating.
- **Tariff developments:** LW has production in China, Argentina, Netherlands. Any tariff escalation on agricultural products or cross-border trade could impact either positively (protecting US operations) or negatively (harming international margins).

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Lamb Weston Holdings, Inc. is the world's largest producer, distributor, and marketer of value-added frozen potato products, headquartered in Eagle, Idaho. The company was organized as a Delaware corporation in July 2016 and spun off from ConAgra Brands, with common stock trading under "LW" on the NYSE since November 2016.

The business is elegantly simple: take raw potatoes, process them into french fries, hash browns, wedges, tater tots, and other frozen potato products, and sell them to restaurants, foodservice distributors, and retailers in over 100 countries. French fries represent the majority of the product portfolio.

**Segments (FY2025):**

| Segment | Net Sales | % of Total | Key Markets |
|---|---|---|---|
| North America | $4,265M | 66% | US, Canada, Mexico -- QSR, foodservice, retail |
| International | $2,186M | 34% | Europe, Asia-Pacific, Latin America, Middle East |
| **Total** | **$6,451M** | **100%** | |

**Key relationships:**
- McDonald's Corporation: ~15% of consolidated net sales (FY2025, FY2024, FY2023) -- the single largest customer
- Top 10 customers: ~50% of net sales
- No other customer exceeds 10%
- 50% ownership in Lamb Weston/RDO Frozen joint venture (single U.S. processing facility)
- 75% interest in an Austrian joint venture (consolidated)

**Manufacturing footprint:** 26 production facilities across 8 countries, with primary potato sourcing from Washington, Idaho, and Oregon in the U.S., and the Netherlands, Austria, Belgium, Germany, France, and the UK in Europe. The company also sources from Canada, China, Australia, and Argentina.

**Competitive landscape:** Highly competitive industry with global players including McCain Foods (Canada, private -- largest global competitor), J.R. Simplot Company (U.S., private), Cavendish Farms, Farm Frites, Agristo, Aviko, Clarebout, and The Kraft Heinz Company. Some local competitors in emerging markets (Saudi Arabia, India) are vertically integrated with lower cost structures.

**Seasonality matters:** LW's fiscal year ends the last Sunday in May. Potatoes are harvested in the Pacific Northwest in July-October (fiscal Q2). Freshly harvested potatoes process more efficiently and avoid storage costs, so fiscal Q3 (November-February) typically has the highest segment adjusted EBITDA margins. Fiscal Q4 (March-May) has the highest net sales and cash flows due to seasonal restaurant traffic patterns. This seasonality explains why the Q3 FY2026 EPS of $0.39 is the lowest of the fiscal year despite being the quarter with the best input cost dynamics.

**The McDonald's dependency:** McDonald's at 15% of net sales is both a strength and a vulnerability. On the strength side, McDonald's is a creditworthy, long-term customer with whom LW has a multi-decade relationship. The fry specification requirements (cut size, coating, freezing methodology) create genuine switching costs. On the vulnerability side, McDonald's has faced its own traffic challenges globally, with same-store sales under pressure in North America and Europe. If McDonald's implements significant cost-cutting on its fry specifications or shifts volume to competitors (most likely McCain Foods), it would be a material headwind for LW.

## PART VI -- COMPETITIVE POSITION AND MOAT

Lamb Weston's moat is narrower than the market thought in 2023 but wider than it thinks today. The business has three durable competitive advantages:

**1. Processing capacity scale.** Building a frozen potato processing plant costs $300-500M and takes 2-3 years. LW operates 26 plants across 8 countries. The fixed-cost leverage on this asset base means that competitors cannot profitably match LW's price-quality combination at scale. The recent capacity expansion in Idaho, Netherlands, China, and Argentina extended this advantage.

**2. Customer lock-in through specifications.** McDonald's, Wendy's, and other major QSR chains do not simply buy "french fries." They buy fries manufactured to exact specifications: cut size, coating formulation, freezing method, and oil content. Switching suppliers requires extensive product qualification, customer testing, and menu reformulation. This creates meaningful switching costs at the product level.

**3. Raw material sourcing network.** LW has multi-decade relationships with potato growers in the Pacific Northwest (Washington, Idaho, Oregon), Europe, and other regions. Potato quality varies dramatically by growing region and harvest year, and LW's grower network provides consistent access to high-quality raw material. This is a genuine competitive advantage in a business where input quality directly determines processing efficiency and product quality.

**Where the moat is narrower than perceived:** International markets are fragmenting. Local competitors in Saudi Arabia, India, and other emerging markets are adding capacity and competing on price. LW's International segment gross margin has been under pressure, and the recent facility closures in the International segment suggest management recognizes that some of this capacity was built ahead of demand.

### The GLP-1 Question -- An Honest Assessment

The market's biggest structural concern about LW is the potential impact of GLP-1 receptor agonist drugs (Ozempic, Mounjaro, Wegovy, Zepbound) on food consumption. The thesis goes: if 10-15% of the U.S. adult population is on GLP-1 drugs by 2030, and these drugs reduce food intake by 20-30%, then total food demand declines 2-4%, with calorie-dense categories like french fries hit disproportionately.

My view: this is a real but overstated risk. Here is why.

First, the adoption curve. Even at maximum bullish projections, GLP-1 penetration in the U.S. is currently 3-5% of adults. At $1,000+/month out-of-pocket (or $300+/month with insurance), broad adoption is supply-and cost-constrained. By 2028-2030, penetration might reach 8-12%, but the demand impact on restaurant visits is much smaller than the headline drug efficacy numbers suggest because (a) people on GLP-1s still eat, just less; (b) restaurant visits are driven by convenience and social occasions, not just hunger; (c) fries are often a side dish, not the caloric center of the meal.

Second, international diversification. GLP-1 adoption outside the U.S. is negligible in the developing markets (China, Latin America, Middle East) where LW has invested heavily. Even in Europe, adoption is lower due to more restrictive healthcare systems. LW's international segment (34% of sales) provides a natural hedge.

Third, historical precedent. Every decade brings a new dietary scare for french fries -- trans fats (2000s), carb consciousness (Atkins, 2003-2005), fat taxes (2010s). Global french fry consumption has grown through all of them because the product is deeply embedded in eating culture worldwide.

I estimate the total GLP-1 impact on LW's North American volume at -1% to -3% by 2030 -- meaningful but not existential, and likely offset by international growth and pricing power.

### International Expansion -- The Untold Story

While North America gets all the attention, LW's International segment is where the long-term growth engine sits. International revenue was $2,186M in FY2025 (34% of total), up from essentially zero at the time of the 2016 spin-off. The segment expanded dramatically through the acquisition of the remaining equity interest in LW EMEA (formerly a joint venture with Meijer Frozen Foods B.V.) and new capacity investments in China and Argentina.

- Netherlands: Expanded existing EMEA operations with additional processing lines
- China: New facility to serve the rapidly growing Chinese QSR market (KFC, McDonald's China)

The International segment's gross margin has been under pressure, which is why management is closing certain international facilities as part of the restructuring. The $83M foreign currency translation gain in the first thirty-nine weeks of FY2026 (vs a $62M loss in the prior year) shows how much FX can swing results. The euro's appreciation against the dollar has been a tailwind for the EMEA business, which is reported in USD but earns in euros. Investors should not count on this continuing.

## PART XII -- ACTIONABLE CONCLUSION

### For Income Investors
Lamb Weston at 3.6% yield is not a traditional income play. The dividend has been maintained through the downturn but future raises will be modest (2-4%) until the balance sheet is repaired. If your primary goal is current income, there are better options in the packaged food space (KHC at 5.3%, CAG at 5.5%). Buy LW if you want income PLUS capital appreciation from a recovery.

### For Total Return Investors
This is a compelling total-return opportunity. The combination of a 3.6% yield, a plausible path to $55-60 in 2-3 years, and a business that is the global monopolist in its niche makes LW one of the more asymmetric setups in consumer staples today. The capex cliff is real, the restructuring is real, and the management team -- while new -- is executing on both.

### Position Sizing
Given the elevated leverage and binary nature of the restaurant traffic recovery, size this at 2-3% of a diversified dividend portfolio. Add on weakness below $40. Reduce above $60 unless earnings growth exceeds expectations.
