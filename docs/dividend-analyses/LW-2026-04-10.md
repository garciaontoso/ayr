## PART I -- Executive Summary

Lamb Weston Holdings is the world's largest producer, distributor, and marketer of value-added frozen potato products, processing raw potatoes into french fries, hash browns, wedges, and other frozen items for restaurants, foodservice distributors, and retailers in over 100 countries. The company was spun off from ConAgra Brands in November 2016 and trades on the NYSE under the ticker "LW." French fries constitute the majority of the product portfolio.

The stock has suffered a severe derating, falling roughly 63% from its September 2023 peak near $115 to approximately $39-42 today. Three forces converged to create this outcome: (1) a multi-year capacity expansion program costing over $2.5 billion that inflated depreciation and interest expense at exactly the wrong moment; (2) a pronounced softening in global restaurant traffic -- particularly in North America and Europe -- that pressured volume and forced pricing concessions; and (3) a CEO transition under activist pressure from Jana Partners, with Tom Werner replaced by company veteran Mike Smith in January 2025. The result was an earnings collapse from $6.98 diluted EPS in FY2023 to $2.51 in FY2025, with trailing twelve-month EPS through Q3 FY2026 falling further to approximately $1.75.

The dividend, currently $1.52 annualized ($0.38 per quarter), yields approximately 3.6-3.9% depending on the reference price. At a GAAP payout ratio near 87% on trailing earnings, the dividend appears stretched. But this masks the true picture: free cash flow has inflected sharply upward as capital expenditures plummet from their peak, and the annualized FCF run rate through Q3 FY2026 points to approximately $400-450 million, covering the $210 million annual dividend roughly 2.0x. The company has raised its dividend every year since becoming public except for a freeze during COVID in FY2021 -- nine consecutive years of increases including a 3% raise to $0.38 announced in December 2025. The streak is intact, but the growth rate has decelerated from double digits to low single digits, reflecting balance sheet constraints.

The investment thesis hinges on whether this is a cyclical trough or a structural inflection. The bull case sees restaurant traffic stabilizing, restructuring savings of $250 million materializing by FY2028, the capex cliff liberating FCF for deleveraging, and earnings recovering toward $3.00-3.50 by FY2028 -- implying significant upside from today's valuation. The bear case sees permanently impaired demand from GLP-1 drugs, persistent margin pressure from international overcapacity, a bloated cost structure that restructuring cannot fully fix, and leverage that limits capital allocation flexibility. The truth, as usual, lies somewhere in between.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield: ~$39-42 / 3.6-3.9%
- GAAP payout ratio (LTM): ~87%
- FCF payout ratio (annualized YTD): ~47%
- Dividend streak: 9 years of increases (frozen in FY2021)

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

Lamb Weston operates a deceptively simple business: it takes raw potatoes, primarily sourced from the Pacific Northwest of the United States and Northern Europe, processes them through capital-intensive production lines into frozen potato products, and distributes them to customers ranging from McDonald's to local restaurants to grocery retailers. The company claims the number one market position in North America and a leading position internationally.

**Sector / Industry:** Consumer Defensive -- Packaged Foods (Frozen)

**Business segments (FY2025):**

| Segment | Net Sales | % of Total | Key Markets |
|---|---|---|---|
| North America | $4,265M | 66% | US, Canada, Mexico -- QSR chains, foodservice distributors, retailers |
| International | $2,186M | 34% | Europe, Asia-Pacific, Latin America, Middle East, Africa |
| **Total** | **$6,451M** | **100%** | |

North America is the profitability engine. In the thirty-nine weeks ended February 22, 2026, North America generated Segment Adjusted EBITDA of $837.6 million versus International's $102.9 million -- a roughly 89% / 11% split. This matters enormously for dividend analysis: the international segment's problems, while real, have a limited impact on the cash flow that actually services the dividend.

**Geographic exposure:** The company operates 26 production facilities across 8 countries: the United States (12 facilities including the 50%-owned Lamb Weston RDO joint venture in Minnesota), Netherlands (4 facilities), plus one each in China (2 facilities, one in Shangdu and one in Ulanqab), Argentina, Australia, Austria (75%-owned JV), Canada, and the United Kingdom. This is a genuinely global manufacturing footprint, with over 10,000 employees as of July 2025.

**Top customers / customer concentration:**
- McDonald's Corporation: approximately 15% of consolidated net sales in FY2025, FY2024, and FY2023 -- consistently the largest customer and growing as a percentage of sales
- Top 10 customers: approximately 50% of net sales
- No other single customer exceeds 10% of consolidated net sales
- Customer concentration is moderate but the McDonald's dependency is material

**Main competitors:** McCain Foods Limited (Canada, privately held, the world's other frozen potato giant), J.R. Simplot Company (US, private), Cavendish Farms, Farm Frites International, Agristo, Aviko, Clarebout Potatoes, The Kraft Heinz Company. International markets are highly fragmented with local players in Saudi Arabia, India, and other emerging markets adding capacity.

**Recent corporate actions:**
- FY2023: Completed acquisition of remaining equity interest in LW EMEA (formerly Lamb-Weston/Meijer v.o.f.) for $610 million net of cash acquired, bringing the European joint venture fully in-house
- FY2024-FY2025: Completed major capacity expansions in Idaho, Netherlands, China (two facilities), and Argentina
- October 2024: Announced FY25 Restructuring Plan including permanent closure of Connell, Washington manufacturing facility and temporary curtailment of production lines across North America
- December 2024: Jana Partners cooperation agreement; CEO transition from Tom Werner to Mike Smith
- July 2025: Announced "Focus to Win" strategic plan and Cost Savings Program targeting at least $250 million in annualized run-rate savings by end of FY2028
- Q3 FY2026: Closed Munro, Argentina facility, consolidated into Mar del Plata; began curtailment of a Dutch production line

**Seasonality matters significantly:** LW's fiscal year ends the last Sunday in May. Potatoes are harvested in the Pacific Northwest in July-October (fiscal Q2). Freshly harvested potatoes process more efficiently and avoid storage costs, so fiscal Q3 (November-February) typically has the highest segment adjusted EBITDA margins. Fiscal Q4 (March-May) typically has the highest net sales and cash flows due to seasonal restaurant traffic patterns. This explains why Q3 FY2026 EPS of $0.39 was the lowest of the fiscal year despite favorable input cost dynamics.

**Inflection points:**

1. **FY2022 -- Margin compression begins.** Gross margin fell from 24.6% (FY2019 average) to 20.3% as potato, oil, and energy costs surged while the company was unable to fully pass through increases to customers. This was the first warning sign that LW's pricing power had limits.

2. **FY2023 -- Peak pricing power and the EMEA buyout.** Revenue surged 30.5% as LW pushed through aggressive price increases, hitting 26.8% gross margin. The company also completed the acquisition of the remaining equity interest in LW EMEA for $610 million, consolidating its European operations. This year marked peak profitability and set the stage for the subsequent earnings decline.

3. **FY2024 -- The twin peaks of capex and revenue.** Capital expenditures hit $930 million as expansion projects in Idaho, Netherlands, China, and Argentina consumed cash. Revenue reached $6.47 billion but the first cracks appeared: the ERP system transition caused approximately $95 million in losses, and a voluntary product withdrawal added another $40 million charge. More critically, an excess raw potato write-off of $95.9 million revealed that demand was softening faster than management anticipated. FCF turned negative at -$131 million.

4. **FY2025 -- The reckoning.** Revenue was essentially flat at $6.45 billion despite 2% volume growth, as price/mix declined 2% from competitive investments. Gross margin collapsed to 21.7%. The FY25 Restructuring Plan was announced in October 2024, including the Connell, WA facility closure and production curtailments. Jana Partners forced a CEO change. Restructuring charges of $185.8 million depressed reported earnings. The silver lining: operating cash flow improved to $868 million and FCF turned positive at $230 million as capex began declining. Depreciation surged 33% year-over-year to $407.5 million, reflecting the new capacity coming online.

5. **FY2026 YTD -- The turnaround takes shape.** Through three quarters (thirty-nine weeks ended February 22, 2026), volume is up 6% driven by customer wins and share gains, but price/mix is down 8% at constant currency as the company invests heavily in customer retention. FCF of $339 million already exceeds all of FY2025's $230 million. The capex cliff is real: $257 million YTD versus $550 million prior year. However, International segment profitability has cratered to $102.9 million in Segment Adjusted EBITDA versus $194.1 million prior year, dragging consolidated results.

The deceleration in dividend growth is dramatic and informative. Management's decision to raise the dividend by only 2.7% in December 2025 -- while the stock was in freefall -- was a deliberately conservative signal: "We are committed to the dividend but not going to stretch for it." ### The FCF vs. GAAP Disconnect

The most critical insight for dividend investors: the GAAP payout ratio (~87%) overstates risk while the FCF payout ratio (~47%) understates it. The true dividend safety lies somewhere in between, because:

1. **Depreciation is real but lumpy.** The $291.8 million in depreciation and amortization YTD (thirty-nine weeks) reflects the new capacity that was built at enormous cost. This depreciation will persist for 15-20 years. Maintenance capex to sustain this asset base will be significant -- management guides to approximately $500 million for full-year FY2026 investing activities including environmental spending.

2. **Restructuring charges are temporary.** The $101.5 million in restructuring and cost savings program charges YTD are depressing reported earnings but will largely cease by mid-FY2027. Remaining charges are guided at $20 million for Q4 FY2026 and $25-50 million for FY2027.

3. **Working capital is a swing factor.** Inventory remains elevated at $1,094 million as of February 22, 2026. The company's own 10-K acknowledged that inventory days on hand exceeded the typical 50-60 day range for two consecutive fiscal years. Normalization of inventory would release cash; further buildup would consume it.

**Annual dividend outlay:** Approximately $211 million (138.98 million shares x $1.52). This is a manageable amount relative to operating cash flow of $868 million (FY2025) and the annualized YTD run rate of ~$795 million. Even in a stress scenario where OCF fell 25%, the company could still fund the dividend.

## PART VI -- Business Quality and Moat

**Moat type:** Scale economies + customer switching costs + raw material sourcing network
**Moat trajectory:** Stable domestically, narrowing internationally
**ROIC trend:** Declining -- from approximately 15%+ in FY2023 to 8.9% trailing (per D1 quality scoring data)

Lamb Weston's competitive advantages are real but more nuanced than the market appreciated during the stock's 2021-2023 premium valuation period.

**1. Processing capacity at scale.** Constructing a frozen potato processing plant costs $300-500 million and requires 2-3 years of construction plus additional time for qualification and ramp-up. LW operates 26 plants across 8 countries, giving it the world's second-largest production capacity after McCain Foods. The fixed-cost leverage inherent in this asset base means that at adequate utilization rates (above 80%), LW's per-unit costs are among the lowest in the industry. The risk emerges below 80% utilization, where the massive depreciation base becomes a drag -- and this is exactly what is happening in the International segment, where the company is curtailing lines and closing facilities.

**2. Customer specification lock-in.** Major QSR chains like McDonald's, Wendy's, and Burger King do not buy generic french fries. They buy fries manufactured to exact specifications: cut dimensions, coating formulations, freezing methodology, and oil content. Qualifying a new supplier requires months of product testing, consumer acceptance validation, and supply chain integration. This creates meaningful switching costs at the product level. The Q2 FY2026 earnings call confirmed this dynamic: CEO Mike Smith emphasized that customers are "turning to Lamb Weston for the quality, innovation, and value for which we are known," and that the company was reopening curtailed North American production lines specifically to maintain "high customer fill rates."

**3. Raw material sourcing.** LW has multi-decade relationships with potato growers in Washington, Idaho, Oregon, and across Europe. Potato quality varies significantly by growing region, harvest conditions, and storage duration. LW's sourcing network provides preferential access to the highest-quality potatoes, which directly affects processing efficiency (higher-quality potatoes produce more usable product per pound of input). The flip side: when potato costs inflate, as they did in FY2024-FY2025, LW's long-term contracts can lock in above-market pricing. The $95.9 million write-off of excess raw potatoes in FY2024 and the $45.6 million International segment write-off in YTD FY2026 illustrate this risk.

**Where the moat is narrowing:** International markets are experiencing structural competitive intensity from local producers in Saudi Arabia, India, and other developing regions who are adding capacity with lower cost structures. In Europe, a strong FY2026 potato crop coincided with soft restaurant traffic and reduced export demand, creating pricing pressure. The Q2 FY2026 earnings call was blunt about this: "In Europe, a strong potato crop has coincided with softer restaurant traffic and lower export demand due to localization of recently added production in other regional markets."

**Pricing power evidence:** Mixed. In North America, the company was able to push through significant price increases in FY2023-FY2024, with revenue growing 30.5% and 20.9% respectively. But this pricing power proved temporary: FY2025 saw price/mix decline 2%, and the first thirty-nine weeks of FY2026 show price/mix down 8% at constant currency, reflecting "planned investments in price and trade support in a competitive environment." The company is giving back pricing to retain volume -- a classic indicator of narrowing moat in the medium term, though it may also reflect tactical decisions in a soft demand environment.

## PART VII -- Capital Allocation

Capital allocation is the most consequential analysis lens for LW at this juncture. The decisions made over the next two years regarding the trade-off between deleveraging, shareholder returns, and reinvestment will determine whether this turnaround succeeds.

**1. Dividends: $207 million (FY2025), ~$211 million annualized FY2026**
The dividend is the sacred cow. Management raised it through the worst earnings recession in LW's public company history. The annual outlay of approximately $210 million represents roughly 24% of FY2025 OCF and a manageable commitment. The slow growth rate (2.7% last raise) is appropriate given leverage.

**2. Share repurchases: $294 million (FY2025), $59 million YTD FY2026**
The dramatic pullback from $294 million to $59 million is the strongest signal that management is prioritizing balance sheet repair. FY2024 saw $225 million in buybacks at an average price of $91.51 -- in retrospect, terrible capital allocation. The FY2025 buybacks of $294 million at an average of $57.94 were somewhat better but still premature given the leverage. The Q3 FY2026 10-Q notes that the company did not repurchase shares during Q3 "due to trading restrictions," but implemented a Rule 10b5-1 plan post-quarter and bought $43.7 million of shares as of March 30, 2026. Approximately $264 million remains authorized under the $750 million program. Given the stock's decline to $39-42, buying back shares at current levels is significantly more accretive than the $91.51 average paid in FY2024.

**3. Capital expenditures: $638 million (FY2025), $257 million YTD FY2026**
The capex cliff is the most important financial development for LW. Peak spend was $930 million in FY2024. FY2025 declined to $638 million. The YTD FY2026 run rate of ~$340 million annualized represents a 63% reduction from peak. Management guides to approximately $500 million for full-year FY2026 including environmental spending (see below). Construction in progress dropped from $552 million at May 2025 to $266 million at February 2026, confirming that major expansion projects are winding down. Going forward, maintenance capex plus environmental compliance should be in the $350-450 million range.

**4. Environmental spending mandate: ~$100 million FY2026, ~$500 million over six years**
This is a frequently overlooked but non-discretionary obligation. The FY2025 10-K states: "We expect to spend approximately $100 million in fiscal 2026 to comply with environmental regulations, including those related to discharges into the air, land, and water. These projects largely focus on wastewater treatment at our production facilities. Further, we expect to spend about $500 million in the aggregate over the next six years." At $83 million per year on average, this is a permanent drag on free cash flow that few analysts incorporate into their models.

**5. Debt paydown: net reduction of ~$142 million YTD FY2026**
Total debt declined from $4,148 million at May 25, 2025 to $4,007 million at February 22, 2026. This is modest but directionally correct. The revolving credit facility balance declined from $333 million to $236 million. Interest rates on variable-rate debt have fallen meaningfully (revolver from 5.94% to 3.83%), providing a tailwind.

**Capital allocation grade: C+**
The FY2024 and early FY2025 capital allocation was poor -- buying back $225 million of stock at $91.51 average while levering up for capacity expansion, then buying another $294 million at $57.94 as earnings were deteriorating. The current posture is improved: management has dramatically slowed buybacks, is reducing capex, and is prioritizing debt reduction. But the damage is done -- the balance sheet is stretched and it will take 2-3 years of disciplined execution to repair.

## PART VIII -- Management Assessment

**CEO: Michael J. Smith** -- Appointed January 2025. 18-year LW veteran who rose through commercial roles (Vice President of Retail from 2011, SVP of Foodservice/Retail/Marketing/Innovation from 2018, COO from May 2023). Smith replaced Tom Werner under pressure from Jana Partners. His appointment signals continuity rather than revolution -- this is an insider who knows the business intimately. His early actions have been operationally focused: the "Focus to Win" strategy announced in July 2025 emphasizes customer partnerships, cost savings, supply chain optimization, and innovation. On the Q2 FY2026 earnings call, Smith was direct and credible, acknowledging that "turnarounds are not linear" while pointing to concrete volume gains and cost reductions.

**CFO: Bernadette M. Madarieta** -- In role since August 2021. Strong financial pedigree (VP/Controller at Packaging Corporation of America). She navigated the company through the capex supercycle and has maintained disciplined balance sheet communication. On earnings calls, Madarieta provides granular detail on cost dynamics, segment performance, and working capital trends. Her presence provides continuity and credibility.

**Board changes:** Jana Partners, in cooperation with Continental Grain Company, secured a cooperation agreement dated June 30, 2025 (filed as Exhibit 10.25 to the FY2025 10-K). This gave the activists board representation and catalyzed the CEO change. The activist presence creates governance tension but ultimately aligns with income investor interests: Jana wants margin recovery, cost discipline, and shareholder returns.

**Insider ownership and compensation:** Executive stock options outstanding at 2.08 million shares with a weighted-average exercise price of $69.72 -- deeply underwater at current stock prices. This means management's equity incentives have zero current value, which is painful for them but creates alignment: they need the stock to recover to realize any compensation from their equity awards. Smith's letter agreement from December 2024 was filed as Exhibit 10.22 to the 10-K.

**Guidance track record:** Mixed. The company guided FY2026 adjusted EBITDA at $1.0-1.2 billion at the start of the year, and as of Q2 FY2026 stated they "expect to finish closer to the midpoint." This implies approximately $1.1 billion in adjusted EBITDA, which would be down from FY2025's $1,220.5 million. The decision not to narrow the range at the midpoint of the fiscal year was viewed negatively by the market (the stock sold off after Q2 results). Historically, management under Werner was criticized for over-promising on capacity utilization and international growth. Smith is adopting a more conservative posture, which is appropriate for a turnaround situation.

**Shareholder activism expense:** The company recorded $5.2 million in shareholder activism costs in FY2025 and an additional $4.0 million in the first thirty-nine weeks of FY2026. These are one-time costs that should fade as the Jana cooperation agreement matures.

### The GLP-1 Question -- An Honest Assessment

The market's structural bear case centers on GLP-1 receptor agonist drugs (Ozempic, Mounjaro, Wegovy, Zepbound) reducing food consumption. My assessment: this is a real but overstated risk for three reasons.

First, adoption economics. At $1,000+/month out-of-pocket (or $300+ with insurance), broad adoption is supply- and cost-constrained. Current US penetration is 3-5% of adults. Even aggressive projections put it at 8-12% by 2028-2030. The impact on restaurant visits is much smaller than drug efficacy numbers suggest because people on GLP-1s still eat, restaurant visits are driven by convenience and social occasions, and fries are typically a side dish.

Second, international diversification. GLP-1 adoption outside the US is negligible in developing markets where LW has invested. International represents 34% of sales and likely 75% of industry volume growth through 2030 (per management's Q2 FY2026 call).

Third, historical precedent. Every decade brings a dietary scare for french fries -- trans fats (2000s), Atkins/low-carb (2003-2005), fat taxes (2010s). Global french fry consumption has grown through all of them.

I estimate the total GLP-1 impact on LW's North American volume at -1% to -3% by 2030 -- meaningful but not existential.

### The Antitrust Risk -- New and Material

The Q3 FY2026 10-Q discloses a class action antitrust complaint filed in November 2024 alleging that LW and other frozen potato producers "conspired to raise the price of frozen potato products above competitive levels in violation of U.S. antitrust laws by coordinating prices and imposing lockstep price increases, allegedly facilitated by the exchange of non-public information about prices and production." Additional complaints were consolidated, and defendants filed a motion to dismiss on December 5, 2025. Similar actions were filed in Canada. This is a potentially material risk: antitrust treble damages in the US can be enormous, and the discovery process could be disruptive. However, the motion to dismiss is pending and LW states it "believes these complaints lack merit."

### Stress Test Scenarios

**Margin compression (-300 bps from current 21%):** Gross margin at 18%. On $6.5B revenue, gross profit = $1.17B (down from $1.40B). Operating income drops to ~$350M. FCF approximately $300M. Dividend coverage: 1.4x. Dividend maintained but no growth.

**Refinancing at +200 bps:** The $500M 4.875% notes due May 2028 would refinance at approximately 6.9%. Additional annual interest expense: ~$10M. Manageable.

### The International Segment Problem -- A Deeper Look

The Q3 FY2026 segment data tells a stark story. North America Segment Adjusted EBITDA was $289.8 million in Q3 (thirteen weeks), down only 4% from $302.6 million prior year. International Segment Adjusted EBITDA was $18.5 million -- down 80% from $94.1 million. For the thirty-nine-week period, International's $102.9 million represents a 47% decline from $194.1 million. This collapse is driven by multiple factors operating simultaneously: (a) a net $45.6 million write-off of excess raw potatoes in the International segment; (b) start-up and ramp-up costs at the new Argentine facility, where the Munro plant was closed in Q3 and production consolidated into Mar del Plata; (c) underutilization of European production facilities amid soft demand; and (d) pricing pressure from local competitors who have added capacity in developing markets, reducing European export volumes.

The critical question for the FY2027 outlook is whether International can return to a $200 million+ annual Adjusted EBITDA level. On the Q2 FY2026 earnings call, CFO Madarieta guided that international volumes would be "flat year over year" in the second half as the company lapped prior-year customer wins, and noted that "price mix headwinds" and "higher manufacturing costs per pound" would persist. The curtailment of a Dutch production line in early Q4 FY2026 is a corrective action, but the structural challenge of European overcapacity remains.

### The Restructuring -- Quantifying the Scope

The combined Cost Savings Program and Restructuring Plan deserve close examination. Cumulative charges through Q3 FY2026 total $270.9 million ($169.4 million in FY2025 plus $101.5 million YTD FY2026), with $45-70 million remaining ($20M Q4 FY2026 + $25-50M FY2027). The total program cost of approximately $316-341 million over two-plus years represents 5% of annual revenue -- this is a massive operational correction by any measure.

The composition of charges reveals the severity: $101.7 million in asset retirements and plant charges (permanent facility closures), $58.3 million in potato contract terminations (paying to exit purchase agreements because the company no longer needs the volume), $31.0 million in inventory write-offs, $32.0 million in employee severance, and $55.3 million in professional services and other (consulting, advisory fees including Jana-related costs). Partially offsetting is a $10.6 million gain on non-core asset sales.

The $250 million annualized savings target by FY2028 (with $200 million expected by end of FY2027) implies a payback period of approximately 1.4 years on the $341 million investment -- attractive if achieved. But restructuring savings estimates frequently prove optimistic. The monitoring point is whether FY2027 margins show 130-200 basis points of improvement versus FY2026 normalized levels.
