## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: BUY below $21, ACCUMULATE at $20-22. · Dividend Safety: 7/10 · Confidence: Moderate-High**

The market is penalizing KRG twice for the same sin. The 2020 dividend cut — an 81% reduction that took the quarterly payout from $0.3175 to $0.06 — was the correct capital allocation decision during a once-in-a-century retail shutdown, and KRG was one of the few open-air peers that never fully suspended the dividend. But the scar tissue is real: institutional memory is long, and the REIT investor base demands reliability above almost all other attributes. The stock trades at a persistent 25-30% P/FFO discount to open-air peers like Regency Centers (REG, ~15x), Federal Realty (FRT, ~17x), and Brixmor (BRX, ~13x).

What the market is not adequately pricing is the structural transformation that has occurred between 2021 and 2025. The RPAI merger was not a financial engineering exercise — it doubled KRG's portfolio from ~60 properties to ~180, shifted geographic exposure decisively toward high-growth Sun Belt MSAs (Dallas, Atlanta, Raleigh-Durham, Nashville), added critical gateway market presence (Washington D.C.'s One Loudoun, Seattle), and brought the company to investment-grade scale. Pre-merger KRG was a sub-$2B enterprise value company with a middling balance sheet. Post-merger KRG is a $4.5B enterprise with BBB ratings from three agencies, a $1.1B undrawn revolver, and the GIC sovereign wealth fund as a capital partner.

The dividend today is on different footing than 2019. The payout ratio on Core FFO is approximately 52-55%, versus an estimated 65-70% pre-COVID. Net Debt/EBITDA has compressed from ~7.0x immediately post-merger to 5.0x. The company has $225M remaining under its share repurchase program and a $500M asset disposition pipeline — both of which signal that capital return capacity exceeds current commitments.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **GIC partnership validates asset quality at a discount.** Singapore's sovereign wealth fund does not form joint ventures with mediocre shopping center operators. The two-JV structure (Legacy West acquisition in Dallas and a three-property contribution) totaling ~$700M+ in assets is a third-party valuation mark that implies the portfolio is worth materially more than the stock price suggests.
2. **Grocery anchoring provides recession resilience.** KRG's top anchors — Kroger, Publix, H-E-B, TJX, Ross, Dollar Tree — are the exact tenants that gained traffic share during COVID and continue to benefit from the trade-down economy. No single tenant exceeds 2.5% of ABR.
3. **12-19% cash leasing spreads on comparable deals** are the engine of organic growth. Q3 2025 saw 167 leases executed on 1.23M sqft at a blended 12.2% cash spread (18.9% on new + non-option renewals). This is not a portfolio bleeding tenants — it is a portfolio with pricing power.

**Top 3 reasons to hesitate:**
1. **The 2020 cut is a permanent mark on the record.** A REIT that cuts 81% cannot credibly promise "never again" without at least a full economic cycle of rebuilding. We are only five years into that cycle.
2. **Midwest/Rust Belt exposure dilutes the Sun Belt narrative.** Indianapolis (HQ market), Ohio, Michigan, and Illinois properties drag the average growth profile. Management is recycling out of these, but it takes time.
3. **Rising interest rates compress the FFO multiple.** KRG's $3.0B+ debt stack is predominantly fixed-rate, but refinancing maturing tranches at higher coupons pressures the interest coverage ratio. Q3 2025 interest expense of $33.2M was up 5% YoY.

- Potential Q4 2025 or Q1 2026 dividend increase to $0.28/q ($1.12 annualized)

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Kite Realty Group Trust (NYSE: KRG) is a publicly traded REIT headquartered at 30 S. Meridian Street, Indianapolis, Indiana. The company operates through its majority-owned subsidiary, Kite Realty Group, L.P. (the "Operating Partnership"), in which the Parent Company holds approximately 97.8% of common partnership interests as of September 30, 2025.

**Portfolio as of Q3 2025:**
- 178 operating retail/mixed-use properties (170 wholly owned, 8 via unconsolidated JVs)
- ~29.3 million square feet of gross leasable area
- 1 active development project (One Loudoun Expansion, Washington D.C. MSA)
- 2 properties with future redevelopment potential
- 2 standalone office properties (0.4M sqft)

**Key geographic markets (by ABR concentration):**
- Texas (Dallas-Fort Worth, San Antonio, Houston, Austin)
- Florida (Tampa, Orlando, Fort Lauderdale, Jacksonville)
- North Carolina (Raleigh-Durham, Charlotte, Chapel Hill)
- Indiana (Indianapolis metro — legacy HQ market)
- Washington D.C. (One Loudoun, Northern Virginia)
- Atlanta, Seattle, Chicago, New York metro

**Tenant profile:** Diversified retail base with no single tenant exceeding 2.5% of ABR. Top 25 tenants represent approximately 30% of ABR. Anchor tenants include grocery operators (Kroger, Publix, H-E-B, Whole Foods), discount/value retailers (TJX, Ross, Dollar Tree, Burlington), home improvement (Home Depot, Lowe's), and fitness (Planet Fitness, LA Fitness). The grocery-anchored positioning is deliberate: these tenants drive non-discretionary foot traffic that benefits in-line shop occupancy and rent growth.

**Management:** John A. Kite serves as Chairman and CEO. He founded the company and has led it through its IPO, the 2014 Inland Diversified merger, the 2020 COVID crisis, and the transformative 2021 RPAI merger. This is a founder-led REIT — rare in the sector and worth noting for alignment of interests.

## PART III · THE 10-YEAR ARC (2015-2025)

KRG's last decade is best understood as three distinct chapters:

**Chapter 1: Pre-COVID Grind (2015-2019)**
KRG entered this period as a small-cap shopping center REIT with approximately 60-70 properties concentrated in the Midwest (Indianapolis, Ohio, Michigan) and select Sun Belt markets. ABR/sqft grew from ~$17.50 (2018) to $18.42 (2020), reflecting steady but unspectacular internal growth. The dividend was stable at $1.27/share annually ($0.3175/q) through 2018 and 2019. Shares outstanding were approximately 84 million. The company operated with a credit facility and no investment-grade rating, limiting capital markets access.

The company's growth strategy centered on selective acquisitions and ground-up development. The 2014 merger with Inland Diversified Real Estate Trust had added scale but also brought lower-quality assets that required recycling. By 2019, management was actively disposing of non-core properties while investing in higher-quality developments.

**Chapter 2: COVID Crisis and the Cut (2020)**
The pandemic hit open-air retail hard in Q2 2020, though materially less than enclosed malls. KRG's management made the decision to slash the quarterly dividend from $0.3175 to $0.06 — a reduction that shocked the shareholder base but preserved approximately $100M of annual cash flow. Total dividends declared in 2020 were $0.4495/share, down from $1.27 in 2019.

Crucially, KRG never fully suspended the dividend. Management took explicit pride in being among the few open-air peers to maintain continuous payments. Rent collections were a particular bright spot: KRG led its peer group in reported collection rates through the crisis period (per company disclosure as of February 2021). The company also acquired Eastgate Crossing in Chapel Hill, NC for $65.5M during the crisis — a counter-cyclical move that demonstrated confidence.

For the year, KRG reported a net loss of $16.2M and declared distributions of $39.7M to common shareholders, down from $137.1M in 2019. The balance sheet remained intact: no debt matured through December 2021, the debt service coverage ratio was 2.9x, and the company ended the year with $566.9M of combined cash and revolver capacity.

**Chapter 3: RPAI Merger and Transformation (2021-2025)**
The defining event of KRG's modern history was the October 22, 2021 merger with Retail Properties of America, Inc. (RPAI). Key terms:
- Transaction value: ~$4.7 billion (including ~$1.8B of assumed debt)
- Consideration: 0.623 KRG common shares per RPAI share (~$2.8B equity value)
- Properties acquired: 100 operating retail properties + 5 development projects
- Debt assumed: $850M revolving facility, $470M unsecured term loans, $450M private placement notes, $350M public notes due 2025, $400M public notes due 2030, $90.1M mortgages

The merger was transformative in several respects:
1. **Scale:** Portfolio roughly doubled from ~80 to ~180 properties and ~15M to ~29M sqft
2. **Geography:** Added strategic gateway market exposure (Washington D.C., Seattle) and deepened existing Sun Belt markets (Atlanta, Dallas, Raleigh)
3. **Balance sheet:** Achieved investment-grade ratings from three agencies (was two)
4. **Capital markets access:** Gained public unsecured bond market access, issuing multiple note series (2031, 2032, 2034 maturities)
5. **Dividend rebuild:** Declared $0.68/share in dividends for 2021, beginning the restoration from the COVID-era lows

Post-merger integration has been executed effectively. Same Property NOI grew 6.1% in 2021 (excluding RPAI properties), and the blended cash leasing spread was 10.7% on 2.6M sqft of executed leases. ABR/sqft increased to $19.36 (+5.1% YoY). The leased-to-occupied spread represented approximately $33.0M of NOI upside, the majority of which came online in 2022.

From 2022 through 2025, the company has steadily deleveraged, grown the dividend, and upgraded the portfolio through dispositions and selective acquisitions. The GIC joint venture partnership (formed 2025) represents the latest chapter in this evolution.

## PART V · DIVIDEND DEEP-DIVE

### The Cut: Anatomy and Aftermath

The Q2 2020 dividend reduction — from $0.3175/quarter ($1.27 annualized) to $0.06/quarter ($0.24 annualized) — is the single most important data point in any KRG dividend analysis. An 81% cut is not a trim. It is a near-elimination that tells you management will prioritize survival over shareholder distributions when existential risk presents itself.

**Was it the right call?** Unequivocally yes. In April 2020, KRG had 113 employees, a $500M enterprise value, no investment-grade rating, and tenants shuttering stores across the country. Preserving ~$100M of annual cash flow (the difference between the old and new dividend) was prudent capital stewardship. The alternative — maintaining the pre-COVID payout and potentially drawing down the credit facility to fund distributions — would have been reckless.

**The rebuild trajectory:**

| Q1 2020 | $0.3175 | $1.27 | Pre-COVID, last "old" rate |

The current $1.08 annualized rate is approximately 85% of the pre-COVID $1.27. At the current rebuild trajectory of $0.01/quarter annual increases, the dividend would match the pre-COVID level by 2027-2028. This is conservative pacing — the payout ratio at current levels (52-55% of Core FFO) could support a faster restoration, but management is clearly prioritizing balance sheet repair and buybacks alongside dividend growth.

The ongoing portfolio upgrade is the right strategy, but it temporarily depresses headline metrics: properties held for sale contribute to non-same-property NOI drag, and disposition proceeds often come at cap rates below the replacement cost of acquisition targets, creating a dilutive short-term FFO impact.

## PART VII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### Competitive Position

KRG's moat is narrow but durable, built on three pillars:

**1. Grocery anchoring and necessity-based tenant mix.** The portfolio is deliberately positioned around grocery-anchored shopping centers — properties where foot traffic is driven by non-discretionary spending. Kroger, Publix, H-E-B, and Whole Foods anchor tenants generate consistent daily traffic that benefits in-line shop occupancy and rent growth. This positioning proved its value during COVID: open-air grocery-anchored centers recovered faster than any other retail format.

**2. Sun Belt demographic tailwinds.** KRG's geographic concentration in Texas, Florida, North Carolina, and Georgia — states experiencing above-average population and household formation growth — provides a structural demand advantage. Net migration into Sun Belt MSAs has been a multi-decade trend that shows no sign of reversing. New supply in these markets, while increasing, remains below long-term averages due to elevated construction costs and restrictive zoning.

**3. Founder-led management with aligned incentives.** John Kite has led this company since founding. Founder-CEOs in REITs are statistically rare and historically associated with better capital allocation discipline, particularly during downturns. The 2020 dividend cut, while painful for shareholders, was arguably evidence of this discipline — prioritizing long-term survival over short-term distributions.

## PART VIII · RISK INVENTORY

### Tier 1 — High Probability, Material Impact

**1. Interest rate / refinancing risk.** KRG has ~$3.0B of consolidated debt. While the near-term maturity profile is manageable ($100M remaining in 2025, the 2026 maturities have been pre-funded), the Exchangeable Notes ($175M) mature in April 2027 and the larger 2028-2030 maturities will need addressing. If risk-free rates remain elevated at 4%+, refinancing will come at higher coupons than the legacy fixed-rate debt, compressing interest coverage and FFO. Q3 2025 interest expense of $33.2M was already up 5% YoY.

**2. Tariff-driven tenant stress (2025 specific).** Management explicitly flagged tariff risk in the Q3 2025 10-Q: tariffs on imported goods may increase prices for products tenants sell, potentially reducing consumer demand and impacting lease negotiations. The grocer-anchored portfolio provides some buffer (grocery demand is relatively inelastic), but in-line shops selling discretionary goods are exposed.

**3. Occupancy trajectory.** Leased percentage declined from 95.0% to 93.8% YoY (Q3 2024 to Q3 2025), and economic occupancy from 92.3% to 91.1%. Part of this is mechanical (properties entering the disposition pipeline, the Eastgate Crossing flood reclassification), but any further deterioration would pressure Same Property NOI and market sentiment.

### Tier 2 — Moderate Probability, Moderate Impact

**4. Midwest/legacy portfolio drag.** Indianapolis, Ohio, and Michigan properties generate lower rent growth and face weaker demographic tailwinds than Sun Belt assets. Disposing of these at acceptable pricing in a higher-rate environment is not guaranteed.

**5. Development execution risk.** The One Loudoun Expansion ($65-75M net funding, 12-24 month timeline) is the only active development project, but it is in the high-stakes D.C. market. Cost overruns or lease-up delays would impact returns on invested capital.

**6. Climate/weather risk.** The Tropical Storm Chantal flooding at Eastgate Crossing (Q3 2025) removed a property from the operating portfolio. While insurance is expected to cover the damage, the event highlights the vulnerability of coastal Southeast properties to increasing severe weather events. KRG disclosed adequate third-party insurance with a $0.3M deductible and business interruption coverage.

### Tier 3 — Low Probability, High Impact

**7. GIC JV structural risk.** The GIC partnership is structured as an unconsolidated joint venture with both members having substantive participating rights. If GIC exercises its rights in ways that diverge from KRG management's preferences (e.g., forced asset sales at inopportune times), it could create friction. This risk is inherent in any JV structure.

**8. CEO succession.** John Kite is the founder and has no publicly disclosed succession plan. Founder-CEO transitions in REITs are often disruptive, particularly when the founder's relationships with tenants, capital partners, and the board are deeply personal.

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

**What the consensus thinks:** KRG is a second-tier open-air shopping center REIT with a tainted dividend history, an unremarkable portfolio, and limited upside in a higher-for-longer rate environment. The stock deserves its 10x multiple because the 2020 cut proved management will sacrifice the dividend when things get tough, and income investors cannot tolerate that uncertainty.

**Where we disagree:** The consensus is anchored to the 2020 cut without adequately weighting the structural transformation since. Three specific mispricings:

**1. The GIC partnership is being valued at zero.** The market treats the JV transactions as routine capital recycling. They are not. GIC manages $770B+ in assets; their due diligence process is among the most rigorous of any institutional investor globally. The decision to partner with KRG — twice, within three months — is an implicit NAV validation that should narrow the discount to peers. If KRG's assets were truly second-tier, GIC would have partnered with Regency or Kimco instead.

**2. The buyback is more accretive than appreciated.** KRG repurchased 3.1M shares at $22.36/share in Q3 2025 alone ($70M). At ~10x FFO, every dollar spent on buybacks generates a ~10% unlevered return to remaining shareholders versus the 6-7% implied by the portfolio cap rate. Management is explicitly choosing buybacks over acquisitions because the stock is cheaper than the properties — this signal is being ignored.

**3. The 52% payout ratio creates optionality.** Most shopping center REITs pay out 65-75% of Core FFO. KRG's 52% means there is approximately $0.99/share of annual excess cash flow that can be directed to any combination of dividend increases, buybacks, deleveraging, or development. This optionality has asymmetric upside: if the economy remains stable, the dividend grows 6-8% annually; if a recession hits, the 52% payout provides a massive buffer that the 2020-era 65% payout did not.

## PART XI · VERDICT

**Rating: BUY below $21. ACCUMULATE at $20-22. HOLD above $24.**

**Dividend Safety: 7/10**

KRG is a post-transformation REIT trading at a pre-transformation multiple. The RPAI merger, GIC partnerships, grocery-anchored portfolio, and improving credit metrics collectively argue for a higher valuation than the market currently assigns. The 2020 cut was five years ago; the company today has 2.5x the scale, a better balance sheet, better geographic exposure, and a lower payout ratio than the entity that cut.

The dividend at $1.08/share ($0.27/q) is well-covered and growing. A 52% payout ratio on Core FFO provides approximately $215M of annual excess cash flow for a company generating ~$463M in Core FFO. This is not a dividend under stress — it is a dividend with substantial room for growth. We project annual increases of $0.01-0.02/quarter (4-8% growth) through at least 2028, assuming no recession.

The stock yields ~5.3% with 4-8% annual dividend growth potential and 15-30% upside to a reasonable NAV estimate. For a senior dividend investor, that is a compelling risk/reward profile — provided you can accept the scar tissue from 2020 as the price of admission to an otherwise attractive franchise.

**Investment horizon:** 3-5 years. The full re-rating requires completion of the disposition program, continued dividend increases, and ideally a full economic cycle without a cut. Patience will be rewarded.

### D. Glossary of REIT Metrics

- **FFO (Funds From Operations):** Net income excluding D&A on real estate, gains/losses on property sales, and impairments. NAREIT standard.
- **Core FFO:** FFO adjusted for non-cash items (straight-line rent, market rent amortization, deferred financing cost amortization, non-cash comp, debt discount/premium amortization). KRG's preferred performance metric.
- **Same Property NOI:** Net operating income from properties owned and operated in both comparison periods, excluding acquisitions, dispositions, developments, and redevelopments.
- **ABR (Annualized Base Rent):** Contractual minimum rent on an annualized basis.
- **Leased %:** Percentage of GLA covered by executed leases (includes signed but not yet commenced).
- **Economic Occupancy %:** Percentage of GLA where tenants are paying cash rent.
- **Net Debt/Adjusted EBITDA:** Company share of net debt divided by annualized adjusted EBITDA — primary leverage metric for investment-grade REITs.
