## PART I -- Executive Summary

Rand Capital Corporation is a sub-$60 million market-capitalization externally managed business development company (BDC) headquartered in Buffalo, New York. The company was incorporated in 1969, completed its IPO in 1971, and for most of its history operated as an internally managed, closed-end, venture-capital-style investment company with a portfolio heavily weighted toward equity positions in early-stage technology and regional businesses. In November 2019, Rand underwent a fundamental transformation when it closed a $25 million stock sale transaction with East Asset Management (a family office affiliated with the Callodine Group's Boston-based credit platform), externalized its management to Rand Capital Management LLC (RCM), and pivoted the strategy from venture-style equity to higher-yielding subordinated debt to lower-middle-market companies. Today, Rand is a very small BDC investing in privately held U.S. companies with revenue above $10 million and EBITDA above $1.5 million, deploying $2-4 million initial checks, typically as a minority sub-debt lender alongside other sponsors.

The most important development since the prior filing is a sharp deterioration in portfolio credit quality during calendar year 2025. Three debt investments went on non-accrual (up from zero non-accruals at year-end 2024), representing 28.6% of the portfolio at cost and 13.7% at fair value. Net unrealized depreciation widened from approximately $2.7 million at year-end 2024 to $8.6 million at year-end 2025, driven principally by a complete write-down of the decade-long Tilson Technology investment (Tilson filed Chapter 11 in Q2 2025 after a contract dispute with its major customer, and Rand recognized a $2.9 million realized loss in Q3 plus approximately $6.6 million of unrealized losses). Net asset value per share collapsed from $25.31 at December 31, 2024 to $17.57 at December 31, 2025 -- a 30.6% decline, of which approximately $3.31 per share came from the dilutive effect of the fourth-quarter 2024 stock dividend (80% paid in new shares), approximately $1.72 per share from regular and special cash dividends, and the remainder from realized and unrealized losses. Portfolio fair value declined from $70.8 million at YE2024 to $48.5 million at YE2025 -- a 31.5% contraction in one year. The weighted average portfolio yield compressed from 13.8% at YE2024 to 11.3% at YE2025.

Management maintained the $0.29 quarterly regular dividend through all four quarters of 2025 and layered a $0.56 per share Q4 2025 special dividend (paid entirely in cash this time, unlike the 2024 special). This kept Rand compliant with the Regulated Investment Company (RIC) rule requiring distribution of 90% of net ordinary income. However, the cash distribution capacity is now stretched: at December 31, 2025, Rand had only $4.2 million in cash against a net-asset base of $52.2 million, and the $25 million senior secured revolving credit facility had $19.2 million in unused capacity (not drawn -- zero outstanding). GAAP net investment income was $5.29 million in FY2025 ($1.78 per share on 2.95 million weighted-average shares), which nominally exceeds the $1.72 in cash dividends paid per share and produces NII coverage of approximately 1.03x. But this calculation masks the composition: 33.2% of 2025 total investment income was non-cash PIK interest (up from 23.1% in 2024 and 16.7% in 2023), and the NII figure was inflated by a $1.6 million non-cash reversal of capital gains incentive fees. Cash NII coverage is meaningfully below 1x.

At the April 2026 share price of approximately $10.67, Rand trades at a 39% discount to its YE2025 NAV of $17.57, implying the market is pricing in further NAV impairments. The annualized $1.16 regular dividend ($0.29 x 4) plus the recurring but volatile special dividend delivers a total yield of approximately 16% on market price (about 10.9% on regular alone) -- eye-catching on the surface but deceptive. This is a micro-cap BDC with only 19 portfolio companies, 14-15 of which were paying income in Q3 2025, a track record of lumpy realized gains that have historically funded special dividends and masked underlying NII weakness, a material weakness in internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR) related to income tax accounting disclosed in the FY2025 10-K, and a controlling shareholder (East Asset Management, 64% owned) whose interests are aligned with management but not necessarily with minority shareholders. **The dividend is mathematically covered on GAAP metrics but fragile on cash metrics. We recommend TRIM for positions purchased above $15 (accept the unrealized loss; the thesis has materially changed) and AVOID new accumulation below $15 until two consecutive quarters show no new non-accruals, portfolio yield stabilizes above 11%, and NAV erosion halts. The dividend is more likely than not to be cut or reduced to a variable/supplemental framework if the non-accrual trend persists into 2026.**

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

## PART III -- Historical Review

Rand Capital has existed publicly since 1971, but the analytically meaningful history began in November 2019 with the East Asset Management transaction and strategic pivot. The pre-2019 Rand was a venture-style BDC with a portfolio heavy in early-stage technology names (many Buffalo-area biotechs and Buffalo/Rochester/Boston-area tech firms). Realized gains were episodic, dividends were a fraction of what they are today, and the share price languished; the 2020 reverse split (1-for-9) was necessary to restore per-share arithmetic after years of dilution and write-downs.

Post-Transaction, Rand shifted to emphasize cash-yielding debt. The transition is evident in the portfolio composition trajectory: debt rose from approximately 35% of portfolio in 2019 to 79% at YE2025. The deployment of capital accelerated in 2022-2023 (leveraged partly via the new $25 million senior secured revolving credit facility with M&T Bank, established June 27, 2022) and peaked with $20.3 million invested in 2023. The following five-year trajectory captures the transformation, the 2024 harvest year, and the 2025 reversal.

## PART IV -- Dividend Deep Dive

This is the section that matters most. Rand's pitch to income investors is an attractive yield sustained by a balance-sheet-strong externally managed BDC. Behind that headline, the dividend quality is substantially weaker than at more mature BDCs, and the three-year trend on several key dividend-coverage metrics has deteriorated.

- **Current regular dividend:** $0.29 per quarter / $1.16 annualized
- **2025 special dividend:** $0.56 per share (declared December 16, 2025, payable December 30, 2025; cash only)
- **Total 2025 dividends declared:** $1.72 per share ($1.16 regular + $0.56 special)
- **Forward yield on regular dividend (price $10.67):** ~10.9%
- **Forward yield on total 2025 dividend level:** ~16.1%
- **Dividend streak (regular dividend increases):** No Dividend Aristocrat / King status. Regular dividend was raised in Q2 2023 (from $0.20 to $0.25) and Q2 2024 (from $0.25 to $0.29), then held flat at $0.29 through Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 2025. Five consecutive quarters at $0.29. No history of raising in 2025.
- **5-year dividend CAGR (regular dividend):** Strong but from a very low base. Q1 2021 quarterly was approximately $0.10; Q4 2025 quarterly is $0.29 -- a 5-year quarterly-dividend CAGR of approximately 23.7%. Most of this growth came 2021-2023 as the company converted from equity-heavy to debt-heavy.
- **10-year dividend CAGR:** Not meaningful. Pre-2019 Rand paid minimal dividends (typically $0.05-$0.10 per quarter), and the company only elected RIC status contemporaneous with the November 2019 Transaction.
- **Pattern of hikes:** Two regular hikes since 2020 (Q2 2023 +25%; Q2 2024 +16%). Five consecutive quarters without a hike (Q3 2024 through Q3 2025). Rand also declares variable special dividends tied to realized capital gains, typically in Q4.
- **NII coverage (FY2025 GAAP):** NII per share = $1.78 ÷ total dividend per share = $1.72 → coverage of 1.03x. Thin.
- **Adjusted cash-NII coverage of regular dividend (per Q3 2025 and YE2025 analysis):** Excluding the $1.565 million capital gains incentive fee reversal (non-cash) and adjusting for 33.2% of investment income as PIK (which is non-cash), adjusted cash NII is approximately $2.5-3.0 million in FY2025. Cash dividends declared totaled $7.3 million, including the $0.56 Q4 2025 special. Cash coverage of the regular $1.16 dividend alone is approximately 0.8-0.9x. **The regular dividend is not covered by cash NII in 2025.**
- **Dividend coverage by FCF:** BDCs do not operate on a traditional FCF framework. The relevant metric is "spillover taxable income" -- undistributed taxable income carried forward. Rand's declaration language suggests management distributes approximately 90-95% of taxable income annually, leaving minimal spillover.
- **Currency of payment:** USD
- **Withholding tax for non-US holders:** BDC dividends paid to non-U.S. holders are generally subject to 30% U.S. federal withholding tax. The portion of distributions designated as "interest-related dividends" may be exempt from this withholding for qualifying non-resident holders. For China-tax-resident U.S. dividend recipients (relevant to this portfolio owner per profile), the U.S.-China treaty rate is 10%, but BDC-level pass-through treatment may complicate the qualifying-dividend classification. Consult tax advisor.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

The regular $0.29 quarterly dividend is a dangerous proposition for a pure income investor who needs reliability. Here is the unsentimental breakdown:

The GAAP arithmetic looks protective: FY2025 NII per share was $1.78, total dividends per share $1.72, coverage 1.03x, asset coverage ratio effectively infinite (zero leverage). On that basis, Rand passes the standard BDC safety screen. But three adjustments move the picture substantially.

First, the $1.78 NII per share includes $1.565 million of non-cash capital gains incentive fee reversal. Strip that out and NII per share drops to approximately $1.25, producing coverage of 0.73x. The capital gains incentive fee reversal is a GAAP accrual that flips when realized and unrealized losses outpace gains -- in a continued down-NAV environment, the reversal could swing again the other way in 2026 (creating incentive-fee expense), which would compress reported NII further.

Second, 33.2% of FY2025 total investment income was non-cash PIK interest -- up from 23.1% in FY2024 and 16.7% in FY2023. PIK dollars are recognized as income and contribute to GAAP NII, but they are not cash. If PIK continues rising (a function of portfolio stress, as borrowers opt for PIK instead of cash payment) and realization events (repayments) stall, Rand's cash income falls faster than GAAP income.

Third, Rand has made limited share repurchases and has been a net share issuer through the 2025 stock dividend. Share count went from 2.58 million to 2.97 million on January 24, 2025 -- a 15% increase. The dilutive effect reduces per-share NII.

The company is currently unlevered, which is both a strength and a concern. Strength: no refinancing risk; $19.2 million in unused credit facility capacity plus $4.2 million cash equals $23.4 million in dry powder. Concern: the January 2025 election to 150% asset coverage gives management room to deploy more debt if desired, and the base management fee structure (1.5% on gross assets ex-cash) creates a misaligned incentive to lever up even when portfolio origination opportunities are marginal. A rapid increase in borrowings in a deteriorating credit cycle would be a red flag.

**Stress test -- could the dividend be cut?** Yes, materially. Scenarios:

1. **Mild stress (2026 base case):** If one or two more investments go on non-accrual and the portfolio yield falls to 10%, total investment income may drop to approximately $5.0 million from $6.5 million. Net investment income could compress to $3-4 million ($1.00-1.35 per share). Dividend coverage would be approximately 0.6-0.8x on $1.16 regular. Management would face pressure to reduce the regular dividend to $0.22-0.25 per quarter. **Probability: 45%**

2. **Moderate stress:** Three additional non-accruals, continued Fed cuts reducing floating-rate component (though Rand is 100% fixed-rate debt, so less exposed), portfolio yield at 9%. NII falls to $2-3 million ($0.70-1.00 per share). Regular dividend cut to $0.18-0.20 per quarter and special dividend eliminated. Market price could fall to $6-8. **Probability: 25%**

3. **Severe stress:** Systemic credit event, Tilson-style blow-up at EFINEA or Caitec (either is 10-11% of portfolio). NAV falls below $13. Dividend suspended or reduced to $0.10 per quarter. Rand could be forced to raise equity at significant discount to NAV. **Probability: 10%**

4. **Bull case:** Portfolio yield stabilizes at 11.5%, non-accruals resolve, Bauer Sheet Metal and Mobile IV Nurses perform well, origination environment improves. Regular dividend held at $0.29 per quarter; special dividend in 2026 of $0.30-$0.50 if realized gains materialize. NAV recovers to $19-20. **Probability: 20%**

The weighted outcome across these four scenarios is a regular dividend of approximately $0.20-0.25 per quarter and a NAV of $14-17 per share within 18 months. That implies a forward dividend yield of 7.5-9.4% on today's $10.67 price -- meaningfully lower than the current headline 16% total yield but still attractive.

The 2008-2020 stress test benchmarks: Rand was largely an equity BDC through the 2008 crisis and had no meaningful dividend to stress-test. In 2020, Rand's portfolio yield was approximately 10%, NAV held flat at $17-18, and the company did not cut its dividend. But 2020 was a very different Rand -- smaller debt exposure, mostly venture-equity positions that had already been marked down in 2019 and had room to recover.

## PART V -- Business Quality and Moat

As a lower-middle-market specialty lender, Rand's moat is shallow at best. The company does have three modest structural advantages: (1) permanent capital from a regulated BDC structure, (2) an exemptive relief order permitting co-investment with Callodine affiliates (expands sourcing), and (3) a niche in the $2-4 million sub-debt check size range where larger BDCs do not compete efficiently. These are real but narrow advantages.

- **Moat type:** Weak. Rand's business model is essentially arbitrage between its cost of equity (expensive, given the 39% discount to NAV) and the 12-14% sub-debt yields it can extract from sub-scale borrowers. There are no switching costs (borrowers refinance readily), no network effects, no brand premium, no regulatory barriers (BDC licenses are not scarce), and no technology edge.
- **Moat trajectory:** Narrowing. The 2025 story is a demonstration that Rand's micro-scale makes it vulnerable to single-name blowups (Tilson was 16% of portfolio at YE2024; three non-accruals at YE2025 are a 28% of cost hit). Larger BDCs diversify across 200-500 borrowers; Rand has 19. One bad underwriting cycle -- like 2025 -- and the economics invert.
- **Pricing power evidence:** Mixed. Rand charges 12-14% fixed on new originations (Bauer Sheet Metal at 13%, Mobile IV Nurses at 14% + 1% PIK, BlackJet at 14% + 1% PIK). This is competitive in the lower-middle-market sub-debt space. But increased PIK usage (16.7% → 23.1% → 33.2% of investment income over 2023-2025) shows borrowers have the ability to push for deferred cash structures when credit is tight -- evidence of declining Rand pricing power at the cash level.
- **Return on invested capital trend:** Deteriorating. FY2025 return on NAV of (23.79)% erased approximately two years of gains. Five-year cumulative total return on NAV (2020-2025) is approximately 40%, roughly 7% annualized -- below the ~10% regular dividend yield over that period, implying NAV has eroded while the company distributed cash.

The external management structure is itself a partial moat (for Callodine, not for Rand shareholders): it enables RCM/Callodine to use a listed BDC as a permanent-capital vehicle for middle-market credit deals that would otherwise cycle through capped private funds. For Rand shareholders, externalization is a cost: the 1.5% base fee on gross assets, plus the 20% incentive fee over a 7% annualized hurdle, plus reimbursed administration costs, represent a structural friction that higher-quality internally managed peers (like Main Street Capital, MAIN) do not pay.

The lower-middle-market focus has some quality merit: sub-$10 million EBITDA borrowers are underserved by regional banks (scale limits) and ignored by large BDCs (minimum check sizes $20M+), so Rand theoretically occupies a defensible niche. But the quid pro quo is portfolio concentration: with a $48M fair-value book, each $2-4M investment is 4-8% of the portfolio. There is no way to diversify away idiosyncratic credit risk at this scale, and the 2025 Tilson loss (26% of prior-year earnings wiped out) is a structural reality.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

Rand's capital allocation is essentially mandated by the Regulated Investment Company tax election: at least 90% of ordinary net income and realized short-term capital gains must be distributed annually. This leaves only marginal discretion for management to retain capital for reinvestment or repurchase. In practice, Rand's capital allocation follows a predictable seasonal pattern: quarterly regular dividends sized to expected NII, Q4 special dividends sized to accumulated capital gains, and opportunistic portfolio deployment as realization events (loan repayments, equity sales) provide cash.

**FY2025 capital allocation summary:**

1. **Dividends:** $7.3 million in cash dividends paid ($1.72 per share). Of this, $4.2 million was quarterly regular dividends and $1.66 million was the Q4 special. Additionally, the January 2025 stock-portion of the prior-year $4.20 special was distributed, adding $6.5 million of deemed-dividend fair value paid in new shares (ticker-neutral but dilutive to NAV per share).
2. **Buybacks:** Zero dollars. Board authorized $1.5 million program on April 23, 2025 through April 23, 2026; unused. This is a red flag: at a 39% discount to NAV, every $1 of repurchase generates $1.63 of NAV per share accretion. Management has chosen not to exercise the authorization, suggesting either (a) a view that cash should be retained for dividends, or (b) discomfort with the balance sheet's appetite for share reduction. Given the Q4 2025 special of $0.56 was paid instead of a larger buyback, management prioritizes current income distribution over accretive shrinkage.
3. **Portfolio deployment:** $6.6 million invested in new and follow-on investments (Bauer Sheet Metal $3.25M, BlackJet $2.5M, ITA follow-on $0.375M, Carolina Skiff follow-on $0.035M, Tilson/Mountain Regional/FCM capitalized PIK $1.2M). Repayments and sales totaled $17.8 million (Mattison $5.6M, Seybert's $7.6M, Pressure Pro $1.7M, Lumious $0.7M, HDI $1.1M, smaller).
4. **Capex:** N/A (BDC doesn't have meaningful physical capex). Administrative overhead is paid as reimbursement to RCM.
5. **M&A:** None at the corporate level. Portfolio-level M&A drives realization events.
6. **Debt paydown:** $0.6 million reduction in credit facility outstanding (from $0.6M at YE2024 to $0M at YE2025). Credit facility now fully undrawn.

**Capital allocation grade: C-.** 

The C- grade reflects a mix of positives and negatives:

*Positives:* (1) Management maintained dividend through a tough year, demonstrating commitment to income; (2) avoided levering up in a deteriorating credit environment; (3) took losses promptly on Tilson rather than marking the investment at stale valuations.

*Negatives:* (1) Failed to repurchase any stock despite 39% discount to NAV -- highly accretive opportunity passed over; (2) continued new deployment ($6.6M in 2025) into the same lower-middle-market segment that produced three non-accruals -- no evidence of underwriting recalibration; (3) the January 2025 stock dividend mechanism was dilutive and probably driven by a desire to preserve cash-dividend rate during a NAV stress period -- effectively a backdoor equity raise at a discount to NAV; (4) the regular-dividend "streak" is a policy commitment, not an earnings-driven outcome, which becomes fragile when earnings retreat; (5) external management structure leaks 2-3% per year in fees, capital that could have repurchased stock.

## PART VII -- Management Assessment

Rand's management structure is layered and externally controlled. This is a critical risk factor for dividend investors.

- **CEO: Daniel P. Penberthy**, President and CEO of Rand, also officer of RCM. Penberthy has held leadership positions at Rand through the 2019 Transaction and the subsequent transformation. Background in small-market commercial credit. His direct compensation comes from RCM, not Rand (Rand reimburses administrative costs that flow into RCM payroll). Total reported compensation not disclosed in 10-K; proxy statement carries full disclosure.
- **CFO: Margaret W. Brechtel**, EVP, Treasurer, CFO, Secretary. Also RCM officer. Similar compensation structure.
- **Investment Committee of RCM:** Penberthy, Scott Barfield, Steven Brannon. All three are principal portfolio managers. Investment decisions require majority vote.
- **Board (5 directors):** Adam S. Gusky and Benjamin E. Godley (East Asset nominees, Callodine affiliates); three independent directors (identities in proxy). East's 64% stake gives it effective control of board composition.
- **Insider ownership (direct):** Limited, per proxy. Insider alignment comes primarily through East's 64% stake -- Callodine-affiliated executives own interests in East. This is indirect alignment, not direct stock ownership, and it does not put the same "eat your own cooking" pressure that an internally managed BDC with insider-owned shares would have.
- **Comp structure:** At RCM, management compensation is primarily indexed to the performance fees RCM earns from Rand (the 1.5% base + 20% incentive over 7% hurdle). This creates partial alignment (management earns more when Rand's NII is high) but misaligned incentives at the margins (base fee is on gross assets including leverage, incentivizing debt deployment; capital gains fee has no hurdle rate).

**Guidance track record:**
Rand provides narrative outlook commentary on quarterly earnings calls but does not issue formal numerical guidance. Management's commentary has been directionally consistent -- each call since 2023 has flagged "cautious environment," "selective deployment," and "commitment to dividend." The Q4 2024 call (February 2025) was notably optimistic, emphasizing record 2024 results, the SciAps windfall, and confidence in 2025. Actual 2025 results materially missed this optimistic tone: three non-accruals, Tilson bankruptcy, portfolio shrinkage by $22M. On the Q2 and Q3 2025 calls, management's tone shifted to "disciplined," "patient," and "maintaining dividend" without upward projection -- an appropriate retrenchment, but the delay between commentary and deterioration suggests forward visibility is limited.

**Red flags:**
1. **Material weakness in internal control over financial reporting (ICFR).** Disclosed in the FY2025 10-K (Item 9A) and a 10-K/A amendment filed March 31, 2026. The weakness relates to "deficiencies in the design and implementation of controls over accounting for income taxes and related disclosures." Management has commenced remediation. While the company notes this weakness did not result in any misstatements of financial statements, a material weakness by SEC definition means there is a "reasonable possibility" of a material misstatement that is not prevented or detected. For a small investment company with complex fair-value accounting across illiquid private investments, a control weakness is more than a clerical issue -- it is a governance concern.

2. **Related-party concentration.** East Asset Management owns 64% of shares, holds two of five board seats, and is affiliated with the same Callodine Group that owns RCM. Co-investment with Callodine affiliates was recently expanded via the November 14, 2025 SEC exemptive relief order. Minority shareholders (the 36% public float) have limited ability to effect change.

3. **Insider ownership is indirect.** The "alignment" comes through Callodine/East's ownership of both Rand common stock and RCM economics, but individual executives' direct ownership of Rand stock is small per proxy disclosures. A purely external advisory model with limited direct insider ownership is the weakest governance structure in the BDC universe.

4. **Material weakness is not resolved.** Until remediation is validated (typically 12-18 months), investors cannot fully rely on the financial-statement integrity. This should weigh heavily on conviction.

**Green flags:**
1. Management maintained the quarterly dividend through a stress year -- demonstrating commitment to income investors.
2. Realized the Tilson loss promptly rather than drawing it out over multiple quarters.
3. Has not levered up opportunistically in a deteriorating credit environment (no new 150% asset-coverage-rule-enabled borrowings).
4. Is transparent on non-accruals and PIK composition on quarterly calls.

**Net governance assessment:** Below-average. An externally managed BDC with a 64% controlling shareholder affiliated with the adviser, a disclosed material weakness in ICFR, and indirect rather than direct insider alignment is structurally inferior to institutional-quality BDCs like MAIN (internally managed, insider ownership significant). We would not add to a position in Rand until the material weakness is remediated and disclosed as such.

### Glossary of BDC-specific terms
- **BDC (Business Development Company):** Public investment company registered under the 1940 Act, required to invest at least 70% of assets in qualifying securities of private U.S. companies.
- **RIC (Regulated Investment Company):** Tax election under Subchapter M of the Internal Revenue Code, requiring distribution of 90%+ of ordinary income to shareholders for pass-through tax treatment.
- **NII (Net Investment Income):** GAAP income measure for BDCs, equivalent to interest/dividend income minus operating expenses. Primary payout-ratio denominator.
- **NAV (Net Asset Value):** Total portfolio fair value plus other assets, minus liabilities, divided by shares outstanding.
- **PIK (Payment-in-Kind):** Interest or dividends that accrete to principal rather than being paid in cash. Non-cash income under GAAP.
- **Non-accrual:** Investment for which income recognition has been suspended due to credit concerns. Reduces future NII.
- **Qualifying Assets:** Investments in U.S. private companies, or listed companies below $250M market cap, per Section 55(a) of the 1940 Act.
- **Asset Coverage Ratio:** Total assets less non-senior liabilities, divided by senior securities. 1940 Act requires at least 150% (post-SBCAA) or 200% (pre-SBCAA).
- **Incentive Fee:** Performance-based compensation to external advisers. Typically structured with a hurdle rate (below which no fee), a catch-up, and a percentage share of excess income.
- **Spillover Income:** Undistributed taxable income carried forward from prior years, available for distribution to preserve RIC status.
