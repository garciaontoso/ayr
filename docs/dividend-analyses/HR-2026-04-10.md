## TL;DR

Healthcare Realty Trust is the largest pure-play medical office building (MOB) REIT in the United States, owning 502 properties totaling 29 million square feet as of December 31, 2025. The stock yields 5.9% at $17.56, which looks generous until you realize this dividend has already been cut once -- slashed approximately 30% in 2022 following the transformative but value-destructive merger with Healthcare Trust of America. The question for income investors is simple: is the current $0.31/quarter ($1.24 annualized, reduced to ~$0.96 actual 2025 payout based on the $0.24/share Q3 rate) safe, or is another cut coming?

My answer: the dividend at the current reduced rate is safe but offers minimal growth for 2-3 years. HR is in the middle of a massive portfolio rationalization -- selling 137 properties for $2.6B in 2024-2025 alone -- and using proceeds to delever and pivot toward higher-quality, on-campus MOB assets. Occupancy has improved from 87.4% (YE 2024) to 90.3% (YE 2025) on a smaller, better portfolio. Same-store NOI is growing low-to-mid single digits. But the GAAP income statement is an impairment graveyard -- $259M in real estate impairments and credit loss reserves in 9M 2025, plus $251M in goodwill impairment in 2024 -- masking the underlying cash flow stability.

The MOB secular thesis is real: outpatient migration, aging demographics, and on-campus physician proximity create durable demand. But HR overpaid for the HTA merger at the top of the market and is now spending years cleaning up the resulting portfolio bloat and elevated leverage. This is a workout story, not a growth story.

**Verdict: HOLD at $17.56. Yield trap risk is moderate. Dividend Safety: 5.5/10. Confidence: Medium.**

## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Healthcare Realty is in year three of digesting the largest MOB merger in REIT history, and the indigestion is still visible. The July 2022 combination of Legacy HR and Healthcare Trust of America created a 700+ property, $12B+ portfolio that was immediately underwater as interest rates spiked. Since then, management has been on a relentless disposition campaign -- 67 properties sold for $1.5B in 2024, and 70 properties sold for $1.1B in 2025 -- at a weighted average cap rate of 6.6-6.7%. The strategy is clear: shrink to improve quality, use proceeds to reduce debt, and eventually re-accelerate growth from a cleaner base.

The numbers tell a story of controlled demolition:

- Total assets: $12.6B (YE 2023) to $10.7B (YE 2024) to $9.2B (YE 2025) -- a 27% reduction in two years
- Total debt: $5.30B (YE 2023) to $4.96B (YE 2024) to $4.15B (YE 2025) -- $1.15B of deleveraging
- Properties: 670+ (post-merger peak) to 502 (YE 2025)
- Occupancy: 87.4% (YE 2024) to 90.3% (YE 2025) -- the portfolio that remains is better-leased

But this shrinkage comes at a cost:
- Total revenue: $1,344M (2023) to $1,268M (2024) to $1,181M (2025) -- declining 6-7% annually
- GAAP net loss: ($278M) in 2023, ($655M) in 2024, ($246M) in 2025 -- persistently negative due to impairments and depreciation
- The $251M goodwill impairment in 2024 was the final admission that the HTA merger destroyed value at the price paid

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **Secular MOB tailwind is real and underappreciated.** Outpatient visits now represent the majority of U.S. healthcare encounters. An aging population requires more physician visits, imaging, outpatient surgery, and diagnostics. MOBs near hospital campuses are mission-critical infrastructure for health systems -- they cannot be replaced by telehealth or Amazon.
2. **Portfolio quality is improving rapidly.** Occupancy jumped 290bps in one year (87.4% to 90.3%). The properties being sold are lower-quality, off-campus, or in markets where HR lacks critical mass. What remains is increasingly concentrated in high-growth Sun Belt and coastal markets near leading health system campuses.
3. **Deleveraging trajectory is credible.** $1.15B of debt reduction in two years, funded by dispositions at reasonable cap rates (6.6-6.7%). Net debt/EBITDA is improving. The company has demonstrated willingness to sacrifice size for balance sheet health.

**Top 3 risks:**
1. **The dividend has already been cut once.** Post-merger, HR reduced its quarterly dividend from $0.31 to approximately $0.24 per share (as indicated by Q3 2025 equity statement showing $0.24/share). Investors who bought for the legacy yield got burned. The precedent exists for further reduction if cash flows deteriorate.
2. **GAAP losses are structural, not just accounting noise.** $491M in combined impairments in 2024, $259M+ in 2025. These are not mark-to-market adjustments on performing assets -- they reflect genuine value destruction on properties that were acquired at peak pricing.
3. **Interest rate sensitivity is extreme.** With $4.15B of debt and a beta of 0.80, HR is a leveraged play on the direction of rates. The company's interest expense of $161M for the first nine months of 2025 ($214M annualized) consumes a massive share of NOI.

**Next 12 months catalysts (ranked by importance):**
1. **Q4 2025 results / FY2025 annual report (February 2026):** Already filed. The 10-K confirms the portfolio shrinkage to 502 properties, occupancy improvement to 90.3%, and debt reduction to $4.15B. The market needs to see FY2026 guidance that shows stabilizing FFO.
2. **2026 disposition activity:** $605M in assets held for sale at September 30, 2025 suggests another $500-700M of sales in the pipeline. The cap rate achieved on these sales will signal whether the MOB transaction market is healthy.
3. **Federal Reserve rate trajectory:** HR is one of the most rate-sensitive REITs in the healthcare space. Every 25bps rate cut adds approximately $5-8M of annual interest expense savings and, more importantly, compresses cap rates, which directly inflates property values and NAV.
4. **Leasing activity and same-store NOI:** Organic growth must replace the NOI lost to dispositions. The weighted average lease term increased from 4.2 to 4.4 years -- a positive sign that new leases are being signed at longer durations with better terms.
5. **Legislative developments on REIT healthcare ownership:** Federal proposals to restrict healthcare providers from selling assets to REITs remain a tail risk. The OBBBA (signed July 2025) permanently extended the 20% REIT dividend deduction, which is a positive, but the regulatory landscape remains uncertain.

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Healthcare Realty Trust Incorporated is a self-managed and self-administered REIT that owns, leases, manages, acquires, finances, develops, and redevelops income-producing real estate properties associated primarily with the delivery of outpatient healthcare services throughout the United States. Headquartered in Nashville, Tennessee, the company trades on the NYSE under the ticker "HR."

**The Merger:** On July 20, 2022, Healthcare Trust of America (Legacy HTA) merged with the original Healthcare Realty Trust (Legacy HR), with Legacy HTA as the surviving corporate entity but operating under the Healthcare Realty name. The merger created the largest pure-play MOB REIT in the U.S. The combined entity retained Legacy HTA's management team and Maryland corporate structure.

**Strategy:** Focus on MOBs located on or near the campuses of acute care hospitals associated with leading health systems. The on-campus/near-campus strategy is critical: CMS site-neutral payment policies have lowered Medicare reimbursement for off-campus hospital outpatient departments, potentially making on-campus properties more valuable as tenants retain higher reimbursement rates.

**Geographic Diversification:**

HR's portfolio is concentrated in high-growth Sun Belt and major metro markets, which is a deliberate strategic choice. The top markets by investment include Nashville (home market), Dallas, Denver, Indianapolis, Houston, Atlanta, and various Florida MSAs. The post-merger disposition strategy has disproportionately exited smaller, secondary markets where HR lacked critical mass for efficient property management.

The geographic concentration creates both advantages (market knowledge, tenant relationships, management efficiency) and risks (regional economic exposure, natural disaster risk). The lack of international diversification means HR is a pure play on U.S. healthcare real estate demand.

**Tenant Credit Quality:**

Unlike hospital REITs (where a single health system might represent 20-30% of revenue), HR's MOB tenant base is extraordinarily granular. With over 30 physician specialties and no single tenant exceeding 10% of revenue, the portfolio has near-perfect tenant diversification. This means that individual tenant defaults have minimal impact on portfolio-level cash flows -- a critical safety feature for dividend investors.

However, the granular tenant base also means that leasing and property management are labor-intensive. HR manages 93% of its portfolio in-house with just 539 employees, suggesting lean operations. The risk is that occupancy improvement requires proportionally more leasing effort as the remaining vacancy is harder to fill (the "last mile" problem in leasing).

### The Impairment Problem -- Unpacking the Losses

The sheer scale of non-cash impairment charges deserves examination because it reveals the magnitude of the merger's value destruction:

Nearly $1 billion in write-downs over three years. To put this in perspective, HR's total stockholders' equity is $4.7B -- the impairments represent roughly 20% of book value. These are not abstract accounting entries; they reflect real properties that were acquired (via the HTA merger or otherwise) at prices that proved to be above fair value once interest rates normalized.

The $251M goodwill impairment in FY2024 was the most damning charge. Goodwill arises when a company pays more for an acquisition than the fair value of the acquired assets. Writing off goodwill is an explicit admission that the merger premium was not justified -- the synergies, growth, and market position acquired did not materialize as expected.

The silver lining: with $950M+ already written down, the remaining portfolio is increasingly marked to current market values. Future impairment charges should diminish as the gap between book value and fair value narrows.

**Why 5.5/10?** The company has already cut the dividend once, which destroys credibility. The current $0.96 rate is better covered, but the business model is still dependent on selling assets to fund the gap between operating cash flow and total capital needs (dividends + maintenance capex + development). If the disposition market freezes -- say, due to a severe credit dislocation -- the company would face liquidity pressure that could force another cut.

**What would need to happen for a second cut?** (1) Disposition market dries up, meaning HR cannot sell properties at acceptable prices; (2) Interest rates spike back above 5%, re-inflating interest expense; (3) Occupancy declines back below 88%, reducing rental income. All three would need to happen simultaneously. I assign this scenario roughly 15% probability.

**What would stabilize the dividend?** (1) Occupancy continues improving toward 92-93%; (2) Same-store NOI growth of 3-4%; (3) Debt reduction continues to bring net debt/EBITDA below 7.0x; (4) Interest rates decline, reducing refinancing costs. This is the most likely path -- call it 55% probability.

### The Unconsolidated Joint Venture Strategy

A frequently overlooked element of HR's financial picture is the growing use of unconsolidated joint ventures. As of December 31, 2025, HR held a weighted average 30% ownership interest in 61 properties through JVs, with an equity investment of $454M. In 2024, HR contributed 30 properties into two JVs in which it retained a 20% interest, generating $172.7M in retained investment.

This strategy serves multiple purposes:
1. **Deleveraging:** Selling 80% of a property to a JV partner generates cash to pay down debt while retaining management fees and a residual equity interest.
2. **Fee income:** HR receives management fees ($19.6M in FY2025, up from $9.8M in FY2024 -- a doubling driven by the new JV contributions). This fee income has high margins and creates a recurring revenue stream that is independent of property ownership.
3. **Optionality:** If markets improve, HR can potentially repurchase JV interests at attractive terms. If markets deteriorate, the JV partner absorbs 80% of the loss.

The risk is complexity. Unconsolidated JVs reduce transparency, and the equity method accounting can mask underlying property performance. Distributions from JVs ($16.5M in 9M 2025 vs $4.9M in 9M 2024) are lumpy and not guaranteed.

### Debt Maturity and Refinancing Risk

HR's $4.15B of notes and bonds payable is the single largest risk factor for the dividend. The company's interest expense was $161M for 9 months of 2025, annualizing to $215M -- roughly 50% of operating cash flow. Any increase in refinancing rates directly compresses the cash available for dividends.

The key question is what maturities are coming in 2026-2028 and at what coupon they were originally issued. Given the rate environment of 2020-2021 (when many bonds were issued at 2.5-3.5% coupons) versus today's 4.5-5.5% rates, refinancing could add $20-40M of annual interest expense on each $1B of maturing debt -- a material headwind.

However, the ongoing debt reduction from $5.30B (YE 2023) to $4.15B (YE 2025) partially offsets this risk. If HR can reduce total debt to $3.5B by YE 2027, the absolute dollar interest expense should remain manageable even at higher rates.

## PART VI -- COMPETITIVE POSITION AND MOAT

Healthcare Realty operates in a favorable sub-sector of real estate. The MOB REIT category benefits from several structural advantages:

**1. Outpatient migration is irreversible.** Healthcare delivery has permanently shifted from inpatient to outpatient settings. Surgery centers, imaging facilities, physician offices, and diagnostic centers all require physical real estate that cannot be replicated by technology. Unlike office REITs (which face remote work headwinds) or retail REITs (which face e-commerce), MOBs benefit from a demand driver -- in-person healthcare visits -- that is growing with demographics.

**2. On-campus positioning creates natural moats.** Properties located on hospital campuses benefit from (a) higher Medicare reimbursement rates under CMS site-neutral policies, (b) physician preference for proximity to hospitals for patient referrals, (c) limited supply -- there are only so many parcels on or adjacent to a hospital campus. HR's strategy of focusing on these locations is sound.

**3. Multi-specialty tenant diversification.** HR leases to over 30 physician specialties. No single tenant exceeds 10% of revenue. This granular diversification means that tenant credit risk is spread across thousands of individual leases, reducing concentration risk.

**Where the moat is narrow:** MOBs are not unique assets. Unlike, say, a data center with specialized infrastructure, a medical office building is fundamentally a Class A office building with medical plumbing. Competitors include Physicians Realty Trust (DOC, now merged with Healthpeak Properties), Healthpeak Properties (PEAK), and numerous private equity funds. The barriers to entry are low for individual properties, though building a national platform with in-house leasing and management at HR's scale is difficult.

**Key competitors:**

| Healthcare Realty | HR | Pure-play MOB | $6.1B | 5.5% |

HR's pure-play MOB focus is a differentiation advantage for investors who want targeted outpatient healthcare exposure without senior housing volatility (Welltower, Ventas) or hospital risk (MPW, which has had its own distribution crisis).

## PART VII -- MANAGEMENT AND GOVERNANCE

**Leadership transition:** The FMP profile lists Peter A. Scott as CEO, succeeding Todd Meredith who led Legacy HR and the combined entity through the merger period. The 10-K executive officer section references the transition.

**Key governance features:**
- Self-managed REIT -- no external advisory fees, no conflicts of interest with an external manager. This is a significant positive. External management structures (common in the REIT space) create misaligned incentives where the manager benefits from asset growth regardless of returns.
- Maryland corporation with typical REIT governance provisions
- Board committees include Audit, Compensation and Human Capital, and Nominating and Corporate Governance
- ESG integration with named executive officer incentive program including ESG performance measures

**Capital allocation track record:** Mixed at best. The HTA merger was executed at the worst possible time (July 2022, just as rates were spiking) and at a premium that has since been written down via goodwill impairment and property impairments totaling $700M+. However, the subsequent disposition strategy has been well-executed -- selling at 6.6-6.7% cap rates and using proceeds to meaningfully reduce debt. Management deserves credit for recognizing the portfolio quality problem and acting aggressively rather than hoping for a market recovery.

**Share repurchases:** The company repurchased $150M of stock in Q3 2024 and $3.6M in common stock redemptions in 9M 2025. The willingness to repurchase below NAV is a positive signal, though it competes with debt reduction for capital.

### The Outpatient Migration Secular Thesis -- Why MOBs Win Long-Term

For income investors evaluating HR, the secular backdrop is the strongest argument for owning the stock despite its near-term challenges. Three structural forces are driving outpatient healthcare demand:

**1. Demographics are destiny.** The U.S. population aged 65+ is growing at roughly 3% per year (10,000 Baby Boomers turn 65 daily). This age cohort consumes 3-4x more healthcare services per capita than younger demographics. The vast majority of these services -- primary care, specialist consultations, imaging, outpatient surgery, physical therapy, dermatology -- are delivered in medical office settings. This demand curve bends only one direction for the next 20 years.

**2. Payers are pushing outpatient.** Both private insurers and Medicare/Medicaid have economic incentives to shift care from expensive hospital inpatient settings to lower-cost outpatient settings. CMS site-neutral payment policies explicitly equalize reimbursement between hospital outpatient departments and physician offices -- this actually benefits on-campus MOBs because hospital systems retain higher reimbursement for on-campus locations while facing pressure to build more outpatient capacity.

**3. Technology enables but does not replace.** Telehealth was the great "disruptor" narrative during COVID. In practice, telehealth visits peaked at 30-40% of total visits in April 2020 and settled at 12-15% by 2023-2024. Physical examinations, imaging, blood draws, minor procedures, and therapy sessions all require in-person visits. Telehealth has supplemented but not replaced the MOB.

**The bear case for MOBs:** (1) Health system consolidation could reduce the number of tenants and increase bargaining power over landlords; (2) Value-based care models could reduce visit volumes if prevention succeeds; (3) AI diagnostics could eventually reduce demand for certain specialist visits. All real but 5-10 year timeline risks, not 2-3 year dividend threats.

## PART VIII -- GROWTH OUTLOOK AND CATALYSTS

**Near-term catalysts (6-18 months):**
1. **Occupancy inflection.** The jump from 87.4% to 90.3% in one year is the most important data point in the entire story. Each 100bps of occupancy improvement on the remaining 29M sq ft portfolio adds approximately $10-12M of incremental NOI (assuming $35-40/sq ft average rent on incremental space). If occupancy reaches 92% by YE 2026, that is $20-25M of additional NOI flowing directly to FFO.
2. **Debt reduction confirmation.** $1.15B of debt reduction in 2024-2025. If another $400-500M is retired in 2026 from ongoing dispositions and FCF, the interest expense savings at 4-5% rates would be $16-25M annually -- meaningful to a company with total interest expense around $215M.
3. **Rate cuts (if they materialize).** HR's beta of 0.80 and its debt load make it one of the most interest-rate-sensitive REITs. Every 50bps of rate reduction on the variable-rate portion of debt saves approximately $5-10M annually. More importantly, lower rates re-inflate MOB cap rates and property values, potentially reducing impairment charges.

**Medium-term thesis (2-3 years):**
- Complete disposition program (sell remaining ~$600M in held-for-sale assets plus incremental sales)
- Achieve occupancy of 92-93% on the core portfolio
- Reduce total debt to $3.5-3.8B, bringing debt/total assets below 40%
- Stabilize FFO per share and resume modest dividend growth (2-3% annually) by 2028
- Re-rate from ~13x FFO to 15-16x FFO as the portfolio shrinks to its highest-quality core

## PART XI -- THE MERGER -- POST-MORTEM AND LESSONS

The HTA merger demands its own section because it is the single most important event in HR's history and the reason the stock trades where it does.

**What happened:** In February 2022, Legacy HR and Legacy HTA announced a merger of equals. The deal closed July 20, 2022, creating a 700+ property MOB portfolio. The combined entity retained the Healthcare Realty name and HTA's corporate structure.

**What went wrong:** The merger was announced when the 10-year Treasury yield was approximately 2.0%. By closing, it was 3.0%. By year-end 2022, it was 3.9%. By October 2023, it hit 5.0%. The entire pro-forma economics of the deal -- predicated on cheap debt, stable cap rates, and synergy realization -- were invalidated by the fastest rate hiking cycle in 40 years.

**The damage:**
- $251M goodwill impairment in FY2024 -- an explicit write-down of merger premium
- $491M in combined property impairments and credit loss reserves in FY2024
- $259M in additional impairments in 9M 2025
- Dividend cut from $0.31/quarter to $0.24/quarter
- Stock decline from $30+ (pre-merger announcement) to $17.56 today
- 137 properties sold in 2024-2025, shrinking the portfolio by ~20%

**The lesson for dividend investors:** Merger-driven dividend strategies are inherently risky. The combined entity's dividend was set based on pro-forma FFO assumptions that assumed a benign rate environment. When rates spiked, the math broke, and shareholders paid the price.

## PART XII -- ACTIONABLE CONCLUSION

### For Income Investors
Healthcare Realty at 5.5% yield (post-cut) is an acceptable income holding for investors who believe in the MOB secular thesis and can tolerate the workout timeline. The dividend at $0.96 annually is safer than the prior $1.24 rate, but there is no prospect of meaningful dividend growth for at least 2-3 years. If you need growing income, look elsewhere (WELL at 2.2% with 4-5% growth, or even Realty Income at 5.5% with a 25-year growth track record). If you are comfortable with stable income and potential capital appreciation from re-rating, HR is reasonable at this price.

### For Total Return Investors
The total return case is more interesting. If the base case plays out -- occupancy to 92%, modest deleveraging, rates decline to 3.5-4.0% -- the stock should re-rate from $17.56 to $19-21 over 12-18 months. Add the 5.5% yield and you are looking at 14-25% total return. The risk/reward is acceptable but not compelling -- there are easier ways to make 20% in REITs.

### Position Sizing
Given the prior dividend cut, the ongoing portfolio shrinkage, and the rate sensitivity, size this at 1.5-2.5% of a diversified income portfolio. This is not a conviction position; it is a recovery bet with an income floor.

### Key Monitoring Points
1. **Occupancy rate (quarterly)** -- Must continue improving toward 92%+. Any reversal below 89% is a warning sign.
2. **Same-store NOI growth** -- Target 3-4% annually. Below 2% signals demand weakness.
3. **Disposition pace and cap rates** -- The $605M in held-for-sale assets needs to be monetized at 6.5%+ cap rates. Any distressed sales below 7.5% cap rates suggest market deterioration.
4. **Debt maturity profile** -- What maturities are coming in 2026-2027 and at what refinancing rates? Every $100M of debt refinanced at 100bps higher costs $1M of incremental interest.
5. **Dividend declaration (quarterly)** -- Watch for any further reduction from $0.24. Even a one-penny cut would devastate the stock.
6. **FFO per share (quarterly)** -- The single most important metric. Must stabilize at $1.55+ annualized to support the current dividend.

## APPENDIX -- REIT-SPECIFIC METRICS

For investors accustomed to analyzing C-corps, REIT financial statements require different metrics. Here is a simplified framework:

| Metric | What It Means | HR Approximate Level |
|---|---|---|
| FFO | Net income + depreciation + impairments - gains on sales | ~$1.55-1.60/share (est.) |
| AFFO | FFO - maintenance capex - straight-line rent adjustments | ~$1.30-1.40/share (est.) |
| NAV | Fair value of assets minus liabilities, per share | ~$22-24/share (est.) |
| P/FFO | Stock price / FFO per share | 11.0-11.3x |
| Implied cap rate | NOI / property value | ~6.5-7.0% |
| Debt/Total assets | Total debt / total assets | ~45% (improving) |
| AFFO payout ratio | Dividend / AFFO | ~69-74% (post-cut) |

The post-cut AFFO payout ratio of 69-74% is within the healthy range for a REIT (most target 70-85%). This confirms that the dividend cut was sized appropriately to create a meaningful cushion.
