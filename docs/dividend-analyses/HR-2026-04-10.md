## PART I -- Executive Summary

Healthcare Realty Trust Incorporated is the largest pure-play medical office building (MOB) REIT in the United States, owning 502 consolidated properties totaling approximately 29 million square feet as of December 31, 2025. The company trades on the NYSE under ticker "HR" at a reference price of $17.56, yielding approximately 5.5% on its current annualized dividend of $0.96 per share. This dividend was reset in Q3 2025, when the quarterly rate was cut from $0.31 to $0.24 per share -- a 22.6% reduction that finally acknowledged the financial reality of the overpaid 2022 merger with Healthcare Trust of America.

The single most important development since the last filing cycle is the completion of the company's strategic transformation under new CEO Peter A. Scott, who joined from Healthpeak Properties in April 2025. Scott and his team have executed an aggressive three-year strategic plan at an accelerated pace: $1.2 billion in asset dispositions completed at a blended 6.7% cap rate (exceeding the high end of expectations), $10 million in run-rate G&A savings achieved, net debt/EBITDA reduced by nearly a full turn to 5.4x, credit outlook upgraded to Stable by both Moody's and S&P, and the dividend right-sized to a sustainable level. On the Q4 2025 earnings call (February 2026), Scott declared that "our noncore dispositions are now behind us" and that the company has "entered 2026 front footed."

The underlying business is performing well: Normalized FFO of $1.61 per share for FY2025 exceeded the midpoint of original guidance by $0.03, same-store NOI growth of 4.8% exceeded original guidance by 140 basis points, and occupancy improved by over 100 basis points year-over-year through same-store absorption of nearly 290,000 square feet. Cash leasing spreads of 2.7%, tenant retention of 77-83% (eight consecutive quarters above 80%), and average contractual escalators of 2.93% all point to a healthy underlying lease portfolio.

However, this remains a workout story in its late innings, not yet a growth story. Revenue declined from $1,344 million (FY2023) to $1,268 million (FY2024) to $1,181 million (FY2025) due to dispositions. GAAP net losses persist at ($246) million for FY2025 due to $361 million in real estate impairments -- the cumulative tally of impairments plus the goodwill write-off now exceeds $1.1 billion since the merger closed. The dividend has been cut once, and while the reset level is better covered, the company still faces $1.3 billion of debt maturities in 2026-2027 that must be refinanced. The stock trades at 10-11x FFO, which Scott himself acknowledged "is typically reserved for companies or sectors that are struggling." The question is whether the new management team can execute on the turnaround to close that valuation gap.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

Our dividend is appropriate, well covered, and under the right conditions, able to grow in the future, while at present offering a nearly 6% current yield to our shareholders."

The phrase "long overdue" is notable. It confirms that management views the prior $0.31 rate as having been unsustainably high for years, masked by asset sales and balance sheet leverage. The cut was not a crisis response but a deliberate recalibration to create breathing room.

The post-cut coverage is significantly healthier. At a Normalized FFO of $1.61/share, the $0.96 dividend represents a 60% payout -- well within the 60-80% range typical for well-run REITs. At a FAD of $1.27/share, the payout is 76% -- tight but acceptable given the company's low maintenance capex relative to gross assets ($35.1M capital expenditures on a $10B portfolio, or $1.07/sq ft in 2025). The key risk is that FAD includes the benefit of dispositions flowing through Normalized FFO adjustments; as dispositions decline in 2026, the underlying recurring FAD will be the true test.

## PART V -- Business Quality and Moat

**Moat type:** Scale advantages + regulatory barriers + location-based switching costs
**Moat trajectory:** Stabilizing (was narrowing post-merger due to portfolio dilution; now improving as quality assets retained)
**Pricing power evidence:** Cash leasing spreads averaged 2.7% in FY2025; contractual rental rate growth averaged 2.93% across the portfolio (up from 2.83% in FY2024); annual escalators across all 2025 leasing activity averaged 3.1%

Healthcare Realty's competitive moat rests on several structural advantages specific to the MOB sub-sector:

**1. Outpatient migration is irreversible.** Healthcare delivery has permanently shifted from inpatient to outpatient settings. Surgery centers, imaging facilities, physician offices, and diagnostic centers all require physical real estate that cannot be replicated by technology. Unlike office REITs (facing remote work) or retail REITs (facing e-commerce), MOBs benefit from in-person healthcare visits that are growing with demographics. The U.S. population aged 65+ is growing at roughly 3% per year, and this cohort consumes 3-4x more outpatient healthcare services per capita.

**2. On-campus positioning creates natural barriers.** HR's strategy focuses on properties located on or near the campuses of acute care hospitals associated with leading health systems. As noted in the 10-K, CMS site-neutral payment policies have lowered Medicare payments for services delivered in off-campus hospital outpatient departments, potentially making on-campus properties more valuable as hospital tenants retain higher reimbursement rates. There are only so many parcels on or adjacent to a hospital campus -- supply is structurally constrained.

**3. Ground lease positioning creates embedded optionality.** As of December 31, 2025, the company had 168 properties held under ground leases representing approximately $4.2 billion of gross investment, with a weighted average remaining term of 60.6 years including renewal options. These ground leases, typically with hospital campuses, embed the company deeply into health system infrastructure.

**4. Self-management eliminates conflicts.** HR is self-managed with no external advisory fees. In the REIT space, external management structures create misaligned incentives where the manager benefits from asset growth regardless of returns. HR's 539 employees manage 93% of the portfolio in-house, creating operational efficiency and direct tenant relationships.

**5. Supply constraint supports fundamentals.** CEO Scott noted on the Q4 call that "deliveries and completions as a percentage of inventory remain near all-time lows" and that new construction costs have increased so significantly post-COVID that "the rental rates needed to get to the return necessary for a developer to want to start that project is pretty significant." This supply constraint supports occupancy gains and rent growth for existing assets.

**Where the moat is narrow:** MOBs are fundamentally Class A office buildings with medical plumbing. The barriers to entry for individual properties are not particularly high. Competition comes from Healthpeak (DOC), private equity funds, and health systems' own development arms. The company's moat comes from platform scale and tenant relationships, not from the uniqueness of individual assets.

The 60 basis point improvement in cash leasing spreads under the new management platform (from approximately 2.1% to 2.7%) is evidence that the post-disposition portfolio is higher quality than the pre-disposition portfolio. CEO Scott specifically attributed this improvement to "the quality of the assets we've retained" and the "more focused asset management approach" under COO Hull. The 2.93% average contractual escalator is a structural tailwind -- it means the in-place portfolio generates nearly 3% revenue growth annually before any leasing activity, providing a foundation for the same-store NOI growth that management is targeting in the 3-5% range.

The 4.4-year weighted average remaining lease term is shorter than the REIT sector average, reflecting the medical office business model where physician practices sign shorter leases than corporate office tenants. This creates higher turnover risk but also more frequent opportunities to mark rents to market. The 82.5% retention rate mitigates the turnover risk -- when tenants leave, it is typically due to practice closure rather than competitor defection, and new tenants are attracted by the on-campus locations.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

Capital allocation is the centerpiece of the Healthcare Realty 2.0 strategy. Under new management, the priorities have been explicitly ranked:

**1. Redevelopments ($136.6M funded in FY2025):** The highest-priority capital deployment. Management targets approximately 10% yields on cost for redevelopment projects, which typically involve "soup to nuts" renovation of older vintage buildings -- reskinning, new windows, new building systems, common area upgrades -- at approximately $200-300 per square foot. CEO Scott described these as "a significant source of NOI upside" and the "premier" capital allocation opportunity.

**2. Share repurchases ($509.8M in FY2024, $50M in Jan 2026):** Management repurchased 30.8 million shares at an average price of $16.56 in FY2024 and authorized an additional $50 million purchase in January 2026. At 10-11x FFO, Scott called the stock price a "significant" undervaluation. As of December 31, 2024, $237 million of authorized repurchases remained.

**3. Dividends (~$335M annualized at current rate):** Reset to a sustainable level. The $0.96 annual rate represents approximately 60% of Normalized FFO, providing buffer for growth investment and debt reduction.

**4. Debt paydown ($796M net debt reduction in FY2025):** Total notes and bonds payable declined from $4,895M (principal, YE 2024) to $4,098M (principal, YE 2025). This included the repayment of the $250M Senior Notes due 2025, a $108.5M term loan repayment, $251M in bonds repaid, and net $149M of credit facility activity. The Fifth Amended and Restated Credit and Term Loan Agreement (July 2025) extended maturities and improved terms.

**5. Joint ventures (potential growth vehicle):** The unconsolidated JV platform grew significantly in 2024 with 30 properties contributed to two JVs at 20% retained interest, generating $172.7M in retained investment. Management fee income doubled from $9.8M (FY2024) to $19.6M (FY2025). On the Q4 call, Scott noted: "We are fortunate to have existing joint venture partners who want to increase their investments in outpatient medical."

**6. Acquisitions (none in FY2024 or FY2025):** Zero acquisition activity in the last two fiscal years. Management is disciplined: "Our current cost of capital and discount to intrinsic asset value limits external growth."

**Capital allocation grade: B-** -- The disposition and deleveraging execution has been excellent. The dividend cut was necessary and appropriately sized. Share repurchases below NAV were value-accretive. However, the B- reflects the legacy of the HTA merger, which was the single worst capital allocation decision in the company's history, destroying over $1 billion of value through impairments and goodwill write-offs. The current management team inherited this problem and is handling it well, but the damage to shareholder returns is permanent.

**Prospect Medical case study -- demonstrating portfolio resilience:**

The Prospect Medical Holdings Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing in 2024-2025 provides a real-world stress test of HR's tenant diversification thesis. The total Prospect exposure represented less than 0.2% of portfolio NOI, and the transition was completed within two quarters without any rent loss -- a testament to the essential nature of MOB real estate and the depth of the healthcare tenant pipeline.

**CMS site-neutral payment policy analysis:**

The FY2025 10-K provides updated language on CMS site-neutral payment policies that is more balanced than prior years. The company notes that "CMS continued to adjust Medicare payment rates in 2025 to implement site-neutral payment policies" which "have lowered Medicare payments for services delivered in off-campus hospital outpatient departments." Crucially, the filing states that HR's "medical office buildings that are located on hospital campuses could become more valuable as hospital tenants will keep their higher Medicare rates for on-campus outpatient services." This is a structural positive for HR's strategy of concentrating on-campus. However, the company hedges by noting it "has not seen a material impact from site-neutral Medicare payment policy, positively or negatively" -- an honest assessment that the theoretical benefit has not yet translated into measurable rent premium.

**OBBBA tax implications for HR shareholders:**

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, permanently extended the 20% deduction for ordinary REIT dividends that was previously set to expire at the end of 2025 under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act. This is a structurally positive development for all REIT shareholders. For an investor in the 37% federal tax bracket receiving HR's $0.96 annual dividend, the 20% deduction reduces the effective tax rate from 37% to 29.6%, increasing the after-tax yield from approximately 3.45% to 3.85% at current prices. This permanent tax advantage makes REIT dividends more competitive relative to corporate bond interest, which does not benefit from this deduction.

### Detailed Risk Discussion

**Risk #1: Refinancing wall.** The $1.3 billion in 2026-2027 maturities is the most immediate threat. The 2026 Senior Notes carry a 3.50% coupon; refinancing today at 5.0%+ would add approximately $9-10 million of annual interest expense per $600 million of notes. The commercial paper program (up to $600M, backstopped by the revolver) provides bridge flexibility, but permanent refinancing at higher rates will compress FFO. Mitigation: principal reduction through free cash flow and modest ongoing dispositions could reduce the refinancing quantum.

**Risk #2: Interest rate sensitivity.** As of December 31, 2025, $620 million of variable-rate debt (revolver + two term loans) is directly exposed to SOFR movements. A 10% increase in market rates would increase annual interest by $2.4 million. More importantly, higher rates compress property values and make refinancing more expensive. The weighted average effective rate of 4.52% across all debt is already high for a portfolio yielding approximately 7% cash-on-cost.

**Risk #3: Legislative and regulatory.** The 10-K discloses "federal legislative proposals that would prohibit payments from federal healthcare programs to healthcare providers that sell assets to REITs" and "state legislation that would prohibit licensure of certain healthcare facilities leased from REITs." While none have passed, the regulatory scrutiny of healthcare REITs is increasing at both federal and state levels. The positive offset: the OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025) permanently extended the 20% deduction for ordinary REIT dividends.

## PART XI -- Thesis and Verdict

**Thesis:** Healthcare Realty is a turnaround story entering its final phase. The new management team has executed the painful but necessary steps -- cutting the dividend, selling $2.6 billion of assets, retiring $1.15 billion of debt, and overhauling the operating platform. The underlying MOB business is performing well, with nearly 5% same-store NOI growth, improving occupancy, and strong tenant retention. The stock trades at a meaningful discount to intrinsic value (10-11x FFO vs 14-16x for the sector) and to estimated NAV ($20-24 per share). However, the dividend has been cut once, the debt load remains substantial at $4.1 billion, and $1.3 billion of near-term maturities must be refinanced. For dividend investors specifically, the post-cut yield of 5.5% is competitive but offers no near-term growth prospect -- management explicitly said the dividend can grow "under the right conditions" but did not signal imminent increases. This is a reasonable HOLD for existing shareholders who believe in the MOB secular thesis and the new management team, but it is not compelling enough to ACCUMULATE given the execution risk, prior cut history, and availability of alternative REIT income opportunities with better dividend track records.
