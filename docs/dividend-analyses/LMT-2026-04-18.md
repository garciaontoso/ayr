## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE at $592. BUY below $540. Dividend Safety: 8/10. Confidence: High.**

Lockheed Martin is the world's largest defense contractor by revenue and the prime contractor on the single largest defense program in history (F-35 Joint Strike Fighter, $400B+ lifetime program value). The company operates across four segments: Aeronautics (F-35, F-16, C-130, Skunk Works classified programs, $30.3B in FY2025), Rotary & Mission Systems (Sikorsky helicopters, naval electronics, integrated warfare, $17.3B), Space Systems (national security space, satellite communications, launch services, $13.0B), and Missiles & Fire Control (Javelin, HIMARS, THAAD, precision strike weapons, $14.5B). FY2025 revenue reached $75.1B, up 5.7% year-over-year, with strong growth across Missiles & Fire Control (+14%) driven by replenishment of munitions supplied to Ukraine and Israel.

At $592 and a market cap of approximately $135B [approx], LMT is a defensive dividend holding with 22 consecutive years of dividend increases and a 5-year DPS CAGR of 7.8%. The current quarterly dividend of $3.30 ($13.20 annualized) yields approximately 2.23%. The company generates $6.9B in FY2025 free cash flow and returned $3.0B in buybacks and $3.1B in dividends to shareholders (101% of FCF distributed in capital returns; net debt financed a small portion).

The central questions for dividend investors: (1) Is the F-35 program at peak or still growing? (2) Can LMT's margin profile withstand fixed-price contract pressure on next-generation platforms? (3) Is the defense budget cycle supportive of sustained FCF growth? Our answers: F-35 deliveries are stable at ~150/year with long-term growth via international sales (notably Germany $8.4B and multiple NATO countries); margin pressure from TR-3 software upgrade and other fixed-price programs has compressed margins from 13% in 2019 to 10.3% in FY2025; and the US defense budget is expected to grow in the low-to-mid single digits per year through FY2030 under current geopolitical conditions.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **F-35 is a 40-year sustained franchise.** The program is in mid-production with 3,000+ aircraft contracted (US plus 17 international partners). Block upgrades through 2040+ provide recurring engineering, sustainment, and training revenue. The sustainment revenue (logistics, training, spare parts) is now approximately $8B annually and growing; the sustainment-to-production ratio will shift favorably as the fleet matures.
2. **Dividend aristocrat with 22-year streak and 7-8% DPS growth.** LMT has raised dividends every year since 2003, including through 2008 (raised 6.5%) and 2020 (raised 8.3%). Payout ratio on FCF is approximately 45%, comfortably sustainable.
3. **Geopolitical tailwinds across multiple theaters.** Ukraine conflict drove replenishment; Middle East tensions drove Patriot and THAAD demand; Taiwan Strait tensions drove F-16 and F-35 sales to Taiwan and Japan. These are multi-year structural tailwinds for defense spending.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to buy:**
1. **F-35 TR-3 (Technology Refresh 3) program has been troubled.** Software development delays led to customer acceptance issues, withheld payments, and margin compression from 2023-2025. Management has committed to delivery improvement in 2026.
2. **Customer concentration risk.** US government represents ~72% of revenue. Any major budget reversal or administration change could compress demand. Congressional Appropriations cycle timing creates quarterly volatility.
3. **Capital discipline could slip.** Buybacks have been aggressive ($3-7.9B/year, $26B cumulative 2020-2025). As the stock trades near $600, this is expensive capital return. Better discipline would preserve dry powder for opportunistic buybacks during downturns.

**Dividend sustainability narrative:**

LMT's dividend is strongly supported by four factors:

*First, FCF coverage of 2.08x.* $6.9B FCF / $3.1B paid dividend. Even a 40% FCF decline would maintain coverage above 1x. FCF has not dropped below $5.0B since 2018.

*Second, payout ratio discipline.* 48% on FCF is well below the 60-70% threshold that would cause concern.

*Third, contract backlog of $176B (FY2025 year-end).* This represents approximately 2.3 years of revenue visibility. The backlog includes both US government and international contracts, providing revenue predictability that manufacturing-intensive companies rarely enjoy.

*Fourth, defense budget secular support.* US defense spending grew from $572B (FY2018) to $842B (FY2025), a 47% increase. The drivers -- Ukraine, Middle East tensions, Taiwan Strait, Korean Peninsula, China modernization -- are structural rather than cyclical.

**The 2008 stress test:** FY2008-2009 saw revenue growth of 3.4% and 5.8% respectively. Dividend was raised 30.3% in 2009 (large catch-up) after 16.7% in 2008. Zero stress.

**The 2020 stress test:** Revenue grew 9.3% (positive defense cycle). Dividend +8.3%. No concerns.

**The FY2024 margin stress:** Revenue grew 5.1% but operating income fell -17.7% due to TR-3 margin pressure. FCF fell from $6.2B to $5.3B. Dividend was unchanged at $3.15 (though this is debated as a year-of-no-increase vs calendar quarter reporting). Dividend coverage remained 2.0x.

**What would break the streak?** A combination of: (a) $2B+ TR-3 write-down over 2 years AND (b) US defense budget nominal cut 5%+ AND (c) loss of a major program like F-35 re-compete. None are high-probability. Cut probability 3y: 4%. Hike probability 12m: 90%.

## PART V -- BUSINESS QUALITY & MOAT

**Moat components:**

1. **Sunk cost barriers to entry.** Developing a 5th-generation fighter takes $100B+ and 15+ years. No new entrants possible in the US market for another 2 decades.
2. **Classified programs and clearances.** Skunk Works and classified space systems create deep IP and personnel moats (thousands of cleared engineers).
3. **Technology accumulation.** Integrated avionics, stealth, radar, datalinks -- LMT has 40+ years of integrated experience unmatched by competitors.
4. **Sustainment economics.** Once F-35 aircraft are deployed, 40+ years of sustainment revenue follows. F-35 sustainment market size estimated at $2 trillion lifetime.
5. **Prime contractor relationships.** Direct-to-DOD relationships in every major domain (air, sea, land, space, cyber).

**Moat trajectory:** Stable. The NGAD loss (Boeing won F-47) was a setback but LMT retained F-35, all major space systems, missile defense, and rotary. The loss does not threaten the core franchise.

**Pricing power:**
- Fixed-price contracts (e.g., F-35 production lots) have limited pricing power; cost overruns absorbed by contractor
- Cost-plus contracts (typical of R&D and new programs) have limited margin upside
- Sustainment and services contracts have better pricing power due to limited alternatives
- International FMS (Foreign Military Sales) sales typically have 10-15% higher margins than domestic

**Return on invested capital trend:**
- FY2019: 52% (extraordinary due to low invested capital)
- FY2020: 43% [approx]
- FY2021: 37%
- FY2022: 25% (margin compression + higher capital)
- FY2023: 28%
- FY2024: 16%
- FY2025: 17% [approx]

ROIC has declined materially but remains acceptable. The decline reflects both margin compression and capital deployment via acquisitions and buybacks.

The F-35 Joint Strike Fighter represents approximately 20-25% of Lockheed Martin's total revenue across all segments (production, sustainment, modernization, and development). Understanding the program is essential to understanding the company.

**Program overview:**
- Program of record: 2,470 aircraft for the US military (Air Force F-35A, Navy F-35C, Marine Corps F-35B)
- International partners: 9 partners + 8 Foreign Military Sales countries = 17 total international customers
- Projected lifetime program value: $400B+ for production, $2T+ for total lifecycle (production + sustainment + upgrades)
- Variants: F-35A (conventional takeoff/land), F-35B (short takeoff/vertical land), F-35C (carrier-based)
- Prime contractor: Lockheed Martin (Aeronautics segment, with content from Missiles & Fire Control for AIM-120 AMRAAM integration, Rotary & Mission Systems for electronic warfare)

**Key program milestones:**
- Initial Operational Capability declared by USMC (2015), USAF (2016), USN (2019)
- 1,000th aircraft delivered (2024)
- Block 4 software upgrade (TR-3) rollout began 2023-2024
- Lot 17 production contract (FY2024) -- modest margin
- Lot 18 negotiations in progress (FY2025-26) -- critical pricing event

**International partners and sales:**
- Tier 1 partners: UK, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Denmark, Australia, Canada
- Tier 2 partners: Turkey (removed 2019 due to S-400 deal), Israel (special status)
- FMS customers: Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Finland, Poland, Belgium, Switzerland, Germany, Czech Republic, Romania, Greece

**Germany contract (signed 2022):** $8.4B for 35 F-35As replacing Tornado aircraft. First deliveries expected 2026-2027. This was a geopolitically important deal representing European commitment to NATO air defense and F-35 interoperability.

**TR-3 (Technology Refresh 3) saga:**
- TR-3 was intended to provide the processing foundation for Block 4 software (hundreds of new capabilities)
- Software development hit significant delays 2023-2024
- US customers (USAF, USN, USMC) refused to accept TR-3 aircraft until certain software maturity milestones
- Payment withholding affected LMT cash flow
- Approximately 100+ TR-3 aircraft parked awaiting acceptance at one point
- LMT absorbed margin pressure of $1B+ cumulatively across 2023-2024
- As of early 2026: LMT has begun gradual deliveries of TR-3 "Truncated" (limited-capability) aircraft to US and allies
- Full TR-3 acceptance target: mid-2027

**F-35 financial contribution by component:**
- Production (Lot 17, Lot 18): $6-7B/year
- Sustainment services: $6-7B/year (growing)
- Modernization/Block 4 upgrades: $2-3B/year
- Classified derivatives: Limited disclosure
- Total: $14-16B/year through 2030

**Long-term F-35 trajectory:**
- Production peak: ~170 aircraft/year (achieved 2023)
- Stabilization: 150-160 aircraft/year through 2030s
- Declining units after 2035 (replaced by 6th-gen aircraft like NGAD/F-47)
- Sustainment peak: $10-12B/year by 2030s as fleet matures

**Program risks:**
- Alternative 6th-gen programs (B-21, F-47 in US; FCAS in Europe)
- Foreign export restrictions or political shifts
- Unit cost overrun on Block 4 integration
- Sustainment cost reduction imperative (DOD has been pressing)

## EXTENDED ANALYSIS -- MISSILES & FIRE CONTROL FRANCHISE

The Missiles & Fire Control (M&FC) segment is the fastest-growing part of LMT, benefiting from war-cycle replenishment and missile defense buildouts. FY2025 revenue of $14.5B grew 14.0%. This growth is expected to continue for 3-5 years.

**Major M&FC product families:**

**PAC-3 Patriot missile interceptor:** The PAC-3 family (PAC-3, CRI, MSE variants) is the primary air and missile defense interceptor used by 15+ allied nations. FY2025 revenue approximately $2.5B. Backlog of several years due to Ukraine-driven replenishment demand and Middle East deployment. Production capacity has been ramped from 350/year (pre-2022) to 650/year (target 2025) to 900/year (target 2027).

**THAAD (Terminal High-Altitude Area Defense):** Theater ballistic missile defense system. Deployed by US Army and allies (UAE, South Korea, Saudi Arabia). FY2025 revenue $1.8B. Interceptor production ramping to address backlog from Middle East and East Asia.

**HIMARS (High Mobility Artillery Rocket System):** Launcher system. FY2025 revenue $1.5B. Used extensively by Ukraine (200+ systems). Multi-year production backlog. Launcher production rate tripled from 2021 to 2025.

**GMLRS (Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System):** Rocket munitions for HIMARS. FY2025 revenue $1.2B. Production ramped from 6,000/year (pre-2022) to 12,000/year target by 2027.

**Javelin anti-tank missile (JV with Raytheon):** FY2025 revenue $0.8B (LMT share). Production tripled from 2020 to 2025. Critical for Ukrainian defense and international allies.

**ATACMS (Army Tactical Missile System):** Long-range precision strike. FY2025 revenue $0.6B. Production being transitioned to PrSM (Precision Strike Missile) replacement.

**Hellfire/JAGM:** Air-to-surface missiles for helicopters and drones. FY2025 revenue $0.9B.

**Other programs:** Various electronic warfare, countermeasures, fire control systems.

**Growth drivers:**
1. Ukraine replenishment (2025-2028): $5B+ cumulative revenue
2. Middle East tensions: Patriot, THAAD, GMLRS demand
3. Taiwan Strait: Harpoon anti-ship, Stinger, HIMARS
4. European NATO buildout: Patriot, GMLRS, HIMARS
5. US munitions replenishment: GMLRS, Javelin, PAC-3

**Competitive dynamics:**
- Raytheon (RTX): Direct competitor in Patriot (shared with LMT), AMRAAM, Tomahawk
- Boeing: Direct competitor in HAWK refurbishment, Harpoon
- MBDA (European): Competitor in international markets
- Northrop Grumman: Interceptor programs (GMD)

**Margin profile:** M&FC historically operates at 12-14% margins, higher than the corporate average. Volume leverage from production ramps should sustain or improve margins.

## EXTENDED ANALYSIS -- ROTARY & MISSION SYSTEMS

Rotary & Mission Systems ($17.3B in FY2025, +0.3% YoY) is the slowest-growing segment. It comprises Sikorsky helicopters, naval electronics, and integrated warfare systems.

**FARA (Future Attack Reconnaissance Aircraft):** The Army canceled FARA in February 2024. LMT's Raider-X competitor was eliminated. Cost savings but strategic concern on Sikorsky's future.

**Integrated Warfare Systems:** Naval electronics (Aegis combat system for US Navy and foreign FMS), integrated missile defense command and control. Revenue ~$5B.

**Mission Systems pipeline:** Electronic warfare, sensor fusion, cybersecurity. Continued organic growth.

**Rotary & Mission Systems outlook:** Low single-digit growth (1-3%) for the next several years. Sikorsky has become a legacy franchise. The segment remains profitable but is not a growth engine.

## EXTENDED ANALYSIS -- NGAD LOSS AND STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

In March 2025, Boeing was selected over Lockheed Martin for the US Air Force's Next-Generation Air Dominance (NGAD) fighter program, designated F-47. This was arguably the single most consequential program decision for LMT since the 2001 F-35 selection.

**Program significance:**
- 6th-generation fighter replacing F-22 Raptor (originally planned retirement 2030s)
- Manned aircraft that integrates with unmanned Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) wingmen
- Intended production: ~200 aircraft over 20-year production cycle
- Total program value: $150-200B (production + sustainment + upgrades)
- Strategic value: establishes air dominance franchise through 2060+

**Why LMT lost:**
- Boeing offered significantly lower cost (widely reported ~15% below LMT proposal)
- Boeing had spent more on risk-reduction and demonstrator aircraft
- LMT's design was technically ambitious but considered higher-cost
- Political dynamics may have favored Boeing's industrial base argument

**Strategic implications for LMT:**
1. **Long-term air dominance franchise transferred to Boeing.** In 20-30 years, LMT will no longer be the #1 US fighter aircraft prime contractor. This is a generational change.
2. **F-35 retains value through 2040+.** The loss of NGAD does not affect the existing F-35 franchise, which will continue through the 2050s.
3. **Boeing's gain increases its defense diversification.** Boeing was heavily concentrated in commercial aerospace; NGAD gives it balanced defense presence.
4. **LMT reallocation of resources.** The approximately $5-10B of cumulative LMT NGAD bid investment needs to be redirected. Possible beneficiaries: Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA), classified projects, new missile systems.
5. **LMT classified aviation work continues.** Skunk Works and Advanced Development Programs retain classified aircraft work. Not disclosed publicly but represents multi-billion-dollar annual franchise.

**Investor implications:**
- Near-term (2025-2027): Minimal revenue impact (NGAD was still in R&D)
- Medium-term (2028-2032): Some margin pressure as LMT consolidates aviation investment
- Long-term (2035+): Material loss of future aviation franchise
- Capital allocation: LMT should prioritize return of capital (buybacks, dividends) rather than investing in long-shot comebacks

**Stock market reaction:** LMT stock declined ~7% in the day following the NGAD announcement. Has largely recovered since as other fundamentals dominated.
