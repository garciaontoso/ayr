## TL;DR

Safehold is the only publicly traded pure-play ground lease REIT, and the stock has been obliterated. From an IPO-era peak above $90, SAFE trades at $14.90 -- a 55% discount to book value ($33.59/share) and a 63% discount to FMP's DCF of $40.11. The dividend yield of ~4.8% ($0.708 annualized) is fully covered by GAAP earnings ($1.59 EPS diluted, FY2025) and the balance sheet carries $9.3 billion of Unrealized Capital Appreciation above cost in the residual portfolio. On paper, this is a deeply mispriced, investment-grade-rated (A-/A3/A-) asset with contractual rent escalators and 99-year lease terms.

So why does the market hate it? Three reasons, and they are all legitimate: (1) $4.5B of debt on a $1.1B market cap -- a 4.2x debt-to-equity ratio that makes the stock behave like a leveraged bond in a rising-rate environment (beta: 1.85); (2) 39% office exposure in a post-COVID world where office valuations are in secular decline; and (3) the Park Hotels litigation, where SAFE terminated the master lease on five hotels and is now in Delaware Chancery Court with no guaranteed outcome.

Our verdict: this is a high-conviction position for investors with a 5+ year horizon and strong stomachs, but it is NOT a dividend safety play. The dividend is safe today. The question is whether the capital structure and office exposure create a scenario where it becomes unsafe. We think not, but the margin of error is thinner than management's presentations suggest.

## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: SPECULATIVE BUY below $15 for long-duration investors. HOLD at $14.90. Dividend Safety: 6/10.**

The bull case is simple and powerful: SAFE owns land underneath $15.9 billion worth of commercial real estate. It paid $6.7 billion for ground lease positions that sit senior to every mortgage, every mezzanine loan, and every equity dollar in the capital stack. At lease expiration (or tenant default), everything on top of the land -- every building, every improvement -- reverts to Safehold at zero incremental cost. The Unrealized Capital Appreciation in this residual portfolio is $9.3 billion as of December 31, 2025 (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 1). The market is pricing the entire company at $1.1 billion. Even after subtracting $4.5 billion of net debt, the implied enterprise value of $5.5 billion is barely above the $6.7 billion cost basis, assigning exactly zero value to the $9.3 billion residual. If you believe that commercial real estate has any residual value in 60-90 years, the stock is absurdly cheap.

The bear case is equally straightforward: SAFE is a levered duration bet masquerading as a REIT. With $4.5B of debt against $2.4B of equity, every 100bps move in long-term rates mechanically destroys ~15-20% of equity value. The weighted average interest rate on the debt stack is 4.18% stated / 3.80% cash (SAFE Q1 2025 10-Q Note 9), and the blended cost has been rising as low-coupon 2021-vintage paper gets refinanced at 5.65-6.10%. Office exposure at 39% of gross book value is a structural headwind that no amount of CPI escalators can offset if tenants default. And the Park Hotels litigation introduces binary outcome risk on 3.2% of portfolio book value.

Three S&P/Moody's/Fitch investment-grade ratings (all single-A territory as of November 2025) provide substantial credibility to the balance sheet, but ratings agencies assess credit risk, not equity risk. The debt is likely money-good. The equity dividend is a different question.

**Top 3 reasons to own:**
1. **Deepest discount to book in REIT-land.** 0.44x P/B is not a typo. Even net-lease REITs hammered by rates trade at 0.8-1.0x book. Either SAFE's book value is wildly overstated (possible but unlikely given independent third-party valuations) or the market is pricing in a catastrophic scenario that has not materialized in 8 years of operations.
2. **Contractual rent escalators are real growth, not hope.** Revenue grew from $159M (2020) to $386M (2025) -- a 19.4% CAGR -- driven almost entirely by new originations and built-in CPI/fixed escalators. This is not cyclical. The rent comes in whether the S&P 500 is up or down.
3. **Triple-A credit upgrade trajectory.** S&P upgraded to A- in November 2025. The $2.0B unsecured revolver at SOFR+85bps and $750M commercial paper program signal institutional-grade access to capital. No near-term maturities create refinancing risk through 2029.

**Top 3 reasons to sell or avoid:**
1. **Leverage is extreme for a REIT.** Debt/Equity of 1.87x, Debt/Assets of 62%, Net Debt/EBITDA of 13.4x. For context, a typical net lease REIT runs at 5-7x Net Debt/EBITDA. SAFE is 2x the sector average. In a world where long rates spike to 6%+, the equity could lose another 30-40% before book value catches up.
2. **Office exposure is a secular risk.** 39% of gross book value is office. The 10-K explicitly warns that the rise in interest rates has adversely affected the U.S. office sector and that certain office assets currently have material vacancies (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 7). A forbearance agreement on a significant New York office asset (undisclosed identity) is a red flag.
3. **Park Hotels litigation is a genuine wildcard.** On October 22, 2025, SAFE terminated the master lease on five Park Hotels properties and sued for breaches related to maintenance and operations (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 3). The tenant disputes termination. If SAFE loses, it keeps a deteriorating lease. If SAFE wins, it inherits five hotels it has never operated. As of January 1, 2026, SAFE already became responsible for operating two hotel properties that reverted (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 7). This is a ground lease company entering the hotel business. That should concern every shareholder.

**Next 12 months catalysts:**
- Park Hotels litigation outcome (Delaware Chancery Court, 2026)
- Federal Reserve rate trajectory -- each 25bps cut is disproportionately beneficial to SAFE given duration
- New originations pipeline (unfunded commitments: $142.3M + $154.8M performance-based as of YE2025)
- Potential ATM equity issuance ($300M shelf, zero sold as of YE2025)
- First hotel operating results (Q1 2026 will show revenue from two reverted properties)
- Share buyback execution ($50M authorized February 2025, unclear if any executed)

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Safehold Inc. is a Maryland corporation that acquires, manages and capitalizes ground leases on commercial real estate across the United States. It is the only publicly traded company focused exclusively on this niche. The business model is conceptually similar to owning the land beneath a commercial building and leasing it back on a 30-99 year term, triple-net, with contractual rent escalators.

The company traces its lineage to iStar Inc., a diversified real estate finance company founded by Jay Sugarman in the 1990s. iStar created the ground lease concept through its subsidiary "Safety, Income & Growth" (Old SAFE), which IPO'd in June 2017. On March 31, 2023, iStar merged into Old SAFE, spinning off its legacy non-ground-lease assets into Star Holdings (a Maryland statutory trust). The surviving entity was renamed Safehold Inc. and retained the SAFE ticker.

## PART III -- THE GROUND LEASE MODEL: WHAT MAKES IT UNIQUE

Ground leases are the most senior position in the commercial real estate capital stack -- senior to first mortgage debt, mezzanine debt, preferred equity, and common equity. This is the core thesis behind SAFE's investment proposition.

**Capital Stack Position:**
```
[Land Owner / Ground Lease Holder]  <-- SAFE sits here
    |
[First Mortgage Lender]
    |
[Mezzanine / Pref Equity]
    |
[Common Equity Owner]
    |
[Building Tenant]
```

If the building owner defaults on the ground lease, the land AND all improvements revert to the ground lease holder (SAFE). This creates a powerful incentive for tenants and their lenders to keep paying ground rent -- it is the last obligation they would stop paying, because defaulting on it means losing everything above it.

SAFE targets ground leases where the initial cost represents 30-45% of Combined Property Value (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 1). As of December 31, 2025, gross book value as a percentage of Combined Property Value was 52% (up from the 30-45% origination target due to office value declines compressing the denominator). This deterioration in the "loan-to-value" metric is a warning sign.

**The Residual Portfolio:**

| Metric | Dec 31, 2025 | Dec 31, 2024 |
|---|---|---|
| Combined Property Value | $15,947M | $15,523M |
| Ground Lease Cost (SAFE's basis) | $6,675M | $6,395M |
| Unrealized Capital Appreciation | $9,272M | $9,128M |

Source: SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 1. Combined Property Value includes pro-rata share of unconsolidated ventures and $616.2M related to unfunded commitments (2025).

The UCA of $9.3B is the theoretical value that would accrue to SAFE at lease expiration. But "lease expiration" is 60-99 years away for most assets. Discounting $9.3B at any reasonable rate over 70+ years produces a present value of a few hundred million dollars at best. The market appears to be doing exactly this calculation.

**CPI Escalators:** Most leases include CPI-linked rent adjustments starting between years 11-21, typically capped at 3.0-3.5% cumulative. In the event cumulative inflation exceeds the cap, rent adjustments may not keep up with inflation (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 1). This is an important limitation: the inflation hedge is capped, meaning SAFE does not fully participate in a high-inflation environment.

**Ground Rent Coverage:** As of December 31, 2025, the estimated portfolio Ground Rent Coverage was 3.4x, defined as the ratio of the property's NOI to the annualized ground rent payable to SAFE (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 7). This means the buildings above SAFE's land generate, on average, 3.4 times the rent SAFE charges. This coverage ratio provides a substantial cushion against tenant default -- the tenant would need to lose two-thirds of its NOI before the ground rent becomes unaffordable. However, the 10-K warns that this metric relies on property NOI as reported by tenants, and that SAFE uses estimates for properties in development, transition, or where current tenant financials are not available. The actual coverage on individual assets, particularly distressed office properties, could be materially lower than the portfolio average.

**New Products and Pipeline Expansion:** SAFE has developed several ancillary products to expand its origination pipeline beyond traditional ground leases. The Leasehold Loan Fund (currently three assets) and Ground Lease Plus Fund (two assets) allow SAFE to offer tenants a full capital stack solution -- mortgage plus ground lease in one package. "SAFExSWAP" enables real estate investors with existing ground leases to exchange into SAFE's modern structure. "SAFExSELL" allows sellers to create a ground lease at the time of property sale, increasing total proceeds. These products are still small relative to the core portfolio but represent intelligent extensions of the franchise.

Source: FMP ratios, cash flow statements. DPS derived from quarterly filings showing $0.177/share declarations.

Here is where the analysis gets uncomfortable. GAAP earnings cover the dividend with room to spare -- the payout ratio is a conservative 44.5%. But operating cash flow tells a very different story.

**The GAAP vs. Cash Problem:**

SAFE's revenue is predominantly non-cash. Interest income from sales-type leases ($286M in FY2025) is largely accounting income from the accretion of the ground lease receivable, not cash collected from tenants. The Q1 2025 cash flow statement shows a $24.4M adjustment for non-cash interest income from sales-type leases against $69.7M of recognized interest income -- meaning roughly 65% of that line item is non-cash in any given quarter.

Similarly, deferred operating lease income ($7.6M adjustment in Q1 2025) reflects straight-line rent recognition that exceeds cash rent collected.

The result: GAAP net income of $114.6M, but operating cash flow of only $47.8M. The dividend outflow of ~$50.9M exceeds OCF. This is funded by the difference between GAAP income and cash income, plus the revolving credit facility.

For a traditional REIT, this would be a red flag. For SAFE, the argument is that the non-cash income represents genuine economic accretion of the ground lease asset -- it is real value being created, just not in the form of current-period cash. This is conceptually similar to how a zero-coupon bond accrues income that is real but not cash-payable until maturity. The analogy is imperfect but not unreasonable.

**Our assessment:** The dividend is safe for the next 2-3 years. SAFE has $1.2B of undrawn revolver capacity, $21.7M of cash, a $300M ATM shelf (unused), and a $50M buyback authorization. The liquidity cushion is substantial. But if interest rates remain elevated and office values continue declining, the structural cash deficit will widen. A dividend cut is not imminent, but it is not unthinkable on a 3-5 year horizon.

A critical nuance: SAFE's REIT distribution requirement is based on REIT taxable income, not GAAP income or cash flow. The company must distribute at least 90% of REIT taxable income annually. Because ground lease accretion is largely non-taxable (it increases basis, not taxable income), the actual REIT distribution requirement is likely significantly lower than the current $0.708/share. This means management has real optionality -- it could cut the dividend and still comply with REIT requirements, or it could maintain the dividend and fund the cash shortfall from the revolver. The choice to freeze rather than cut signals that management views the current level as sustainable but does not have the confidence to raise it.

The 4% nondeductible excise tax on undistributed REIT income provides an additional incentive to distribute, but at current levels the dividend appears to exceed the minimum requirement, giving management meaningful discretion.

## PART VII -- THE CARET PROGRAM: HIDDEN VALUE OR GOVERNANCE RISK?

The Caret program is SAFE's attempt to monetize the Unrealized Capital Appreciation in the residual portfolio. It works by splitting the ground lease portfolio into two components:

1. **GL units** -- track the bond-like income stream (rent + return of investment basis). SAFE holds all GL units.
2. **Caret units** -- track the UCA above cost basis. Distributed among SAFE (83.8% as of YE2025), management/employees (~14.9% of outstanding, including 6.1% held directly/indirectly by CEO Jay Sugarman), and outside investors (MSD Partners affiliates + others).

**Our opinion on CARET:** The concept is intellectually elegant but practically problematic. The UCA only becomes realizable at lease expiration (decades away) or upon disposition. In the meantime, Caret unit holders have an economic interest that can create conflicts with common shareholders:

- Management may prefer investments that maximize long-term UCA (Caret value) over near-term cash distributions to GL unit holders (and therefore common shareholders).
- Caret distributions are NOT reduced for interest and principal repayment obligations (SAFE 2025 10-K Risk Factors). This means debt service comes out of the GL unit cash flows, not the Caret stream. The common shareholder bears the leverage risk; the Caret holder does not.
- The Caret units have not achieved public market liquidity. The February 2022 investors exercised redemption rights in April 2024 when public listing was not achieved by the two-year deadline. This is not a confidence-inspiring outcome.

CEO Jay Sugarman's 6.1% direct/indirect Caret ownership creates a governance concern. His economic incentives are partially decoupled from common shareholders. This is not necessarily nefarious -- Sugarman also holds significant common equity through Star Holdings and directly -- but it is a structural misalignment that investors should price in.

## PART VIII -- THE PARK HOTELS PROBLEM

On October 22, 2025, SAFE sent a termination notice for all five Park Hotels properties and filed litigation (In re Park Hotels Litigation, C.A. No. 2025-1210-LWW, Delaware Court of Chancery) alleging breaches related to maintenance and operations (SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 3). The Park Tenant disputes termination and has filed counterclaims.

This is a material event. The Park Hotels Portfolio represents 3.2% of gross book value and consists of five hotel properties under a single master lease with percentage rent. The lease had an initial maturity of December 2025, with individual asset extension rights. The tenant extended three of five hotels. SAFE is arguing the lease should be terminated entirely due to maintenance breaches.

**Scenario Analysis:**

| Outcome | Probability (est.) | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| SAFE wins termination, takes all 5 hotels | 30% | Positive long-term but operational risk -- SAFE has never run hotels. Capital expenditure required. Revenue from hotel operations beginning Q1 2026 (two properties already reverted Jan 1, 2026). |

The 10-K disclosure that SAFE "became responsible for operating two hotel properties that reverted to us" as of January 1, 2026 and expects to have "revenues and expenses associated with room occupancy, food and beverage services" going forward is a meaningful business model shift. Ground lease companies collect rent checks. Hotel operators manage housekeeping, F&B, and convention sales. These are fundamentally different businesses.

## PART IX -- INTEREST RATE SENSITIVITY

SAFE is, by any measure, one of the most rate-sensitive equity REITs in the market. Beta of 1.85 confirms this empirically. The mechanism is straightforward:

Ground leases are ultra-long-duration assets. A 99-year lease with 2.5-3.5% rent escalators has a duration somewhere between 25-40 years, depending on assumptions. When discount rates rise, the present value of these long-dated cash flows falls dramatically.

On the liability side, ~34% of debt is floating-rate or near-term (revolver + trust preferreds + commercial paper capacity). Every 100bps increase in SOFR adds approximately $8-10M of annual interest expense on the drawn revolver balance alone.

**Rate Scenario Table:**

| 10-Year Treasury | Estimated Impact on SAFE NAV | Dividend Safety |
|---|---|---|
| < 3.50% | NAV recovery to $25-30+; strong upside | Very Safe |
| 3.50 - 4.25% | Modest NAV recovery to $18-22 | Safe |
| 4.25 - 5.00% (current) | Status quo; NAV ~$15-18 | Adequate |
| 5.00 - 5.50% | Further NAV pressure; stock $10-13 | Under Pressure |
| > 5.50% | Severe stress; potential dividend cut discussion | At Risk |

This is a crude framework, but it captures the essential dynamic. SAFE is a levered long-duration bet. If you are bullish on rates declining over the next 3-5 years, SAFE offers enormous asymmetric upside. If you think rates are going higher, there are few worse places to be in the REIT universe.

**Hedging Activity:** SAFE uses interest rate swaps to manage floating-rate exposure. As of Q3 2025, accumulated other comprehensive income included $41.1M related to derivatives (down from $49.0M at YE2024), reflecting mark-to-market losses on swap positions as rates fluctuated. The Q1 2025 10-Q shows a $13.1M unrealized loss on derivatives in the first quarter alone -- a single quarter's derivative mark wiped out 25% of the quarter's net income. These swaps hedge the economic risk but create GAAP earnings volatility.

**Duration Mismatch as a Feature, Not a Bug:** Management would argue that the duration mismatch between long-dated assets and shorter-dated liabilities is deliberate -- it captures the positive slope of the yield curve. In a normal yield curve environment (short rates below long rates), SAFE borrows short at SOFR+85bps and lends long at 4-6% effective yields. The spread is the business model. The problem is that inverted or flat yield curves, which have persisted for much of 2023-2025, compress or eliminate this spread. SAFE needs the yield curve to normalize to return to its historical earnings trajectory.

**Peer Comparison on Rate Sensitivity:** Among REITs, SAFE's beta of 1.85 is among the highest. For comparison, typical net lease REITs (NNN, O, STORE) carry betas of 0.7-1.0. Even mortgage REITs (AGNC, NLY), which are explicitly leveraged rate bets, often have betas of 1.3-1.6. SAFE's stock behaves more like a 20-year Treasury strip than a REIT.

**Executive Change in Control Severance Plan:** The 10-K discloses a newly adopted Executive Change in Control Severance Plan (Exhibit 10.31). This is standard corporate governance, but its timing -- adopted alongside the Michael Trachtenberg hiring -- could signal either routine modernization or preparedness for a scenario where control changes hands. Given that Star Holdings' 18.8% stake will eventually be sold into the market, a change of control provision is prudent.

**Our valuation framework:**

SAFE should be valued on an adjusted NAV basis, not earnings multiples. The GAAP book value of $33.59/share understates NAV because it excludes the UCA ($9.3B / 71.8M shares = ~$129/share of UCA). Even discounting the UCA at 80% for time value and uncertainty, and subtracting all debt at face value, we arrive at an adjusted NAV of approximately $55-65 per share. The stock is trading at 23-27% of adjusted NAV.

The counterargument: UCA is theoretical. Much of it sits behind 70+ year leases. The present value of $1 receivable in 70 years at a 5% discount rate is $0.03. If most of the UCA is concentrated in the long-dated portion of the lease portfolio, its present value contribution to NAV is negligible.

Truth, as usual, is somewhere in between. At $14.90, the market is pricing in either (a) permanent value destruction in the office portfolio, (b) a forced equity raise at distressed prices, or (c) a structural inability to ever close the gap between GAAP income and cash generation. None of these is our base case, but none is impossible.

**The "What If They Raise Equity" Scenario:** SAFE has a $300M ATM shelf and has sold zero shares. At $14.90, selling $300M of stock would require issuing ~20.1M new shares, diluting existing holders by 28%. Management knows this. The ATM is a backstop, not a plan. But if the Park Hotels litigation goes badly or office defaults accelerate, the ATM becomes the lender of last resort. This is the scenario bears are implicitly pricing.

## PART XII -- CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION

**Rating: SPECULATIVE BUY -- Dividend Safety 6/10**

Safehold is the most intellectually interesting REIT in the market and one of the most polarizing. The ground lease model is genuinely innovative -- senior capital stack position, contractual escalators, embedded residual value, investment-grade balance sheet. On every structural metric, SAFE looks like it should trade at a premium to book value.

But it trades at 44 cents on the dollar, and the market is not stupid. The leverage is extreme, the office exposure is structural, the dividend is frozen, the CEO's incentives are partially misaligned through Caret, and the company is now -- for the first time -- operating hotels. These are not temporary headwinds that resolve with a rate cut. They are fundamental characteristics of the current business.

For an investor with a 5-10 year horizon, high conviction in declining interest rates, and comfort with the office exposure, SAFE at $14.90 offers asymmetric upside. A return to $25-30 (still well below book value) represents 70-100% upside plus ~5% annual dividend yield. The downside is capped by book value support, investment-grade credit ratings, and the contractual nature of the revenue stream.

For a dividend-focused investor seeking safety and predictable income growth, SAFE is the wrong stock. The dividend is frozen, the cash flow coverage is negative, and the rate sensitivity makes the payout vulnerable in a sustained high-rate environment. There are dozens of REITs with higher yields, better coverage, and less volatility.

**Position sizing recommendation:** No more than 2-3% of a diversified portfolio. This is a high-conviction, high-volatility position that requires patience and tolerance for drawdowns. Add on weakness below $13; reduce above $20.
