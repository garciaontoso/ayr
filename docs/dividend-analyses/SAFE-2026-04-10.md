## PART I -- Executive Summary

Safehold Inc. is the only publicly traded pure-play ground lease REIT in the United States, and perhaps the most polarizing small-cap REIT in the market today. The company owns land underneath $15.9 billion worth of commercial real estate, collected via 164 ground leases across major U.S. metropolitan areas. At a reference price near $13.50, the stock trades at roughly 0.40x book value -- a discount that implies the market assigns zero value to $9.3 billion in Unrealized Capital Appreciation embedded in the residual portfolio. The annualized dividend of $0.708 per share produces a current yield of approximately 5.3%, and has been frozen at this level since 2023. GAAP earnings of $1.59 per share (FY2025) comfortably cover the dividend at a 44.5% payout ratio, but operating cash flow of only $47.8 million against a $50.9 million annual dividend payment tells a far more troubling story. The GAAP-to-cash gap is structural and inherent to the ground lease accounting model, not a temporary distortion.

Three forces dominate the investment case. First, the company carries $4.6 billion of total debt against $2.4 billion of equity, producing leverage ratios that are approximately double the typical net lease REIT. Each 100 basis point move in long-term interest rates mechanically destroys or creates 15-20% of equity value, giving the stock a beta of 1.85 -- more bond proxy than equity. Second, 39% of the ground lease portfolio by gross book value is exposed to the office sector, which remains under structural pressure from remote work, rising vacancy rates, and declining valuations. The 10-K explicitly acknowledges that certain office assets currently have material vacancies and that a forbearance agreement is in place on a significant New York office asset. Third, the Park Hotels litigation -- in which Safehold terminated the master lease on five hotels and is now in Delaware Chancery Court -- introduces binary outcome risk on 3.2% of portfolio book value and has pushed the company into its first-ever hotel operating role as of January 1, 2026.

On the positive side, investment-grade credit ratings from all three major agencies (A3/A-/A-, all stable), $1.2 billion of undrawn revolver capacity, no near-term debt maturities through 2029, and the November 2025 S&P upgrade to A- collectively signal a well-managed balance sheet. The Q4 2025 earnings call introduced Michael Trachtenberg as President, signaling potential strategic evolution, and management articulated three concrete priorities for 2026: accelerate origination volume, create Caret visibility, and begin share buybacks under the $50 million authorization from February 2025.

Our verdict is HOLD. The dividend is safe for the next 24-36 months but lacks any growth catalyst. The extreme rate sensitivity makes the position a leveraged macro bet rather than a traditional dividend holding. Investors who believe long-term rates will decline meaningfully over the next 3-5 years have an asymmetric setup; investors seeking predictable, growing income should look elsewhere.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield / Payout ratio / Streak: ~$13.50 / ~5.3% / 44.5% GAAP, ~106% OCF / 0 years growth (frozen since 2023)
- Last dividend hike: 2023 (to $0.708 from $0.70, +1.1%)
- 5y dividend CAGR: ~2.0% (2020-2025, now stalled)
- Net debt / EBITDA: ~13.7x (FMP: EBITDA $332.8M, net debt ~$4.56B)
- Free cash flow coverage: <1.0x (OCF $47.8M vs dividend ~$50.9M)

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

Safehold Inc. is a Maryland corporation headquartered at 1114 Avenue of the Americas, New York. The company acquires, manages, and capitalizes ground leases on commercial real estate throughout the United States. It operates through a single reportable segment and conducts all business through Safehold GL Holdings LLC ("Portfolio Holdings"), a Delaware limited liability company.

The company traces its lineage to iStar Inc., a diversified real estate finance company founded by Jay Sugarman in the 1990s. iStar created the modern ground lease concept through its subsidiary "Safety, Income & Growth" (Old SAFE), which completed its initial public offering in June 2017. On March 31, 2023, iStar merged into Old SAFE, with iStar surviving and renaming itself Safehold Inc. The merger spun off iStar's legacy non-ground-lease assets into Star Holdings, a Maryland statutory trust that Safehold externally manages for declining fees. This corporate simplification was a crucial milestone that resolved years of structural complexity and related-party conflicts. As of December 31, 2025, the company had 72 employees, triple-A-category credit ratings, and a portfolio of 164 ground lease assets.

- **Sector / Industry:** Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) -- Ground Lease
- **Business segments:** Single segment -- ground lease acquisition, management, and capitalization
- **Geographic exposure:** 100% United States; top markets by gross book value: Manhattan 22% (27% including broader NY MSA), Washington DC ~8%, Boston ~7%, Los Angeles ~5%, San Francisco ~4%, Denver ~3%, Honolulu ~3%, Nashville ~3%, Miami ~2%, Atlanta ~2%
- **Portfolio composition by property type:** Multifamily 42%, Office 39%, Hotel 11%, Life Science 6%, Mixed Use/Other 2%

**The Affordable Housing Pivot -- A Strategic Inflection:**

The most significant strategic development in FY2025 is the pivot toward affordable and workforce housing ground leases. Of the 17 new ground leases originated in FY2025, twelve were classified as affordable housing -- a category that did not exist in SAFE's portfolio before 2024. This pivot is strategic on multiple levels:

First, affordable housing benefits from structural government support. Federal Low-Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC), state housing finance agency subsidies, and municipal zoning incentives create a more predictable tenant capital stack than market-rate development. Ground rent coverage for affordable housing tends to be higher and more stable because project economics are supported by tax credits and government subsidies rather than purely market rents.

Second, the political optics of providing ground lease capital for affordable housing are vastly more favorable than financing luxury office towers or hotels. In an era of increasing regulatory scrutiny of institutional real estate ownership, positioning as a facilitator of affordable housing construction creates a beneficial narrative that could protect against adverse legislation.

Third, the naming of Michael Trachtenberg's inducement RSU award plan as the "Affordable Housing Plan" (Exhibit 10.28 to the FY2025 10-K) signals that this is not a tactical allocation but a core strategic priority tied to senior executive compensation. This is the clearest evidence that SAFE's origination pipeline will shift materially toward residential housing ground leases in 2026-2027.

The risk is execution: affordable housing ground leases are typically smaller ($10-30 million) than SAFE's historical institutional deals ($50-200 million), requiring more originations to deploy the same capital. The infrastructure for sourcing, underwriting, and managing a higher-volume, lower-ticket portfolio is still being built.

## PART III -- The Ground Lease Model: Why It Matters

Ground leases are the most senior position in the commercial real estate capital stack -- senior to first mortgage debt, mezzanine debt, preferred equity, and common equity. When a building owner enters into a ground lease with Safehold, the owner retains the building and all improvements but leases the land from Safehold on terms typically ranging from 30 to 99 years. The lease is triple-net, meaning the tenant is responsible for all taxes, maintenance, insurance, operating costs, and capital expenditures. If the building owner defaults on the ground lease, the land and all improvements revert to Safehold at zero incremental cost. This creates an extraordinarily powerful incentive for tenants and their lenders to prioritize ground lease payments -- it is the very last obligation they would stop paying, because defaulting means losing everything above it in the capital stack.

**Capital Stack Position:**
```
[Ground Lease Holder / Land Owner]  <-- SAFE sits here (most senior)
    |
[First Mortgage Lender]
    |
[Mezzanine / Preferred Equity]
    |
[Common Equity Owner / Building Operator]
    |
[Building Tenant / Occupier]
```

Safehold targets ground leases where the initial cost represents 30-45% of Combined Property Value. As of December 31, 2025, actual gross book value as a percentage of Combined Property Value was 52%, having drifted above the origination target primarily due to office value declines compressing the denominator. This deterioration in the effective loan-to-value ratio is a warning sign that warrants ongoing monitoring, even though it does not represent a cash loss to Safehold.

**The Unrealized Capital Appreciation (UCA):**

| Metric | Dec 31, 2025 | Dec 31, 2024 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Combined Property Value | $15,947M | $15,523M | +$424M |
| Ground Lease Cost (SAFE's basis) | $6,675M | $6,395M | +$280M |
| Unrealized Capital Appreciation | $9,272M | $9,128M | +$144M |

Source: SAFE 2025 10-K ITEM 1. Combined Property Value includes pro-rata share of unconsolidated ventures and $616.2M related to unfunded commitments (2025).

The UCA of $9.3 billion is the theoretical value that would accrue to Safehold at lease expiration when all buildings and improvements revert to the ground lease holder at no cost. However, the vast majority of these lease expirations are 60 to 99 years in the future. Discounting $9.3 billion at any reasonable rate over 70+ years produces a present value measured in the low hundreds of millions. The market appears to be performing exactly this calculation and assigning negligible present value to the UCA. The Q4 2025 earnings call showed the first quarterly UCA increase in several periods, which management highlighted as a potential inflection driven by new originations rather than office revaluation.

**CPI Escalators and Their Limitations:** Most leases include CPI-linked rent adjustments starting between years 11 and 21, typically capped at 3.0-3.5% cumulative. The 10-K explicitly states that "in the event cumulative inflation growth for the lookback period exceeds the cap, these rent adjustments may not keep up fully with changes in inflation." This means Safehold's inflation hedge is partial, not complete. In a sustained high-inflation environment, real rent growth could lag, reducing the long-term compounding advantage that bulls emphasize. The company has CPI lookbacks in 81% of its ground leases, per the Q4 2025 earnings call.

**Ground Rent Coverage:** As of December 31, 2025, estimated portfolio Ground Rent Coverage was 3.4x, defined as the ratio of a property's net operating income to the annualized ground rent payable to Safehold. This means the buildings above Safehold's land generate, on average, 3.4 times the rent it charges. This coverage ratio provides a substantial cushion against tenant default. However, the 10-K warns that this metric relies on property NOI as reported by tenants, and that Safehold uses estimates for properties in development, transition, or where current financials are unavailable. Individual distressed office properties almost certainly have coverage well below the portfolio average.

**New Products and Pipeline Expansion:** Safehold has developed ancillary products to broaden its origination pipeline. The Leasehold Loan Fund (currently three assets) and Ground Lease Plus Fund (two assets) offer tenants a full capital stack solution. "SAFExSWAP" enables exchange into Safehold's modern ground lease structure. "SAFExSELL" allows sellers to create a ground lease at time of property sale, increasing total proceeds. These remain small relative to the core portfolio but represent intelligent franchise extensions. In 2025, the company also began originating individual leasehold loans outside the fund structure, adding $152 million across four loans for the year.

**Inflection points:**
1. **2019-2020: Rapid origination expansion.** Revenue grew from $116M to $159M as the company aggressively deployed capital into new ground leases across multifamily and office. Debt grew from $1.1B to $1.7B. The stock peaked above $90 in late 2021 as low interest rates made the duration bet enormously profitable.
2. **2022: Rate shock begins.** The Federal Reserve commenced its aggressive rate-hiking cycle. Interest expense nearly doubled from $80M to $129M. Revenue continued growing on new originations, but the cost of that growth was rising rapidly. The stock fell from $60 to $30.
3. **2023: The iStar merger and nadir.** The March 2023 merger brought iStar's legacy complexity into SAFE, triggering a $145.4M goodwill impairment and $17.9M in other charges. The net loss of $55M was entirely non-cash and merger-related, but it destroyed the earnings continuity story. The dividend was frozen. The stock fell below $20.
4. **2024-2025: Stabilization at a lower level.** Net income recovered to $106M (2024) and $114M (2025). Revenue growth slowed to 5.4% as origination volume moderated. The balance sheet was strengthened through the $2.0B revolver, $700M in senior notes, the S&P upgrade, and the $400M unsecured term loan. But the stock continued drifting lower to mid-teens as rates remained elevated.

The critical story these numbers tell: revenue and assets have compounded at ~19%, but debt has compounded at ~22% and interest expense at ~27%. The spread between asset growth and liability growth is the fundamental structural problem.

**The GAAP vs. Cash Problem -- The Most Important Paragraph in This Report:**

Safehold's revenue is predominantly non-cash. Interest income from sales-type leases ($286M in FY2025) is largely accounting income from the accretion of the ground lease receivable, not cash collected from tenants. The Q1 2025 cash flow statement shows a $24.4 million adjustment for non-cash interest income from sales-type leases against $69.7 million of recognized interest income -- meaning roughly 65% of that line item is non-cash in any given quarter. The nine-month Q3 2025 cash flow statement confirms this at scale: $75.3 million non-cash interest adjustment against $212.7 million of recognized interest income (35% non-cash for the period).

Deferred operating lease income adds another layer: $22.8 million in the nine months through September 2025 reflects straight-line rent recognition exceeding cash rent collected. The result: GAAP net income of $114.6 million, but operating cash flow of only $47.8 million for FY2025 (up from $37.9 million in FY2024, per the 10-K). The dividend outflow of approximately $50.9 million exceeds OCF. This structural cash deficit is funded through the revolving credit facility.

For a traditional REIT, this would be a disqualifying red flag. For Safehold, the argument -- which has merit -- is that the non-cash income represents genuine economic accretion of the ground lease asset. The ground lease receivable is increasing in value, and this accretion is real economic value creation, simply not in the form of current-period cash. The analogy to zero-coupon bond accretion is imperfect but not unreasonable. The cash will eventually be collected as the ground lease matures and either continues generating rent or reverts the property.

**REIT Distribution Requirement and Management Discretion:** Safehold must distribute at least 90% of REIT taxable income annually. Because ground lease accretion is largely non-taxable (it increases basis without generating current taxable income), the actual REIT distribution requirement is likely significantly below the current $0.708 per share. This means management has real optionality: it could cut the dividend and still comply with REIT requirements, or maintain the dividend and fund the cash shortfall from the revolver. The choice to freeze rather than cut signals that management views the current level as sustainable but lacks the confidence to raise it. The 4% nondeductible excise tax on undistributed REIT income provides an additional incentive to distribute, but the current dividend appears to exceed the minimum requirement by a comfortable margin.

But if interest rates remain elevated and office values continue declining, the structural cash deficit will widen. A dividend cut is not imminent, but it is not unthinkable on a 3-5 year horizon if rates do not normalize.

## PART VI -- Business Quality and Moat

- **Moat type:** Regulatory / structural barriers to entry + first-mover scale advantage
- **Moat trajectory:** Stable, with early signs of widening through product innovation
- **Pricing power evidence:** Contractual rent escalators provide built-in pricing power; 2025 originations underwritten at 7.3% economic yield
- **Return on invested capital trend:** Difficult to assess on traditional ROIC basis due to ground lease accounting; GAAP interest income yield on portfolio is 5.4% annualized

Safehold possesses what might be called a "conceptual moat" -- the company invented the modern institutional ground lease and has spent nine years building the infrastructure, legal frameworks, credit rating history, and customer relationships that would take a competitor years to replicate. The investment-grade ratings from all three agencies are particularly important, as they enable Safehold to borrow at spreads that make ground lease origination economically attractive. A new entrant would need to build a portfolio of sufficient size and credit quality to achieve investment-grade ratings before it could compete on cost of capital.

The Caret program, however it is ultimately valued, represents intellectual property that has no parallel in the market. The ability to bifurcate ground lease value into bond-like (GL units) and equity-like (Caret units) components is a structural innovation that could eventually unlock monetization pathways.

The moat's vulnerability lies in the product's relative simplicity. A ground lease is fundamentally a land lease -- sophisticated real estate investors, private equity firms, and insurance companies could replicate the structure if the economics become sufficiently attractive. The 10-K acknowledges competition from "numerous commercial developers, real estate companies (including other REITs), financial institutions (such as banks and insurance companies) and other investors." As rates rose and ground lease spreads widened, new competitors entered the market, and the 10-K explicitly warns this "may result in higher costs for properties, lower returns and impact our ability to grow."

## PART VII -- Capital Allocation

Capital allocation at Safehold must be evaluated through the lens of a company that is simultaneously managing a maturing portfolio, servicing significant debt, and attempting to grow through new originations while maintaining a frozen dividend.

1. **New ground lease originations:** $277M across 17 ground leases in FY2025, plus $152M across 4 leasehold loans. Total capital committed: $429M. FY2025 originations were an acceleration from FY2024 and were concentrated in affordable housing (12 of 17 ground leases) and multifamily (4 of 17), with one hotel. Economic yield on new originations: 7.3% with 3.2x coverage and 34% GLTV. This is intelligent deployment at attractive spreads.
2. **Dividends:** ~$50.9M annually, consuming approximately 106% of OCF and 44.5% of GAAP net income. The dividend is being funded partially from the revolver, which is a concerning but not unusual dynamic for ground lease economics.
3. **Debt management:** $400M unsecured term loan (November 2025) replaced the nearest maturity. $2.0B revolver (April 2024) replaced prior facilities. $700M in senior notes issued across 2024. Defeasance of $227M mortgage eliminated 2027 maturity risk. This is proactive, intelligent liability management.
4. **Buybacks:** $50M authorized in February 2025, but zero shares repurchased through December 31, 2025. The Q4 earnings call acknowledged buybacks as a 2026 priority, with management noting they want to execute in a "leverage-neutral way," potentially through capital recycling. The failure to buy back any stock at $13-15 (well below book value of $33.59) is disappointing.
5. **Equity issuance:** $300M ATM shelf authorized in April 2023. Zero shares sold as of December 31, 2025. This is a backstop, not a plan, but at current prices, issuing equity would be massively dilutive (20M+ new shares).

**Capital allocation grade:** B-minus. The debt management has been excellent, and origination discipline is sound. But three years of frozen dividends, zero buyback execution despite massive price-to-book discount, and the structural dependence on the revolver to fund dividends from cash flow warrant a below-average grade. The affordable housing pivot is strategically smart but unproven at scale.

**Key Debt Observations:**

1. **No near-term maturity wall.** The October 2025 defeasance of $227M mortgage due April 2027, replaced by the $400M unsecured term loan maturing November 2030 (with two one-year extension options), eliminated the most pressing refinancing risk. No significant maturity until the revolver in May 2029 (with extension options).

2. **The stairstep notes are creative but deceptive.** The 5.15% notes pay only 2.50% cash interest for years 1-10. The difference accrues as PIK interest added to principal. The $150M face value has already grown to approximately $160M. By maturity in 2052, compounding will significantly inflate the principal. This is real economic cost disguised as low current cash expense.

3. **Secured mortgage portfolio is genuinely well-matched.** Non-recourse, full-term interest-only, weighted average maturity of approximately 26 years at ~4.03%. This long-duration, low-cost financing matches the long-duration ground lease assets. This is the strongest part of the capital structure.

4. **Hedging program provides near-term protection.** Per the Q4 earnings call: $500M SOFR swap locked at 3% through April 2028, plus $250M of long-term Treasury locks at a weighted average rate of 4% with approximately $30M in current gain position.

5. **$1.2 billion of undrawn revolver capacity** plus the $750M commercial paper program (zero drawn at year-end) provides substantial liquidity. However, the revolver is floating-rate, making heavy draws expensive in a high-rate environment.

## PART XI -- Risks and Stress Tests

1. **Interest rate risk (probability: HIGH, severity: HIGH, mitigant: hedging, long-duration debt).** SAFE's beta of 1.85 confirms the stock behaves more like a 20-year Treasury strip than a REIT. The 10-K sensitivity table shows that a 100bps increase in floating rates reduces annual net income by approximately $7.0 million. But the equity price impact is far larger -- each 100bps in the 10-year Treasury destroys 15-20% of equity value through duration-driven multiple compression. The $500M SOFR swap at 3% through April 2028 provides near-term protection, but the fundamental duration mismatch between 70-year assets and 5-18 year liabilities is a structural feature, not a bug that hedging can fully address.

2. **Office concentration risk (probability: MEDIUM-HIGH, severity: HIGH, mitigant: 3.4x coverage, senior position).** The 39% office exposure by gross book value is concentrated in Manhattan (top three assets = 14.3% of GBV, all NYC office). The 10-K explicitly warns that "the rise in interest rates has adversely affected the U.S. office sector" and that "certain office assets currently have material vacancies." A forbearance agreement on a "significant New York office asset" (identity undisclosed) is a red flag. The Q4 earnings call provided mild reassurance -- Michael Trachtenberg noted "a strengthening in some core markets like New York" but acknowledged "other places are a little bit behind." On the positive side, the ground lease sits senior to all other claims, and a tenant default triggers reversion of all improvements to Safehold. But operating a defaulted office building is fundamentally different from collecting ground rent.

3. **Park Hotels litigation (probability: BINARY, severity: MODERATE, mitigant: 3.2% of portfolio).** The October 22, 2025 termination notice and Delaware Chancery Court litigation introduces binary outcome risk. Two hotels already reverted to Safehold as of January 1, 2026, making the company a hotel operator for the first time. The Q1 2026 financials will be the first to include hotel operating revenue and expenses. A ground lease company entering the hotel business -- even involuntarily -- should concern every shareholder.

**Park Hotels Litigation -- The Full Story:**

The Park Hotels dispute is the single most unusual event in Safehold's history and merits comprehensive analysis. On October 22, 2025, Safehold terminated the master lease covering five hotels (Hilton Hawaiian Village, Hilton New Orleans Riverside, Casa Marina Key West, W New York Union Square, and Hilton San Francisco Union Square) after Park Hotels & Resorts failed to pay ground rent. The termination triggered a dispute that is now in Delaware Chancery Court.

The background: Park Hotels rejected the Hilton San Francisco Union Square's ground lease in bankruptcy in 2023, ceasing rent payments on that property. The master lease structure meant that default on one property could theoretically cascade to the other four. Safehold exercised its termination rights on the master lease, asserting that the combined default entitled it to take possession of all five hotels. Park Hotels disputes this interpretation, arguing the default was limited to the San Francisco property.

As of January 1, 2026, Safehold took operational control of two of the five hotels (the Q4 2025 earnings call confirmed this without identifying which two). This means SAFE is now, involuntarily, a hotel operator -- generating hotel operating revenue and incurring hotel operating expenses for the first time in its history. The Q1 2026 10-Q will be the first to reflect this operating income.

The financial exposure is material but bounded: the five Park Hotels assets represent approximately 3.2% of portfolio gross book value. In the bear case (litigation loss, Safehold required to return hotels and loses lease termination claim), the impact would be approximately $200-250 million in write-downs. In the bull case (litigation win, Safehold retains all five hotels and either re-leases, sells, or operates them), the value of the improvements that revert to Safehold could be substantial -- hotel assets of this caliber in Hawaii and New Orleans have significant residual value.



4. **Star Holdings overhang (probability: MEDIUM, severity: LOW-MEDIUM, mitigant: governance agreement).** Star Holdings holds ~13.5M shares (18.8% of outstanding). As it completes orderly liquidation, these shares will be sold into the market, creating persistent selling pressure. The declining management fee (from $25M to $7.5M and eventually lower) adds a known revenue headwind.


**S&P Upgrade Context and Implications:**

The November 2025 upgrade from BBB+ to A- by S&P Global was the culmination of a multi-year credit trajectory. Safehold now holds investment-grade ratings from all three major agencies at the single-A level (A3/A-/A-), with stable outlooks across the board. This achievement is significant for several reasons:

First, the A-minus rating enables access to the investment-grade commercial paper market at spreads approximately 50-75 basis points tighter than BBB-rated issuers. For a company that routinely draws on its $2.0 billion revolver to fund operations and originations, lower short-term borrowing costs directly improve net interest income.

Second, the rating positions Safehold as a preferred counterparty for institutional ground lease transactions. Large developers, pension funds, and insurance companies that are restricted to investing with investment-grade counterparties can now more easily transact with SAFE.

Third, the $750 million commercial paper program (zero drawn at year-end 2025) becomes a viable, low-cost funding source for new originations, reducing dependence on the revolver for day-to-day liquidity management.

The risk to the credit rating comes primarily from leverage. At 1.88x debt-to-equity, SAFE operates near the upper bound of its 2:1 target. Each $240 million of new funding increases leverage by approximately one-tenth of a turn, per the CFO's Q4 2025 call commentary. If leverage exceeds 2.0x due to a combination of new originations and office-related write-downs, the rating agencies could place the outlook on negative watch -- reversing the hard-won upgrade.


5. **Leverage and refinancing risk (probability: LOW near-term, severity: HIGH if realized, mitigant: rating upgrades, no near-term maturities).** At 2.0x debt-to-equity and ~$4.9B total debt, Safehold is one of the most leveraged REITs in the market. The covenant package (1.25x unencumbered assets to unsecured debt, 1.15x fixed charge coverage, 50% secured debt to total assets cap) provides guardrails. The company confirmed covenant compliance as of December 31, 2025. But every $240M of new funding increases leverage by one-tenth of a turn, per the CFO's Q4 call commentary, limiting growth capacity without capital recycling.

## PART XII -- The Caret Program: Hidden Value or Governance Risk?

The Caret program deserves its own section because it is simultaneously the bull case's most powerful argument and the governance skeptic's most troubling concern. Caret units are designed to track the UCA above cost basis in Safehold's ground lease portfolio. As of December 31, 2025, Safehold holds 83.8% of outstanding Caret units. Management and employees hold approximately 14.9% (including 6.1% held directly and indirectly by CEO Jay Sugarman). Outside investors (MSD Partners affiliates and others) hold the remainder.

The concept is elegant: separate the bond-like income stream (GL units, held entirely by Safehold) from the equity-like residual appreciation (Caret units, distributed among multiple holders). The bull case argues that Caret is worth $50-130+ per SAFE share if the UCA is eventually realized. The bear case counters that (a) the UCA is decades from realization, (b) Caret unit holders do not bear the cost of debt service or leverage risk, and (c) management's Caret ownership creates a structural misalignment where leadership may prefer investments that maximize long-term UCA over near-term cash distributions.

The failed public listing attempt is instructive. In February 2022, Safehold sold 137,142 Caret units to investors and agreed to use commercially reasonable efforts to provide public market liquidity within two years. By February 2024, no listing had been achieved. In April 2024, all investors exercised their redemption rights at original purchase price. This is not a confidence-inspiring outcome for a product meant to unlock hidden value.

On the Q4 2025 call, Sugarman called Caret "Safehold's trust fund" and emphasized the company's intention to find "liquidity or sales or monetization of some sort" to help investors recognize the value. This remains aspirational rather than concrete.

## PART XIII -- Interest Rate Sensitivity: The Dominant Variable

Safehold is, by any quantitative measure, one of the most rate-sensitive equity REITs in the market. The mechanism is straightforward: ground leases are ultra-long-duration assets. A 99-year lease with 2.5-3.5% rent escalators has effective duration somewhere between 25 and 40 years. When discount rates rise, the present value of these long-dated cash flows falls dramatically.

**Rate Scenario Table:**

| 10-Year Treasury | Est. Impact on SAFE NAV | Dividend Safety |
|---|---|---|
| Below 3.50% | NAV recovery to $25-30+; strong upside | Very Safe |
| 3.50% - 4.25% | Modest NAV recovery to $18-22 | Safe |
| 4.25% - 5.00% (current range) | Status quo; stock $12-16 | Adequate |
| 5.00% - 5.50% | Further NAV pressure; stock $8-12 | Under Pressure |
| Above 5.50% | Severe stress; dividend cut risk | At Risk |

**Duration Mismatch as Business Model:** Management would argue the duration mismatch is the business model -- borrow at SOFR+85bps, lend at 5.9% economic yield, capture the term premium. In a normal upward-sloping yield curve environment, this spread is profitable. But inverted or flat yield curves, which persisted for much of 2023-2025, compress or eliminate this spread. The Q4 2025 earnings call noted that new originations were underwritten at 7.3% economic yield against an effective permanent debt cost of 4.3%, implying a healthy 300bps spread on new deployments. This is encouraging for the marginal investment but does not fix the legacy portfolio's lower-yielding vintage.

## PART XIV -- Thesis and Verdict

**Thesis (one paragraph):**
Safehold is a structurally sound but highly rate-sensitive ground lease REIT that offers asymmetric upside if long-term interest rates decline meaningfully over the next 3-5 years. The dividend is safe but frozen, the balance sheet is investment-grade but heavily leveraged, and the office exposure is a structural headwind that will take years to resolve. At 0.40x book value, the stock prices in significant pessimism. For a long-duration investor who is bullish on declining rates and comfortable with the volatility, SAFE at current levels is a reasonable hold with potential for meaningful capital appreciation. For a dividend-focused investor seeking predictable, growing income, this is the wrong stock -- there are dozens of REITs with higher yields, better cash flow coverage, and less volatility.

**Verdict:** HOLD
**Position sizing:** No more than 2-3% of a diversified portfolio. This is a high-volatility, high-conviction position that requires patience and tolerance for significant drawdowns.
