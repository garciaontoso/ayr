## TL;DR (10 lines)

1. **MDV is the only publicly traded REIT focused exclusively on single-tenant industrial manufacturing properties** -- a niche thesis built on the onshoring/reshoring tailwind that theoretically offers differentiated exposure versus the broader net-lease universe (NNN, O, EPRT, STAG). At $160M market cap with 42 properties and $39.1M ABR, this is a micro-cap that trades like a private equity vehicle with a ticker attached.
2. **AFFO per share was $1.38 in 2025 vs $1.34 in 2024** -- a 3.0% increase that is barely sufficient to cover the current $1.20 annual distribution. The AFFO payout ratio of 87% is uncomfortably high for a REIT with this level of leverage and concentration risk. There is almost no retained cash flow for organic growth.
3. **The dividend is real but precarious.** At $1.20/share annualized ($0.10/month), the yield of ~7.8% at $15.14 is attractive on paper. But with AFFO of $17.2M against total distributions to common + OP units + preferred of $15.9M in 2025, the cushion is approximately $1.3M -- essentially one bad tenant outcome away from a cut.
4. **Leverage is the elephant in the room.** Net debt of $254M against $33.2M EBITDA gives a net debt/EBITDA ratio of 7.7x -- well above the 5.5-6.5x range that institutional investors consider acceptable for net-lease REITs. The $250M term loan matures July 2028 (recently extended from January 2027), and refinancing risk in a higher-rate environment is the single largest threat to the dividend.
5. **The portfolio transformation from non-traded diversified REIT to listed industrial-focused REIT is 82% complete** (by ABR). CEO Aaron Halfacre has executed the strategic pivot with discipline: industrial ABR rose from ~50% in 2021 to 82% by December 2025, WALT extended to 14.0 years, and the team was cut from 12 to 9 employees. The question is whether the remaining 18% of non-core assets (KIA Carson, OES, Solar Turbines) can be recycled at acceptable prices.
6. **Tenant concentration is extreme.** Lindsay Corporation (9 properties, 14.5% of rental income) and KIA of Carson (1 property, 11.0%) together account for 25.5% of total rental income. A single tenant default could reduce AFFO by 10-15%, immediately threatening the distribution.
7. **The KIA Carson property is a paradox**: it represents 13.7% of total assets ($65.2M net carrying value), generates 11% of rental income, is technically a retail auto dealership (not industrial), and was acquired via OP Unit contribution at $25/unit -- now worth ~$15. It is the largest single asset and the hardest to sell.
8. **Interest expense consumed 38% of revenue in 2025** ($17.5M interest expense on $46.4M revenue). Even after the swap buydown to a 4.15% fixed rate for 2026, the debt service burden is structurally high because the $250M term loan is the dominant funding source for a $476M asset base.
9. **FFO per share declined from $1.50 to $1.32 in 2025** -- driven by a $5.8M impairment charge on the Saint Paul, Minnesota property. AFFO, which strips out impairments and non-cash items, improved modestly, but the FFO trajectory reveals the fragility of the earnings base.
10. **Verdict: HOLD at $15.14. The yield is compelling but the risk profile is too concentrated for aggressive accumulation. BUY below $13 where the yield exceeds 9% and prices in meaningful credit risk. ## PART I -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD at $15.14 -- Buy below $13 -- Trim above $17 -- Dividend Safety: 5/10 -- Confidence: Medium**

Modiv Industrial occupies a genuinely unique position in the listed REIT universe. No other publicly traded REIT focuses specifically on single-tenant industrial manufacturing properties with sale-leaseback structures and 15+ year lease terms. That differentiation matters for investors who believe in the structural onshoring thesis -- the idea that tariffs, supply chain diversification, and geopolitical risk will drive sustained demand for domestic manufacturing real estate. Aaron Halfacre, the CEO since the pre-listing era, has articulated this vision clearly and executed the portfolio repositioning with notable discipline, taking the company from a non-traded diversified REIT with office and retail exposure to a listed industrial-focused platform.

But differentiation and execution discipline do not automatically translate into a safe dividend. MDV's fundamental challenge is structural: a $476M asset base funded by $268M of debt (56% debt-to-assets), generating $46.4M of revenue and only $17.2M of AFFO, against which $15.9M of distributions must be paid to common stockholders, OP unit holders, and preferred stockholders. The math leaves approximately $1.3M of retained cash flow -- roughly $0.10 per fully diluted share/unit. That is not a margin of safety; it is a rounding error.

The 2025 numbers tell a story of a company that is operationally stable but financially stretched. Revenue was essentially flat at $46.4M (down 0.8% from $46.8M in 2024) because two lease expirations (Costco in July 2025, Solar Turbines in September 2025) offset contractual rent escalations. G&A was cut from $7.9M to $5.8M (a 26% reduction driven by headcount reduction from 12 to 9 employees and the CEO forgoing salary in exchange for Class X OP Units). Interest expense was relatively flat at $17.5M despite the mortgage repayment on the Costco property because derivative amortization costs replaced the mortgage interest. And the $5.8M impairment on the Saint Paul property -- which management attributes to current market conditions -- is a reminder that small portfolios of single-tenant properties carry meaningful idiosyncratic risk.

**Top 3 reasons to own at $15.14:**

1. **The yield is genuinely attractive at 7.8%**, paid monthly, from a portfolio of mission-critical industrial properties with a 14.0-year WALT and 98% occupancy. For income-focused investors willing to accept micro-cap risk, this is among the highest-yielding listed industrial REITs available. The monthly dividend cadence compounds well in a DRIP portfolio.
2. **The onshoring/reshoring thesis is structurally sound.** Tariffs, supply chain security concerns, and the IRA/CHIPS Act are driving sustained investment in domestic manufacturing capacity. MDV's tenants -- companies like Lindsay Corporation (agricultural infrastructure), Northrop Grumman (defense), and optical systems manufacturers for aerospace/defense -- are direct beneficiaries of this trend. This is not a speculative bet; it is a real secular tailwind.
3. **Management is aligned through the OP Unit structure.** CEO Halfacre holds Class X OP Units that vest over five years and has forgone salary since April 2025. The 19% non-controlling interest in the Operating Partnership means management and insiders eat what they cook. The ATM program is used sparingly ($3.3M in 2025), and the preferred stock repurchase program ($7.1M in 2025) shows capital allocation discipline.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own:**

1. **Leverage of 7.7x net debt/EBITDA is dangerously high** by net-lease REIT standards. Realty Income (O) operates at 5.4x; NNN at ~5.3x; STAG at ~5.2x. MDV's leverage is closer to a B-rated credit than the BBB/BB profile its equity valuation implies. The $250M term loan maturity in July 2028 is a potential existential event if capital markets are inhospitable at refinancing time.
2. **Tenant and asset concentration risk is extreme.** Two tenants (Lindsay + KIA) generate 25.5% of rental income. The KIA property alone is 13.7% of total assets. A single-tenant default at Lindsay would reduce AFFO by approximately $2.5M, immediately pushing the payout ratio above 100%. There is no diversification buffer.
3. **Liquidity is a real constraint.** Average daily volume of ~53,000 shares means a $50,000 position requires a full day's volume. Institutional investors cannot build meaningful positions without moving the price. The bid-ask spread regularly exceeds 1%, and the stock can gap 5-10% on no news. This is not a REIT you can trade; it is a REIT you must commit to.

**Next 12-18 months catalysts:**
- **Saint Paul property sale** (expected to close Q1 2026 at ~$4.0M): validates the impaired carrying value and generates cash for debt reduction or redeployment
- **Northrop Grumman Melbourne sale** (expected Q2 2026): material disposition that tests the market's appetite for single-tenant industrial assets
- **Term loan maturity** (July 2028): becomes a front-of-mind concern starting mid-2027; management will need to demonstrate refinancing capacity well in advance
- **OES purchase option** (exercisable through December 31, 2026): if the State of California exercises its option on the Rancho Cordova property, it would remove a non-core asset and generate cash
- **Rate cycle**: every 50 bps of Fed cuts reduces the all-in swap cost and improves the interest expense trajectory for 2027+

## PART II -- COMPANY SNAPSHOT

Modiv Industrial, Inc. (NYSE: MDV) is an internally-managed Maryland corporation headquartered in Denver, Colorado. The Company was incorporated on May 15, 2015, changed its name from Modiv Inc. to Modiv Industrial, Inc. effective August 11, 2023, and has operated as a REIT for federal income tax purposes since the year ended December 31, 2016. The seven-year trajectory captures both eras and illuminates why the current financial profile looks stretched: the company entered the listed phase with a balance sheet calibrated for a higher-return diversified portfolio and has been steadily refinancing that balance sheet to fit a lower-yield but more durable industrial portfolio.

### Inflection Points

**FY2022 -- NYSE Listing (February 11, 2022).** The listing of Class C Common Stock transformed MDV from a non-traded REIT with limited liquidity into a publicly traded small-cap. The listing opened access to secondary market capital via ATM offerings, the ability to use listed preferred stock, and provided price discovery that the non-traded structure had lacked. The listing also subjected the company to the scrutiny, volatility, and valuation pressure of the public markets -- a transition for which the share price reflected near-immediate discount to NAV.

**FY2022 -- KIA Carson Acquisition (January 2022).** Pre-listing but close to it, the KIA Carson, CA auto dealership was acquired via OP Unit contribution at $25/OP Unit for 1.3M units (total consideration approximately $32M). This single asset has become the largest single-property concentration (13.7% of total assets as of December 31, 2025) and the most strategically awkward asset in the portfolio -- a retail auto dealership sitting inside an "Industrial" REIT. The OP Unit consideration at $25/unit vs current common stock price of $15 represents approximately $13M of paper loss for the seller, though the long-term lease (through 2039) provides meaningful cash flow visibility.

**FY2023 -- Name Change to Modiv Industrial, Inc. (August 11, 2023).** The formal rebranding from "Modiv Inc." to "Modiv Industrial, Inc." signaled the strategic commitment to the industrial-focused thesis. This was not just cosmetic -- it was accompanied by a commitment to dispose of all non-core office and retail assets within a defined timeline. Industrial ABR rose from 55% to 73% during FY2023 via targeted dispositions of office properties in suburban markets.

**FY2023 -- GAAP Operating Loss.** FY2023 GAAP operating margin turned negative primarily due to a large impairment charge ($11.2M) on legacy office properties being prepared for disposition. This was a non-cash charge but reflects the real economic reality that the diversified predecessor portfolio was carrying office and retail properties at historical cost that exceeded market value in a post-COVID commercial real estate environment.

**FY2023 -- G&A Explosion.** G&A of $17.8M in FY2023 was driven by $11.2M of stock compensation expense associated with the conversion of Class P and Class R OP Units. This was a one-time non-cash compensation event that unwound legacy equity incentive structures from the pre-listing era. Excluding the one-time stock comp, FY2023 G&A was approximately $6.6M.

**FY2024 -- Operational Stabilization.** FY2024 was the first post-listing year where MDV reported a clean operating profile: $46.8M revenue, 13.5% operating margin, $18.2M FCF. The balance sheet was stable, the portfolio transformation was 78% complete, and management's capital recycling narrative had visible execution.

**FY2025 -- Cost Discipline and Saint Paul Impairment.** FY2025 marked a pivot toward operational efficiency: G&A dropped from $7.9M to $5.8M (-26%), driven by headcount reduction from 12 to 9 and the CEO's salary elimination. However, a $5.8M impairment on the Saint Paul, MN industrial property (related to its planned disposition below carrying value) disrupted the earnings trajectory. FFO per share fell from $1.50 to $1.32 while AFFO per share rose from $1.34 to $1.38 -- reflecting the non-cash nature of the impairment and the continued cash-generation capacity of the core portfolio.

**FY2026 Outlook (forward).** Management has extended the term loan maturity to July 2028, completed swap coverage through 2026 at a 4.15% effective rate, and retained approximately $14M of cash. The two pending dispositions (Saint Paul and Northrop Grumman Melbourne) should close in H1 2026 and provide $25-30M of disposition proceeds for debt reduction, preferred buyback, and selective industrial acquisitions.

### Revenue Trajectory Details

MDV's revenue has been remarkably stable at $46-47M since 2023, reflecting a portfolio that is largely static in size with modest contractual rent escalations offset by dispositions. The company is not a growth REIT in any traditional sense; it is a yield vehicle that recycles non-core assets and makes selective acquisitions to improve portfolio quality.

The FY2025 revenue decline of 0.8% was driven by two lease expirations: Costco (office property in Issaquah, WA -- lease expired July 31, 2025, property sold December 2025) and Solar Turbines (legacy office in San Diego -- lease expired September 30, 2025). These expirations were expected and part of the strategic plan to exit non-core assets (FY2025 10-K ITEM 7 Results of Operations).

Revenue breakdown by property type for FY2025 (FY2025 10-K ITEM 7 Property Portfolio):
- Industrial core: $35.8M rental + $0.1M other = $35.9M (77.5% of total)
- Non-core: $10.0M rental + $0.5M other = $10.5M (22.5% of total)

### Earnings Quality -- The FFO/AFFO Divergence

For REITs, GAAP net income is largely meaningless because depreciation and amortization dwarf economic reality. MDV reported GAAP net income of $0.6M in FY2025 -- a number rendered useless by $15.1M of D&A and $5.8M of impairment charges. The relevant metrics are FFO and AFFO.

| Metric | FY2025 | FY2024 | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| GAAP Net Income | $0.6M | $6.5M | -91% |
| FFO | $16.5M | $16.8M | -1.9% |
| AFFO | $17.2M | $15.0M | +15.0% |
| FFO/share (diluted) | $1.32 | $1.50 | -12.0% |
| AFFO/share (diluted) | $1.38 | $1.34 | +3.0% |
| Diluted shares/units | 12.48M | 11.19M | +11.5% |

The FFO decline from $1.50 to $1.32 per share is alarming at first glance, but it is almost entirely driven by the $5.8M Saint Paul impairment (which is added back for FFO purposes under Nareit's definition) and by dilution from the 11.5% increase in weighted average diluted shares/units outstanding. The share/unit count expansion reflects the Class C OP Unit exchange for common stock (1.675M units exchanged in 2024), the ATM offering (213K shares sold in 2025 at $15.66 average price), and the Class X OP Unit awards to management (FY2025 10-K ITEM 7 FFO Reconciliation).

**The AFFO improvement from $1.34 to $1.38 per share is the more meaningful signal**, but it must be interpreted with caution. The $4.2M AFFO add-back for amortization of off-market interest rate derivatives is a non-recurring item related to the premium paid to buy down swap rates below market. Without this add-back, AFFO would have been approximately $13.0M, or $1.04 per share -- well below the $1.20 annual distribution. Management is entitled to use the Nareit-standard AFFO calculation, but investors should understand that the 2025 AFFO number benefits from aggressive swap structuring that will recur only if the company continues to pay premiums for below-market swaps.

### Payout Ratio Analysis

| Metric | FY2025 | FY2024 |

| Distributions to CS + OP holders | $14.6M | $12.7M |

**The payout ratio is the single most important number in this analysis.** When preferred dividends are included, total distributions of $17.8M exceed AFFO of $17.2M, implying a 103% total payout ratio. This means the company is distributing more than it earns on an AFFO basis and funding the shortfall through asset sales, ATM equity issuance, or balance sheet cash. This is not sustainable in the long term.

However, the preferred stock repurchase program provides a partial offset. MDV repurchased 298,500 preferred shares in 2025 (from 2.0M to 1.7M outstanding), reducing future preferred dividends by approximately $0.5M annually. If the company continues to retire preferred shares, the total distribution burden declines, improving payout math. At $7.1M for 298,500 shares ($23.79 average, vs $25 liquidation preference), the buyback was accretive.

### Distribution Source Analysis

Management states that distributions are funded by internally generated funds from operations (FY2025 10-K ITEM 7 Distributions). However, FY2025 operating cash flow was $15.0M against total distributions paid of $15.9M ($12.6M to common/OP + $3.3M to preferred). The $0.9M gap was funded by the $27.1M in proceeds from property sales, net of acquisitions. This is mathematically fine in an asset-recycling year, but it means the distribution depends on continued access to the disposition market.

### Dividend Safety Narrative

The dividend is being paid, has been modestly increased, and is technically covered by AFFO if you exclude preferred dividends. But the margin of safety is extremely thin, the leverage is high, and the concentration risk means a single tenant event could push payout above 100%. A score of 5/10 reflects "maintained with elevated risk" -- not "safe."

**What would have to break for a dividend cut**:
1. Loss of Lindsay Corporation as a tenant (9 properties, 14.5% of rental income) -- most likely trigger
2. Refinancing failure or dramatically higher cost on the July 2028 term loan maturity
3. Further goodwill/property impairments forcing a write-down cycle
4. A material recession reducing tenant credit quality across the portfolio
5. Interest rate environment where swap economics become prohibitively expensive

**Stress test: 2020 (COVID) replay equivalent**:
During 2020, MDV (then Modiv Inc.) maintained distributions through the pandemic via a combination of government support programs for tenants, rent deferrals with catch-up provisions, and balance sheet flexibility. The monthly distribution was not cut. If a similar-magnitude shock occurred in 2026-2027, MDV has less flexibility (higher leverage, narrower AFFO coverage) and dividend risk would be meaningfully higher.

**Stress test: 2008-09 (GFC) replay equivalent**:
MDV's predecessor entities existed during 2008-09 but in a different structural form. The current configuration -- 42 single-tenant industrial properties, 14-year WALT, 82% industrial core -- has not been stress-tested through a financial crisis. In a replay, tenant credit quality (particularly for non-investment-grade Lindsay) would be the primary risk factor. A 20% reduction in rental income would eliminate AFFO coverage entirely.

## PART V -- BUSINESS QUALITY AND MOAT

### Moat Type and Durability

MDV does not possess a traditional economic moat in the Morningstar/Berkshire sense. The underlying business is owning physical real estate and collecting rent from tenants under long-term leases. Any investor with sufficient capital could replicate the portfolio by competing at property-level cap rates. The moat, to the extent one exists, is:

1. **Structural moat**: The UPREIT OP Unit acquisition strategy provides access to seller inventory that all-cash buyers (or other REITs who lack the UPREIT structure) cannot access. This is a modest competitive advantage but creates a sourcing channel.
2. **Portfolio moat**: The 14.0-year WALT is by far the longest in the listed net-lease industrial space (STAG at ~4.5 years is the closest peer). Tenants on 15+ year sale-leaseback structures cannot exit without significant termination costs, providing cash flow visibility that short-WALT peers cannot match.
3. **Relationship moat**: The off-market sourcing relationships with specific manufacturers (Lindsay, Solar Turbines, Northrop Grumman) create sticky deal flow. However, these relationships can be replicated with effort.

**Moat trajectory**: Stable-to-narrowing. As industrial net-lease has become a more competitive real estate category, other REITs and private funds have built UPREIT structures and long-WALT portfolios. MDV's differentiation is real but not unique.

### Pricing Power Evidence

MDV's pricing power is effectively capped by the contractual rent escalators embedded in each lease at acquisition:
- Industrial core leases typically have 2.0-2.5% annual escalators or CPI-linked provisions
- Some newer leases have CPI-linked provisions with floors and caps
- Renewal terms (when leases reach expiration) are negotiated at market cap rates, which may be above or below the previous lease rate

There is no evidence of meaningful "pricing power" in the conventional sense of setting prices above market for differentiated products. MDV is a rent-collecting REIT with a portfolio of industrial properties.

### Return on Invested Capital Trend (Last 5 Years)

ROIC for a REIT is meaningful only when calculated on cash basis rather than GAAP (which is distorted by depreciation). Calculating cash-basis ROIC (AFFO / invested capital):

| Year | AFFO ($M) | Invested Capital ($M) | Cash ROIC (%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY2025 | 17.2 | ~$450 | 3.8% |
| FY2024 | 15.0 | ~$460 | 3.3% |
| FY2023 | ~14.0 | ~$480 | 2.9% |
| FY2022 | ~12.5 | ~$490 | 2.6% |
| FY2021 | ~8.5 | ~$400 | 2.1% |

The ROIC trajectory is modestly improving as the industrial pivot has produced a more productive asset mix. However, the absolute level of 3-4% is low relative to the cost of equity capital (typical REIT equity cost is 8-10%) and insufficient to generate value creation above the required return. This is consistent with MDV trading at or below NAV for most of its listed history.

## PART VI -- CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### Cash Deployment Priority (FY2025, in order of $ amount)

1. **Distributions**: $15.9M total ($12.6M common/OP + $3.3M preferred) -- the largest cash use by a wide margin
2. **Mortgage principal payments**: $18.8M -- paid down the mortgage on Costco Issaquah property ahead of sale
3. **Preferred stock repurchases**: $7.1M to retire 298,500 preferred shares
4. **Capex / Tenant Improvements**: $0.5M -- minimal maintenance capex
5. **New property acquisitions**: ~$0.3M cash component (plus $5.9M in OP Units) for the Jacksonville, FL industrial property

### Capital Allocation Grade: C+

The grade reflects a mix of thoughtful and less-thoughtful decisions:

**Positive capital allocation decisions**:
- Preferred stock repurchases at 5% discount to par value with implied 7.7-8.0% return on capital (accretive)
- Costco property sale at $26.0M with $2.4M gain on sale, avoiding holding risk on expiring lease
- Jacksonville acquisition funded 95% with OP Units, minimizing cash outflow
- Disciplined use of the ATM program ($3.3M proceeds in 2025 vs $60M+ capacity utilized by peer REITs with similar market caps)

**Questionable capital allocation decisions**:
- Continued dividend payment at payout ratios >100% when considering preferred dividends

## PART VII -- STRATEGIC TRANSFORMATION ANALYSIS

### From Non-Traded to Listed: The Journey

MDV's corporate history is atypical for a listed REIT. The company began as a non-traded REIT under the RealtyMogul platform, accumulating a diversified portfolio of office, retail, and industrial properties through the non-traded channel. The listing on NYSE in February 2022 was the inflection point -- and the subsequent 4-year period has been defined by the strategic pivot toward industrial manufacturing.

**Portfolio transformation timeline:**

| Date | Industrial % of ABR | Event |
|---|---|---|
| December 2021 | ~50% | Pre-listing; diversified portfolio |
| February 2022 | ~55% | NYSE listing of Class C Common Stock |
| January 2022 | ~58% | KIA Carson acquisition (non-core, OP Unit contribution) |
| August 2023 | 70% | Name change to Modiv Industrial, Inc. |
| December 2023 | 73% | Continued office/retail dispositions |
| December 2024 | 78% | Further recycling |
| December 2025 | 82% | Costco office sold, 3 non-core remaining |

**The transformation is genuine and well-executed.** Management has identified 12-15 assets for recycling over the next 24 months, including the two remaining office assets and certain legacy industrial properties with shorter lease terms (FY2025 10-K ITEM 1 Disposition Policies). The question is pace and pricing: in a higher-rate environment, disposition proceeds may be lower than hoped, and the carrying costs of vacant or soon-to-expire properties erode AFFO.

### The Onshoring Thesis

MDV's investment thesis centers on the belief that domestic manufacturing will experience sustained secular growth driven by:
1. **Tariffs and trade policy**: import duties incentivize onshore production
2. **Supply chain security**: COVID-era disruptions demonstrated the fragility of global supply chains
3. **Government incentives**: the Inflation Reduction Act, CHIPS Act, and related legislation direct capital toward domestic manufacturing
4. **Geopolitical risk**: US-China tensions accelerate friend-shoring and near-shoring

This thesis is fundamentally sound -- and it is the primary reason MDV trades at a premium to its net-asset-value on most days. However, the thesis has a timing problem: the demand for industrial manufacturing real estate is a 10-20 year trend, while MDV's balance sheet is structured for a 2-3 year horizon (term loan maturity July 2028). The company needs to survive the near-term leverage challenge to benefit from the long-term secular tailwind.

### Acquisition Strategy and Capital Recycling

MDV's acquisition approach is distinctive in the REIT space. Rather than competing with the large net-lease REITs on broadly marketed deals, MDV targets sale-leaseback transactions with manufacturers who need to monetize their real estate while maintaining operational control of their facilities. This off-market sourcing gives MDV access to properties that Realty Income or NNN would not pursue because of the small deal size ($5-15M per property) and the non-investment-grade credit profile of many tenants.

The March 2025 acquisition of a Jacksonville, FL industrial property illustrates the model: a $6.1M purchase, funded 95% with OP Units ($5.9M at $17.00/unit) and only $0.3M in cash, with a lease term through December 2032 and CPI-linked rent escalations (FY2025 10-K Note 3). The adjacent land parcel provides optionality for future development. The OP Unit funding structure is critical -- it allows MDV to grow without raising equity at dilutive prices or drawing on the credit facility.

However, the OP Unit model has a dilution consequence. Each OP Unit contributed is economically equivalent to issuing a new share of common stock. The fully diluted share/unit count has grown from approximately 9.1M in FY2023 to 12.5M in FY2025 -- a 37% increase in two years. This dilution is the price of capital-light growth, and it directly reduces per-share metrics even as absolute AFFO grows.

The disposition side of the recycling strategy has been effective: MDV has sold numerous non-core office and legacy industrial properties since the 2022 listing, with FY2025 generating $27.1M in net sale proceeds from two dispositions (Costco Issaquah for $26.0M and New Vision Endicott for $2.4M). The Costco sale was particularly well-timed -- the property had a lease expiring in July 2025, and selling in December 2025 for $26.0M (with a $2.4M gain on sale) avoids the risk of holding a vacant office building in a secondary market (FY2025 10-K ITEM 7 Dispositions).

Pending dispositions as of the filing date include the Saint Paul, MN industrial property (expected ~$4.0M, with $2.0M in non-refundable deposits received) and the Northrop Grumman property in Melbourne, FL (expected Q2 2026). The Melbourne disposition is noteworthy because Northrop Grumman recently extended its lease to May 2031 -- selling a freshly extended lease at a potentially attractive cap rate while redeploying proceeds into higher-yielding manufacturing properties is exactly the kind of value-accretive recycling MDV should be doing.

### The Impairment Question

The $5.8M impairment charge on the Saint Paul, MN property in FY2025 deserves scrutiny. Grant Thornton, the auditor, identified impairment of real estate investments as the sole critical audit matter in the FY2025 audit opinion (FY2025 10-K Auditor's Report). This is not unusual for REITs, but it signals that the auditor viewed the impairment evaluation as particularly complex or judgmental.

The impairment was triggered by market conditions and represents the excess of carrying value over estimated sale price less selling costs. With the property under contract for approximately $4.0M and non-refundable deposits received, the impairment appears appropriately sized. However, investors should be aware that several other properties in the portfolio -- particularly the legacy industrial assets with shorter lease terms that management has identified for recycling -- may face similar impairment charges when marketed for sale. The book value of the total real estate portfolio ($435.5M net) almost certainly exceeds what the properties would fetch in a liquidation scenario given the current cap rate environment.

## PART XI -- THESIS AND VERDICT

### Final Verdict

**HOLD at $15.14. Dividend Safety: 5/10. Confidence: Medium.**

Modiv Industrial is an interesting, well-managed, genuinely differentiated REIT that is one bad quarter away from a dividend crisis. The 7.8% yield is real, the monthly cadence is attractive, the onshoring thesis is sound, and management alignment through OP Units is genuine. But the leverage is too high, the concentration is too extreme, the liquidity is too thin, and the AFFO margin of safety is too narrow for this to be a high-conviction income holding.

**Thesis (one paragraph):** MDV is a satellite yield position for a diversified portfolio -- a monthly-paying 7.8% yielder whose differentiated industrial-manufacturing net-lease thesis provides genuine exposure to onshoring tailwinds that larger net-lease REITs cannot match. The position works if management executes on (a) completing the portfolio transformation to 90%+ industrial, (b) retiring preferred stock to reduce the total distribution burden, (c) maintaining or modestly extending the term loan through the 2028 refinancing window, and (d) avoiding any material tenant default events. The position fails if any one of these four conditions breaks, with the dividend being the first casualty. The appropriate position size is 0.5-1.0% of portfolio: large enough to matter for yield, small enough to absorb a 30-40% drawdown without material portfolio impact.

**For a $1.35M portfolio**, a 0.5-1.0% position in MDV delivers $530-$1,060 of gross annual income with monthly compounding. That is attractive if you accept the risk that this income stream could be reduced by 20-30% in a stress scenario. Position sizing should reflect the micro-cap risk premium: this is not a core holding, it is a satellite position that pays well for the risk. As a China tax resident with 10% WHT on REIT distributions under the US-China treaty, the after-tax yield at $15.14 is approximately 7.0% -- still attractive relative to alternatives but with a narrower margin of safety.

**Buy below $13.00** (9.2% yield): at that level, the market is pricing in meaningful credit stress, and you are compensated for the risk. The implied AFFO multiple of 9.4x at $13 gives a margin of safety against a 15-20% AFFO decline.

**Trim above $17.00** (7.1% yield): at that level, the spread over Treasuries and investment-grade net-lease REITs is no longer wide enough to compensate for the structural risks. An AFFO multiple of 12.3x at $17 is approaching fair value for a REIT with this leverage profile.
