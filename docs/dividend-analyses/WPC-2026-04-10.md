## PART I -- Executive Summary

W. P. Carey Inc. is a post-surgical dividend story. The 2023-2024 office exit -- a strategic masterstroke executed with painful timing -- destroyed a 25-year dividend growth streak but created a fundamentally superior portfolio. The surgery is over. The patient is recovering. The question for dividend investors is whether the scar tissue disqualifies WPC from a long-term income portfolio, or whether the higher yield and improving coverage represent genuine opportunity.

The numbers argue persuasively for the latter. AFFO attributable to W. P. Carey was $1,098.2 million in FY2025 (10-K, Item 7, Supplemental Financial Measures), up 6.0% from $1,035.9 million in FY2024. Dividends paid were $790.0 million, producing an AFFO payout ratio of 71.9% and coverage of 1.39x. The quarterly dividend has been raised every quarter in 2025 -- $0.890, $0.900, $0.910, $0.920 -- a deliberate cadence that signals management's commitment to rebuilding the growth trajectory. At a $0.920 quarterly run-rate ($3.68 annualized), the stock yields approximately 5.2% at $70.25 (last price per portfolio system, April 2026).

The portfolio that emerged from the office exit is objectively better: 1,682 net-leased properties across 25 countries, 98.0% occupancy, 12.0-year weighted-average lease term, 371 tenants with no single tenant exceeding 2.7% of ABR, and zero office exposure. The property mix is concentrated in industrial (38% of ABR), warehouse (25%), and retail (22%) -- all categories with stronger secular tailwinds than the office portfolio WPC abandoned. Nearly half (48.4%) of ABR benefits from CPI-linked rent escalators, providing a structural inflation hedge that most U.S.-centric net lease peers lack.

The primary risk is balance sheet leverage. Total debt rose to $8.72 billion at December 31, 2025 (10-K, Note 11), driven by aggressive acquisition spending ($2.04 billion in 2025). Net debt to EBITDA is approximately 6.5x -- elevated relative to Realty Income at approximately 5.5x and NNN REIT at approximately 5.8x. With $979.8 million of scheduled principal payments due in 2026 and $597.9 million in 2027, refinancing execution is the near-term operational priority.

**Key numbers at a glance**

- Price / Yield / AFFO payout: $70.25 / 5.2% / 71.9%
- Dividend streak: 2 years (post-reset; 25 years prior to 2024 cut)
- Last dividend hike: Q4 2025, $0.920/quarter (+1.1% sequential, +5.1% YoY quarterly)
- 2025 total DPS: $3.620 (+3.7% vs 2024)
- AFFO/share (2025): $4.97 (10-K: $1,098.2M / 221.1M diluted shares)
- Net debt / EBITDA: approximately 6.5x
- AFFO coverage of dividend: 1.39x

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

W. P. Carey Inc. is an internally-managed net lease REIT founded in 1973, listed on the NYSE since 1998, and reorganized as a REIT in 2012. The company invests in operationally critical, single-tenant commercial real estate in the United States and Europe, primarily under long-term triple-net leases where tenants bear substantially all operating costs including property taxes, insurance, and maintenance.

- **Sector / Industry:** Real Estate Investment Trust -- Net Lease
- **Business segments:** Single reportable segment (net lease real estate). Revenue sources: (1) lease revenues from net-leased properties ($1,479.2M in FY2025), (2) income from finance leases and loans receivable ($90.9M), (3) operating property revenues from self-storage, hotels, and student housing ($112.5M), (4) investment management fees from NLOP and CESH ($9.2M).
- **Geographic exposure:** Approximately 61% U.S. / 33% Europe / 6% other (Canada, Japan, Mexico, others) by ABR at December 31, 2025 (10-K, Item 1). 25 countries. Key European markets: Germany, Spain, Italy, Poland, Croatia, Netherlands, United Kingdom.
- **Top customers / customer concentration:** Exceptionally well diversified. Top 10 tenants represent only 18.8% of total ABR. Largest tenant is Extra Space Storage at 2.7% of ABR. Three of the top 10 are European food retailers (Metro Italia, Fortenova, Eroski), providing low-cyclicality anchor tenancy.
- **Main competitors:** Realty Income (O), NNN REIT (NNN), Agree Realty (ADC), Essential Properties Realty Trust (EPRT), Broadstone Net Lease (BNL). **Inflection points:**

1. **October 2018 -- CPA:18 Merger.** WPC acquired CPA:18 -- Global, issuing approximately 13.8 million shares plus $423M in cash. This merger added substantial scale, including self-storage operating properties and additional European net lease assets. It also introduced the office exposure that would later necessitate the 2023 exit.

2. **2020-2021 -- COVID resilience.** WPC's net lease portfolio proved remarkably resilient through the pandemic, with minimal rent deferrals and stable AFFO. The triple-net structure insulated WPC from operating cost volatility. Occupancy remained above 98% throughout.

3. **September 2023 -- Office exit announcement.** The board decided to exit office entirely by spinning off 59 properties into NLOP and selling retained office assets. This was the defining strategic event in WPC's modern history and triggered the first dividend cut in 25 years.

4. **November 2023 -- NLOP Spin-Off.** The Spin-Off was completed, distributing one NLOP share per 15 WPC shares. Total non-cash assets derecognized: $1.36 billion. The net effect was a larger, more focused portfolio generating 6.0% higher AFFO.

This is the most critical section for a dividend investor evaluating WPC. The company's dividend history must be understood across three distinct eras.

**The cut: anatomy and assessment**

The pre-cut trajectory was metronomic: approximately $0.002/quarter increases every quarter for over 20 years. This mechanical pattern generated a compounding total DPS growth of approximately 0.8% per year -- slow but unbroken. By 2022, the annual dividend of $4.242 consumed roughly 77% of AFFO from Real Estate operations, leaving thin but adequate headroom.

The crisis began in September 2023 when management announced the simultaneous office exit via Spin-Off and the Office Sale Program. The Q4 2023 dividend was set at $0.860 -- below the $1.071 of Q3 2023 by 19.7%. The cumulative impact from peak Q3 2023 ($1.071 quarterly, $4.284 annualized) to the trough Q1 2024 ($0.865 quarterly, $3.46 annualized) was a 19.2% cut on an annualized basis.

**Was the cut necessary?** Yes, unambiguously. The 59 office properties spun into NLOP plus the retained office assets sold in 2024 represented approximately 15-17% of portfolio ABR at the time of exit. Removing that income stream while maintaining the prior dividend level would have pushed the AFFO payout ratio above 90%, leaving no margin for debt service, capital expenditure, or reinvestment. Management's decision to right-size the dividend to the remaining portfolio was financially prudent.

**Was the cut well-executed?** Debatable. The simultaneous announcement of the office exit and the implicit dividend cut created maximum shareholder pain and triggered forced selling by income mandates requiring unbroken growth streaks. An alternative phased approach might have preserved the Aristocrat status for another year, but management judged (probably correctly) that a clean, fast break was better for long-term value than prolonged uncertainty.

The trend is clearly positive: AFFO is growing while the payout ratio is declining. The $308M of retained AFFO in FY2025 ($1,098M - $790M) provides a substantial buffer for capital expenditure ($191M), debt service, and incremental investment. At this pace, WPC could sustain dividend increases of 3-5% annually for the foreseeable future without any improvement in AFFO growth.

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The dividend is safe. Full stop. At 1.39x AFFO coverage, WPC has the widest coverage margin since before the CPA:18 merger. The retained AFFO of $308M is sufficient to absorb a 10% AFFO decline before the payout ratio would breach 80% -- a level still within normal REIT parameters.

A second cut would require a confluence of severe adverse events: a major tenant bankruptcy wave reducing ABR by 10%+ simultaneously with inability to refinance 2026-2027 maturities at any reasonable cost. While not impossible, this scenario is improbable given the portfolio's diversification (371 tenants, no single tenant above 2.7%) and WPC's BBB+/Baa1 investment-grade credit ratings, which ensure continued access to unsecured debt markets.

**Stress test: what would break the dividend?**

- A 15% decline in AFFO (to approximately $933M) with no change in dividend would push the payout ratio to approximately 85% -- elevated but manageable.
- A 25% decline in AFFO (to approximately $824M) would push the payout ratio to approximately 96% -- crisis territory requiring a dividend reduction.
- For AFFO to decline 25%, WPC would need to experience a combination of: (a) multiple large tenant defaults, (b) inability to re-lease or sell vacated properties, (c) sharply higher interest expense from refinancing, and (d) significant FX headwinds. This is a severe recession scenario, not a base case.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

**Moat type:** Scale + switching costs + contractual lock-in (long-term net leases)
**Moat trajectory:** Stable to widening (post-office exit improved portfolio quality)

WPC's competitive moat is not the traditional brand-based moat of a consumer company. It is structural, built on three pillars:

1. **Contractual rent escalation embedded in long-duration leases.** The 12.0-year weighted-average lease term creates a decade-plus visibility window for rental income. 99.7% of leases by ABR include rent adjustment provisions: 48.4% CPI-linked, 48.2% fixed increases, 3.1% other (10-K, Item 1). This is effectively a contractual inflation hedge that requires no renegotiation.

2. **Scale-driven origination advantage.** With 1,682 properties across 25 countries and $1.55 billion in ABR, WPC is among the largest net lease platforms globally. Scale enables access to large sale-leaseback transactions that smaller competitors cannot underwrite. The 2025 acquisition volume of $2.04 billion demonstrates continued deal flow.

3. **Triple-net structure eliminates operating cost volatility.** Under WPC's standard lease, the tenant pays property taxes, insurance, and maintenance. Property expenses excluding reimbursable tenant costs were only $53.8M in FY2025 against $1.48B of lease revenue -- an operating expense ratio of just 3.6% (10-K, Item 8, Consolidated Statements of Income). This is the cleanest operating model in real estate: the tenant bears virtually all operating risk.

**Pricing power evidence:** Rent escalations on existing net-leased properties generated $61 million of incremental revenue in FY2025 (10-K, Item 7, Lease Revenues section), comprising approximately $40M from CPI-linked and fixed escalations, approximately $11M from self-storage conversions to net lease, and approximately $10M from favorable FX translation. The 3% organic rent growth on the existing base exceeds the fixed 1-2% bumps typical of purely U.S. net lease peers.

**CPI-linkage as structural advantage:** With 48.4% of ABR tied to CPI indices (primarily European HICP), WPC automatically captures above-average rent growth in inflationary environments without the renegotiation friction that characterizes market-rate lease renewals. In a world where inflation remains sticky above 2% central bank targets, this is a genuine competitive differentiator versus Realty Income, NNN REIT, and Agree Realty, which are overwhelmingly fixed-escalation.

**Tenant credit risk -- the moat's vulnerability:** The single-tenant net lease model concentrates credit risk at the individual tenant level. If a tenant fails, the property generates zero income until re-leased. The Hellweg restructuring in 2024 (abated rent in Q1 2024, permanently reduced annual base rent, plus $0.6M of lease terminations at certain Hellweg properties in Q3 2025 per 10-K Item 7) is a live example. The allowance for credit losses was $35.3 million on finance receivables at December 31, 2025 (10-K, Note 6), up from $14.3 million at December 31, 2024. While diversification mitigates this risk, it does not eliminate it.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

WPC's capital allocation framework in FY2025 deployed cash across four primary channels:

1. **Dividends:** $790.0M (72% of AFFO). This is the appropriate priority for a REIT. The quarterly escalation pattern signals that management views dividend growth as a core objective, not a residual after investment.

2. **Acquisitions:** $2,038.5M gross acquisition volume (31 investments comprising approximately 270+ properties, per 10-K Item 7). WPC also completed three construction projects at a cost of $68.9M and committed to fund 11 additional construction projects totaling $277.3M expected to complete in 2026-2027. The pace of acquisition is aggressive -- representing roughly 1.9x the annual AFFO. This is funded through a combination of disposition proceeds, incremental debt, and ATM equity issuance.

3. **Capital expenditures:** $191.2M for building improvements, redevelopments, and tenant improvements (10-K, Cash Flows). For a net lease REIT, this is relatively high and reflects the self-storage conversion program and build-to-suit construction pipeline.

4. **Dispositions (capital recycling):** $1.5 billion in total disposition proceeds from 128 properties, including 63 self-storage operating properties for $772.2M, one student housing property for $77.8M, and 64 net-leased properties (10-K, Item 7, Dispositions). The self-storage dispositions at approximately $12.3M per property (average) represent WPC's strategic retreat from active property management back to its core passive ownership model.

5. **Debt management:** Repaid $450M of 4.000% Senior Notes at maturity in February 2025. Refinanced the EUR 500M Unsecured Term Loan, extending maturity by three years to April 2029 at approximately 2.80% all-in rate. Issued $400M of new 4.650% Senior Notes due 2030 in July 2025. Repaid $265.1M of non-recourse mortgage debt.

6. **Equity issuance:** Sold 6.26 million shares through the ATM Forward program at a weighted-average gross price of $67.53/share, for anticipated gross proceeds of approximately $422.6M (unsettled at year-end 2025, per 10-K Item 7). ATM issuance is a standard REIT funding tool but does create modest dilution.

**Capital allocation grade: B+**

The self-storage exit was the right strategic call -- WPC is not an operating REIT and should not be managing properties. The acquisition pace is aggressive and creates refinancing risk but has been accretive to AFFO. The dividend growth cadence is well-calibrated to AFFO growth. Deducting points for the elevated leverage that results from simultaneous high-volume acquisitions and ATM issuance at prices ($67.53) below estimated fair value.

## PART X -- Risks & Stress Tests

**Risk #1: Leverage and refinancing execution (Probability: Medium | Severity: High)**

Total debt of $8.72B and net debt/EBITDA of approximately 6.5x is elevated relative to peers. With $979.8M of maturities in 2026 (including EUR 500M at 2.250% and $350M at 4.250%) and $597.9M in 2027, WPC must refinance approximately $1.58B within 18 months. If the refinancing cost averages 4.5% (versus the current sub-3% weighted average on maturing notes), the incremental annual interest expense would be approximately $25-30M -- modest as a percentage of AFFO but directionally negative.

Mitigant: Investment-grade credit ratings (BBB+/Baa1) ensure continued access to public bond markets. Available liquidity of $2.0B+ ($1.6B revolver + $412M ATM forwards). 92% of existing debt is fixed rate at a weighted average of 3.1%.

**Risk #2: European political, regulatory, and currency exposure (Probability: Medium | Severity: Medium)**

With approximately 33% of ABR from Europe (and 39% of ABR from international operations per 10-K Item 7A), WPC is materially exposed to EUR/USD translation risk, European regulatory changes (including Pillar Two global minimum tax implementation per 10-K Item 1A), and country-specific economic conditions.

Mitigant: Euro-denominated debt partially hedges the EUR ABR exposure. A 1% change in EUR/USD exchange rate affects projected 12-month cash flow by $2.3M (10-K, Item 7A). WPC also uses foreign currency collars for additional hedging. Diversification across multiple European countries (Germany, Spain, Italy, Poland, Croatia, Netherlands, UK) reduces single-country concentration.

**Risk #3: Tenant credit deterioration (Probability: Medium | Severity: Medium)**

The Hellweg restructuring (Q1 2024 rent abatement, permanent reduction, plus Q3 2025 lease terminations at certain properties) is the most notable recent credit event. Allowance for credit losses on finance receivables increased to $35.3M at December 31, 2025 from $14.3M at December 31, 2024 (10-K, Note 6). Real estate impairment charges were $70.4M in 2025, $43.6M in 2024, and $86.4M in 2023 -- a persistent annual cost.

Mitigant: Extreme tenant diversification (371 tenants, no single tenant above 2.7% of ABR). 22% investment grade or implied investment grade (10-K, Item 1: 15% IG + 7% implied IG). Portfolio is concentrated in operationally critical, single-tenant properties that tenants are incentivized to maintain through bankruptcy proceedings.

**Risk #4: Tariff impact on industrial tenants (Probability: Medium-Low | Severity: Medium)**

The 10-K risk factors specifically reference "the imposition of tariffs" as a factor that could "severely affect our tenants' businesses, financial condition and liquidity" (Item 1A). With 38% of ABR in industrial properties and 5.3% in auto parts & equipment, WPC has meaningful exposure to supply chain disruptions and tariff-driven cost increases for manufacturing tenants. This is a new risk factor that was not prominent in pre-2024 filings.

Mitigant: Net lease structure passes operating cost increases to tenants. Long lease terms (12.0-year WALT) provide time for tenants to adapt. European industrial exposure may be partially insulated from U.S.-centric tariff disputes.

**Risk #5: NLOP residual exposure (Probability: Low | Severity: Low)**

WPC still externally manages NLOP and earns declining advisory fees ($4.96M in 2025 vs $6.60M in 2024, per 10-K Item 7, Asset Management Revenue). As NLOP sells its remaining office assets, this revenue approaches zero. The reputational linkage to NLOP's office portfolio creates a modest overhang.

Mitigant: NLOP advisory fees are immaterial (0.5% of AFFO). The wind-down is progressing. Once NLOP completes its asset sales, the last vestige of WPC's office era will be permanently closed.

**Risk #6: Occupancy erosion (Probability: Low-Medium | Severity: Medium)**

Occupancy declined from 98.6% to 98.0% in FY2025 (10-K, Item 7, Portfolio Summary). While still healthy, this is the lowest in recent years. Approximately 19% of leases by ABR expire within the next five years (10-K, Item 1A), creating re-leasing risk in a potentially challenging economic environment.

Mitigant: The 60 basis point decline is modest and partially reflects portfolio composition changes from acquisitions and dispositions. The 12.0-year WALT means the vast majority of ABR is locked in for a decade or more.

### Tone evolution across filings (2019-2025)

Reading all seven 10-Ks chronologically reveals a distinctive tonal arc:

**2019-2021 (Confident/Methodical):** The filings during this period reflect a company operating on autopilot. The business description emphasizes "stable," "predictable," and "diversified." Office exposure is mentioned as a standard property type alongside industrial and warehouse without any defensive framing. The dividend increases of $0.002/quarter every quarter for over 20 years reflect mechanical, low-conviction growth -- enough to maintain the streak but not enough to signal genuine confidence.

**2022 (Transition/Complexity):** The CPA:18 merger integration is complete but the portfolio has grown more complex. Self-storage operating properties and the investment management business add revenue volatility that does not match the clean net lease narrative. The 10-K begins to reference "office" in a way that hints at growing discomfort with the category, though no explicit exit plan is mentioned.

**2023 (Decisive/Disruptive):** The September 2023 office exit announcement transforms the filing tone. The 10-K uses unusually direct language: "we announced a plan to exit the office assets within our portfolio." The Q4 2023 dividend of $0.860 "reflects both our strategic exit from the office assets within our portfolio and a lower payout ratio." There is no hedging or apologetic framing -- management is clearly convinced the decision is correct and wants to execute quickly.

**2024 (Post-Crisis/Rebuilding):** The 2024 10-K reads as a transition document. The Office Sale Program is "completed." NLOP is operational but clearly being wound down. The filing focuses heavily on acquisition volume ($1.477B) as proof of the reinvestment strategy. The dividend increase pattern (quarterly $0.005 raises) is calibrated to rebuild confidence without overshooting.

**2025 (Growth/Confidence):** The most recent 10-K represents the clearest return to a growth narrative. Acquisition volume of $2.04B, self-storage operating properties substantially exited (down from approximately 80 to 11), AFFO growing 6.0%. The quarterly dividend increases accelerate from $0.005 to $0.010 per quarter. The filing barely mentions NLOP except as a line item in investment management revenue. The office era is being consigned to footnotes.

### Cross-filing consistency check

Management's statements about strategic priorities are internally consistent across all filings reviewed. Two specific items warrant noting:

1. **Investment grade target.** All filings reference maintaining investment grade credit ratings and a "conservative capital structure." The actual leverage trajectory (net debt/EBITDA rising from approximately 5.5x in 2023 to approximately 6.5x in 2025) is in tension with the "conservative" language. Management may define "conservative" relative to the assets (long-duration, investment-grade tenanted) rather than absolute leverage levels, but the gap between language and metrics is worth monitoring.

2. **Self-storage narrative flip.** In 2022-2023, WPC presented self-storage operating properties as a growth opportunity and acquired a substantial portfolio. By 2024-2025, management systematically converted these to net leases (leasing 16 properties to Extra Space Storage) or sold them outright (63 properties for $772M). The pivot from "growth opportunity" to "non-core asset for disposition" occurred over roughly 18 months with minimal explicit acknowledgment of the strategic reversal in the filings. The correct interpretation is that management recognized WPC's core competence is passive ownership, not active property management -- but the speed of the about-face raises questions about initial underwriting discipline.

### Topics they stopped discussing

**Investment Management as a growth driver.** In the 2019-2021 filings, the investment management business (advisory fees from managed programs) was presented as a complementary revenue stream. By 2025, investment management revenue has shrunk to $9.2M (0.5% of total revenue) and is explicitly described as "expected to decline as assets are sold." Management no longer positions this as a meaningful business line.

**Percentage rent.** Earlier filings discussed percentage rent (tenant revenue-sharing above a stated level) as a lease feature. By 2025, this language is limited to a brief mention in Item 1 and is not quantified separately in revenue disclosures, suggesting the amount is immaterial.
