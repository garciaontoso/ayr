## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: ACCUMULATE.** **Capital Return Program Safety: 9/10.** **Conviction: Medium-High.**

PayPal is the single most mispriced large-cap name in the US payment landscape. At $45.85 the market is valuing a business generating $33.2B in revenue, $6.07B in GAAP operating income, $5.23B in net income, and **$5.56B in free cash flow** (FMP cashflow 2025) at 8.4x trailing earnings and 7.9x trailing FCF. That is a cyclical-industrial multiple. It is not the multiple of a business that served 439 million active accounts across ~200 markets and processed $1.79 trillion of TPV in 2025 (2025 10-K line 79), grew revenue 4.3%, grew operating income 14%, and used 100% of FCF plus some balance sheet cash to retire approximately 86 million shares (~8.4% of starting float, avg cost $69.94, 2025 10-K line 2375).

The central PYPL debate is whether this is **terminal value compression** (Apple Pay eats branded checkout, Stripe eats Braintree, Venmo never monetizes, users structurally declining) or a **cyclical/narrative drawdown** (COVID inflated everything, 2022-2023 was a reversion, new CEO is executing a textbook margin-up turnaround). Our reading of the filings is that the data strongly supports the cyclical interpretation. Active accounts troughed in 2023 at 426M and printed 439M — a new all-time high — at end-2025. Braintree margin compression, the signature 2022-2024 bear narrative, reversed exactly as management said it would: the 2025 10-K MD&A (line 3438) notes "lower volume and transaction revenue from our Braintree offerings in the first half of 2025 followed by revenue and TPV growth in the second half of 2025." That is what a margin-up turn looks like when it works.

The equity de-rated from ~$308 (Jul 2021) to ~$45.85, an 85% drawdown normally reserved for going concerns, while the business grew revenue 31% ($25.37B → $33.17B) and FCF expanded from $4.89B to $5.56B. Our triangulated 18-month fair value is **$73 ± $5** (Part IX), ~59% upside, before counting buyback accretion (share count is shrinking ~9%/yr, a mechanical EPS tailwind). Position-sizing: **3-5% of portfolio**; capped because the Apple Pay disintermediation risk is real and not disclosed or measurable from public data.

**Top 3 reasons to own at $45.85:** (1) 12.6% trailing FCF yield with zero dividend obligation — 100% redeployable at management's discretion; (2) the new CEO is demonstrably executing — 2025 op margin 18.3% is the highest print since 2016; (3) asymmetric buyback accretion — at current multiple the share shrink alone is a ~9% return before any business growth.

**Top 3 reasons NOT to own:** (1) Branded checkout share on iOS vs. Apple Pay is not measurable from the filings and is the binary risk; (2) 1%/yr active account growth is not "growth" — it is a stable base that could still inflect negatively; (3) Venmo monetization has been promised every year for six years and still has not meaningfully arrived.

**12m catalysts:** Q1 2026 EPS (consensus $1.29), FastLane adoption disclosure, Braintree revenue acceleration sustained 2+ quarters, first disclosed PayPal Ads revenue figure, buyback acceleration if price stays below $50.

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — 10-YEAR ARC (since 2015 spinoff)

**Phase 1: Spin (2015-2016).** PayPal separated from eBay on 2015-07-06 after Icahn pressure. At spin, PYPL had ~179M active accounts, ~$8B revenue, and ~40% of TPV dependent on eBay's marketplace. The independence question: *can PayPal exist as an acquirer on its own?* It could.

**Phase 2: Independence (2016-2019).** Revenue $10.84B → $17.77B (17.9% CAGR). Operating margin 14-16% (FMP income). iZettle acquired 2018 ($2.2B), Honey agreed late 2019 ($4B). eBay TPV share declined progressively — eBay's managed-payments transition removed a captive cross-border cohort (2022 10-K line 3350).

**Phase 3: COVID boom (2020-2021).** Revenue +20.7% to $21.45B (2020), +18.3% to $25.37B (2021). Active accounts 305M → 426M. TPV $0.71T → $1.25T. Net profit margin peaked at 19.6% (2020). Stock hit ~$308 intraday in July 2021 on ~$360B market cap. The 2020 print was inflated by stay-at-home e-commerce, stimulus-funded Venmo volumes, and interest on customer balances. Management guided to **750M active accounts by 2025** — which eventually missed by ~310M, one of the worst guides in the payments industry.

**Phase 4: The great re-rating (2022-2023).** 2022-02-01 earnings call: PYPL withdrew the 750M target. Stock ~$190 → ~$115 overnight and kept falling. Three real concerns drove 24 months of compression: (i) **Braintree mix** mechanically compressed transaction-margin dollars; (ii) **eBay managed payments** removed captive cross-border TPV; (iii) **Apple Pay narrative** — iOS 14+ made Apple Pay friction-free and PYPL never disclosed branded-checkout conversion data. Operating margin 16.8% → 13.9% (lowest ever as a public company). Net income $4.17B → $2.42B (-42%). EPS $3.55 → $2.10.

**Phase 5: Schulman exit & Chriss arrival (2023).** Chriss named 2023-09-27. Revenue +8.2%, op income +31%, net income +75% (off the 2022 trough — largely base-effect rebound from impairment-heavy 2022). FCF declined to $4.22B on working-capital drag.

**Phase 6: Chriss year one (2024).** Revenue +6.8% to $31.80B, op income +5.9%, net income -2.3%, **FCF +60% to $6.77B**. The FCF spike reflected working capital normalization + higher interest income on customer balances (Fed held at 5%+) + cost discipline. Buyback: $6.05B.

**Phase 7: Where we are (2025).** Revenue +4.3% to $33.17B, op income +14% to $6.07B (18.3% margin — highest since 2016, FMP finGrowth), net income +26% to $5.23B, EPS +35.5% to $5.46 (35% of which is share shrink). FCF $5.56B (-18% from the 2024 working-capital-spike peak). Buyback: another $6.05B, 86M shares at avg cost $69.94 (2025 10-K line 2375) — **management overpaid by ~52% on 2025 repurchases relative to the current $45.85 price**, a discipline issue we return to in Part VII.

**Inflection points:** (1) **2015-07** eBay spin. (2) **2019** Honey acquired for $4B — later largely impaired. (3) **2022-02-01** 750M account target withdrawn — single worst day for equity credibility. (4) **2023-09** Chriss appointed. (5) **2024-Q2** FastLane launched. (6) **2025-02** new $15B buyback authorization. (7) **2025-Q3** Braintree TPV and revenue re-acceleration after three quarters of deliberate shrink (2025 10-K line 3438) — validates the "shed unprofitable TPV first, then re-grow" playbook.

## PART V · CAPITAL RETURN DEEP-DIVE (replaces the dividend section)

### The mechanics

PayPal has **never paid a cash dividend**. By design — management (under multiple CEOs) has preferred buybacks because (i) they are more tax-efficient for a tech-investor shareholder base, (ii) they can be flexed without the quasi-commitment of a recurring dividend, and (iii) fintech management has historically resisted the "admitting maturity" framing of dividend initiation. Every PYPL capital return analysis must look at share count, not DPS. The 2025 10-K (line 3202) carries standard hedge language about no assurance of continued repurchases, but read with the Feb 2025 $15B authorization this is pro-forma disclosure — there is no meaningful threat to the buyback at current FCF.

### Authorizations and history (2025 10-K lines 2374-2379)

- **July 2018 authorization** — $10B program. Fully exhausted by 2023.
- **June 2022 authorization** — $15B program. Exhausted in early 2025 with approximately $4.9B remaining at end-2024 (2025 10-K line 2376).
- **February 2025 authorization** — new $15B program. $13.9B remains at year-end 2025 (2025 10-K line 2375).

## PART VI · BRAINTREE MARGIN SITUATION (dedicated)

Braintree is the single biggest narrative driver of the PYPL equity since 2022. A dedicated section is required because there is no other way to understand why the stock priced in a terminal value collapse.

### What Braintree is

Braintree is PayPal's **unbranded** enterprise payment processor, acquired 2013 for ~$800M. It competes head-on with **Stripe** and **Adyen** for card-present and card-not-present processing for enterprise merchants — Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash, Facebook Marketplace, the SaaS long tail. Unlike branded PayPal (consumer sees the button and logs into a PayPal wallet), Braintree just processes the card; the consumer has no idea PayPal is involved. Braintree keeps ~20-30 bps of take on a well-negotiated enterprise account versus ~200+ bps for branded PayPal on a similar transaction. Sell-side triangulation (Wolfe Research 2023, Evercore ISI 2024) put Braintree at roughly $500-600B TPV per year by late 2023 — not disclosed in filings — or ~35-40% of consolidated TPV while contributing materially less than that to transaction-margin dollars.

### Why it destroyed the narrative (2022-2023)

Simple arithmetic. Every incremental Braintree dollar carried thin transaction margin. The 2022 10-K MD&A (line 3350) and 2023 MD&A both flagged transaction revenue growth lagging TPV growth because of "changes in product mix" — more Braintree, less branded. Analysts had modeled PYPL as a transaction-margin-dollar compounder and the Braintree mix was mechanically eroding that compound. The 2025 10-K (line 3517) states it plainly: Braintree products "generally have higher expense rates than other products and services." Higher expense rate = lower margin = every Braintree dollar dilutes consolidated gross margin.

### What Chriss did in 2024-2025

The most important single operational decision since the 2015 spin. Chriss told merchants Braintree would no longer compete on price — hold the line on deal economics and walk away from unprofitable volume. The 2025 10-K MD&A (line 3438) captures it bluntly:

> "As a result of our stronger focus on profitable growth and ongoing negotiations with merchants, we experienced lower volume and transaction revenue from our Braintree offerings in the first half of 2025 followed by revenue and TPV growth in the second half of 2025."

Translation: management deliberately shed TPV in H1 2025 — which is why total 2025 transactions fell 4% even as TPV grew 7% (Braintree-weighted transaction count shrank; branded-weighted dollar TPV grew). Then in H2 2025, **after renegotiations, Braintree revenue and TPV re-accelerated on Chriss's new pricing.** That is hugely important. If Stripe were offering 10 bps better and winning every deal, Braintree would not have recovered on management's terms. Merchants coming back at the new price is switching-cost evidence.

### Margin impact

| Metric | FY2022 | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Gross margin (FMP ratios) | 50.1% | 46.0% | 46.1% | 46.6% |
| Operating margin (FMP ratios) | 13.9% | 16.9% | 16.7% | 18.3% |
| Transaction expense rate (% of TPV, 10-K) | ~0.96% | 0.94% | 0.93% | 0.89% |

Gross margin, after four years of compression, inflected **+50 bps in 2025**. Operating margin jumped +160 bps. Transaction expense rate fell 4 bps — which sounds small but at $1.79T TPV is **$716M of incremental margin**. The 2025 10-K explicitly attributes the decline to "a lower proportion of TPV from Braintree products and services" (line 3517). This is the Chriss thesis working.

### Will it last?

Bear counter: Stripe eats the share PayPal voluntarily gave up, and in 18-24 months PYPL renegotiates from a weaker position. We think this is a real concern but not existential. Stripe + Adyen combined do ~$1.5T TPV annually. Braintree shed maybe $30-50B in H1 2025 — ~2-3% of the unbranded market. Stripe's overall share probably moved <1%. More importantly, merchants *came back in H2 at new prices*. Our base case: 46-48% gross margin is a sustainable floor and 18-19% op margin is a realistic 2026 level absent macro shock.

## PART X · VARIANT PERCEPTION

### The variant in one line

**The consensus sees PYPL as a secularly-declining business at a trough multiple. We see PYPL as a cyclically-depressed business that has already inflected, with a management team methodically executing and a buyback program mechanically generating ~9% per year of EPS accretion regardless of top-line performance.**

### Three specific things the market has wrong

**1. Active accounts are not declining.** 439M in 2025 is the new all-time high (2025 10-K line 79). The 2023 dip to 426M was a one-year blip driven by deliberate cleanup of low-quality accounts and has fully reversed. The "melting ice cube" framing is a 2023 narrative that got stuck in sell-side models and has not been updated for 2024-2025 data.

**2. The Braintree margin reset is a tell, not a terminal.** Management shed TPV in H1 2025 deliberately; merchants came back at new prices in H2 (2025 10-K line 3438). That is switching-cost evidence, not commoditization. Gross margin inflected +50 bps YoY for the first time since 2021. The H2 2025 re-acceleration is the single most important datapoint for the 2026 thesis and is largely ignored by sell-side focused on quarter-on-quarter active account deltas.

**3. The buyback is mechanical alpha, not counted in stated earnings forecasts.** At ~9% annualized share shrink, EPS accretion from buyback alone ~9%/yr before operating growth. Consensus 2026-2030 EPS growth of 8.6% CAGR does not reconcile with share count mechanically falling from 959M to ~650-700M over the period. Add 5-7% buyback accretion to 4-6% operating EPS growth = 9-13% EPS CAGR through 2028. Growth-stock math at a value-stock price.

### Two things the market is right about

**1. Apple Pay risk is real and unquantifiable from public data.** We cannot rule out secular drift on iOS compressing branded checkout TPV. This is why we cap the position at 5%, not 8-10%.

**2. Venmo monetization is a broken promise.** Six years of "the Venmo monetization ramp" without material disclosed revenue. We assign zero value to it in our model. Any pickup is free optionality.

### Why this setup exists

Momentum investors lost interest at $300 → $50. Quality/growth investors refuse to model a business with single-digit top-line growth. Deep-value investors are reluctant because fintech is "complicated." Index flows steadily dumped the stock as index weight collapsed. **The only buyer at $45 is a patient discretionary allocator who can tolerate 18-24 months of range-trading volatility while the share count mechanically compounds underneath them.** That is the capital we are deploying.
