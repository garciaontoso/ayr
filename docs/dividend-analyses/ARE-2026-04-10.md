## PART I -- Executive Summary

Alexandria Real Estate Equities is a fallen angel in the truest sense of the term. The company that pioneered life science real estate, built an irreplaceable network of Megacampus ecosystems across America's most important biotech corridors, and traded above $220 per share in late 2021 now sits at roughly $43, having suffered an 80%+ decline from peak. The proximate cause was a 45% dividend cut in Q4 2025 -- from $1.32 to $0.72 per quarter -- accompanied by $2.2 billion in impairment charges in FY2025 alone, the largest single-year write-down in the company's 31-year history. Occupancy has fallen from the historical 95%+ average to 90.9% as of December 31, 2025, and management's own guidance projects a further decline to approximately 88.5% by year-end 2026. The life science real estate market is enduring its worst supply/demand imbalance in recorded history, with availability in ARE's top three markets surging to approximately 29% from just 4% in 2021.

The post-cut dividend, however, is now well-covered. At $2.88 annualized against $1.41 billion in operating cash flow, the payout ratio stands at approximately 35% of OCF -- a level that provides meaningful cushion even under further occupancy deterioration. FFO per share as adjusted was $9.01 in FY2025, placing the payout ratio at 32% of adjusted FFO -- among the most conservative levels in ARE's history. The company retains investment-grade credit ratings (BBB+/Baa1), $5.3 billion in total liquidity, and the longest weighted-average debt maturity among S&P 500 REITs at 12.1 years. The balance sheet, while heavily leveraged in absolute terms at $12.4 billion of total debt, is structured to avoid near-term refinancing crises.

For the dividend investor, ARE presents a genuine dilemma. The post-cut yield of approximately 6.7% is well-covered and unlikely to be cut again absent catastrophic occupancy loss. But the company offers zero dividend growth prospects for the foreseeable future, carries existential leverage risk if the life science market does not recover, and requires a 3-5 year investment horizon that demands tolerance for significant interim volatility. This is not a traditional income stock. It is a deep-value wager on a cyclical recovery in a specialized real estate niche, with the dividend providing a paid-to-wait component while the thesis plays out.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield / Payout ratio / Streak: ~$43 / ~6.7% / 32% of FFO adj. / streak broken (cut in 2025)
- Last dividend change: Q4 2025, -45.5% (from $1.32/qtr to $0.72/qtr)
- 5y dividend CAGR: negative (was $4.24 in 2020, now $2.88 run-rate)
- Net debt / EBITDA (Q4 2025 annualized): 5.7x
- Operating cash flow coverage of dividend: 2.8x

## PART II -- Company Snapshot

Alexandria Real Estate Equities, Inc. (NYSE: ARE) is a Maryland-incorporated REIT founded in October 1994 by Joel Marcus. The company pioneered the life science real estate niche -- purpose-built laboratory and research space for pharmaceutical, biotechnology, medical device, and advanced technology companies. For three decades, ARE carved out a market position that was functionally unassailable: specialized, clustered lab campuses in the corridors where drug discovery physically occurs, anchored by proximity to the world's leading research universities and medical centers.

As of December 31, 2025, ARE operates 340 properties in North America comprising approximately 35.9 million rentable square feet (RSF) of operating properties and 3.5 million RSF of Class A/A+ properties under construction. Total market capitalization (equity plus debt) stands at $20.75 billion, with an equity market cap of approximately $7.4 billion.

- **Sector / Industry:** Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) -- Life Science / Specialized Lab Space
- **Business segments:** Eight geographic segments: Greater Boston, San Francisco Bay Area, San Diego, Seattle, Maryland, Research Triangle, New York City, and Texas
- **Geographic exposure:** Greater Boston and San Francisco Bay Area together represent the dominant share of rental revenue (estimated 55-65%); San Diego is the third-largest market. Seattle, Maryland, Research Triangle, NYC, and Texas are smaller contributors.
- **Top customers / customer concentration:** Investment-grade or publicly traded large-cap tenants represent 53% of annual rental revenue. Multinational pharmaceutical companies, public and private biotechnology firms, research institutions, and government agencies (including NIH-adjacent tenants). The top 20 tenants provide 84% investment-grade or large-cap credit quality. Tenant collections have averaged 99.8% since 2021.
- **Main competitors:** BioMed Realty (owned by Blackstone), Healthpeak Properties (DOC), Ventas (VTR) in select markets, plus a wave of new entrants who converted office space to lab during 2020-2022.
- **Recent corporate actions:** $1.81 billion of dispositions completed in FY2025; 45% dividend cut in Q4 2025; $208 million share repurchase program in 2025; Gateway Boulevard campus in South San Francisco sold; exiting Montreal and non-core Canadian assets; $500 million buyback authorization reloaded in December 2025 (no repurchases assumed in 2026 guidance).
- **Employees:** 514 as of December 31, 2025.
- **Triple net lease structure:** 92% of rental revenue from triple net leases; 97% of leases contain approximately 3% annual contractual escalators.

### The Megacampus Model

The Megacampus strategy is the nucleus of ARE's competitive moat. These are large-scale (each exceeding one million RSF), clustered campus environments located in AAA life science innovation hubs -- Cambridge/Kendall Square, Mission Bay, Torrey Pines, Lake Union -- designed for optionality and scalability. Tenants can expand within the portfolio, amenities attract talent, and the density of innovation creates a flywheel effect. As of December 31, 2025, 78% of ARE's annual rental revenue derives from Megacampus properties. During 2023-2025, ARE captured approximately 94% of the combined leasing volume of the five largest life science real estate owners in Greater Boston, San Francisco, and San Diego. This concentration of leasing share in a depressed market is perhaps the strongest evidence that the Megacampus model retains its competitive power even in a severe downturn.

The weakness of the model is equally clear: hyper-specialization in a single asset class leaves no diversification buffer when the life science funding cycle turns. Lab buildings cost $150-300/RSF to build out, cannot easily be repurposed, and when biotech tenants stop expanding, the owner sits on expensive, vacant, single-use space. That is exactly the current predicament.

### The 45% Dividend Cut -- Anatomy of a Forced Reset

The Q4 2025 dividend reduction from $1.32 to $0.72 per share was the defining event for ARE's investment case. Management framed it as a "proactive" decision to preserve liquidity, but three converging pressures left them little choice:

1. **Occupancy erosion:** Same-property occupancy dropped from 95.2% average in 2024 to 92.5% in 2025, with spot occupancy hitting 90.9% by year-end. Management's own guidance projects further decline to 87.7%-89.3% by December 2026. Each 100 basis points of occupancy loss translates to roughly $30-35M in annual rental income.

2. **Capital needs:** $1.03 billion in committed construction costs remained as of year-end 2025, plus $650M of debt maturing in 2026 (of which $300M was repaid in January 2026). The 2026 guidance calls for $1.75B in construction spending and $2.9B in dispositions -- an aggressive capital plan that requires retaining more cash internally.

3. **Disposition-driven revenue erosion:** Selling $1.81B of assets in 2025 and targeting $2.9B in 2026 necessarily reduces the revenue base. The Q4 2025 dispositions alone carried $118M of annualized NOI.

The 2025 dividend tax composition further illuminated the stress: 18.9% was classified as return of capital (non-taxable), up from effectively zero historically. Only 66.6% was ordinary income eligible for the QBI deduction, and 13.9% was capital gains at the 20% rate. The presence of meaningful return of capital indicates that REIT taxable income was insufficient to fully cover distributions -- a classic pre-cut warning signal that, in hindsight, was visible as early as Q3 2025.

### Post-Cut Sustainability Assessment

At the new $0.72/qtr ($2.88 annualized) rate, the dividend mathematics improve substantially:

| Metric | Pre-Cut (FY2024 basis) | Post-Cut (FY2026E) |
|--------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Annual Dividend Cost | ~$911M | ~$497M |
| FFO per share (adj.) | $9.47 | $6.25-$6.55 |
| FFO Payout Ratio (adj.) | 55% | 44-46% |
| Operating Cash Flow | $1,505M | ~$1,200M (est.) |
| OCF Payout Ratio | 61% | ~41% |
| Retained Cash After Dividends | ~$594M | ~$703M |

Even at the depressed 2026E FFO trough of $6.40 per share (midpoint), the $2.88 dividend represents a 45% payout ratio -- well within safe territory for a REIT. At the Q4 2026 trough run rate of $1.40-$1.60 per share ($5.60-$6.40 annualized), the payout ratio would be 45-51%, still manageable.

**Assessment: The post-cut dividend is safe for the next 12-24 months.** To force a second cut, occupancy would need to deteriorate materially below 85% (versus 87.7%-89.3% guidance), dispositions would need to generate minimal proceeds (undermining the deleveraging plan), or a significant tenant bankruptcy cluster would need to occur. The probability of a second cut within 24 months is estimated at 10-15%. The probability of any dividend growth within 36 months is effectively zero.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

- **Moat type:** First-mover advantage / location lock-in / tenant switching costs / ecosystem network effects
- **Moat trajectory:** Narrowing cyclically; structurally intact but under stress
- **Pricing power evidence:** Rental rate increases on renewed/re-leased space averaged 7.0% (GAAP) and 3.5% (cash) for FY2025. However, free rent concessions increased from 0.6 months (2023) to 1.5 months (2025), and management's 2026 guidance projects further degradation with rental rate changes of -2.0% to +6.0% (GAAP) and -12.0% to -4.0% (cash). TI allowances per RSF rose from $26.09 (2023) to $55.34 (2025). These metrics collectively indicate that while headline rental rates are holding, the effective economic terms for tenants are deteriorating significantly.
- **Return on invested capital trend:** Difficult to compute cleanly for REITs, but the trend is clearly negative. $2.9B in cumulative impairments across 2023-2025, coupled with rising interest costs, has destroyed capital deployed during 2020-2022.

### Competitive Advantages (Still Intact)

1. **First-mover advantage (30 years):** ARE has relationships with every major pharma and biotech company, the FDA, NIH, and leading research institutions. No competitor has comparable tenure.
2. **Irreplaceable land positions:** Cambridge/Kendall Square, Mission Bay, Torrey Pines, Lake Union. These locations are physically impossible to replicate. Supply is constrained by zoning, academic proximity, and community resistance.
3. **Tenant switching costs:** Lab buildouts at $150-300/RSF create substantial moving costs. Once a biotech company installs ventilation, fume hoods, and specialized equipment, relocating is a multi-million dollar undertaking that disrupts research timelines.
4. **Scale:** 35.9M RSF dwarfs any single competitor in pure life science real estate. This enables ARE to serve as a "one-stop shop" for tenants expanding across markets.
5. **Lease structure:** 92% triple net, 97% with approximately 3% annual contractual escalators. This provides inflation-linked organic growth on occupied space regardless of market conditions.
6. **Leasing dominance:** In 2023-2025, ARE captured approximately 94% of the combined leasing volume of the five largest life science real estate owners in its top three markets. The Megacampus model, even in a depressed market, attracts a disproportionate share of tenant demand.
7. **Brand and sponsorship:** 82% of FY2025 leasing activity came from the existing tenant base, demonstrating deep incumbent relationships.

### Competitive Vulnerabilities (Growing)

1. **New supply catastrophe:** Life science real estate availability in ARE's top three markets surged to approximately 29% during 2025, up from approximately 4% in 2021. This is the single most important headwind. Average tenant demand measured by RSF requirements has declined by more than 60% since 2021. The mismatch is structural, not merely cyclical.
2. **Biotech funding winter (fifth consecutive year):** Life science venture capital fundraising declined to its lowest level since 2016 in 2025. VC funds remain highly risk-averse, focusing on clinical-stage investments that do not drive significant lab space needs. The IPO market for biotech companies remains largely closed.
3. **Regulatory and policy disruption:** FDA senior leadership turnover exceeded 50% in H1 2025, accompanied by layoffs and review delays. The proposed 15% cap on NIH institutional indirect grant spending (subsequently ruled unlawful by an appellate court) created uncertainty for academic research tenants. Drug pricing scrutiny has constrained pharmaceutical R&D investment.
4. **Asset rigidity:** Lab buildings cannot easily be repurposed. ARE has already converted some assets to residential use (SoMa project, Gateway campus). When lab demand is insufficient, the alternatives are limited and value-destructive.
5. **Geographic concentration risk:** Greater Boston and San Francisco Bay Area dominance means simultaneous weakness in these markets creates portfolio-wide pain. This is precisely what occurred in 2024-2025.

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

### FY2025 Capital Deployment

1. **Dividends:** $911M paid in FY2025 (includes three quarters at $1.32 and one at $0.72). Represents 64% of OCF on the old rate; drops to approximately 35% on the new rate.
2. **Share repurchases:** $208M in FY2025 at uncertain average prices (the stock traded between $45 and $100 during 2025). An additional $50M was spent in FY2024 at an average price of $97.26. Both tranches represent destroyed capital relative to the current $43 stock price. The $500M buyback authorization was reloaded in December 2025, but 2026 guidance assumes zero repurchases.
3. **Additions to real estate (capex + development):** $1,871M in FY2025, down from $2,423M in FY2024 and $3,418M in FY2023. Remaining committed construction costs of $1,030M. 2026 guidance: $1,750M at midpoint, of which a meaningful portion is revenue- and non-revenue-enhancing capex on existing properties (guided at $510M, up from $324M in 2025).
4. **Dispositions:** $1,813M in FY2025 (ARE's share of aggregate sales price). 2026 guidance: $2,900M at midpoint (25-35% land, 25-35% core, 35-45% non-core).
5. **Debt activity:** Repaid $154M of secured notes; issued $549M of unsecured senior notes at 5.50%; heavy commercial paper activity ($25.4B issued, $25.1B repaid). Net debt reduction of $1,075M-$2,275M targeted for 2026 (midpoint $1,675M).
6. **Venture investments:** $239M in additions, $169M in distributions and sales. Net outflow of approximately $70M. Management targets "close to neutral" for the venture platform.

### Capital Allocation Grade: C-

A blunt assessment of management's capital allocation over the last five years reveals significant value destruction:

- **$5.8B in real estate additions during 2022-2023**, much of which has been impaired. ARE was building into the peak of the biotech cycle, funded partly by debt issuance at historically low rates.
- **$258M in stock repurchases at $70-$100+** when the stock now trades at $43 and the company carries $12.4B in debt. Every dollar spent on buybacks instead of debt reduction has been amplified in negative impact by the equity decline.
- **$178M pre-construction investment in the NYC Option Parcel**, now stuck in litigation with NYC Health + Hospitals over a floodwall dispute. This is a binary outcome: either the development proceeds or it becomes another write-off.

The pivot to discipline in late 2025 -- cutting the dividend, halting new development, selling $2.9B of assets -- is the correct strategy but came 12-18 months too late. The current capital allocation framework is sound: dispositions fund debt reduction and committed construction, retained operating cash flow covers the dividend with substantial surplus, and no new development is being commenced without significant pre-leasing. If management maintains this discipline through the cycle, the grade will improve. The question is whether the disciplined posture survives the eventual market recovery, or whether ARE repeats the 2020-2022 overextension.

### Management Assessment: Honest but Imperfect

The leadership transition from Marcus (visionary builder) to Moglia (disciplined operator) appears appropriate for the current phase. On the Q4 2025 earnings call, management was notably candid about challenges: explicitly referencing the "fifth year of a life science bear market," acknowledging that public biotech leasing remains weak, and providing unusual granularity on Q1 2026 headwinds. ### Occupancy, Leasing & Tenant Quality

The occupancy trajectory is the single most important metric for ARE's dividend thesis. It determines rental income, same-property NOI, and ultimately whether the post-cut dividend remains safe.

**Source:** ARE 10-K filings FY2020-FY2025, supplemental packages, and 2026 guidance.

The 370 basis point decline from 94.6% (December 2024) to 90.9% (December 2025) is deeply concerning for a life science portfolio where leases typically run 7-15 years. In normal markets, occupancy moves in increments of 50-100 basis points per year. A nearly 400 basis point single-year decline signals structural, not merely cyclical, weakness in tenant demand.

The 2026 outlook is even more sobering. Management guides to 87.7%-89.3% year-end occupancy, which includes an approximately 2% benefit from assets designated as held for sale (vacant properties removed from the denominator). The true "operating portfolio" occupancy, excluding this adjustment, is likely 85.7%-87.3% -- levels not seen since ARE was a fraction of its current size. Key 2026 lease expirations aggregate 1.2 million RSF with a weighted-average expiration date in April 2026 and $71M of annual rental revenue, with expected downtime of 6-24 months. An additional 1.2 million RSF of lease expirations with significant expected downtime has been identified for 2027.

**Leasing Activity and Pricing Trends**

Despite the occupancy decline, leasing volume has been resilient at the Megacampus level. FY2025 leasing volume was 4.2 million RSF, with Q4 at 1.2 million RSF -- the highest quarter in the trailing year and 14% above the prior four-quarter average. Importantly, 82% of leasing activity in 2025 came from the existing tenant base, and the weighted-average lease term for new leases was 11.9 years, indicating that tenants who do sign are committing long-term.

However, the economics of leasing have deteriorated sharply:

| Metric | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 | 2026E |

The story these numbers tell is alarming. While headline GAAP rental rate changes remain positive (7.0% in 2025), cash rental rate changes are guided to -4% to -12% for 2026, meaning tenants are paying less in actual cash rent than their predecessors. The difference is driven by escalating free rent concessions (1.5 months in 2025, trending higher) and substantially increased tenant improvement allowances ($55/RSF versus $26/RSF just two years ago). First-generation space in shell condition increasingly requires the landlord to fund the entire buildout -- a radical shift from the era when tenants would invest their own capital in customized labs.

Revenue- and non-revenue-enhancing capital expenditures are guided to $510M in 2026, nearly double the $260M level of 2023. This reflects the reality that maintaining existing tenants in a buyer's market requires significant reinvestment in the physical portfolio -- an additional cash drain not captured in same-property NOI figures.

**Tenant Credit Quality**

The tenant base remains ARE's strongest defensive characteristic. Investment-grade or publicly traded large-cap tenants represent 53% of annual rental revenue. The top 20 tenants provide 84% investment-grade or large-cap credit quality. Tenant rent collections have averaged 99.8% since January 2021 -- an extraordinary number during a five-year bear market. The general allowance for credit losses decreased from $21.3M to $14.3M in FY2025, a positive signal.

The vulnerability lies in the 47% of rental revenue from non-investment-grade tenants, predominantly early-stage and mid-stage biotech companies dependent on venture capital or public equity markets for survival. Management's 2026 guidance explicitly assumes $20-$25M ($6M per quarter at midpoint) in rent reduction from "potential tenant wind-downs," an acknowledgment that some of these smaller tenants will fail.

### Development Pipeline & Future NOI Generation

Active development as of December 31, 2025 stands at 3.5 million RSF of Class A/A+ properties under construction. This is down substantially from peak levels but still represents significant capital commitment:

- $1.03B in remaining committed construction costs
- Projects expected to be placed into service from 2026-2028, generating $220M in future incremental annual NOI (64% leased or under lease negotiations)
- An additional 1.2M RSF of projects (8% leased or under negotiations) where management is "evaluating business strategy" -- a euphemism for potentially pausing, selling, or mothballing these projects

Critically, four specific projects currently under construction have "significant remaining capital needs" and are under go-forward business strategy evaluation. If management decides to pause or sell, capitalization of interest and other costs would cease, further increasing reported interest expense and reducing FFO.

The future development and redevelopment pipeline totals 21.6 million RSF, of which 77% is concentrated within Megacampus ecosystems. Management has stated a goal of reducing non-income-producing assets (land and development) as a percentage of gross assets from 17% (end of 2025) toward approximately 10%, which implies substantial additional land sales. The company's December 2025 Investor Day presentation included selling two redevelopment projects and reducing future funding needs by more than $300M through project pivots (including the Fenway office conversion).

### Market & Segment Analysis

ARE operates in eight geographic segments. Their relative health as of early 2026:

**Greater Boston (Cambridge/Inner Suburbs, Seaport, Fenway):** Remains the crown jewel. Cambridge/Kendall Square adjacent to MIT and Harvard Medical School is the most supply-constrained life science market globally. Tenant-in-market requirements in Greater Boston showed an 11% increase in Q4 2025 -- the first quarterly increase in recent memory (per Peter Moglia on the Q4 call). However, even here, ARE took $541M in impairments on Seaport land and Cambridge development projects. Secondary locations within Greater Boston (Somerville, Alewife) may take 4-5 years to recover per management estimates.

**San Francisco Bay Area (Mission Bay, South San Francisco):** The most distressed of ARE's core markets. South San Francisco experienced historic supply/demand imbalance, and ARE exited the Gateway Boulevard campus (1.6M RSF) at impaired values. Mission Bay assets, anchored by the Genentech/UCSF ecosystem, have more long-term defensibility. The SoMa project (88 Bluxome Street) is being sold to residential developers -- a full capitulation from the original 2017 life science use thesis. ARE pivoted to office use at the Fenway campus for at least one building, acknowledging that lab demand alone cannot fill the space.

**San Diego (Torrey Pines, University Town Center, Sorrento Mesa):** Mixed. Torrey Pines, adjacent to UCSD and Scripps Research, remains premium -- ARE signed its largest-ever life science lease there in July 2025 (466,598 RSF build-to-suit with a 16-year term for a multinational pharmaceutical tenant). UTC and Sorrento Mesa have seen more pressure, with multiple dispositions and impairments.

**Seattle (Lake Union, Bothell):** Lake Union assets near the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center have long-term positioning. Bothell properties were written down and sold. Management is evaluating future pipeline projects in Seattle with milestones averaging May 2026.

**Secondary Markets:** Maryland (stable, NIH-adjacent), Research Triangle (struggled with biomanufacturing demand, $114M impairments), NYC (ACLS campus under litigation risk), Texas (new market, early positioning), Canada (exiting Montreal entirely).

### Balance Sheet Assessment

The balance sheet is simultaneously ARE's greatest strength and its most misunderstood risk. The covenant metrics, measured against GAAP book value of assets, show substantial headroom -- 32% total debt to total assets versus a 60% limit. But book value, as the $2.9B in cumulative impairments demonstrate, can significantly overstate real values. Net debt to equity market cap of 1.60x is uncomfortable for an equity trading at a deep discount to book. If the stock declines another 30% to $30, this ratio balloons to 2.3x -- territory where equity issuance becomes prohibitively dilutive.

The saving graces are structural:
1. **Duration:** 12.1-year weighted-average remaining term means ARE has minimal near-term refinancing pressure. Only 11% of debt matures through 2028.
2. **Fixed rate:** 97.2% of debt is fixed-rate, insulating operating cash flows from short-term rate movements.
3. **Liquidity:** $5.3B total liquidity ($549M cash plus $4.65B undrawn on the $5.0B credit facility) provides multi-year runway.
4. **Credit ratings:** BBB+/Baa1 maintain access to investment-grade debt markets.

Management targets net debt to Adjusted EBITDA of 5.6x-6.2x for Q4 2026 annualized, implying meaningful deleveraging from the 5.7x level at Q4 2025. However, they warned that Q1 2026 leverage will temporarily spike 1.0x-1.5x higher (to approximately 6.7x-7.2x) due to lower quarterly EBITDA before trending down through the year.

### Ground Lease Obligations

A frequently overlooked liability: 31 of ARE's 340 properties sit on ground leases with remaining terms of 29-97 years. The present value of remaining contractual payments aggregates $773.4M as of December 31, 2025. The Cambridge Technology Square ground lease extension required $270M in prepayments across 2024-2025 -- cash that could not be directed to deleveraging. Ground leases create incremental leverage not always visible in the debt schedule.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Exposures

- **Venture capital platform:** $1.50B in total investments with $370.3M in additional funding commitments (weighted-average 8.1 years to expiration).
- **Unconsolidated joint ventures:** Three joint ventures with ARE's share of approximately $31M in net assets and $61M in secured debt at the JV level.

### Biotech Funding Cycle Risk (The Single Most Important Factor)

ARE's business model depends fundamentally on a healthy pipeline of biotech company formation, venture funding, IPO liquidity, and subsequent R&D expansion that drives demand for laboratory space. The current environment represents the convergence of multiple negative forces:

- **Venture capital:** VC fundraising for life science declined to its lowest level since 2016 in 2025. While deployment remains steady, the shrinking pool of dry powder constrains future activity. VCs are focused on clinical-stage, asset-heavy investments that may not drive lab space needs.
- **IPO market:** Effectively closed for early-stage biotech. Without IPO liquidity, private companies cannot raise the capital needed for expansion. On the Q4 call, Hallie Kuhn noted that "without that next bolus of new companies that are IPO-ing that tend to be earlier stage, they still seem to be on the back burner."
- **FDA disruption:** More than 50% turnover in senior FDA leadership during H1 2025, accompanied by layoffs and review delays. Clinical trial requirements are in flux. This creates uncertainty that delays tenant expansion decisions.
- **NIH funding uncertainty:** The proposed 15% cap on institutional indirect grant spending was ruled unlawful, but the threat has caused academic institutions to defer real estate commitments.
- **Drug pricing scrutiny:** Government actions to reduce prescription drug prices weigh on pharmaceutical R&D investment appetites.

Peter Moglia on the Q4 call provided the most honest recovery timeline: 2-3 years for core markets where ARE is heavily invested (Cambridge, Mission Bay, Torrey Pines), and 4-5 years for secondary/tertiary markets where new supply was developed speculatively. If this timeline is correct, the life science lab market does not normalize until 2028-2029 at the earliest.

### Rate Sensitivity

ARE's 97.2% fixed-rate debt structure provides significant insulation from short-term rate movements. However, $1.43B matures through 2028 and must be refinanced. If refinanced at 5.5% versus the current blended 3.91%, annual interest expense increases by approximately $23M over three years. This is manageable but directionally negative.

The larger rate risk operates through the equity side: higher discount rates compress the value of ARE's long-duration lease cash flows, driving down NAV and stock price. The stock's 80%+ decline from peak already reflects this repricing.
