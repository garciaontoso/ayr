## PART I -- Executive Summary

H&R Block is the largest retail tax preparation company in the United States, Canada, and Australia, having prepared 22.8 million tax returns worldwide in fiscal year 2025. The company operates through a hybrid model of approximately 8,714 U.S. offices (6,701 company-owned plus 2,013 franchise), virtual tax professional services, and do-it-yourself software products. The stock has been cut roughly in half from its ~$65 peak in late 2024 to approximately $31 as of early April 2026, creating the widest valuation disconnect between business fundamentals and stock price since the 2008 financial crisis.

The dividend thesis remains intact and has arguably strengthened. Management raised the quarterly dividend to $0.42 per share in FY2026 (annualized $1.68), a 12% increase from FY2025's $1.50, continuing a streak of annual increases that has lifted the payout from $1.00 in FY2019 to $1.68 today -- a 68% cumulative increase in seven years. Free cash flow coverage of the dividend stands at approximately 3.0x ($599M FCF versus $197M dividends in FY2025), and payout ratios on both an earnings basis (~34%) and cash flow basis (~33%) remain comfortable. FY2025 marked all-time records for revenue ($3.76B), EBITDA ($976M), and diluted EPS ($4.42), while FY2026 guidance of $3.875-3.895B in revenue and $4.85-5.00 in adjusted EPS implies continued growth from those records.

The critical development since the last filing cycle is the termination of the IRS Direct File program under the current administration, which removes what was arguably the single greatest existential threat to the commercial tax preparation industry. Simultaneously, a new CEO -- Curtis Campbell, formerly of TaxAct and Intuit -- has taken the helm with a digital-first, AI-forward orientation that represents a credible strategy for the next decade. The sell-off reflects market anxiety about AI disruption of white-collar services, a minor Q2 FY2026 EPS miss ($0.02 on a seasonal loss quarter), and Goldman Sachs' price target reduction -- none of which represent structural impairment to the business or dividend.

**Key numbers at a glance**
- Price / Yield / Payout ratio / Streak: ~$31 / 5.4% / ~34% (NI) / continuous quarterly payments (froze FY2020-21, never cut)
- Last dividend hike: August 2025, 12% increase ($0.375 to $0.42 quarterly)
- 5y dividend CAGR: ~10.9% (FY2020 $1.04 to FY2025 $1.50, annualized)
- 7y dividend CAGR: ~7.7% (FY2019 $1.00 to FY2026E $1.68)
- Net debt / EBITDA: ~1.5x at fiscal year-end (seasonal peak ~2.5x during off-season)
- Free cash flow coverage: 3.0x (FY2025 $599M FCF / $197M dividends)

## PART III -- Historical Review (FY2019-FY2025)

H&R Block's seven-year financial trajectory tells the story of a mature franchise that has used aggressive capital allocation -- principally share buybacks -- to manufacture double-digit EPS growth from a business growing revenue in the low single digits. This is not a criticism; it is a highly effective strategy for a predictable, cash-generative enterprise. The key question for the dividend investor is whether this strategy remains sustainable.

**The arc in three acts:**

**Act I -- The Price War and Reset (FY2019-FY2020).** When Jeff Jones arrived as CEO in late 2017, he inherited a business losing market share to TurboTax in DIY and to independent preparers in assisted. His response was to slash prices for millions of assisted clients, accepting a short-term revenue hit ($3.09B in FY2019) to rebuild the value proposition. FY2019 EPS bottomed at $2.15 on approximately 207 million shares outstanding. The market punished the stock into the low-$20s. In hindsight, this was the critical inflection point -- Jones was investing in the customer base at the expense of near-term profitability.

**Act II -- COVID, Digital Pivot, and Block Horizons 2025 (FY2020-FY2022).** COVID disrupted the FY2020 tax season (filing deadline extended to July), but HRB adapted rapidly to virtual tax preparation. The company launched its Block Horizons 2025 transformation strategy, centering on three growth pillars: (1) Small Business solutions through Block Advisors and the Wave Financial acquisition (closed FY2020 for approximately $405M), (2) Financial products through Spruce mobile banking and expanded Emerald Card services, and (3) AI and digital innovation through AI Tax Assist and platform modernization. Revenue recovered from $3.0B (FY2020, shortened season) to $3.46B (FY2022). Share count declined from approximately 200M to approximately 160M. The dividend was frozen at $1.04 during FY2020-FY2021 -- a sign of management discipline under stress, choosing to preserve the payout rather than cut it.

**Act III -- Record Results and Market Dislocation (FY2023-FY2026).** Revenue hit consecutive all-time highs: $3.47B (FY2023), $3.61B (FY2024), $3.76B (FY2025). EBITDA expanded from $915M to $963M to $976M. EPS compounded from $3.56 to $4.14 to $4.42, driven predominantly by the 30%+ reduction in share count. The dividend growth rate accelerated from low-single-digits to double-digits, with the FY2025 increase to $1.50 per share representing a 17.2% hike. Then the stock collapsed: from $65 in late 2024 to approximately $31 by early 2026, a 52% drawdown driven by AI disruption narrative, a minor Q2 FY2026 earnings miss, and institutional de-risking. The business continued performing well throughout -- Q2 FY2026 revenue was up 11.1% year-over-year.

**Key takeaways:**
- Revenue CAGR of approximately 3.3% (FY2019-FY2025) -- modest but remarkably consistent for a mature consumer services company
- EPS CAGR of approximately 12.7% (FY2019-FY2025) -- almost entirely buyback-driven, which is the strategic intention

4. FY2025 -- Record revenue, EBITDA, and EPS; dividend hiked 17.2%; IRS Direct File terminated
5. FY2026 -- CEO transition to Campbell; stock collapses 52% creating a generational yield opportunity

## PART IV -- Dividend Deep Dive

This is the core section for the dividend investor. H&R Block's dividend profile is unusual among mid-cap companies: a high current yield (5.4%), moderate payout ratio (~34%), strong growth trajectory (10.9% five-year CAGR), and a management team that has demonstrated both commitment (never cutting) and discipline (freezing during stress rather than overextending).

- **Current yield:** ~5.4% ($1.68 annualized at ~$31)
- **Payout ratio (EPS basis):** ~34% ($1.50 / $4.42 FY2025)
- **Payout ratio (FCF basis):** ~33% ($197M dividends / $599M FCF FY2025)
- **Dividend streak:** Continuous quarterly payments without interruption. Froze at $1.04 during FY2020-FY2021 (COVID), so the formal "consecutive increase" streak restarted in FY2022. Not a Dividend Aristocrat.
- **5y dividend CAGR:** ~10.9% (FY2020 $1.04 to FY2025 $1.50)
- **7y dividend CAGR:** ~7.7% (FY2019 $1.00 to FY2026E $1.68)
- **Last hike:** August 2025, 12% increase ($0.375 to $0.42 quarterly)
- **Pattern of hikes:** Annual, declared at the August board meeting coinciding with the 10-K filing. Growth rate has accelerated from low-single-digit (FY2019-FY2022 era: +4% per year) to double-digit (FY2023-FY2026: 7-17% per year).
- **Dividend coverage by FCF:** 3.0x (FY2025), 3.6x (FY2024), 4.1x (FY2023). Coverage is declining as dividend growth outpaces FCF growth, but remains well above danger levels.
- **Currency of payment:** USD
- **Withholding tax for non-US holders:** Standard 30% WHT (reducible under treaty; 10% under U.S.-China treaty)

**Dividend sustainability narrative**

The dividend is safe under all scenarios short of a catastrophic, multi-quarter disruption to the U.S. tax filing season. The 33% FCF payout ratio provides significant cushion -- management could absorb a 40% decline in free cash flow before the dividend would consume 100% of FCF. Even in FY2020 (COVID-disrupted season with a compressed filing window), operating cash flow remained well above dividend obligations.

The structural vulnerability is HRB's extreme seasonality. Nearly all annual profit is generated in a single quarter (Q3, January-March), with the company operating at significant losses during the other three quarters. In Q1 FY2026, the net loss was $165M; in Q2 FY2026, the net loss was $242M. The six-month cumulative loss was $408M. This means HRB must generate approximately $1.0B or more in Q3-Q4 profit to achieve annual targets. If a black swan event disrupts tax season -- a prolonged government shutdown preventing IRS processing, a pandemic-scale event, or a rapid shift in filing behavior -- the combination of operating losses already incurred, seasonal debt outstanding ($945M drawn on the CLOC at December 31, 2025), and committed dividends could create a genuine cash squeeze.

The 7/10 dividend safety rating reflects this structural reality: excellent current coverage combined with a single-point-of-failure business model that concentrates all cash generation into an approximately 90-day window. Higher-rated dividend payers do not have this concentration risk.

## PART V -- Business Quality & Moat

- **Moat type:** Brand + regulatory complexity + switching costs (narrow but durable)
- **Moat trajectory:** Stable with potential narrowing over a 10-year horizon due to AI
- **Pricing power evidence:** FY2025 U.S. assisted net average charge increased 5.1%; FY2025 U.S. DIY paid net average charge increased 9.8%. Both grew above inflation, demonstrating pricing power. FY2024 showed similar dynamics (assisted NAC +4.0%, DIY NAC +6.8%). Multi-year trend of consistent mid-to-high single-digit NAC increases is the strongest evidence of pricing power in the portfolio.
- **Return on invested capital trend:** Difficult to calculate conventionally due to negative equity, but the business generates approximately $600M in net income on approximately $800M in tangible assets -- an exceptionally high capital efficiency ratio that underscores the asset-light franchise model

**Moat components assessed individually:**

**1. Brand recognition (strong, stable).** H&R Block is arguably the most recognized consumer brand in the U.S. tax preparation industry. The green square logo has been synonymous with "getting your taxes done" for 70 years. CNET named HRB the best online tax product for 2026. Brand trust is particularly important in tax preparation because filing errors have financial penalties -- clients are effectively purchasing an accuracy guarantee backed by the HRB brand. This creates a trust premium that new entrants, including AI startups, cannot replicate quickly.

**2. Regulatory complexity as a moat (strong, widening).** The U.S. tax code is approximately 6,871 pages. State tax codes add thousands more. The passage of the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (H.R. 1, signed July 4, 2025) introduced significant new tax law changes, including new deductions, exemptions, and eligibility rules that increase complexity for individual filers. As CEO Campbell stated on the Q2 FY2026 call: "The net effect is greater complexity, more questions, and a heightened desire for confidence as clients navigate new deductions, exemptions, and eligibility rules." Every tax law change is a demand driver for professional preparation. The political incentive to simplify the tax code is effectively zero.

**3. Physical network effects (moderate, slowly declining).** 8,714 U.S. offices create convenience and accessibility, particularly in underbanked and rural communities. However, this network is a gradually declining asset as digital adoption grows. Management is rationalizing the footprint at approximately 100 offices per year while investing in virtual delivery. The office network remains a moat today -- 70 years of real estate site selection in high-traffic retail locations is not replicable -- but its value will diminish over the next decade.

**4. Switching costs (moderate).** Clients who build relationships with specific preparers, store prior-year returns in HRB's system, and rely on the accuracy guarantee and Peace of Mind extended service plan have meaningful switching costs. The "Second Look" program (reviewing three years of prior returns for new clients to identify missed refund opportunities) is a direct investment in creating switching barriers.

**5. Scale economics (strong in DIY, moderate in assisted).** In DIY, HRB's software development costs are spread across 3.8M+ paid returns. In assisted, scale helps with marketing, training, and brand, but the labor-intensive model limits operating leverage -- field wages alone consumed $927M in FY2025 (24.7% of revenue).

## PART VI -- Capital Allocation

H&R Block's capital allocation is the most important driver of shareholder returns. Management runs the company as a leveraged cash-flow-return vehicle, explicitly prioritizing capital return (dividends plus buybacks) over balance sheet conservation. This is the right strategy for a mature, predictable, asset-light franchise, but it creates vulnerability to disruption scenarios.

1. **Dividends:** $197M in FY2025 (33% of FCF, 29% of OCF). Annual growth rate has accelerated to double-digits. FY2026 run-rate approximately $213M at $0.42/quarter on approximately 127M shares.

2. **Buybacks:** $400M in FY2025 at average $61.10/share (4.7% of shares retired); $400.1M in Q1 FY2026 alone at average $50.90/share (5.9% of shares retired). This completed the FY2026 repurchase plan in a single quarter. Since FY2016, cumulative capital return to shareholders approaches $5 billion. Current authorization: $700M remaining under the $1.5B program with no expiration date. At $31 per share, $400M would retire approximately 12.9M shares -- roughly 10% of the float in a single year.

3. **Capex:** $82M in FY2025 ($64M in FY2024). Relates primarily to recurring retail office improvements, computers, software, and cloud infrastructure. Low capital intensity is the hallmark of the business model; maintenance capex of approximately $60-80M per year is sufficient.

4. **M&A:** $35.5M in FY2025, $43.4M in FY2024 (primarily franchise acquisitions at attractive EBITDA multiples). This is a steady, disciplined program of consolidating franchisees into company-owned offices, which converts royalty revenue (~30% of gross) into full company-owned revenue, improving per-location economics. The Wave acquisition (FY2020, ~$405M) was the only transformational deal in recent history.

5. **Debt management:** Issued $350M of 5.375% Senior Notes due 2032 in August 2025 to refinance the maturing 5.250% 2025 notes. CLOC extended to July 2030 at $1.5B capacity. Net debt at fiscal year-end was approximately $160M ($1.14B debt minus $983M cash), but this is misleading -- seasonal CLOC borrowings peaked at $945M by December 2025, pushing total debt to $2.4B during the off-season. The debt-to-EBITDA covenant requires 3.50x or less at March/June/September quarter-ends and 4.50x or less at December quarter-ends, reflecting the known seasonality.

**Capital allocation math for FY2025:**

| Use of Cash | Amount ($M) | % of FCF |
|---|---|---|
| Dividends | 197 | 33% |
| Share repurchases | 400 | 67% |
| Franchise acquisitions | 36 | 6% |
| **Total deployed** | **633** | **106%** |
| Free cash flow | 599 | 100% |

Source: HRB FY2025 10-K, Consolidated Statements of Cash Flows.

Management is deploying slightly more than 100% of FCF to shareholder returns, funding the deficit from the balance sheet. This is aggressive but sustainable in the near term given the $983M cash position at fiscal year-end. The risk manifests if FCF declines materially while the buyback program continues at current pace.

**Capital allocation grade: B+.** The buyback program is exceptionally effective at current prices and the dividend growth trajectory is strong. The one-notch deduction from an A reflects the aggressive leverage -- management is running the balance sheet hot for a business with single-quarter cash concentration risk.

## PART VII -- Management Assessment

- **CEO:** Curtis A. Campbell, age 52, effective January 1, 2026. Previously CEO of TaxAct (2018-2023), Managing VP at Capital One Financial (2017-2018), VP of Product Management and Strategy at Intuit/TurboTax (2014-2017). Campbell joined HRB in June 2024 as President, Global Consumer Tax and Chief Product Officer -- a 15-month overlap with departing CEO Jeff Jones that ensured strategic continuity. His background is uniquely suited: he has competed against HRB (at TaxAct and Intuit), led fintech operations (at Capital One), and brings deep digital product expertise.
- **CFO:** Tiffany L. Mason, age 50, since September 2024. Previously EVP and CFO of Driven Brands Holdings (2020-2023) and multiple senior finance roles at Lowe's Companies (2006-2019), including interim CFO in 2018. Her franchising background (Driven Brands operates franchise models) is directly relevant to HRB's franchise operations.
- **Insider ownership:** Limited public disclosure in 10-K (incorporated by reference to proxy). Historically, executive ownership has been modest relative to market cap.
- **Comp structure:** Performance metrics include EBITDA from continuing operations and pretax income (both subject to permitted adjustments), which align management incentives with the key metrics that drive dividend sustainability.
- **Guidance track record:** Management has consistently set conservative initial guidance and achieved within or above ranges. The FY2026 outlook ($3.875-3.895B revenue, $1.015-1.035B EBITDA, $4.85-5.00 adjusted EPS) was reaffirmed at Q2 FY2026 without modification despite market turbulence. In FY2025, actual results came in within guided ranges across all metrics.

**Assessment of the CEO transition:**

The Jones-to-Campbell handoff is one of the smoother CEO transitions in recent mid-cap history. Jones provided 16 months of notice, personally selected and mentored his successor, and remained in an advisory role through the transition. Campbell's first earnings call (Q2 FY2026, February 2026) demonstrated strategic clarity: he emphasized client-centricity, technology-enabled human expertise, disciplined experimentation, and AI as an enabler rather than a threat. Key quotes from the transcript:

"This is a fundamental change in how I intend to lead -- shifting from a short-term seasonal lens to a clear long-term view of the ideal client and tax pro experience."

"AI is a critical enabler of our long-term vision. Our approach is intentional, disciplined, and responsible -- not focused on using AI for its own sake, but on applying it to real client and associate challenges at scale."

The risk in Campbell's tenure is not strategic incoherence (his vision is clearly articulated) but rather fintech ambition exceeding the franchise's natural boundaries. His stated interest in making HRB "a fintech company that also does taxes" requires careful capital allocation to avoid the empire-building trap. The Wave integration, which Campbell pledged to complete by fiscal year-end, will be an early test.

**Negative stockholders' equity discussion:** The ($823M) equity deficit as of December 31, 2025 is a mechanical consequence of cumulative share buybacks exceeding retained earnings. This is common among mature, buyback-heavy companies (McDonald's, Starbucks, Colgate-Palmolive have similar dynamics) and does not indicate insolvency. The company's earning power, cash generation, and access to $1.5B in revolving credit provide ample liquidity regardless of the GAAP equity position.

**Operating cash flow declining trend:** OCF decreased from $765M (FY2023) to $721M (FY2024) to $681M (FY2025). The decline is attributable to changes in working capital (income tax reserves, accounts payable timing) rather than deteriorating core earnings. EBITDA expanded over the same period. This working capital drag is worth monitoring but is not structural.

**Goodwill impairment risk:** Total goodwill of $816M includes approximately $159M attributable to Wave (the FY2020 acquisition). The FY2025 10-K identifies Wave goodwill as a critical audit matter due to fair value sensitivity. Wave revenue grew 13.2% in FY2025 to $109M and CFO Mason highlighted continued double-digit growth in the Pro-Tier subscription product during Q2 FY2026. An impairment appears unlikely under current growth trajectories but would represent a non-cash charge of up to $159M if Wave materially underperforms.

**Litigation contingencies:** As of December 31, 2025, HRB's accrued liabilities for litigation and other related contingencies totaled $10.0M (up from $6.2M at June 30, 2025). The primary exposures relate to governmental inquiries and class actions regarding the IRS Free File Program and the use of tracking pixels in DIY tax preparation services. The company states that the aggregate range of reasonably possible losses in excess of amounts accrued is "not material." The discontinued Sand Canyon Corporation mortgage subsidiary continues to appear in filings as a legacy risk but has been dormant for over 15 years.

## PART IX-B -- Competitive Threat Assessment: TurboTax, AI, and the IRS

This section addresses the three competitive vectors that dominate the bear narrative on HRB. Each is assessed on a factual basis using the SEC filings and earnings transcripts.

### TurboTax Competition

Intuit's TurboTax dominates the DIY category with an estimated 30%+ share of self-prepared returns. HRB's DIY business ($384M, 10.2% of FY2025 revenue) competes directly with TurboTax, and HRB is the clear number-two player in a category where the leader has structural scale and brand advantages.

But the bear case misses a critical segmentation point: HRB's core business is assisted tax preparation, not DIY. Assisted revenue was $2,413M in FY2025 -- 64% of total revenue, growing at 6.1% year-over-year. TurboTax does not meaningfully compete in the in-person assisted channel. The competitive overlap is in DIY, where HRB is actually growing faster: FY2025 DIY revenue was up 9.7% with a 9.8% increase in paid net average charge. FY2024 showed a similar pattern, with DIY revenue up 11.1% on a 6.8% NAC increase combined with 5.4% growth in paid returns.

The market treats HRB as a TurboTax victim. The reality is that HRB and TurboTax serve largely different customer segments. HRB's core assisted client is older, less digitally fluent, has more complex tax situations (rental properties, small business income, multi-state filing), and is willing to pay $232+ for a human preparer, accuracy guarantee, and audit representation. TurboTax's core client is younger, digitally native, and has a simpler W-2 return. At the margins there is overlap, but these are not perfect substitutes, and the evidence shows HRB gaining value share even as the competitive narrative intensifies.

### AI/ChatGPT Disruption

This is the more serious long-term threat and the primary driver of the stock's 52% decline. The bear case is: "ChatGPT or equivalent AI will prepare most tax returns within 5 years, making HRB's human-preparer model obsolete."

Three counterarguments deserve consideration:

**1. Tax preparation is a liability product, not an information product.** When a client hires HRB, they are purchasing an accuracy guarantee (HRB reimburses penalties and interest from preparer errors), audit representation (Peace of Mind at $87M revenue in FY2025), and legal protection. If an AI makes an error on a tax return and the client is audited, there is no recourse. This liability gap is HRB's most durable moat and does not close with better AI technology -- it requires a fundamental change in the legal and regulatory framework around tax liability.

**2. HRB is adopting AI offensively, not defending against it.** The company launched AI Tax Assist in FY2025, offering generative-AI-powered help to DIY filers at no additional cost. In FY2026, HRB deployed a nationally launched AI-enabled tax pro assistant that provides real-time guidance to human preparers during client interactions. As Campbell stated on the Q2 FY2026 call: "The real advantage comes when technology enhances, not replaces, the judgment of tax pros." HRB's scale -- 22.8M returns, 70,000 seasonal tax pros -- provides a training data and feedback loop advantage that pure-play AI startups cannot match.

**3. Tax code complexity favors incumbents.** The U.S. tax code comprises approximately 6,871 pages at the federal level, with thousands more at the state level. The passage of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act in July 2025 added significant new complexity. The IRS processes approximately 160 million individual returns annually; the "simple W-2" return that AI could handle reliably represents perhaps 40-50% of filings. The other 50-60% involve judgment calls, optimization decisions, and audit risk that most filers will not trust to an unsupervised AI for years to come. The realistic AI timeline suggests a compression of HRB's DIY revenue over 5-10 years and a slowing of assisted volume growth, but not a destruction of the core assisted business within the next decade.

### IRS Direct File -- The Threat That Died

The single most dangerous competitive threat to HRB was not TurboTax or ChatGPT -- it was the IRS itself. The IRS Direct File program, launched in tax seasons 2024 and 2025, offered a limited free filing system directly from the government. If expanded to cover most filers, it would have undercut the entire commercial tax preparation industry.

The current administration has terminated this program. The FY2025 10-K acknowledges the risk factor but notes: "In tax seasons 2024 and 2025, the IRS offered a limited free direct tax filing system, which it may continue or expand in the future." The "may continue or expand" language is now moot under the current administration, which has simultaneously reduced IRS staffing and resources. This is a material positive catalyst: the government as direct competitor has been removed from the competitive landscape for the duration of this administration. Political risk remains (a future administration could revive the program), but the near-term threat is neutralized.

## PART X -- Risks & Stress Tests

**Tier 1 -- Material risks that could impair the dividend:**

1. **AI-driven commoditization of basic tax preparation (probability: 15-20% over 5-10 years).** If generative AI progresses to the point where it can reliably prepare 80%+ of tax returns with legal liability protection, HRB's assisted business (64% of revenue) faces structural decline. The mitigation is HRB's own AI deployment (AI Tax Assist, AI-enabled tax pro assistant), its liability advantage (100% accuracy guarantee with reimbursement of penalties and interest), and the trust gap between AI-generated and professionally-prepared returns. The realistic timeline is 5-10 years for simple returns, longer for complex returns.

2. **Government re-entry into tax preparation (probability: 10-15% in 4 years, contingent on election outcomes).** A future administration could revive IRS Direct File or implement pre-populated returns. While currently terminated, the political risk persists and would be catastrophic if implemented at scale. The FY2025 10-K acknowledges: "The adoption or expansion of any measures that significantly simplify tax return preparation... could reduce demand for our services and products and could have a material adverse effect."

3. **Balance sheet stress during a disrupted tax season (probability: 5-10% in any given year).** With $2.4B peak seasonal debt and single-quarter cash concentration, a scenario where tax filing season is severely disrupted could force borrowing at unfavorable terms or constrain shareholder returns. The $1.5B undrawn CLOC capacity (as of June 30, 2025) provides a buffer, and the 2025 CLOC extension to 2030 strengthens the liquidity position.

**Tier 2 -- Significant risks that could impair returns but likely not the dividend:**

4. **CEO transition execution risk.** Campbell's fintech ambitions may not align with the company's core franchise value. The Wave integration, scheduled for completion in FY2026, is the first material execution test.

5. **Declining operating cash flow trend.** OCF has declined from $765M to $681M over three years. If this trend persists, the buyback program faces funding pressure before the dividend does, but a sustained decline would eventually compress coverage ratios.

6. **Wave goodwill impairment risk.** $159M of goodwill attributable to Wave is identified as a critical audit matter. A write-down would be non-cash but signals strategic failure in the small business pillar.

7. **Legal contingencies related to Free File Program and pixel tracking.** Class actions and mass arbitrations are pending. Accrual as of December 31, 2025 was $10.0M, but outcomes are uncertain and potentially material.

8. **2028 debt refinancing at higher rates.** The $500M of 2.500% notes maturing in July 2028 will likely refinance at 5.0%+ based on current market conditions, adding approximately $12.5-15M in annual interest expense.

**Tier 3 -- Background risks:**

9. **Franchise relationship deterioration.** Franchise offices contribute $193M in royalties. If franchise quality degrades or franchisees exit, HRB loses distribution without proportional revenue replacement (partially mitigated by the ongoing franchise acquisition program).

10. **Foreign currency risk.** Canada and Australia operations ($247M combined) are exposed to CAD/AUD movements. Not material to consolidated results but creates quarterly noise.

11. **Cybersecurity incident.** Tax returns contain the most sensitive personal and financial data. A major breach would cause reputational damage, regulatory consequences, and potential loss of electronic filing authorization from the IRS.

**Stress test scenarios:**

The stress tests confirm that the dividend is safe under mild-to-moderate stress scenarios but vulnerable to a catastrophic Q3 disruption. The probability-weighted expectation strongly favors continued payment and growth.

## PART X-B -- Variant Perception and Buyback Economics

### What Consensus Believes

The consensus view, reflected in the 7x earnings multiple, is that HRB is a slowly dying business. The narrative is: TurboTax won DIY, ChatGPT will win assisted, and the physical office network is an expensive relic. Goldman Sachs cut its price target from $48 to $32 in February 2026, triggering institutional selling. The stock trades as a value trap.

### Where the Consensus Is Wrong

**1. The buyback compounding at depressed prices is not priced in.** This is the single most underappreciated dynamic in the stock. At $31, every $400M of annual buybacks retires approximately 12.9M shares -- roughly 10% of the float per year. If management maintains $400M in annual buybacks for three years at an average price of approximately $35, they will retire roughly 34M shares, bringing the count from approximately 127M to approximately 93M. On $600M of net income (flat from FY2025), that produces $6.45 EPS -- a 46% increase from FY2025's $4.42 on zero earnings growth. The market is pricing terminal decline while management is engineering mechanical EPS growth through capital allocation.

The historical buyback record demonstrates consistent execution:

Management's $1.5B authorization ($700M remaining) represents approximately 18% of the current market cap. The combination of aggressive buybacks at depressed prices, a 5.4% dividend yield, and flat-to-growing earnings creates a compelling total shareholder return profile regardless of whether the stock re-rates.

**2. The IRS Direct File termination is not priced as a positive catalyst.** The removal of the government as a direct competitor to commercial tax preparation is a material change in the competitive landscape. Under the prior administration, Direct File was expanding in scope and represented a genuine existential threat. Its termination reduces the bear case's probability distribution significantly, yet the stock has continued to decline.

**3. The CEO transition is a positive signal, not a risk.** Campbell's background -- running TaxAct (HRB's third competitor), holding VP roles at Intuit (TurboTax), and operating at Capital One (fintech) -- gives him competitive insight that no other HRB CEO has possessed. He understands the TurboTax playbook from the inside, the fintech opportunity from Capital One, and the challenger dynamics from TaxAct. His stated ambition to accelerate technology adoption while respecting the core franchise is strategically sound.

### Where Consensus May Be Right

**1. AI disruption is a question of when, not if, for simple returns.** The approximately 40% of U.S. returns that are straightforward W-2 filings will eventually be highly automatable with liability protection. HRB's DIY business will face pricing pressure from free or near-free AI alternatives within 5-10 years.

**2. The physical office network is a gradually wasting asset.** 8,714 offices represent $439M in annual occupancy expense (12% of revenue). While the network provides convenience and accessibility today, its value will diminish as virtual delivery improves and client demographics shift toward digital-native cohorts.

**3. The leveraged balance sheet limits strategic flexibility.** If a genuine competitive shock arrives -- whether from AI, government policy, or an unforeseen disruptor -- HRB has less financial room to invest in response than a net-cash competitor would. The negative stockholders' equity and seasonal debt structure constrain the company's ability to simultaneously maintain capital returns and fund transformational investment.

## PART XI -- Thesis & Verdict

**Thesis:**
H&R Block is a deeply out-of-favor, cash-generative franchise trading at distressed multiples (7x trailing earnings, 5.4% yield) while delivering record financial results. The business benefits from a narrow but durable moat composed of brand trust, regulatory complexity, and switching costs. The single largest existential threat (IRS Direct File) has been terminated. AI disruption is real over a 10+ year horizon but is paradoxically benefiting HRB in the near term through its own AI deployment and the increased complexity driving demand for professional preparation. The buyback program is mechanically accretive at depressed prices -- at $31, every $400M of repurchases retires approximately 10% of shares outstanding. The new CEO brings directly relevant competitive experience from TaxAct and Intuit. For a dividend investor with a 3-5 year horizon, the combination of 5.4% yield, double-digit dividend growth, and significant multiple expansion potential from deeply compressed valuations represents a compelling total return opportunity.

**Verdict:** BUY below $35. Accumulate aggressively below $30.
