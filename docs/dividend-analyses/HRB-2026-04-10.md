## SECTION 1 -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: BUY below $35. Accumulate aggressively below $30. Dividend Safety: 7/10. Confidence: Medium-High.**

H&R Block is the most misunderstood dividend stock in the mid-cap universe right now. The market is pricing in terminal decline for a business that just posted record revenue, record EBITDA, and is buying back stock at an unprecedented pace. The disconnect between the stock price ($31, down ~52% from the 52-week high of $64.62) and the underlying business (FY2025 revenue $3.76B, net income $609M, EPS $4.42) is the widest it has been since the 2008 financial crisis.

The sell-off has three identifiable catalysts: (1) Goldman Sachs cut the price target from $48 to $32 in February 2026, triggering a wave of institutional selling; (2) Q2 FY2026 EPS of -$1.91 missed consensus of -$1.89, with a higher-than-expected tax rate (~25% vs 22% prior year) creating a $0.19 headwind; (3) broad market anxiety about AI-driven disruption of white-collar service businesses, with tax preparation as the poster child.

None of these catalysts are structural. Goldman's cut was driven by multiple compression, not fundamental deterioration. The EPS miss was $0.02 on a seasonal loss quarter that contributes nothing to annual earnings. And the AI threat, while real over a 10+ year horizon, has paradoxically benefited HRB in the near term -- the company's AI Tax Assist product earned industry recognition, and complexity-averse filers are migrating toward trusted brands rather than away from them.

**Why 7/10 on dividend safety (not 8 or 9):** The leverage ratio is real. HRB carries ~$2.4B of long-term debt against a business that generates essentially all of its annual profit in a single quarter (January-March). If a black swan disrupts tax season -- a government shutdown extending the filing deadline, a pandemic-scale event, or a rapid adoption of government-provided tax filing -- the combination of operating leverage, financial leverage, and dividend commitment could create a genuine cash squeeze. The FY2025 payout ratio on a cash basis was approximately 29% of operating cash flow ($197M dividends / $681M OCF), which is comfortable, but the seasonal concentration of cash generation makes this a "safe until it isn't" profile. The 7/10 reflects excellent current coverage with a structural fragility that higher-rated dividend payers lack.

**Top 3 reasons to buy now:**
1. Record business results at 7x earnings and 5.4% yield -- this is empirically cheap relative to any trailing valuation metric.
2. The single largest existential threat (IRS Direct File) has been terminated. This is a material positive catalyst the market has not fully priced.
3. Buyback-driven EPS compounding continues to work -- at $31, every $400M of buybacks retires ~13M shares (~10% of float), creating a mechanical EPS tailwind even on flat revenue.

**Top 3 risks to the thesis:**
1. AI disruption may eventually commoditize basic tax preparation, pressuring both assisted pricing power and DIY conversion rates over a 5-10 year horizon.
2. CEO transition during a period of stock weakness creates an opening for strategic missteps -- Curtis Campbell's fintech ambitions may not align with the company's core franchise value.
3. Leverage plus extreme seasonality means a single bad tax season could force a dividend freeze or cut.

Founded in 1955 in Kansas City, Missouri, the company prepared 22.8 million tax returns worldwide in FY2025 through a hybrid model combining physical retail offices, virtual tax professional services, and do-it-yourself (DIY) software products.

## SECTION 3 -- THE 10-YEAR ARC (FY2016-FY2025)

H&R Block's story over the past decade is one of shrinking the denominator (shares outstanding) faster than the numerator (net income) can grow, creating the illusion of growth in a fundamentally mature business. This is not a criticism -- it is a highly effective capital allocation strategy for a cash cow franchise.

**The arc in three acts:**

**Act I -- The Price War and Reset (FY2016-FY2019):** Jeff Jones arrived in 2017 and immediately slashed prices for millions of assisted clients, accepting a short-term revenue hit ($3.16B to $3.09B) to stem market share losses to TurboTax. FY2019 EPS bottomed at $2.15. The market punished the stock into the low-$20s. In hindsight, this was the critical inflection -- Jones was investing in the customer base at the expense of near-term profitability, a decision that seeded the recovery.

**Act II -- COVID Volatility and Pivot to Digital (FY2020-FY2022):** COVID disrupted the FY2020 tax season (extended to July), but HRB adapted rapidly to virtual tax preparation. Revenue recovered from $3.0B (FY2020, shortened season) to $3.47B (FY2023). More importantly, HRB invested heavily in its digital platform, launching AI Tax Assist, upgrading the mobile experience, and integrating Spruce (mobile banking). The Wave acquisition (closed FY2020) added small business financial services. Share count declined from ~188M to ~157M over this period.

**Act III -- Record Results and Market Dislocation (FY2023-FY2026):** Revenue hit all-time highs ($3.47B, $3.61B, $3.76B for FY2023-FY2025). EPS climbed from $3.56 to $4.14 to $4.42. Buybacks accelerated ($350M-$400M/year). The dividend was raised from $1.16 to $1.50/share. And yet the stock -- after briefly reaching $65 in late 2024 -- collapsed 52% to $31 on AI disruption fears and a minor earnings miss. This is where we are today.

## SECTION 6 -- THE TURBOTAX AND AI THREAT: HONEST ASSESSMENT

This is the section that matters most for the investment thesis. Every bear case on HRB reduces to some version of "TurboTax and/or AI will make this business obsolete." Let me address both directly.

### TurboTax Competition

Intuit's TurboTax is the 800-pound gorilla of DIY tax preparation. It dominates online filing with estimated 30%+ market share of self-prepared returns. HRB's DIY business ($384M, 10% of revenue) competes directly with TurboTax, and frankly, HRB is the #2 player in a category where #1 has scale and brand advantages.

But here is what the bears miss: **HRB's core business is assisted tax preparation, not DIY.** Assisted revenue was $2,413M in FY2025 -- 64% of total revenue and growing at 6.1%. TurboTax does not meaningfully compete in assisted. The competitive overlap is in DIY, where HRB is actually growing faster (+9.7% in FY2025 vs Intuit's overall consumer tax growth). HRB's net average charge for DIY has been rising (9.8% increase in FY2025), suggesting pricing power rather than margin compression.

The market treats HRB as a TurboTax victim. The reality is that HRB and TurboTax serve largely different customer segments: HRB's core assisted client is older, less digitally fluent, has more complex tax situations (rental properties, small business income, multi-state filing), and is willing to pay $232+ for the peace of mind of a human preparer and an accuracy guarantee. TurboTax's core client is younger, digitally native, and has a simpler W-2 return. There is overlap at the margin, but these are not perfect substitutes.

### AI/ChatGPT Disruption

This is the more serious long-term threat. The bear case goes: "ChatGPT can prepare a simple tax return for free. Within 5 years, AI will handle 80% of tax situations. HRB's human-preparer model becomes obsolete."

I take this seriously but believe it is overblown for three reasons:

**1. Tax preparation is a liability product, not an information product.** When you hire HRB, you are not just buying data entry. You are buying an accuracy guarantee (HRB reimburses penalties and interest from errors), audit representation (Peace of Mind at $87M revenue), and legal protection. If ChatGPT makes an error on your taxes and you get audited, there is no one to call. This liability gap is HRB's moat, and it does not close with better AI.

**2. HRB is adopting AI, not fighting it.** The company launched AI Tax Assist in its FY2025 product lineup, offering generative AI-powered help to DIY filers at no additional cost. Management explicitly acknowledged AI in their FY2025 10-K risk factors as both a risk and an opportunity. The new CEO Curtis Campbell comes from TaxAct and Intuit -- he understands the digital landscape. HRB's scale (70,000 seasonal employees, 22.8M returns) gives it a training data advantage that pure-play AI startups cannot match.

**3. Complexity and trust favor incumbents.** The U.S. tax code is 6,871 pages. State tax codes add thousands more. Small business, freelance, investment, real estate, international, and multi-state returns are genuinely complex. The IRS processes ~160M individual returns annually; the "simple W-2" return that AI can handle represents perhaps 40-50% of filings. The other 50-60% involve judgment calls, optimization decisions, and audit risk that most filers will not trust to an unsupervised AI for years to come.

**The realistic AI timeline:** AI will likely compress HRB's DIY revenue over the next 5-10 years and may slow assisted volume growth. But it is unlikely to destroy the assisted business in the next decade. The more probable outcome is that HRB uses AI to reduce labor costs (fewer tax preparers per return), increase throughput (more returns per office), and improve upsell conversion (AI-identified optimization opportunities) -- making AI a net positive for margins even if negative for top-line volume.

### IRS Direct File -- The Threat That Died

The single most dangerous competitive threat to HRB was not TurboTax or AI -- it was the IRS itself. The IRS Direct File program, launched in tax seasons 2024 and 2025, offered a limited free filing system directly from the government. If expanded to cover most filers, it would have undercut the entire commercial tax preparation industry.

The current administration has terminated this program. The FY2025 10-K acknowledges this risk factor but notes that Direct File was limited in scope. HRB's competitive threat disclosure states: "In tax seasons 2024 and 2025, the IRS offered a limited free direct tax filing system, which it may continue or expand in the future." The "may continue or expand" language is now moot.

This is a material positive catalyst that the market has not fully digested. The elimination of IRS Direct File removes the government as a direct competitor and ensures that the ~160M annual individual returns remain addressable by commercial preparers. Political risk remains (a future administration could revive the program), but the near-term threat is neutralized.

## SECTION 7 -- MOAT ANALYSIS AND BUYBACK ECONOMICS

### The Moat (Narrow but Durable)

HRB's competitive advantages, honestly assessed:

**1. Brand recognition (strong).** H&R Block is arguably the most recognized brand in tax preparation. The green square logo is synonymous with "getting your taxes done." Brand surveys consistently rank HRB as the first or second brand consumers associate with tax filing. This brand creates a trust premium that is difficult for new entrants -- including AI-native startups -- to replicate.

**2. Physical network effects (moderate, declining).** 8,714 U.S. offices create convenience and accessibility, particularly in underbanked and rural communities. However, this network is a declining asset as digital adoption grows. Management is rationalizing the footprint (~100 office reduction per year), converting franchise to company-owned, and investing in virtual delivery. The office network is still a moat today but will be less so in five years.

**3. Regulatory complexity as a moat (strong).** The U.S. tax code's complexity is HRB's best friend. Every new tax law, every regulation change, every new form creates demand for professional preparation. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, COVID stimulus provisions, and state-level tax changes have all driven complexity higher. As long as the tax code remains complex -- and there is no political incentive to simplify it -- demand for professional preparation persists.

**4. Switching costs (moderate).** Tax clients who build a relationship with a specific preparer, store prior-year returns in HRB's system, and rely on the accuracy guarantee have meaningful switching costs. Client retention rates in assisted are not disclosed but are estimated at 75-80% based on industry norms.

**5. Scale economics (strong in DIY, moderate in assisted).** In DIY, HRB's software development costs are spread across 3.8M+ paid returns, creating per-unit cost advantages. In assisted, scale helps with marketing and brand, but the labor-intensive model limits operating leverage.

### Buyback Economics -- The Real Story

The buyback program is the single most important driver of shareholder returns at HRB. Let me quantify:

| Period | Shares Repurchased | Avg Price | Total Spent | % of Shares Retired |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FY2019 | ~7.9M | $23.51 | $185M | ~3.8% |
| FY2023 | ~14.6M | ~$33 | $476M | ~8.5% |
| FY2024 | 8.0M | $43.66 | $350M | ~5.1% |
| FY2025 | 6.5M | $61.10 | $400M | ~4.7% |
| Q1 FY2026 | 7.9M | $50.90 | $400M | ~5.9% |

Source: HRB 10-K and 10-Q filings, share repurchase disclosures.

**The beautiful math of buybacks at $31:** At the current stock price, $400M annually retires ~12.9M shares, or ~10% of the ~127M outstanding. If HRB earns $600M in net income on 114M shares (post another year of buybacks), that is $5.26 EPS -- 19% higher than FY2025's $4.42 on *flat* net income. This is the mechanical power of buybacks at depressed prices. Management's $1.5B authorization ($700M remaining) represents roughly 55% of the current market cap.

The risk: if the stock recovers quickly, management buys fewer shares at higher prices and the compounding effect diminishes. The optimal scenario for long-term shareholders is that the stock stays low while management deploys maximum buyback capital -- exactly what appears to be happening.

## SECTION 8 -- RISK INVENTORY

**Tier 1 -- Material risks that could impair the dividend:**

1. **AI-driven commoditization of basic tax preparation.** If AI progresses to the point where it can reliably prepare 80%+ of tax returns with legal liability protection, HRB's assisted business (64% of revenue) faces structural decline. Timeline: 5-10 years. Probability: 15-20% in that timeframe.

2. **Government re-entry into tax preparation.** A future administration could revive IRS Direct File or go further with pre-populated returns. While currently terminated, the political risk of government-provided tax filing persists and would be catastrophic if implemented at scale. Probability: 10-15% in 4 years (contingent on election outcomes).

3. **Balance sheet stress during a disrupted tax season.** With $2.4B of long-term debt and peak-season-dependent cash generation, a scenario where tax filing season is severely disrupted (government shutdown, pandemic, IRS system failure) could force borrowing at unfavorable terms or constrain shareholder returns. The $1.5B CLOC provides a buffer, but not an unlimited one.

**Tier 2 -- Significant risks that could impair returns but likely not the dividend:**

4. **CEO transition execution risk.** Curtis Campbell is strategically sensible (digital-first DNA) but unproven at HRB's scale. His fintech ambitions may lead to capital misallocation or strategic drift from the core franchise.

5. **Wave goodwill impairment risk.** The FY2025 10-K identifies Wave's goodwill ($159M) as requiring a critical audit matter due to fair value sensitivity. If Wave underperforms, a write-down is possible.

6. **Legal and regulatory contingencies.** HRB faces class actions and mass arbitrations related to the IRS Free File Program and pixel tracking. An accrual exists, but outcomes are uncertain.

7. **Interest rate exposure on seasonal borrowing.** The CLOC is variable-rate. During the off-season (July-January), HRB borrows heavily. If rates remain elevated, off-season interest costs increase.

**Tier 3 -- Background risks:**

8. **Franchise relationship deterioration.** Franchise offices contribute royalties ($193M) and expand reach. If franchise quality degrades or franchisees exit, HRB loses distribution without offsetting revenue.

9. **Foreign currency risk.** Canada and Australia operations ($247M combined) are exposed to CAD/AUD movements. Not material to the consolidated P&L but can create noise.

10. **Data breach / cybersecurity incident.** Tax returns contain the most sensitive personal and financial information. A major breach would cause reputational damage and regulatory consequences.

At 10x trailing earnings ($44.20), HRB would be priced ~43% above current levels. At 12x ($53), it would be 71% above. I am not suggesting the stock immediately re-rates, but the compression from 15x to 7x without a commensurate decline in business fundamentals is historically anomalous.

The stock is cheap by any reasonable methodology. The question is whether the market's AI-fear discount is warranted over a 3-5 year horizon. I believe it is not, but I acknowledge the uncertainty and size the position accordingly.

## SECTION 10 -- VARIANT PERCEPTION

**What consensus believes:** HRB is a slowly dying business that will be disrupted by AI within 5-10 years. TurboTax already won the DIY war, and ChatGPT will win the assisted war. The stock deserves a value-trap multiple.

**Where I disagree:**

1. **Consensus underestimates the trust and liability moat.** Filing taxes is not like asking ChatGPT a question. It is a legal obligation with financial consequences for errors. The accuracy guarantee, audit representation, and preparer liability create a trust relationship that AI cannot replicate in its current form. This is more analogous to medical diagnosis than information retrieval -- people will use AI to *supplement* professional advice, not replace it entirely.

2. **Consensus ignores the buyback compounding at depressed prices.** At $31, every dollar of buyback capital is ~2x as effective as it was at $61 (FY2025 average). If management maintains $400M annual buybacks for 3 years at ~$35 average, they will retire ~34M shares, bringing the count to ~93M. On $600M net income, that is $6.45 EPS -- a 46% increase from FY2025 on flat earnings. The market is pricing terminal decline while management is engineering EPS growth through capital allocation.

3. **Consensus is pricing IRS Direct File as a continuing threat.** It has been terminated. This is a material change in the competitive landscape that reduces the bear case's probability significantly.

4. **The CEO transition is seen as risky, but Campbell's background is bullish.** He ran TaxAct (HRB's third competitor), held VP roles at Intuit (TurboTax), and comes from Capital One (fintech). He understands the competitive landscape from the inside. His stated ambition to make HRB a fintech company that also does taxes may be the right evolution of the franchise.

**Where consensus may be right:**

1. **AI disruption is a when, not an if, for simple returns.** The 40% of U.S. returns that are straightforward W-2 filings will eventually be highly automatable. HRB's DIY business will face pricing pressure from free AI alternatives.

2. **The physical office network is a wasting asset.** 8,714 offices is still a lot of real estate ($439M occupancy expense, 12% of revenue) in a world moving toward virtual delivery.

3. **The leveraged balance sheet limits strategic flexibility.** If a genuine competitive shock arrives, HRB has less room to invest than a net-cash competitor.

## SECTION 11 -- VERDICT

**Rating: BUY. Target: $42 (12-month). Position size: 2-3% of a dividend-focused portfolio.**

H&R Block at $31 is a classic "hated-but-healthy" deep-value dividend stock. The business posted record results in FY2025, is buying back stock at the most advantageous prices in years, raised the dividend 17%, and faces a competitive landscape that actually improved (IRS Direct File termination). The market is pricing in a future that has not arrived and may not arrive for a decade.

For a senior dividend investor, HRB offers:
- **5.4% current yield** with a demonstrated commitment to annual increases
- **12-15% total return potential** (5.4% yield + 6-10% EPS growth from buybacks + modest multiple expansion)
- **Downside protection** from a floor on the buyback program -- at $31, management is retiring shares at a pace that creates a natural bid for the stock

**Entry strategy:** Initiate a position at current levels ($30-32). Add aggressively on any dip below $28 (the 52-week low). Scale to full position size over 2-3 months to average into potential further weakness.

**Conditions that would change the thesis:**
- Dividend cut or freeze (immediate sell)
- Revival of IRS Direct File under a new administration (reduce to half position)
- Consistent revenue decline for 2+ consecutive fiscal years (reduce)
- AI competitor captures >5% of the assisted tax preparation market (reassess)
