## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: TRIM to starter position or EXIT outright · Dividend Safety: 6/10 · Confidence: Medium-High**

Kraft Heinz is the single most debated value-or-trap situation in dividend investing, and after reading eleven years of SEC filings end to end the verdict from this desk is unequivocal: **the current $1.60 indicated dividend is more defensible than the $2.50 cut in February 2019, but owning KHC at $22.95 is not a value investment — it is a wager that the September 2025 spin-off into two public companies unlocks value that the combined entity has been destroying continuously since the 2015 merger.** If that wager is wrong, investors get a 6.6% yield and a business that has cumulatively written off **$20.2 billion of goodwill** and a further **$21.3 billion of indefinite-lived intangibles** since inception (KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Note 8). That is not a premium staple; that is a slow-motion liquidation with a coupon.

The headline numbers look seductive. At $22.95 the stock trades at **11.3x consensus FY2026 EPS of $2.03** (FMP analyst aggregate, 10 EPS analysts), a **price-to-book of 0.69**, a **price-to-FCF of 7.8x**, and a **6.6% dividend yield** that is roughly 210 basis points above the 10-year KHC average. Free cash flow in FY2025 was **$3.66B** against dividends paid of **$1.90B** — a genuinely comfortable **1.93x FCF coverage** and a **52% FCF payout ratio**. Net debt/EBITDA on adjusted numbers is in the 3.0–3.3x range, credit ratings are **BBB/BBB/Baa2 (stable)** at S&P, Fitch, and Moody's, and the company has $3.68B of cash and short-term investments on the balance sheet (FMP FY2025). If this were a scoreboard reading, KHC would score a solid "hold" or even "buy".

But the scoreboard lies. Organic net sales fell **-3.1%** for 9M FY2025, with volume/mix down **-3.9 percentage points** offset by only **+0.8 points of pricing** (KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Item 2). That is the worst volume print for any mega-cap US packaged-food company this cycle — worse than GIS, CAG, CPB, K, or SJM — and it happened during a year when private label share is accelerating and tariff pressure on coffee and hydration inputs is compounding. The 9-month FY2025 GAAP operating loss was **$5.75 billion**, driven by a **$9.3B second-quarter interim impairment** ($6.7B goodwill on TMS/MC/CNAC/AFH/Western Europe + $2.6B intangible on Kraft, Velveeta, Lunchables, and Maxwell House). This is the second time the Kraft brand itself has been written down inside the last seven years. In 2018 Kraft Heinz wrote off **$15.9 billion** of goodwill and brand intangibles in a single year (KHC FY2018 10-K Item 1A), triggered a 36% dividend cut, and suffered a Warren Buffett mea culpa. **In 2025 the company ran the same play with smaller numbers but essentially the same conclusion: the brands are worth materially less than 3G Capital and Berkshire paid for them in 2015.**

The September 2, 2025 announcement of a tax-free spin-off into **"Global Taste Elevation Co."** (Heinz, Philadelphia, ABC, Master, condiments, sauces, infant nutrition, European operations) and **"North American Grocery Co."** (Kraft, Oscar Mayer, Velveeta, Lunchables, Kool-Aid, Jell-O, Maxwell House, Ore-Ida) is the only reason to own the stock at current prices (KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Note 2). This is a direct reversal of the entire 2015 merger thesis. The company that was assembled by 3G Capital and Berkshire Hathaway at a valuation of roughly $100 billion is now voluntarily pulling itself apart, having destroyed $73 billion of market capitalization along the way and impaired $41 billion of goodwill and intangibles. Management's explanation — that "the Separation is expected to allow each company to have greater strategic and operational focus to better serve customers, customize capital allocation, and accelerate profitable growth" — is boilerplate. The real reason is that the diversified KHC structure is not generating enough cash to cover the $20.9B senior notes stack across 2026–2050 maturities AND support capex AND sustain the dividend AND leave anything for shareholders. Splitting the company offloads leverage, narrows operational focus, and — critically for dividend holders — gives Berkshire Hathaway a clean exit path from either or both entities post-separation without depressing the entire share price.

**The central question for a dividend portfolio is this: is the current $1.60 dividend safer than the $2.50 dividend was in 2018?** My answer is yes, but only modestly so, and the safety is coming from different sources than fundamental business quality. In 2018, KHC paid out $3.18 billion in dividends against $1.75B of FCF — a **182% FCF payout ratio** that was unsustainable and was fixed by a 36% cut to $1.60 in Q1 2019. In 2025 the coverage ratio is 193% the other direction — $3.66B FCF / $1.90B dividends. The math says there is a 40% FCF cushion even if free cash flow halves. But reading the 10-Q carefully, that cushion exists only because management has been actively shrinking the company: $436M of buybacks in FY2025 (down from $988M in FY2024), no acquisitions, capex of only $801M (the lowest since the merger, vs $1.16B in FY2024), restructuring charges of $18M for the nine months that eliminated 580 positions. This is "dividend-first" capital allocation, not "growth-first" capital allocation. The dividend is safer because management has stopped investing in the business.

Post-separation in H2 2026 the dividend math resets entirely. The legacy $1.60 indicated dividend will almost certainly be allocated between the two spin-off entities based on their respective EBITDA contribution. **History from the FY2018–FY2019 cut cycle suggests the combined initial dividend post-separation will land between $1.40 and $1.60 — a 0% to 12% further cut from current.** The task of a dividend investor today is not to bet on the $1.60 holding; it is to bet on the economic value of the two separate companies at the spin date. On current fundamentals, the Taste Elevation business (Heinz ketchup, Philadelphia cream cheese, Asian sauces) is the clearly superior asset and deserves a premium multiple. The North American Grocery business is the problem child — mature, shrinking, volume-losing — and will trade at a deep discount.

**Top 3 reasons to own (at this price):**
1. **6.6% yield covered 1.93x by FCF** with cash cushion of $3.68B, balance-sheet deleveraging from $30.0B net debt at peak to $18.6B today (FY2018 vs FY2025, FMP panel)
2. **Optionality on the H2 2026 spin-off**. Taste Elevation Co has the better brand portfolio (Heinz ketchup is the #1 condiment brand globally, 75%+ share in UK/Netherlands/Germany) and should trade at an 18–22x multiple versus the 10x the combined entity gets today
3. **Berkshire 27.2% ownership** is a structural floor. Buffett has written down the position publicly but has not sold a single share since 2015. If KHC trades below $20 materially, a Berkshire increase or full take-private conversation becomes plausible at the $25 territory

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add:**
1. **Kraft Heinz has impaired $41.5 billion of goodwill and brand value since 2015** — $15.9B (2018), $3.6B (2024), $9.3B (Q2 2025), plus smaller amounts in 2022–2023. This is not cyclical. It is the public markets marking down the value of brands that management paid too much for in 2015. **The brands that remain at risk of further impairment** (per KHC FY2024 10-K Item 1A): Oscar Mayer, Lunchables, Claussen, Wattie's ($2.6B carrying), Kraft, Velveeta, A1, Bagel Bites ($14.2B carrying after 2025 writedowns). **Another $10–15B impairment is plausible** if organic volume declines continue.
2. **Organic volume is in persistent decline.** 9M FY2025 volume/mix -3.9%, FY2024 volume/mix -3.5%, FY2023 volume -5.5%. Three consecutive years of volume decline offset by pricing. **The pricing window has closed.** 9M FY2025 pricing contribution fell to +0.8% from +8.9% in FY2023. When you cannot raise price AND you cannot grow volume, organic growth goes structurally negative. Consensus FY2030 revenue is $24.96B — **essentially flat versus FY2016's $26.3B**, implying 14 years of no organic growth.
3. **The spin-off creates execution risk and may not fix the underlying business.** Separating a troubled business into two smaller companies does not improve the brands or the consumer preferences that are killing them. GLP-1 weight-loss drugs are a category headwind for snacks (Lunchables, Kraft Mac & Cheese) and for processed meats (Oscar Mayer). Private label condiments are taking share in North America even from Heinz. Splitting a problem in two does not solve the problem; it just gives the market two smaller things to short.

**Catalysts (next 12 months):**
- **Q1 FY2026 print (late April 2026)** — will confirm whether the volume bleed continues and whether FY2026 guidance implies a dividend hold. Consensus Q1 revenue $5.88B, EPS $0.50.
- **Form 10 filings for the two spin-cos** (expected Q2/Q3 2026) — will disclose for the first time the separate income statements, balance sheets, pro-forma leverage, and — critically — the dividend policy of each entity
- **H2 2026 spin distribution date** — shareholders will receive shares in both entities; the Berkshire stake will split between them
- **Ratings agency reviews** — any downgrade from BBB to BB would trigger forced selling from investment-grade-only funds and would almost certainly force a second dividend cut in the transition period
- **FY2025 10-K filing (early February 2026)** — will confirm the final FY2025 impairment charge and provide updated goodwill carrying amounts for each remaining reporting unit

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

### Corporate history

Kraft Heinz was formed on **July 2, 2015** through the merger of Kraft Foods Group, Inc. (the 2012 cereal-and-mac-and-cheese spin-off of the Kraft Foods / Mondelēz split) with H. J. Heinz Holding Corporation. Heinz itself had been taken private in **June 2013** by Berkshire Hathaway and 3G Capital in a $28 billion leveraged buyout led by 3G co-founder Jorge Paulo Lemann and endorsed by Warren Buffett (KHC FY2018 10-K Item 1). The 2015 merger created what was, at the time, **the fifth-largest food and beverage company in the world and the third-largest in North America**, with approximately $28 billion in annual revenue, 50,000 employees, and a portfolio spanning condiments, cheese, meats, coffee, beverages, and snacks.

The merger was the largest application of the **3G "zero-based budgeting"** operational model to a US CPG business. 3G's playbook at AB InBev, Burger King, and Heinz (2013–2015) had been to acquire mature, low-growth businesses, slash operating costs aggressively through zero-based budgeting reviews, and use the resulting margin expansion to fund dividends, debt service, and further acquisitions. Kraft Heinz was supposed to be the vehicle for rolling up Campbell Soup, Mondelēz, or Unilever — the 2017 attempted takeover of Unilever at $143 billion was publicly rejected by Paul Polman within 48 hours of disclosure and ended 3G's roll-up thesis.

### Current business (FY2024 10-K Description)

Kraft Heinz manufactures and markets food and beverage products across condiments and sauces, cheese and dairy, ready meals, refreshment beverages, coffee, processed meats, and other grocery categories. Operations are managed through **four operating segments** (as of FY2024 restructure): **North America, Europe & Pacific Developed Markets (EPDM), West & East Emerging Markets (WEEM), and Asia Emerging Markets (AEM)**. Reportable segments for SEC purposes are North America (65% of revenue), International Developed Markets (~15%), and Emerging Markets (~20%).

**Eight consumer-driven product platforms** (per KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Item 2):
1. **Taste Elevation** — condiments, sauces, dressings, spreads (Heinz, Philadelphia, ABC, Master, Classico) — **largest single platform at ~$11.3B in FY2025**
2. **Easy Ready Meals** — Kraft Mac & Cheese, frozen meals, Ore-Ida
3. **Substantial Snacking** — Lunchables, frozen snacks, pickles (Claussen)
4. **Hydration** — Kool-Aid, Crystal Light, Capri-Sun (license), Tang
5. **Meats** — Oscar Mayer, Lunchables hot dogs
6. **Cheeses** — Kraft Singles, Velveeta, Cracker Barrel, Philadelphia cream cheese
7. **Coffee** — Maxwell House, Gevalia, McCafé (licensed)
8. **Desserts** — Jell-O, Cool Whip, Kool-Aid puddings

Platforms are assigned roles: **Accelerate** (Taste Elevation, Easy Ready Meals, Substantial Snacking) — higher growth, higher margin, higher share; **Protect** (Desserts, Hydration) — moderate growth, higher margin; **Balance** (Cheese, Coffee, Meats) — commodity-heavy, flat growth, brand-footprint maintenance.

### Customer Concentration

**Walmart Inc. represented approximately 21% of KHC net sales in 2018** (KHC FY2018 10-K Item 1 — Sales and Customers), and the ratio has remained at approximately 20–22% since. The top 5 US customers account for roughly 49% of US segment net sales. In Canada, the top 5 customers account for 71% of Canada segment net sales — an extraordinary concentration risk given Loblaws' house-brand No Name and President's Choice compete directly with Kraft brands. This customer concentration gives private-label-friendly retailers substantial pricing leverage that has materially compressed gross margins.

### Ownership Structure

**Berkshire Hathaway owns approximately 27.2% of outstanding shares** as of the FY2024 10-K filing (KHC FY2024 10-K Item 1A). The stake originated from the 2013 Heinz LBO in which Berkshire provided $8B of preferred equity and $4B of common, converted through the 2015 merger. 3G Capital exited the position gradually through 2021–2023 per public disclosures. **Berkshire is now the only sponsor shareholder of significance** and has not sold a single share since the 2015 merger despite publicly acknowledging the investment was a mistake. In Berkshire's 2019 annual letter Buffett wrote that Kraft Heinz had "paid too much for Kraft" — an unusually direct admission.

## PART III · HISTORICAL REVIEW — 10 YEAR ARC (including the 2019 cut + impairment)

### Phase 1: Merger and the cost-cutting honeymoon (FY2015–FY2017)

The combined Kraft Heinz entity took its first full-year financial snapshot in FY2016: **net sales of $26.3B, gross margin 34.8%, operating margin 21.3%, diluted EPS $2.81, free cash flow $3.99B** (FMP FY2016). This was the fiscal-year number everyone bought into: a 21% operating margin on a mature packaged-food business was roughly 400 basis points above industry average, and management publicly committed to driving it higher through continuous zero-based budgeting reviews and supply-chain rationalization. The **FY2015 announced $1.5B synergy target was achieved ahead of schedule** by FY2017.

The problem was that margin expansion was coming entirely from **cutting, not growing**. Volume trends were already weak. Cereal, macaroni-and-cheese, processed meats, and processed cheese were all losing shelf to private label, natural/organic entrants (Annie's, Siggi's, Chobani), and better-funded branded competitors (Sargento, Lactalis, Ferrero). 3G's answer was to keep cutting: marketing spend, R&D, plant headcount, brand support, and — crucially — innovation investment. In Q4 2017 the company absorbed the **$10.9B net income boost from the TCJA tax reform deferred-tax revaluation** (FMP FY2017: GAAP net income $10.99B on just $527M of operating cash flow), which masked what was actually a negative cash-flow year for the underlying business (FY2017 GAAP OCF $527M; FCF **-$690M**).

### Phase 2: The $15.9 billion impairment and the February 2019 dividend cut (FY2018)

On **February 21, 2019**, along with its Q4 2018 earnings release, Kraft Heinz disclosed three things simultaneously that remain the worst single day in US packaged-food investor history:

1. **A goodwill impairment loss of $6.9 billion and an indefinite-lived intangible impairment loss of $8.6 billion in Q4 2018**, bringing the full-year 2018 impairment charge to **$15.9 billion** (KHC FY2018 10-K Item 1A). The brands impaired most aggressively: **Kraft, Oscar Mayer, Velveeta, Miracle Whip, Planters, A1, Cool Whip, Stove Top**, plus reporting units in US Grocery, US Refrigerated, Canada Retail, Latin America Exports, and Southeast Europe. After the writedown, reporting units with heightened future-impairment risk still held aggregate goodwill of **$29.0 billion**, of which **$9.3B had zero excess fair value over carrying amount**. Brands with heightened risk aggregated **$29.3 billion**, of which $24.0B had zero excess.

2. **The quarterly dividend was cut from $0.625 to $0.40 — a 36% reduction** (from $2.50 annual to $1.60 annual). Management framed the cut as enabling deleveraging: the company ended FY2018 with $31.2B of total debt and $30.0B net debt — 4.5x net debt to the impaired EBITDA base.

3. **SEC subpoena disclosure**. KHC disclosed it had received an SEC subpoena in October 2018 related to its procurement accounting policies, agreements, and internal controls (KHC FY2018 10-K Item 1A). Following the February 2019 earnings release and the impairment disclosure, the SEC expanded its investigation to include goodwill and intangible asset impairment assessments and management communications with shareholders. The company subsequently **restated its 2016 and 2017 financial statements** and filed the FY2018 10-K **six months late** — on June 7, 2019. Four securities class actions, an ERISA class action, and five stockholder derivative actions followed.

The stock dropped from $48.18 on February 21, 2019 to $34.95 in a single day — a **27% decline**. Warren Buffett gave a CNBC interview the following morning in which he admitted, "We overpaid for Kraft. We didn't overpay for Heinz." Berkshire Hathaway's Q4 2018 13-F showed an unrealized loss of approximately **$3.0 billion** on its KHC stake. Bernardo Hees — the 3G-aligned CEO who had succeeded Alex Behring — announced his departure in April 2019 and was replaced by **Miguel Patricio**, formerly the Chief Marketing Officer of AB InBev, in July 2019.

**Why did the impairment happen?** The FY2018 10-K (Item 1A, page discussing impairment) lists seven specific triggers management identified:
1. Sustained share-price decline in Nov–Dec 2018 pushing market cap below book value
2. Q4 2018 results below management expectations due to higher supply-chain costs and increased competition
3. The Dec 2018 approval of the 2019 operating plan, which showed "lower growth and margin expectations" for coming years
4. The announcement to sell natural cheese assets in Canada
5. Foreign currency fluctuations
6. December 2018 Fed rate hike
7. "Increased and prolonged economic and regulatory uncertainty"

The honest explanation, not in the 10-K, is that **3G's cost-cutting model finally met its limit**. You cannot cut marketing and R&D on brands in secular decline and expect share to hold. Kraft, Oscar Mayer, and Velveeta were losing shelf space because private label was better supported and natural-food alternatives were winning the mindshare war. The 2015 merger goodwill was priced on the assumption that 3G could replicate its AB InBev margin expansion playbook on brands that no consumer was demanding more of. That assumption was wrong, and the 2018 10-K writedown was the accounting recognition of the wrong assumption.

### Phase 3: Patricio's stabilization (FY2019–FY2023)

Miguel Patricio took over in July 2019 with a clearly different playbook: **divest non-core, invest in core brands, rebuild innovation, and deleverage the balance sheet**. The execution over five years was competent by the low standard of what he inherited.

**Divestitures:**
- **Planters nuts business sold to Hormel in February 2021 for $3.35B cash** (recognized in FY2021 cash flow as "acquisitionsNet: $4,940M" net of other transactions)
- **Natural cheese businesses (Breakstone's, Cracker Barrel licensing, parts of Canada)** sold to Lactalis in 2020 for $3.2B
- **Infant nutrition businesses** in Russia and Papua New Guinea divested in 2024 for nominal cash
- **Italy infant nutrition business** sold to NewPrinces in Q1 2026 for $140M (KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Note 5)
- **FY2025 $35M goodwill impairment** recognized on the Italy transaction

**Deleveraging:** Total debt declined from $31.2B (FY2018) to $21.2B (FY2025) — **a $10.0B reduction over seven years**. Net debt fell from $30.0B to $18.6B, a $11.4B reduction. This is genuine deleveraging and gets management significant credit.

**FCF and margins:**
- FY2019 FCF $2.78B (post-cut)
- FY2020 FCF $4.33B (pandemic stockpiling boost)
- FY2021 FCF $4.46B
- FY2022 FCF $1.55B (inventory-build cycle — very weak)
- FY2023 FCF $2.96B
- FY2024 FCF $3.16B
- FY2025 FCF $3.66B

**Adjusted operating margin** (excluding impairments) went from ~15% in 2018 to 20% in FY2023 back down to ~19% in FY2024. Patricio rebuilt brand investment modestly — advertising/marketing spend increased roughly 15% from FY2020 to FY2024 per management commentary — but the investment cadence was nowhere near what Unilever, Nestlé, or PepsiCo were doing on comparable brands.

The **dividend was held flat at $1.60 annual from FY2019 through FY2025** — no cuts, no raises. This is a dividend "freeze" by the S&P Dividend Aristocrats methodology and disqualifies KHC from the index for the next **25 years minimum** regardless of future raises.

### Phase 4: The Abrams-Rivera era and the Q2 2025 impairment (FY2024–current)

**Carlos Abrams-Rivera** was promoted from President of North America to CEO effective **January 1, 2024**. He is Cuban-born, ex-Mondelēz (Kraft legacy side) and ex-Campbell Soup, bringing the first operational CPG CEO to KHC since the merger. Miguel Patricio stepped into an **Executive Chairman** role.

The FY2024 financial results were the first clear warning that Patricio's "stabilization" had masked rather than fixed the underlying decline:

- **Net sales $25.85B, down -3.0% reported and -2.1% organic** (KHC FY2024 10-K Item 7)
- **Volume/mix -3.5%, pricing +1.4%** — the first year pricing could not offset volume decline
- **GAAP operating income collapsed to $1.68B** from $4.57B in FY2023 due to **$3.6B of impairments** ($1.6B goodwill + $2.0B intangibles on Oscar Mayer, Lunchables, Claussen, Wattie's brands and Continental Europe, LATAM, AFH reporting units — KHC FY2024 10-K Item 7)
- **Adjusted operating income +1.2% to $5.36B** — the non-GAAP metric preferred by management
- **$988M of buybacks at average $36.53/share** — again, a meaningful unrealized loss versus current $22.95

The Q2 2025 impairment was the breakpoint. On July 30, 2025, KHC reported Q2 2025 earnings and disclosed a **second interim impairment test** triggered by "the sustained decline in our share price and market capitalization" (KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Note 8). The test resulted in:

- **$6.7 billion goodwill impairment** split $3.1B TMS + $1.6B MC + $805M CNAC + $400M AFH + $819M Western Europe
- **$2.6 billion intangible impairment** on Kraft ($1.9B), Velveeta ($382M), Lunchables ($175M), Maxwell House ($100M), and two other brands ($42M)
- **Total Q2 2025 impairment: $9.3 billion**

Management attributed the impairment to "the market's perceived risk of our ability to achieve our future cash flow projections, due, in part, to uncertainty in the macroeconomic environment." The honest version: **the brands are worth less because the analyst community finally lowered long-term growth assumptions to reflect the pattern of continuous volume decline.**

Five weeks later, on **September 2, 2025**, the Board announced the separation.

### Phase 5: The Separation announcement and the new thesis (September 2025 to H2 2026 close)

The spin-off structure (KHC Q3 FY2025 10-Q Note 2):

**Global Taste Elevation Co.** — will contain Taste Elevation and shelf-stable meals platforms:
- Heinz (ketchup, mayo, BBQ, beans)
- Philadelphia cream cheese
- ABC (Indonesia)
- Master Foods (Asia)
- Classico
- Claussen
- Much of International Developed Markets and Emerging Markets operations

**North American Grocery Co.** — will contain certain North American staples:
- Kraft (Mac & Cheese, salad dressings, singles cheese)
- Oscar Mayer
- Velveeta
- Lunchables
- Kool-Aid, Crystal Light
- Jell-O, Cool Whip
- Maxwell House
- Ore-Ida
- Capri-Sun (license)

Management's stated rationale: "greater strategic and operational focus to better serve customers, customize capital allocation, and accelerate profitable growth."

**The economic rationale is different from the stated rationale.** Taste Elevation is a global premium-brand business with 25%+ EBITDA margins (Heinz ketchup alone generates roughly $1.5B EBITDA on ~$4.5B revenue, per sell-side estimates). North American Grocery is a mature, declining, margin-compressed business with ~15% EBITDA margins and structural volume headwinds. Combining them inside one corporate structure means:
1. Global Taste Elevation's multiple gets dragged down by North American Grocery
2. Leverage is shared between both businesses when only one actually has the cash flow to support it
3. Capital allocation decisions are compromised: investments in Asian sauce innovation compete for the same capex budget as defending Kraft Mac & Cheese volume

Separating them means Taste Elevation can trade at 14–18x EBITDA (in line with MKC McCormick at ~21x) while North American Grocery can trade at 6–8x EBITDA (in line with the worst of US canned goods — CAG Conagra at ~9x). If both businesses trade at their "fair" multiples post-separation, **sum-of-the-parts value is approximately $28–$34 per share versus today's $22.95** — implying 22–48% upside. But that math assumes:
- The Form 10 disclosures don't reveal nasty surprises about inter-company transfers
- Leverage is cleanly allocated (Taste Elevation should take <2.5x, NA Grocery <3.5x)
- The combined dividend is approximately maintained
- No hidden liabilities (pension, environmental, legal) torpedo either entity

**The execution risk is material and under-priced.** Spin-offs of mature CPG businesses have a mixed track record. Kellanova/WK Kellogg (2023) is trading above pre-split combined levels. The Mondelēz/Kraft split (2012) worked for Mondelēz and hurt Kraft. The Fortune Brands split (2011) was value-accretive. The Sara Lee split (2012) was disastrous for Hillshire Brands. Sample size is small and outcomes are bimodal. KHC investors are buying a 50/50 bet on which side of that distribution the split lands.

**But the 2018 comparison understates the risk** because it doesn't account for the volume trajectory. In 2018 KHC volume was approximately flat and pricing was taking the hit. In 2025 volume is down -3.9% and pricing is barely positive — the business is losing ground on both sides of the organic growth equation simultaneously. **If FCF continues to grow, the dividend is safe; if FCF begins declining 5–8% annually (a trajectory matching the revenue decline), the coverage ratio gets to 1.2x in roughly 3 years.** At 1.2x coverage the market starts pricing in a cut.

### The Spin-Off Dividend Risk

This is the risk the 2018 comparison doesn't capture. At the spin-off date in H2 2026:

1. **The combined KHC dividend of $1.60 will be allocated between two new entities.** There is no way to know the allocation until the Form 10 is filed (expected Q2-Q3 2026). Industry precedent in spin-offs like Kellogg/WK Kellogg (2023): the pre-spin dividend was maintained in aggregate but skewed toward the higher-quality entity. The sum of Kellanova + WK Kellogg dividends was approximately equal to the pre-split Kellogg dividend initially.
2. **The more likely pattern for KHC:** Taste Elevation Co might receive $1.00 to $1.20 of the initial dividend; NA Grocery Co might receive $0.40 to $0.60. Combined: $1.40 to $1.80, centered on $1.60 (maintained). Best case, no effective cut. Base case, 10% cut at the sum level.
3. **NA Grocery Co will face the most pressure.** A standalone entity with declining volumes in Kraft, Oscar Mayer, Velveeta, and Lunchables will likely want to allocate less cash to dividends and more to brand investment and deleveraging. Standalone ratings are uncertain — BB is plausible, forcing either a cut or forced selling from investment-grade funds.
4. **Taste Elevation Co will look better standalone** but will need to invest heavily in growth platforms (Asian sauces, premium condiments) to justify its premium multiple, which competes with dividend allocation.

## PART VI · BRAND PORTFOLIO HEALTH

This is the single most important section for understanding whether the KHC business is fixable. Every brand is not equal. Some brands are generating cash and deserve investment; others are in secular decline and should be harvested or divested. Kraft Heinz has not made this distinction cleanly, which is one reason the impairment cycle keeps repeating.

### Brand Investment vs Impairment Narrative

A common thread in the FY2018, FY2024, and Q2 2025 impairment disclosures: management cites "higher supply chain costs and increased competition" and "market's perceived risk of our ability to achieve future cash flow projections." What is conspicuously absent is **any reference to under-investment in brand marketing or innovation**. This is because acknowledging under-investment would be an admission that the cost-cutting playbook was too aggressive.

The reality, as public market data shows, is that **Kraft Heinz consistently spends 15-25% less on advertising and R&D per dollar of revenue than peers**. Mondelēz, Unilever, and PepsiCo all maintain advertising-to-revenue ratios above 8% for comparable brand categories; KHC runs at approximately 5-6% (derived from SG&A disclosures in 10-K filings). That 300-basis-point gap is roughly **$750 million per year of under-investment** versus what it would take to support the brands at peer levels. Compounded over ten years, that is $7.5B of "saved" marketing — which happens to be roughly the incremental impairment versus a no-impairment base case.

This is a real finding worth stating plainly: **Kraft Heinz has converted roughly $7-10 billion of advertising and R&D investment into dividends and interest payments over the last decade, at the cost of impairing approximately $41 billion of brand goodwill and intangibles.** The savings are real cash. The impairments are non-cash. From a pure cash-flow perspective the trade may have been rational. From a brand-value perspective it has been catastrophic.

### Private Label Threat

The FY2024 10-K (Item 1A) identifies "the growing presence of e-commerce retailers, large-format retailers, and discounters" as a competitive risk and notes "competition from private label products sold by retailers, wholesalers, and cooperatives." Private label share in US packaged food rose from approximately 17% in 2020 to 21% in 2024 (Circana/IRI industry data, not KHC-disclosed). In categories where KHC is dominant:

- **Ketchup**: Private label has grown to ~15% share of category; Heinz still dominates but with shrinking share
- **Mac & cheese**: Private label (Great Value) is now ~25% share; Kraft brand is declining
- **Salad dressings**: Ken's Foods and private label increasingly taking share from Kraft
- **Cream cheese**: Philadelphia holds ~60% share but private label and Boursin are eroding
- **Lunchables category**: Store-brand lunch kits at Target, Walmart, and Kroger now priced 25-30% below Lunchables

The private label threat is more severe in "Tier 3" than in "Tier 1" brands. This is the core of the case for the spin-off: separating the high-quality, private-label-resistant Tier 1 brands from the vulnerable Tier 2/3 brands creates value through multiple expansion on the former.

### GLP-1 Weight-Loss Drug Impact

Novo Nordisk's Ozempic and Wegovy, Eli Lilly's Mounjaro and Zepbound, and the broader GLP-1 class are now used by an estimated **15-20 million Americans** as of early 2026 (Morgan Stanley/JPMorgan industry estimates, not KHC-disclosed). Clinical data from the SELECT trial, STEP trial, and real-world retail purchase studies consistently show GLP-1 users reduce food purchases by 9-15% in aggregate, with the biggest reductions in:

1. **Snack foods (Lunchables, frozen snacks)** — 15-20% reduction
2. **Sweet desserts (Jell-O, Kool-Aid, ice cream)** — 18-22% reduction
3. **Processed meats (Oscar Mayer bacon, hot dogs)** — 12-15% reduction
4. **Carbonated soft drinks** — 20-30% reduction

Kraft Heinz has meaningful exposure to three of these four categories. **Lunchables, Oscar Mayer, and Jell-O combined represent approximately 15% of KHC US revenue** — implying a 2-3 percentage point revenue headwind if GLP-1 penetration reaches 25-30% of US adults by 2028. Management has not quantified this risk in any 10-K filing.

The FY2024 10-K Item 1A risk factors added a generic reference to "consumer preferences changing in favor of less processed, healthier alternatives" but does not mention GLP-1 drugs by name or provide quantification. **This is a material under-disclosure** in a pattern consistent with KHC historical tendency to under-disclose risks until they become impairment charges.

### Moat Assessment: narrow and narrowing in most categories, wide and stable only in condiments

A credible analysis of Kraft Heinz's moat must decompose it by brand category because the answer is genuinely different across the portfolio.

**Wide moat brands (Tier 1, ~30% of revenue):**

- **Philadelphia cream cheese**: Global #1 cream cheese brand with ~60% share in most developed markets. Cream cheese is a culinary category with rising global penetration (bagels, cheesecake, Asian desserts). Brand moat is real.

### Why is KHC so cheap?

**Reason 1: The impairment cycle has destroyed faith in the reported book value.** Every 3 years since 2018 there has been a new multi-billion-dollar impairment. The market does not trust the FY2025 $22.2B goodwill carrying amount or the $37.5B intangibles carrying amount — an implicit assumption is that another $5-10B will be impaired over the next 3 years. **At a P/B of 0.69, the market is implicitly writing down book value by 31% already**.

**Reason 2: Organic growth is in persistent structural decline.** Every other packaged-food peer has at least demonstrated they can grow revenue with pricing. KHC has demonstrated it can grow revenue with pricing only when inflation is high (FY2022-FY2023 pricing was +7-9%). In low-inflation environments (FY2024-FY2025 pricing barely positive), the structural volume decline overwhelms. Analyst FY2030 consensus revenue is $24.96B — **flat versus FY2016's $26.3B**, implying 14 years of no growth.

**Reason 3: The spin-off creates uncertainty over dividend allocation, leverage allocation, and capital structure.** Until Form 10 is filed, investors cannot evaluate the two resulting companies individually. This creates a holding discount of approximately 15-20% versus the sum-of-the-parts fair value.

**Reason 4: GLP-1 and private label are real category risks.** Staple food stocks have universally de-rated since 2023 as investors price in the impact of GLP-1 drugs on snack/soda/meat categories. KHC has more exposure than most (Lunchables, Oscar Mayer, Jell-O, Kool-Aid).

**Reason 5: Berkshire overhang.** The 27.2% Berkshire stake is simultaneously a floor (unlikely to be sold rapidly) and a ceiling (a public Berkshire sale would crater the price). Investors price in this asymmetry.

7. **The dividend is not going to be cut**: Management has stated commitment to investment-grade rating and dividend support. Free cash flow is strong. The 2018 cut happened because leverage was unsustainable; current leverage is fine.
8. **Valuation is mathematically compelling**: P/FCF of 7.8x is the cheapest in mega-cap staples. At 10x (still below peer average) the stock is $37. At 12x it is $44. The asymmetry is overwhelming.

**Where this bull case might be right**: if the Heinz ketchup franchise proves durable enough on its own to anchor a 14-16x multiple on Taste Elevation Co, and if NA Grocery Co stabilizes at even 7-8x EBITDA, the sum-of-parts arithmetic works and the 3-year total return could reach 35-45%. This is an achievable outcome under my "bull case" scenario (15% probability).

**Where this bull case is most wrong**: It assumes the Heinz franchise is as durable as Coca-Cola trademark or Hershey cocoa trademark, which it is not. Heinz ketchup has structural growth of perhaps 1-2% globally (vs KO at 3-4%) and faces real private-label competition in every developed market except UK/Ireland. The premium multiple (18x+) bull case implies Heinz growth catches up to McCormick (2-3% organic plus pricing) which has not happened in 10 years.

### A10. Final note on the 2018 cut precedent

The single most important question I've been asked is whether this dividend is safer than the 2018 version. I've quantified this with the coverage tables above. But the qualitative answer matters too:

**In 2018, the cut was mandatory.** The combination of impairment charges, negative working capital, balance sheet leverage, and an SEC investigation forced the board's hand. The dividend was cut in the same 8-K as the earnings release and the impairment announcement — there was no orderly review, no investor preparation, no analyst day. It was disclosed with the worst possible market timing.

**In 2025-2026, a dividend adjustment is optional but strategically appealing.** The balance sheet is fine. The FCF is covering. There is no forcing function. However, the spin-off creates a natural "reset" moment where the board can allocate dividends between the two new entities in a way that makes each entity's standalone leverage math work. **This makes a modest dividend reset (10-15%) very likely but prevents an emergency cut (>25%) absent a disaster.**

The specific scenario I find most plausible: the pre-spin dividend is held at $1.60 through H1 2026. At the spin distribution, shareholders receive Taste Elevation Co and NA Grocery Co shares. Taste Elevation Co declares an initial dividend of $1.00-$1.10 per share. NA Grocery Co declares an initial dividend of $0.40-$0.50 per share. **Aggregate initial dividend: $1.40-$1.60, centered at $1.50** — approximately a 6% effective cut from today's $1.60.

This is a much softer landing than the 2019 experience. But it is still a reset.
