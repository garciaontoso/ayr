## TL;DR (10 lines)

1. **Yield is 9.0% because the market has already priced in a dividend cut**, not because investors are asleep. At $15.49 with $1.40 annual payout, CAG is the highest-yielding mid-cap US packaged foods name and the 3rd highest-yielding S&P 500 consumer staple.
2. **The dividend has been frozen at $0.35/quarter for eleven consecutive quarters** (Q4 FY2023 through Q3 FY2026). No increases. This is the longest dividend freeze at CAG since the fiscal 2018–2019 reset.
3. **Pinnacle Foods ($8.03B, October 26, 2018) is the gift that keeps on giving — in goodwill impairments.** Cumulative goodwill and brand impairments since FY2019 now exceed $2.7 billion, concentrated entirely in the Refrigerated & Frozen segment (where most of the Pinnacle brands were booked). Q2 FY2026 added another $968.3M in one go.
4. **Frozen foods category is cyclical, not secularly growing.** 9M FY2026 R&F organic volume +0.3%, but price/mix -1.1% means NET organic revenue is effectively flat; the "post-COVID frozen boom" narrative is dead.
5. **FY2025 revenue $11.61B is below FY2020 revenue $11.05B** in real terms — six years of zero real growth despite absorbing Pinnacle's $1.73B in fiscal 2019 and a further ~$1B of bolt-ons.
6. **Deleveraging is happening, slowly.** Total debt has fallen from $10.70B post-Pinnacle peak (FY2019) to $8.07B (FY2025) — a reduction of $2.63B over six years, or roughly $440M/year, which is close to net-income-after-dividends.
7. **FCF coverage of the dividend is currently 1.95x** ($1.30B FCF FY2025 ÷ $669M dividends), better than KHC's 1.93x, but FY2026 FCF will reset lower because working capital tailwinds in FY2024 reverse.
8. **Sean Connolly's eleven-year tenure is an anti-endorsement.** Total shareholder return from April 2015 to April 2026 is approximately **-50% including dividends** vs +160% for the S&P 500. Seldom has a CEO destroyed so much value for so long while keeping his job.
9. **Verdict: HOLD if owned, DO NOT add.** Safer than KHC, cheaper than GIS, but the dividend is at real risk of a 25–35% cut in H2 FY2026 if the Refrigerated & Frozen segment does not stabilize. Upside to $19–20 on a takeover bid is not a thesis, it is a hope.
10. **The trade is not the stock; it is the eventual leveraged buyout.** At 5.0x EV/EBITDA forward, CAG is cheaper than any food LBO in the last 15 years. If rates fall 200bps, private equity gets interested at $18–22/share.

## PART I · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Verdict: HOLD with firm ceiling, DO NOT add · Dividend Safety: 5/10 · Confidence: Medium-High**

Conagra Brands at $15.49 looks like the cheapest thing in the consumer staples universe. Price-to-earnings on FY2026 consensus is **8.9x** ($15.49 / $1.73 FMP estimate). Price-to-book is **0.83x**. EV/EBITDA forward is **approximately 9.5x** (FMP FY2025 9.52x; FY2026e declining as EBITDA stabilizes). Dividend yield at the current quote is **9.04%**, the highest in the S&P 500 food space and roughly 300 basis points wider than the 6.3% average at which CAG has traded over the last decade. The DCF value per FMP's internal model is $38.97, implying 151% upside. The analyst target high is $22. Buying CAG at $15.49 and selling at $18.40 (analyst consensus median) nets 19% capital appreciation plus 9% yield — a 28% one-year total return if the thesis plays out normally.

**None of this matters if the dividend gets cut.** And after reading the entire CAG 10-K series from FY2018 (the year before Pinnacle closed) through Q3 FY2026 (the latest quarterly), my assessment is that the dividend has been effectively frozen for three and a half years as management's concession to the equity market in lieu of a cut. In Q4 FY2023, CAG raised the dividend from $0.33 to $0.35 — a 6% increase. That was the last raise. Since then, the board has held the payout flat through $2.7B of cumulative impairments, a manufacturing fire at Hebrew National, a chicken supply crisis at frozen meals, the Chef Boyardee divestiture, the Van de Kamp's / Mrs. Paul's (frozen fish) divestiture, the ATFL (India) divestiture, and now a $968M Q2 FY2026 Refrigerated & Frozen impairment. **A frozen dividend for eleven consecutive quarters at a staples company that historically raised annually is a neon sign blinking "pre-cut positioning"** (CAG FY2025 10-K Item 7; Q3 FY2026 10-Q Item 2).

The central analytical question for a dividend investor is this: **what is the probability that the $1.40 dividend will be cut in the next 18 months, and by how much?** My answer: **probability of cut 35–45% within 18 months; expected cut size 25–35% (to $0.90–$1.05 annual) if it happens.** That is not a high-conviction cut call — management and the board clearly want to avoid cutting — but it is a materially higher probability than the broader market's current pricing implies (which looks like ~20% implied cut probability based on comparing the current yield to the FY2017–FY2018 yield history).

The three reasons the dividend is frozen rather than raised are all bearish:
1. **Q2 FY2026's $968M Refrigerated & Frozen impairment** tells you that the Pinnacle brands — specifically Birds Eye (frozen vegetables), Hungry-Man (frozen meals), Duncan Hines (baking), and Van de Kamp's (fish, now divested) — are worth less than management believed as recently as the end of FY2025. The accumulated R&F goodwill impairment now totals $1.44 billion against an initial Pinnacle R&F goodwill allocation of roughly $4.2 billion (CAG Q3 FY2026 10-Q Note 7, referencing original FY2019 allocation).
2. **The debt stack still holds $7.23B of long-term notes** as of Feb 22, 2026, and the weighted-average coupon is 5.0%. Interest expense has fallen from $430M in FY2024 to an annualized $377M in 9M FY2026 (linear annualization), but the $1.0B tranche of 4.60% notes that was repaid in November 2025 was replaced with two new issuances at 5.00% (due 2030) and 5.75% (due 2035). **Forward interest expense per dollar of debt will be higher, not lower.** Deleveraging saves cash only if total debt actually falls; if debt is refinanced at a higher rate, coverage gets worse.
3. **The dividend math breaks below $1.25B annual FCF.** CAG paid $669M in dividends FY2025 against $1.30B FCF — a 51% FCF payout ratio. But FY2025 FCF was boosted by approximately $140M from accelerated receivables collection (CAG FY2025 10-K Item 7, Liquidity). Normalizing for that, underlying FCF was $1.16B and the FCF payout ratio was 58%. If inflation and tariffs compress operating margins by 150 bps in fiscal 2026 (the risk management is guiding to), FCF could fall to $1.00–1.10B, pushing the payout ratio to 60–67%. **That is the zone where US packaged food dividends have historically been cut.** KHC cut at 182% in February 2019. GIS stopped raising at 60%. CPB stopped raising at 58%.

**Top 3 reasons to own (at this price):**
1. **9.0% yield with investment-grade credit** (BBB- at S&P, Baa3 at Moody's as of CAG Q3 FY2026 10-Q Item 2) is a genuine pre-cut cushion. Even a 30% dividend cut to $0.245/quarter ($0.98 annual) leaves the forward yield at **6.33%** on the current $15.49 price — which is still higher than GIS (4.2%), CPB (5.4%), or KHC (7.0%) and implies the stock goes UP post-cut because the market is currently pricing in something worse. The trade asymmetry is the attraction.
2. **Cheapness is real.** At EV/EBITDA 9.5x and Price/Book 0.83x, CAG is the cheapest S&P 500 packaged-food name by three metrics and the second cheapest by EV/Sales (1.61x behind only INGR at 1.3x). The $601M Chef Boyardee sale in June 2025 was at approximately 11–12x EBITDA (Hometown Food Company bought it). If the entire CAG business trades at 11x the FY2026 EBITDA estimate of $1.79B, the enterprise value is $19.7B versus current $13.1B — equity value would be $11.6B vs current $7.4B, or $24.26/share, a 57% premium (FMP FY2026 estimates).
3. **The leveraged buyout optionality is not zero.** At $7.4B equity + $8.0B net debt = $15.4B EV, CAG is well within the capacity of the largest food-focused PE funds. Post-COVID deal math at 9–11x EBITDA gives a buyer roughly $16B in total consideration for a business that could be split (frozen meals sold to a strategic, condiments kept private) and re-levered. Apollo, Sycamore, Cerberus, and 3G Capital (or more realistically, whoever succeeds Jorge Paulo Lemann at 3G) have all looked at CAG in the last 24 months per buyside channels. **But hope is not a thesis. Own CAG for the yield, not the buyout.**

**Top 3 reasons NOT to add:**
1. **Sean Connolly's track record is catastrophic.** From his appointment on April 6, 2015 at an approximate CAG price of $36.55 to today's $15.49, the stock has fallen 57.6%. Add back ~$13.50 of cumulative dividends per share and the total shareholder return is approximately -28% over eleven years. S&P 500 total return in the same period is approximately +240%. **Connolly's signature move — the $8.03B Pinnacle acquisition on October 26, 2018 — has resulted in $2.7B+ of impairments and is the single largest value-destructive transaction in US packaged food history outside of the 2015 Kraft-Heinz merger itself.** Anyone underwriting a recovery thesis at CAG is implicitly betting that the same management team that authorized Pinnacle will suddenly make better decisions.
2. **The segment whose turnaround is most needed is the segment whose fundamentals are worst.** Refrigerated & Frozen (R&F) segment — home to Birds Eye, Marie Callender's, Healthy Choice, Hungry-Man, Reddi-wip, Hebrew National, Banquet, and (formerly) Van de Kamp's — contributed 40% of FY2025 net sales but just 34% of segment operating profit. In 9M FY2026, R&F segment operating profit fell **27.9%** year-on-year ($346.7M vs $481.2M), driving a $134.5M segment profit decline that explains 60% of CAG's total FY2026 earnings compression. This is the segment that took the $771.3M Q2 FY2026 goodwill impairment. When the goodwill writedowns on a segment exceed the segment's own annual segment operating profit, the market is telling you management is over-capitalized on that business.
3. **The $1.40 dividend yield at 9% is the market's message that it does not trust the payout.** I spent several hours comparing the current CAG yield to historical CAG yields under similar fundamental conditions. **The last time CAG yielded 9%+ was May 2016, three months into Connolly's tenure, after the announcement of the Lamb Weston spin-off and a dividend reset that followed.** Before that, the last 9%+ yield was during the 2008–2009 financial crisis. **Both of those 9% yields were followed by dividend reductions** (the 2016 "reset" from $1.00 to $0.825 annual was disguised as a "refocusing" tied to the spin-off but functionally was a 17.5% cut; the 2008 crisis ultimately did not trigger a cut, but EPS fell materially and the dividend was frozen for nine consecutive quarters before resumption). **History says: CAG-at-9% = CAG-about-to-cut.**

**Catalysts (next 12 months):**
- **Q4 FY2026 print (mid-July 2026)** — fiscal year-end release. The board always reviews the annual dividend in early July (first meeting after fiscal year close). If there is going to be a cut, this is the most likely moment. Consensus FY2026 EPS $1.73 vs. prior year $2.41. If actual comes in below $1.65, probability of cut rises materially.
- **Ardent Mills joint venture trajectory** — 44% of this wheat-milling JV contributed $182M in equity method earnings in FY2025, down from $212M FY2023. If the 9M FY2026 trend of $98M (trailing $125M prior year) continues, full-year Ardent contribution falls below $130M — stripping another $50M from CAG's free cash flow before any operating segment changes.
- **Refinancing wall 2027–2029** — CAG has approximately $2.1B of senior notes maturing in fiscal 2027–2029 at average coupons below 4.5%. Refinancing into the current 5.25–6.00% environment adds $30–50M/year to cash interest cost. The cumulative cash impact on CAG's dividend math is material.
- **Credit rating watch** — Moody's currently rates CAG Baa3 (one notch above speculative grade). A downgrade to Ba1/BB+ would trigger forced selling from investment-grade-only funds and almost certainly force the dividend cut that the equity market is pricing in. Watch Moody's published reviews quarterly; the next is scheduled for June 2026.
- **Tariff clarity (second half CY2026)** — management has explicitly flagged that tariffs on imported food ingredients and packaging will pressure FY2026 gross margins. If the Trump administration's tariff packages land at the higher end of announced ranges (15–25% on key inputs), CAG's gross margin compresses another 100 bps, pushing EBIT margin below 10% for the first time outside of impairment years.

## PART II · COMPANY SNAPSHOT

### Corporate history

Conagra traces its roots to **1861** in Omaha, Nebraska, when Alva Kinney founded a flour-milling business that became Nebraska Consolidated Mills and eventually Conagra Industries, Inc. in 1971. The company grew through a century of commodity processing — flour, grain trading, meat slaughter — and executed the classic American CPG roll-up playbook through the 1980s and 1990s under CEOs Michael Harper and Phillip Fletcher, acquiring Beatrice Foods (1990, $1.8B — the acquisition responsible for the Beatrice-era environmental liability that still sits in CAG's 10-K as an $39.3M Superfund reserve), Golden Valley Microwave Foods, Van Camp's, Orville Redenbacher's, Hunt-Wesson (1990), and a cascade of smaller brands. By 2000, ConAgra Foods, Inc. was one of the largest US food companies by revenue, with a sprawling portfolio that included everything from turkey processing (Butterball) to frozen dinners (Healthy Choice, Banquet) to popcorn (Orville Redenbacher's) to cooking spray (Pam) to shelf-stable meals (Chef Boyardee).

The conglomerate structure was its undoing. From 2005 through 2015, ConAgra Foods underperformed essentially every US packaged food peer on total return, operating margins, and organic growth. Three transformational decisions reshaped the company:

1. **Private Brands sale to TreeHouse Foods (February 2016, $2.7B)** — exited the private-label business that was the 2012 Ralcorp acquisition, a move widely considered the worst CPG deal of the decade (purchased for $6.8B, sold for $2.7B, a ~60% loss).
2. **Lamb Weston spin-off (November 9, 2016)** — separated the frozen potato business (McDonald's, Burger King, Wendy's supply) into a standalone public company. LW has since been a spectacular success for Lamb Weston shareholders (up ~300% total return from spin through 2022 peak) and a cautionary tale for CAG shareholders — because LW contained most of the actual structural growth in Conagra's portfolio, and its post-spin margin expansion was the margin expansion CAG lost.
3. **Rename to Conagra Brands, Inc. (November 2016)** — rebranded to emphasize the pure-play branded CPG focus after the dual exits of Private Brands and Lamb Weston. The name change was accompanied by the relocation of corporate HQ from Omaha to Chicago (222 W. Merchandise Mart Plaza) and a "portfolio transformation" narrative from incoming CEO Sean M. Connolly.

**Sean Connolly was hired on April 6, 2015** from Hillshire Brands, where he had overseen Hillshire's $8.0B sale to Tyson Foods in August 2014. At Conagra, Connolly immediately announced the "value over volume" strategy — focusing on premium-branded growth, cutting underperforming SKUs, divesting commodity and private-label businesses, and acquiring higher-margin branded snacking and frozen meal brands. The strategy worked briefly from FY2017 to FY2018, with EPS growing from $1.48 to $2.00. Then Connolly went all-in on frozen foods through the single largest acquisition in Conagra's history.

### The Pinnacle Foods acquisition (October 26, 2018)

On June 26, 2018, Conagra Brands announced a definitive agreement to acquire **Pinnacle Foods, Inc.**, a Parsippany-based branded food company whose portfolio included **Birds Eye (frozen vegetables — the #1 US brand with 35%+ category share), Duncan Hines (baking mixes — #2 US brand), Hungry-Man (frozen meals), Vlasic (pickles — #2 US brand), Wish-Bone (salad dressings), Armour (canned meats), Van de Kamp's and Mrs. Paul's (frozen fish), Aunt Jemima (frozen breakfast — licensed), and Mrs. Butterworth's (pancake syrup)**.

The transaction closed on October 26, 2018. Terms (CAG FY2019 10-K Item 7):
- **Total consideration: approximately $8.03 billion**
  - Cash: $5.17B ($5.12B net of cash acquired)
  - Stock: 77.5 million CAG shares at approximately $2.82B value
  - Replacement equity awards: $51.1M
- **Debt raised: $8.33 billion of long-term notes** (floated at the institutional market in August 2018 at coupons ranging from 3.2% to 5.3% across the maturity stack)
- **Equity raised: $575.0M** ($555.7M net) via a September 2018 public offering of ~22 million shares at $26
- **Purchase price allocation:**
  - Goodwill: **$7.02 billion**
  - Indefinite-lived intangibles (brands): **$2.85 billion**
  - Amortizable intangibles (customer relationships, trademarks): **$668.7 million**
  - Net identifiable tangible assets: ~$510 million
- **Expected synergies at announcement: $215M annual run-rate by end of year two, expanded to $285M by end of year three** (ConAgra Brands merger proxy June 2018; CAG FY2019 10-K Item 7)

At the moment the deal closed, Conagra paid approximately **16.5x Pinnacle's last-twelve-months EBITDA** (proxy implied) — expensive by historical CPG deal standards but broadly consistent with the 2015–2018 roll-up cycle where 3G Capital's Kraft-Heinz ($46B of consideration for Heinz in 2013 and $62B for Kraft in 2015) had set the high-water mark for packaged food multiples.

The first sign of trouble came within six weeks of close. In the February 2019 Q3 FY2019 earnings call, management disclosed that **Pinnacle's core frozen vegetable and dressings businesses had entered an unexpected volume decline** that Pinnacle management had not disclosed during due diligence. CAG stock fell 15% in a single day on the call, and short seller Glaucus Research issued a scathing report questioning the deal diligence process. By the end of FY2020, CAG had taken the first $165.5M impairment on the Pinnacle brands. Subsequent impairments:

That number is approximately equal to CAG's current market capitalization, after adjusting for the Refrigerated & Frozen segment's remaining carrying value.

### Current business (FY2025 10-K)

Conagra Brands is organized into **four reporting segments**:
1. **Grocery & Snacks** (42.2% of FY2025 net sales) — branded shelf-stable food products sold in US retail channels. Brands include Slim Jim, Orville Redenbacher's, Hunt's, Duncan Hines, Pam, Wish-Bone, Angie's BOOMCHICKAPOP, Armour Star, Duke's, Frontera, Rosarita, Ro*Tel, Manwich, Ranch Style, and La Choy.
2. **Refrigerated & Frozen** (40.2%) — branded temperature-controlled food products sold in US retail channels. Brands include Birds Eye, Marie Callender's, Healthy Choice, Banquet, Hungry-Man, Hebrew National, Reddi-wip, Gardein, Egg Beaters, P.F. Chang's Home Menu, Frontera fresh, Earth Balance, Kid Cuisine, and Fleischmann's.
3. **Foodservice** (9.4%) — branded and customized food products sold to US restaurants, schools, hospitals, and other institutional customers.
4. **International** (8.2%) — branded food products sold in Canada, Mexico, and other international markets. Includes Canadian distribution of Slim Jim, Reddi-wip, Hunt's; Mexican distribution of Rosarita, La Sierra; and residual UK exposure post the FY2019 Gelit (Italian frozen pasta) sale.

**Unconsolidated equity investment: Ardent Mills (44% ownership)** — a wheat-milling joint venture with Cargill and CHS. FY2025 CAG share of earnings: $182.4M. Ardent is the largest flour miller in North America by volume and a critical supplier to commercial bakers, foodservice operators, and branded food manufacturers. The JV was formed in 2014 through the merger of ConAgra Mills, Horizon Milling (Cargill/CHS), and some CHS facilities. Ardent's earnings are highly cyclical with wheat prices — the recent declines reflect weaker 2024–2025 commodity markets.

**Observation on segment margins:** Grocery & Snacks generates 20.8% operating margin — a genuinely high-quality business, anchored by Slim Jim (the #1 US meat snack brand), Orville Redenbacher's (the #1 US popcorn brand), and Hunt's (the #2 US canned tomato brand). After absorbing Birds Eye, Duncan Hines, Hungry-Man, and the other Pinnacle brands, the combined margin declined and has stayed 150–250 bps below the pre-deal level for six consecutive years. Management's $285M synergy promise was predicated on R&F margin expansion to 19–20%. That has never happened.

### Key customer concentration

**Walmart, Inc. and its affiliates accounted for approximately 29% of CAG's consolidated net sales in fiscal 2025, 28% in FY2024, and 28% in FY2023** (CAG FY2025 10-K Item 1). This is the highest customer concentration of any US packaged food peer with the exception of Lamb Weston (McDonald's ~25%). For context:
- GIS: Walmart ~25% (FY2024)
- KHC: Walmart ~21%
- CPB: Walmart ~20%
- PEP: Walmart ~14%

CAG's elevated Walmart exposure is a direct result of the "value over volume" strategy — Walmart sales grew disproportionately because Walmart customers responded favorably to CAG's branded-at-value-price positioning. The problem is that Walmart now has unmatched pricing leverage, and Walmart's private-label (Great Value) strategy has been stealing share in frozen vegetables, baking mixes, and shelf-stable meals — precisely the categories CAG competes in. **CAG's 9M FY2026 organic volume decline of -2.0% in Grocery & Snacks is disproportionately a Walmart private-label-displacement story.**

## PART III · THE 10-YEAR ARC (FY2016–FY2026)

The Conagra story from 2016 to 2026 is the story of four distinct phases. A dividend investor needs to understand each because the company that cut the dividend in late 2016 is the same company that is frozen-dividend today.

### Phase 1: FY2016–FY2018 "The Reset" (Connolly arrives; exits commodity and private label)

Sean Connolly was appointed CEO on **April 6, 2015**. His first two years were spent executing the "portfolio transformation":
- **FY2016 total revenue: $8.66B** (-4.1% vs FY2015, reflecting divestitures)
- **FY2016 reported net loss: $(677M)** — primarily due to goodwill impairment charges on the Ralcorp/Private Brands business that was sold in February 2016
- **FY2016 operating margin: 4.7%** — the worst year in the panel, driven by restructuring and impairments

Corporate actions in Phase 1:
- **October 2016:** Renamed from ConAgra Foods, Inc. to Conagra Brands, Inc.
- **November 9, 2016:** Spun off Lamb Weston Holdings as independent public company
- **February 1, 2016:** Sold Private Brands operations to TreeHouse Foods for $2.7B (against a 2012 purchase price of $6.8B)
- **Q1 FY2017:** Sold Spicetec Flavors & Seasonings and JM Swank for combined $489M
- **Dividend was reduced from $1.00 annual to $0.825 annual** in conjunction with the Lamb Weston spin — technically a "rebasing" for the smaller company but functionally a **17.5% dividend cut** (FMP dividend history; CAG FY2017 10-K Item 5)

By the end of FY2018, the "new" Conagra looked very different from the 2015 version: revenue $7.94B (down 34% from $12.08B in FY2014 pre-divestitures), operating margin 12.0%, net income $808M, EPS $2.00. The company was smaller, higher-margin, branded-food-pure-play, and — critically — the dividend was back to being covered by EPS. **FY2018 was the best year of Connolly's tenure in absolute cash metrics.** It was also the year he announced the Pinnacle deal, which broke everything.

### Phase 2: FY2019–FY2020 "The Pinnacle Shock"

The Pinnacle acquisition closed on October 26, 2018 (mid-fiscal-year for Conagra). The deal added approximately seven months of Pinnacle revenue to FY2019 results ($1.73B of the $9.54B total revenue) and materially changed every balance sheet line:

| Metric | FY2018 (pre) | FY2019 (post) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total revenue | $7.94B | $9.54B | +20.2% |
| Total assets | $10.39B | $22.21B | +113.8% |
| Total debt | $3.82B | $10.70B | +180.1% |
| Goodwill | $4.49B | $11.44B | +155.0% |
| Intangibles (indefinite) | $1.28B | $4.54B | +254.7% |
| Stockholders' equity | $3.68B | $7.38B | +100.5% |
| Shares outstanding (weighted avg) | 407M | 446M | +9.6% |
| EPS (diluted) | $2.00 | $1.53 | -23.5% |

*Source: FMP FY2018 and FY2019 income/balance sheet data; CAG FY2019 10-K Item 7.*

**The deal was accretive to revenue but dilutive to EPS — immediately, in year one.** Management had guided to full-year accretion by FY2020 assuming $215M of synergies captured by end of year one. That never happened. FY2020 diluted EPS was $1.72 — still 14% below FY2018's $2.00, three years after Connolly took over the combined company.

The first $165.5M of Pinnacle impairments came in FY2020, concentrated in the Wish-Bone and Aunt Jemima brands. At the time, management described these as "isolated" and reiterated confidence in Birds Eye and Duncan Hines. With the benefit of hindsight, these were the canary in the coal mine.

### Phase 3: FY2021 "The COVID Sugar High"

March 2020–September 2020 was the single best period in US packaged food history since World War II. COVID-19 lockdowns drove restaurants to close, students home from school, and every US household into a pantry-loading frenzy that saw frozen foods, shelf-stable meals, baking mixes, and snacks experience 20–40% volume increases year-on-year for eight consecutive months. **For Conagra, the timing was almost too good to be true.** The Pinnacle brands — Birds Eye frozen vegetables, Hungry-Man frozen dinners, Duncan Hines baking mixes — were exactly the categories that saw the biggest demand spikes.

FY2021 results (fiscal year ended May 30, 2021):
- **Revenue: $11.18B** (+1.2% vs FY2020, but +17% vs FY2019 comparable including full-year Pinnacle)
- **Operating margin: 15.9%** (highest since FY2014)
- **Net income: $1.30B** (highest since FY2013)
- **Diluted EPS: $2.67** (highest in the 10-year panel)
- **Free cash flow: $962M**

Connolly and the board read this as proof that the Pinnacle integration was working and frozen foods had a secular tailwind. They began raising the dividend again, moving it from $0.8500 annual (FY2020) to $0.9769 (FY2021), a 15% increase over two fiscal years.

**This was the peak.** Revenue at $11.18B FY2021 has not been exceeded since. Net income at $1.30B has not been exceeded. EPS at $2.67 has not been exceeded. **FY2021 was the false dawn.** Management invested in capex ($506M that year, highest in the panel) and marketing, convinced that the category tailwind was durable.

### Phase 4: FY2022–FY2026 "The Inflation Trap and the Second Pinnacle Wave"

From June 2021 onward, US packaged food entered the inflation trap. Commodity inputs (wheat, vegetable oils, tomato paste, pork, beef, energy) rose 30–60% over eighteen months. CAG passed through pricing aggressively — FY2022 pricing contribution +8%, FY2023 +15% — but volumes collapsed as consumers responded to price increases by trading down to private label, reducing portion sizes, or substituting meals.

### The ultimate dividend question: will they cut?

Three data points argue against a cut:
1. **Connolly's personal incentive alignment** — his long-term incentive compensation is tied to total shareholder return, and a dividend cut would destroy the stock on announcement day. He does not want to cut.
2. **CAG's investment-grade credit rating** — BBB- is one notch above speculative. A dividend cut signals financial distress and could itself trigger the downgrade the company is trying to avoid. Paradoxically, this argues for holding the dividend even as it becomes uncomfortable.
3. **The $601M Chef Boyardee sale proceeds (June 2025)** and $42M Van de Kamp's / Mrs. Paul's sale proceeds were used to prepay debt, not returned to shareholders via buybacks. Management is prioritizing balance sheet over equity return, which buys time for the dividend.

Four data points argue for a cut:
1. **$968M of Q2 FY2026 Refrigerated & Frozen impairments** signals that management's internal fair value estimates for the R&F business have declined substantially from the FY2025 annual test.
2. **Eleven consecutive quarters of no dividend increase** is a behavioral tell. Staples companies that cut within 24 months almost always freeze first. The freeze is the warning shot.
3. **Ardent Mills JV earnings are declining** ($212M FY2023 → $182M FY2025 → annualized ~$131M FY2026), removing an important non-operating support for CAG's cash generation. The JV's structural earnings contribution to CAG's FCF payout capacity has fallen by approximately $80M (post-tax) in three years.
4. **The refinancing wall 2027–2029** — approximately $2.1B of senior notes mature at coupons of 3.25–4.25%, which will need to be refinanced at current rates of 5.5–6.5%. The incremental interest expense burden is $40–70M/year once fully refinanced. This directly reduces FCF available for dividends.

**My estimate: 40% probability of a dividend cut within 18 months, with expected magnitude of 25–35% (from $1.40 annual to $0.90–$1.05). Not a high-conviction call, but materially more probable than the 20% implied by option market pricing on consumer staples peer cut outcomes.**

### What happens to the stock on a cut?

Reference precedents:
- **KHC February 2019 cut** (36% cut from $2.50 to $1.60): stock fell 27% on the day of announcement, bottomed another 10% over the next six months, and has traded sideways in a $20–$40 range for seven years since.
- **GE January 2018 cut** (50% cut from $0.96 to $0.48): stock fell 13% on announcement, bottomed 40% lower eight months later, and did not recover until the 2020 spin-off.
- **CAG's own November 2016 "reset"** (17.5% effective cut): stock fell 8% on announcement, bottomed ~15% lower six weeks later, and recovered fully within nine months as the Lamb Weston spin closed and buyback activity ramped.

**CAG at $15.49 with a 25–35% cut would likely see an initial 10–15% drop to the $13–$14 range, followed by a gradual recovery to the $16–$18 range over 6–12 months** as the post-cut yield of 6.0–6.5% becomes attractive on a de-risked basis. Investors who hold through the cut and reinvest the "new" dividend at $14 would earn ~7% yield going forward and be positioned for capital appreciation if the R&F segment stabilizes. **This is actually the base-case return profile for a patient holder today.** The issue is that there is no asymmetry — the upside from a dividend HOLD scenario is roughly equal to the upside from a dividend CUT-THEN-STABILIZE scenario, while the dividend-CUT-AND-KEEP-FALLING scenario offers 25–40% downside.

## PART VI · PINNACLE INTEGRATION AND DEBT DELEVERAGING

### Did the synergies materialize?

At the June 26, 2018 deal announcement, management promised $285M of annual run-rate synergies by the end of year three post-close (October 2021). The composition was:
- **Supply chain / manufacturing consolidation: $155M** (facility rationalization, distribution network optimization, procurement scale)
- **SG&A reduction: $100M** (corporate overlap elimination, shared services, eliminating duplicate finance/HR/IT)
- **Revenue / commercial: $30M** (cross-sell opportunities, shared distribution channels)

The synergy tracking was reported quarterly through FY2021 and then dropped from investor presentations. My read of the cumulative MD&A disclosures across CAG's FY2019, FY2020, and FY2021 10-K filings suggests actual run-rate synergies achieved by the end of FY2021 were **approximately $180–200M**, meaningfully below the $285M promise but not zero. The shortfall came primarily from the supply chain bucket, where closing Pinnacle's Parsippany headquarters and consolidating into Conagra's Chicago and Omaha facilities proved slower and more expensive than projected.

**Synergies are effectively meaningless now.** The $2.7B of cumulative goodwill/brand impairments dwarfs the $180M of achieved annual synergies by a factor of 15x. Even if CAG captured the full $285M for the entire ten years post-close, the total synergy value ($2.85B undiscounted) would only roughly offset the impairments. **In effect, Conagra paid $8 billion for Pinnacle, wrote off $3 billion, captured $2 billion of cumulative synergies, and retained Pinnacle's revenue base (~$3.1B at acquisition, ~$2.7B now) at a lower margin than Conagra's pre-deal business.**

## PART VIII · MOAT AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

### What is CAG's moat?

Conagra Brands does not have a moat in the Morningstar sense (sustainable competitive advantage that allows for above-average returns on capital over an extended period). **CAG's historical and forward ROIC has averaged 5–8%** (FMP FY2016–FY2025 panel), which is roughly equal to its weighted-average cost of capital. A moat-holding business generates 3–5 percentage points of excess return above WACC for decades; CAG generates zero on average.

What CAG does have is **brand equity in specific niche categories** where switching costs are psychological rather than economic. A Slim Jim buyer does not choose Jack Link's because Slim Jim has different taste profiles and texture; a Reddi-wip buyer does not switch to store brand because of the familiar Reddi-wip can and nozzle. These are **narrow moats** in the Morningstar framework — sufficient to maintain market share under stable conditions but insufficient to generate pricing power during a private-label acceleration cycle (which is what 2024–2026 has been).

The specific brands where CAG has defensible positions:
1. **Slim Jim** — #1 US meat snack with ~40% share. Competes directly with Jack Link's and Oberto. Brand loyalty is high among the core 18–34 male demographic. Legitimate moat, worth $2B+ standalone.
2. **Orville Redenbacher's** — #1 US microwave popcorn. Competes with Act II (CAG sub-brand), Pop Secret, and private label. Moderate moat.
3. **Reddi-wip** — #1 aerosol whipped topping. Competes only with store brand and Kraft Cool Whip (shelf, not aerosol). Strong moat in the aerosol sub-category.
4. **Hunt's** — #2 US canned tomato after Contadina/Del Monte. Moderate position; private label has been winning here.
5. **Hebrew National** — #1 kosher hot dog. Small niche but loyal; some moat via kosher certification.
6. **Birds Eye** — #1 US frozen vegetables. **Moat is eroding fast.** Market share has declined 300+ bps since 2021 to private label. The original thesis for the Pinnacle deal hinged on Birds Eye being a durable #1 brand; that thesis is currently breaking.

**CAG does not have a moat in baking mixes, dressings, Mexican sauces, frozen meals, or Foodservice** — these are all commodity-like categories where price and distribution matter more than brand. The dominant trend in all six categories since 2022 has been private-label share gain.

### Capital allocation scorecard

Connolly has been CEO for eleven years. His capital allocation decisions in aggregate:

| Action | Total $ | Year range | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dividends paid | $4.97B | FY2016–FY2025 | OK — stable income return |
| Buybacks | $2.53B | FY2016–FY2025 | **Mediocre** — $1B in FY2017, $967M in FY2018 at avg $35–40/share vs today's $15; net value destruction ~$750M on repurchase timing alone |
| Pinnacle acquisition | $8.03B | October 2018 | **Disastrous** — $3.1B+ impaired, $2B of capital permanently destroyed |
| Other bolt-on M&A (Angie's, Frontera, Sandwich Bros., Sweetwood, cooking spray, Thanasi) | ~$850M | FY2017–FY2025 | Mixed — some winners (Angie's), some losers (Sandwich Bros., Sweetwood still early) |
| Debt repayment (net of refi) | $2.73B | FY2019–FY2025 | **Good** — disciplined deleveraging, though slower than desired |
| Divestitures | +$1.9B proceeds | FY2016–FY2025 | Good — exits of Private Brands, Lamb Weston, Wesson, Gelit, ATFL, Chef Boyardee, Van de Kamp's were all correct decisions |

**Composite grade: D+.** The buybacks at the wrong time of the cycle and the Pinnacle disaster overwhelm the otherwise-disciplined dividend, debt, and divestiture decisions. Connolly's defense is that he inherited a deeply troubled conglomerate and executed a necessary portfolio transformation; the problem is that the transformation's centerpiece (Pinnacle) is precisely what created the current distress. **Judged on realized shareholder returns, the Connolly era is one of the worst tenures in US consumer staples history.**

### What would I do differently?

This is the part where a research report usually offers constructive suggestions. For CAG:
1. **Cut the dividend to $1.00 annual ($0.25 quarterly)** — buy back stock aggressively at $15 instead. A $500M annual buyback at $15/share retires ~33 million shares per year (6.9% of diluted shares), which is more accretive to EPS and total return than maintaining the current payout.
2. **Sell the Refrigerated & Frozen segment entirely** — probably to Nestlé or a Private Equity firm for $6–8B. Use proceeds to retire $5B of debt and fund a one-time $2B special dividend or tender offer at $20/share.
3. **Focus the remaining Grocery & Snacks business** — invest in Slim Jim, Orville Redenbacher's, and Angie's; harvest Hunt's, Duncan Hines, Pam for cash flow. Reposition as a focused snack-and-pantry CPG with 15-18% EBIT margins and 2-3% organic growth.
4. **Replace Connolly.** Eleven years is enough. The company needs a fresh perspective from a CPG veteran with turnaround experience — Bharat Puri (ex-Mondelez India), Ramon Laguarta (Pepsi CEO, unlikely to leave), or an external hire from a competitive staples company.

None of this will happen under the current governance structure. The board has shown no appetite for dramatic action, and Connolly's compensation is structured such that a slow-decline management strategy is personally rational for him.

## PART IX · RISKS

### Business risks (primary)

1. **Private label acceleration** — the single biggest threat. US private label food share rose from 19.0% (2020) to 21.6% (2024) per NielsenIQ data, and is projected to hit 23% by 2027. Every 1 percentage point of private label gain costs CAG approximately $90–110M in annual revenue. The specific categories where CAG is most exposed are canned tomatoes (Hunt's), frozen vegetables (Birds Eye), baking mixes (Duncan Hines), and frozen meals (Banquet, Hungry-Man, Marie Callender's).

2. **GLP-1 weight-loss drug category impact** — quantified in the CAG FY2025 10-K Item 1A risk factors for the first time ("growing use of weight loss medication may cause shifts in consumer preferences"). Approximately 9% of the US adult population has tried a GLP-1 in 2024 (double 2023), and the target demographic is the overweight-and-obese cohort that was CAG's core customer base for frozen meals, snacks, and baking mixes. The estimated drag on US packaged food category volume from GLP-1 is 0.3-0.8% per year and rising. CAG's disproportionate frozen meal exposure makes it one of the most at-risk packaged food companies.

3. **Walmart concentration risk** — 29% of sales to a single customer with unmatched pricing leverage. Walmart has been aggressive about expanding Great Value private label at the expense of branded products. CAG's pricing concessions to maintain Walmart shelf space are visible in the 9M FY2026 "strategic trade investment" narrative, which is corporate-speak for giving up margin to protect distribution.

4. **Input cost inflation and tariffs** — management explicitly flagged in Q3 FY2026 that tariffs will pressure FY2026 gross margins. The specific exposures are imported wheat (for Duncan Hines), imported vegetable oils, imported packaging materials, and Mexican peso translation (for the Mexican business). Tariffs of 15–25% on imported inputs would compress CAG's gross margin by 80–120 bps.

5. **Refrigerated & Frozen chicken supply** — FY2025 saw a primary chicken supplier outage that disrupted Marie Callender's, Healthy Choice, and Banquet production for most of H2 FY2025. The reliance on single-source or limited-source poultry suppliers is a persistent risk.

### Financial risks

6. **Dividend cut** — 40% probability within 18 months per my analysis. Triggering event would likely be either (a) a ratings downgrade below investment grade, or (b) Q1 or Q2 FY2027 earnings disappointment that pushes forward FCF below $1.0B. The magnitude of a cut would likely be 25–35%.

7. **Credit rating downgrade** — Moody's Baa3 is one notch above speculative. Any deterioration in EBITDA or increase in net debt/EBITDA above 4.5x for two consecutive quarters would likely trigger a downgrade review. Current level is 4.07x (FY2025) — the cushion is narrow.

8. **Additional Refrigerated & Frozen impairments** — the $1.44B accumulated R&F goodwill impairment as of Q3 FY2026 could grow another $500M–$1B over the next 18 months if the segment's operating performance continues to deteriorate. Each impairment is non-cash but damages EPS, pushes the payout ratio higher, and signals to the market that the dividend is at risk.

9. **Refinancing risk 2027–2029** — $2B+ of debt maturing at low coupons (3.20–4.30%) will need to be refinanced at current rates (5.50–6.00%), adding $25–35M/year to cash interest expense by FY2029. In isolation this is manageable; combined with the other pressures, it is another turn of the screw.

10. **Ardent Mills JV cyclicality** — the 44%-owned wheat milling joint venture contributed $182M of equity method earnings in FY2025 (15% of CAG's operating profit), but Q3 FY2026 annualized is running at $131M. Every $50M decline in Ardent JV earnings reduces CAG's free cash flow by approximately $40M post-tax.

### Governance and strategic risks

11. **Management incentive misalignment** — Connolly's compensation is heavily weighted to total shareholder return, but the TSR measurement period is relatively short (3-year rolling) and his personal ownership of CAG shares is relatively modest (~450,000 shares, ~$7M value). There is no strong personal financial incentive for him to take the painful structural actions (break up the company, replace himself, cut the dividend) that would likely maximize long-term value.

12. **Board composition** — no activist investor presence, no private equity alumni on the board, and no track record of board-driven strategic reviews. The board has been supportive of Connolly through an eleven-year tenure that has destroyed substantial shareholder value, which suggests low probability of an activist-driven change in direction.

13. **Product liability exposure** — the ongoing Pam cooking spray class action litigation and lead paint-related legacy claims from the Beatrice Foods (1990 acquisition) era are a small but real tail risk. Accrued litigation reserves of ~$76M as of FY2024 have been relatively stable but could grow.

### The honest answer on risk-adjusted value

- 40% probability: Dividend cut 25–35% within 18 months, stock drops to $13–14 then recovers to $16–18 → total return over 24 months approximately +10% including post-cut dividends

- 10% probability: Deep crisis (2nd impairment, downgrade, forced cut of 50%+) → stock drops to $10–12 → total return approximately -25% after dividends

**Probability-weighted expected return: +17% over 24 months, or ~8% annualized.** That is roughly equal to or slightly below the S&P 500 expected return over the same horizon. For a 9%-yielding staples stock, that is a "fair" but not "exciting" risk-reward. **The 9% yield looks attractive on the surface but the cut risk converts a 9% yield into something like a 5.5-6.5% effective forward yield after adjusting for the probability of a cut.** That is a yield that is genuinely comparable to KHC (7.0%), CPB (5.4%), and GIS (4.4%) — not a dramatic arbitrage.

## PART XI · VARIANT PERCEPTION

### The bullish thesis (and why I don't buy it)

The consensus bullish pitch for CAG goes something like this:

**"CAG is a classic deep-value staples name. At 9x forward P/E and 9% yield, you're getting paid to wait for the R&F segment to stabilize. Management has been disciplined on deleveraging, the balance sheet is under control, and the investment-grade rating is holding. The Pinnacle impairments are backward-looking noise — most of the damage is done. Once organic volumes stabilize in calendar 2026, the stock will re-rate to a 12–13x multiple and the dividend yield will compress back to 5.5–6.5%, driving 40–50% capital appreciation on top of 9% current yield. Worst case, it's an LBO candidate at $20+."**

**Where this thesis is wrong:**

1. **"Most of the damage is done" is a claim made in every CAG earnings call since FY2020.** Each time, another $500M–$1B of impairments has followed. Q2 FY2026's $968M impairment was the largest single charge since FY2019 and is a reset of management's internal fair value assumptions — not an acknowledgment that "the damage is done." Expect more.

2. **Organic volumes have not stabilized and the conditions for stabilization do not exist.** Private label is accelerating, GLP-1 impact is growing, Walmart pricing pressure is relentless, and the R&F category growth has decelerated from +2% COVID era to +0.3% current. The environment is structurally hostile to CAG's mix.

3. **"Management has been disciplined on deleveraging" is a backward-looking compliment.** The deleveraging pace ($455M/year) is no longer enough to offset the cost headwinds and ratings pressure. The 2025–2029 refinancing wall adds $25–35M/year of interest expense. The Ardent Mills contribution is declining by $50M/year. These are all net cash drains that reduce the forward deleveraging capacity.

4. **"9% yield gets you paid to wait" is only true if the dividend is paid.** A cut to $1.00 annual at $15.49 is a 6.4% yield — still attractive, but materially below the 9% narrative. And the stock-price drop on a cut announcement (likely 10–15%) means the cumulative return from owning through the cut is no better than buying post-cut.

5. **LBO is not a thesis. LBO is a hope.** The last food LBO of CAG's size was Heinz/3G in 2013 at 10x EBITDA in a ZIRP environment. Rates are now 200 bps higher and LBO debt markets have tightened. The probability of CAG being taken private in the next 18 months is <10%.

### The bearish thesis (and why I don't fully buy it either)

The consensus bearish case is: **"CAG is a value trap. The dividend is going to be cut. Management is incompetent. Pinnacle was a disaster and the rest of the business is in structural decline. Stay away at any price."**

**Where this is too negative:**

1. **Even a dividend cut to $1.00 annual would leave CAG yielding 6.4% at current prices, which is still attractive** in absolute terms and relative to the 10-year Treasury at 4.4–4.8%. The post-cut scenario is not catastrophic for new buyers.

2. **The Grocery & Snacks segment is genuinely high-quality.** Slim Jim, Orville Redenbacher's, Reddi-wip, and Angie's BOOMCHICKAPOP together generate $500M+ of segment operating profit and have defensible market positions. At 10x EBITDA, this subset of CAG is worth $5–6B — roughly 75% of the current market cap.

3. **Deleveraging is real, even if slow.** Net debt has fallen from $10.46B to $7.91B, a genuine reduction. The dividend + buyback + debt reduction capital return machine is working, just not fast enough for the market's patience.

4. **The P/B of 0.83x is unusual for a branded staples company.** Market says CAG is worth less than book, implying either (a) another $1.5B of impairments coming, or (b) the market is wrong. Both are possible; history suggests the answer is "some of both."

### My variant view

**Neither the bulls nor the bears are right.** CAG is not a clean value play, but it is also not a zero. The market has priced in most of the bad news but has not priced in the optionality from a potential dividend cut that could actually be a catalyst for stock appreciation.

**The interesting variant perception is that a dividend cut is bullish, not bearish, for the stock price six months after the cut.** This is counterintuitive but historically supported:
- KHC cut February 2019, stock was +12% twelve months later (despite continued poor fundamentals)
- GE cut December 2018, stock was +45% twelve months later (combined with spin-off catalyst)
- Ford cut March 2020, stock was +120% twelve months later (combined with EV re-rating)
- **CAG cut November 2016 (the Lamb Weston spin reset), stock was +22% twelve months later**

The reason is that a dividend cut for a troubled company removes the overhang of "when will they cut" uncertainty, allows management to redirect capital to debt reduction or buybacks, and resets the income investor base to a sustainable level. **For CAG specifically, a cut to $1.00–$1.05 annual would free up approximately $170–190M of annual cash flow that could be used to accelerate debt reduction by approximately 40%.** This could pull forward the 3.5x net debt/EBITDA target from FY2028 to FY2026, potentially triggering a ratings upgrade and a multiple re-rating.

**The trade this suggests: buy CAG at $15.49 with the expectation that either the dividend holds (in which case you earn 9% yield plus modest appreciation) or the dividend is cut moderately (in which case you earn 6.0–6.5% post-cut yield plus 15–25% appreciation as the overhang resolves). The asymmetric downside is limited to the scenario of a catastrophic R&F impairment combined with a forced ratings downgrade — which is a realistic 10% probability, not 30%.**

This is not a recommendation to buy. It is an explanation of why the reflexive "value trap, stay away" thesis may be missing the optionality in the setup.

### Overall rating: HOLD if owned, DO NOT add as new buyer

1. ## APPENDIX

### Key CAG metric definitions (as used in this report)

- **Organic revenue growth:** Revenue growth excluding the impact of acquisitions, divestitures, and foreign exchange. This is management's preferred metric but should be interpreted carefully — organic growth can be positive on pricing alone even when volumes decline (which has been CAG's situation for most of FY2023–FY2026).

- **Organic volume:** Change in units sold on an organic basis (same methodology as above). Volume is the "cleanest" measure of demand strength.

- **Price/mix:** The contribution to revenue from price increases AND from mix shifts (moving from low-margin to high-margin products within the same SKU assortment). Management has used price/mix as the primary growth lever during FY2022–FY2024 inflation passthrough.

- **Segment operating profit:** Revenue minus cost of goods sold minus segment-allocated SG&A, EXCLUDING general corporate expenses and items impacting comparability (impairments, restructuring, legal settlements, etc.). This is management's preferred segment profitability measure.

- **Net debt / EBITDA (leverage ratio):** Total debt minus cash, divided by trailing twelve months EBITDA. CAG's credit covenant is 4.5x; current level is 4.07x as of FY2025 and approximately 4.0x as of Q3 FY2026.

- **FCF payout ratio:** Cash dividends paid divided by free cash flow. The "safe" zone is <60%; the "warning" zone is 60-80%; the "danger" zone is >80%.

### Fiscal year convention

Conagra Brands operates on a fiscal year ending the last Sunday in May. Fiscal 2025 ended May 25, 2025. Fiscal 2026 will end May 31, 2026. The 10-Q periods are approximately 13 weeks each, with Q1 ending in late August, Q2 in late November, Q3 in late February, and Q4 being the fiscal year end in late May. **This off-calendar fiscal year is unusual and can create confusion when comparing to peers on calendar quarters** — for example, CAG's FY2025 (June 2024–May 2025) overlaps mostly with calendar 2024 for P&L purposes but with calendar 2025 for market comparisons.
