## ¿Qué hace exactamente esta empresa?

T. Rowe Price es una **gestora pública de fondos** (mutual funds principalmente, también separate accounts y ETFs). Cobra fees a los inversores que ponen dinero en sus fondos.

### Las 3 unidades principales

1. **Mutual funds (~50% AUM)**: fondos de inversión vendidos a inversores individuales (retail) y a través de retirement plans (401k). Marcas conocidas: T. Rowe Price Blue Chip Growth, Capital Appreciation, Equity Income, International Stock, etc. Estos fondos cobran ~0.6-0.85% management fee.

2. **Target Date Retirement Funds (~30% AUM, growing)**: fondos auto-piloto donde tú dices "me jubilo en 2050" y el fondo automáticamente ajusta tu mix de stocks/bonds según te acercas a la jubilación. T. Rowe es **#3 en USA en target-date** (después de Vanguard y Fidelity), y este es el segmento de mayor crecimiento estructural — los 401k corporate cada vez más usan target-date como default.

3. **Institutional Separate Accounts (~20% AUM)**: cuentas individuales para grandes inversores (pension plans, sovereign wealth, endowments). Fees más bajos (0.3-0.5%) pero AUM grande.

### Métricas operativas

- **AUM**: ~$1,600B (ene 2026)
- **Net flows trailing 12m**: -$30B a -$50B (outflows persistentes desde 2022)
- **Fee rate average**: ~45-48bps (bajando)
- **Operating margin**: ~37-40% (buenísimo)
- **Active vs Passive split**: 90%+ active (es su core competency, no es ETF puro)

### El gran problema estructural

Desde ~2015, hay un **shift masivo de active funds a passive** en USA. Los ETFs Vanguard/iShares cobran 0.05-0.10% mientras T. Rowe cobra 0.65%. Para muchos inversores, especialmente en 401k defaults, los pasivos ganan. Esto significa que:
- AUM crecimiento orgánico (net flows) es **negativo** para T. Rowe (-2 a -4% anual).
- AUM total fluctúa con market performance (S&P 500 sube → AUM sube; bear → cae).
- En 2024 con S&P 500 +25%, AUM creció a pesar de outflows.
- En 2022 con S&P 500 -19%, AUM cayó dramáticamente.

T. Rowe defiende su valor proposition argumentando que **ciertos active funds bate consistentemente índices** — particularmente growth equity, bonds, international. Pero el mercado está escéptico.

## Riesgos clave

### 1. Outflows persistentes (el grande)

Si net flows continúan -$30 a -$50B/año, AUM se erosiona aunque mercados suban. Eventually el revenue se contrae. La pregunta clave: ¿pueden estabilizar / revertir flows en próximos 2-3 años?

### 2. Fee compression continuada

El AUM-weighted fee rate cayó de 50bps (2018) a 47bps (2023) y siguiendo. Si baja a 40bps, revenue cae en moneda constante.

### 3. Active management commoditization

Si el debate "active vs passive" se resuelve definitively pro-passive (pocos fondos active outperform sostenidamente), T. Rowe pierde su raison d'être.

### 4. Mercados bear severos

T. Rowe AUM cae 1:1 con S&P 500. En -30% bear, AUM cae 30% → revenue cae 30%. Es altamente operating-leveraged.

### 5. Key talent retention

Algunos portfolio managers son very valuable. Salidas (David Giroux, Henry Ellenbogen ya salió a Durable Capital, etc.) impactan brand perception.

### 6. Regulatorio

Cambios en regulación (DOL fiduciary rule, SEC fee disclosure) pueden afectar revenue. Hasta ahora manejable.

## Análisis profundo: el "active vs passive" debate

### Where active is winning

A pesar del shift hacia passive, hay segments where **active managers outperform consistently**:

1. **Small-cap equity**: less efficient market, opportunities for skilled stockpickers. T. Rowe has top-performers like New Horizons Fund.
2. **Emerging markets**: market inefficiency + corporate governance issues = active managers can add alpha. T. Rowe's Emerging Markets Fund has track record.
3. **High yield bonds**: complex credit analysis required, active wins. T. Rowe's high yield offerings competitive.
4. **Multi-asset / target-date**: active asset allocation can add value vs static index. T. Rowe is #3 in target-date.
5. **International developed**: less efficient than USA markets.

T. Rowe is **stronger than average** in #1, #4, #5. Weaker in core USA equity (where passive dominates).

### Where passive has won

Passive ETFs dominate:
1. **USA large-cap equity**: SPY, VOO captures most flows.
2. **USA total market**: VTI, ITOT.
3. **International developed**: VEA, IEFA.
4. **Bonds (broad investment grade)**: BND, AGG.
5. **Sector specific**: most sector ETFs.

T. Rowe loses much of these segments to passive ETFs. AUM "stickiness" comes mainly from retirement plan defaults.

### The 401k angle

US retirement plans have ~$10T in assets. T. Rowe is provider/fund manager for many 401k plans. Critical aspects:
- Once chosen as plan provider, very sticky.
- Default investment options matter enormously.
- Target-date funds increasingly the default.

If 401k plans shift defaults from T. Rowe target-date to Vanguard target-date (cheaper), T. Rowe loses massive AUM. So far hasn't happened materially, but ongoing risk.

### Active ETF launches

T. Rowe (and others) launched **active ETFs** in 2020-2024 to compete:
- Lower fees than mutual funds (but higher than passive).
- Tax efficiency similar to passive.
- Combines stock-picking with ETF wrapper.

T. Rowe Active ETFs:
- TCAF (Capital Appreciation Equity ETF).
- TCHP (US High Yield Bond ETF).
- TGRT (Growth ETF).
- Several others.

**AUM tracking**: ~$5-10B across all active ETFs as of FY25. Modest contribution but growing.

## Análisis profundo: el OHA (Oak Hill Advisors) acquisition

### Why T. Rowe bought OHA

In December 2021, T. Rowe acquired Oak Hill Advisors for **$4.2B** (cash + stock). OHA is a private credit / alternative asset manager:
- AUM at acquisition: ~$53B.
- Strategies: private credit, distressed credit, structured credit.
- Track record: 30+ years successful.

Strategic rationale:
1. **Diversification**: T. Rowe was 95%+ traditional equity + bonds. OHA gives alternatives exposure.
2. **Higher fees**: alternatives charge 1-1.5% vs 0.5% for traditional.
3. **Faster growth**: alternatives growing 15-20% vs flat for traditional.
4. **Competitive response**: Apollo, Ares, BlackRock all expanding alternatives.

### The acquisition timing — questionable

T. Rowe paid 2021 peak alternative valuations. Multiples at that time were 18-22x EBITDA. By 2024-2025, similar firms trading at 12-15x EBITDA. So **bought near top**.

Performance since:
- AUM: $53B → ~$80B (+50% in 4 years).
- Revenue: ~$300M → ~$600M.
- Contribution to T. Rowe net income: ~$200M annually.

Mathematically: paid 14x current EBITDA contribution. Reasonable but not bargain.

### Going forward

OHA's contribution will grow:
- Private credit market growing.
- T. Rowe distribution channels selling OHA strategies.
- New funds launched annually.

By 2030, OHA could contribute $400-500M annually = ~25% of T. Rowe net income. Significant.

## Análisis profundo: net flows analysis

### The structural problem

T. Rowe net flows by year:
- 2018: -$5B (modest outflow)
- 2019: +$5B (inflow)
- 2020: -$10B
- 2021: -$15B
- 2022: -$30B (heavy outflow during bear market)
- 2023: -$45B
- 2024: -$40B
- 2025 (TTM): -$30B

Cumulative since 2018: ~-$170B in net outflows. Yet AUM has grown $1.4T → $1.6T because **markets appreciated** more than outflows took.

### What's driving outflows

1. **Active large-cap equity rotations**: investors moving to passive.
2. **Bond fund redemptions**: 2022-2023 bond market crisis.
3. **Sub-advisory client losses**: institutional clients moving to other providers.
4. **Performance disappointment** in some funds.

### Could flows turn positive?

Catalysts:
1. **Performance turnaround**: if T. Rowe funds beat benchmarks for 3 consecutive years, flows return.
2. **Active ETF success**: if T. Rowe's active ETFs scale to $30-50B.
3. **Target-date dominance**: if more 401k plans choose T. Rowe.
4. **OHA scaling**: alternatives flows usually positive even when traditional outflows.
5. **New product launches**: emerging markets, ESG, thematic.

If flows go from -$40B to -$10B (improvement, still outflow), AUM could grow with markets. If flows turn +$10B, AUM growth accelerates.

### The "natural floor"

T. Rowe's institutional retirement business is sticky enough that **complete outflow collapse** is unlikely. Floor probably $1.2-1.4T AUM. At that level, T. Rowe still profitable.
