## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2005: The Doug Daft Era (post-Goizueta hangover)
Roberto Goizueta, the legendary CEO who transformed KO from 1981–1997 (stock 30-bagger), died in October 1997. His successor Doug Ivester lasted only two years and was pushed out in December 1999 after a series of stumbles: Belgium contamination scare, failed Orangina acquisition blocked by European regulators, clumsy handling of a discrimination lawsuit. Doug Daft took over in 2000 and inherited a company with decelerating revenue, bloated SG&A, and accounting irregularities that later (2005) led to an SEC settlement over "channel stuffing" with Japanese bottlers.

Despite the operational turbulence, **the dividend kept rising at double-digit rates** (9–14% annually 2000–2004). This was deliberate: with EPS growth slowing, management leaned harder on the payout to maintain the total-return narrative that had been Goizueta's legacy. Payout ratios crept from ~40% to ~50% over the period.

### 2004–2007: Neville Isdell Returns, Reaccelerates
Isdell — a retired KO lifer — was brought back from retirement in 2004 to right the ship. Key moves: refocused on emerging markets (China, India, Eastern Europe), relaunched the core Coca-Cola brand, re-invested in marketing. Dividend raises stayed in the 10–14% range.

### 2008–2009: The Global Financial Crisis — KO Accelerates
When Lehman failed in September 2008, KO was in the middle of a ~10% dividend raise year. **No cut. No freeze. No step-down in growth rate.** The 2009 raise from $1.52 to $1.64 (+7.9%) was smaller than the 2008 raise (+11.8%) but remained comfortably ahead of inflation. KO's cash flow actually *grew* in 2009 as emerging-markets volume held up and commodity costs (sugar, aluminum, PET) collapsed. Muhtar Kent (CEO since July 2008) used the crisis to tighten cost discipline.

**Why KO was resistant:** Coca-Cola as a category is one of the most income-inelastic consumer products on earth. Unit cases declined less than 1% globally in 2009. Free cash flow was $6.8B against $3.8B of dividends — 1.8x coverage.

### 2010: CCE North America Acquisition
In October 2010, KO acquired the North American operations of Coca-Cola Enterprises (the largest independent bottler) for $12.3B, reversing decades of refranchising strategy. This was a strategic U-turn driven by slowing North American volume and the perceived need for operational control. **Dividend raise continued at +7.3%.** The debt taken on (~$10B) temporarily pushed net debt / EBITDA from ~0.8x to ~1.7x but remained investment-grade with ease.

### 2012: 2-for-1 Split
On August 13, 2012, KO split its stock 2-for-1, lowering the share price from ~$80 to ~$40 to improve retail accessibility. **This was NOT a dividend cut** — each holder received twice the shares, and each share received half the dividend, so total income was unchanged. The 50th consecutive annual increase was declared in February 2012 on a pre-split basis.

### 2013–2017: Muhtar Kent Refranchising Era
Recognizing that the 2010 CCE acquisition had over-reached, Kent reversed course and began divesting bottling operations back to independent partners ("refranchising"). Over 2013–2017, KO sold ~$10B of low-margin bottling revenue. **Operating margin expanded from 23% to 27%.** Dividend growth slowed from 8–10% to 5–6% as reported revenue shrunk, but free cash flow per share stayed resilient.

### 2017: James Quincey Becomes CEO
Quincey (Kent's deputy since 2015) takes over May 2017. Continues refranchising. Launches Costa Coffee acquisition (£3.9B / $5.1B, announced August 2018, closed January 2019) — KO's biggest non-beverage deal ever. **Dividend growth decelerates to 5–6% as Quincey focuses on buybacks and deleveraging.**

### 2019: "Beverages for Life" Strategy Launch
Quincey announces pivot from "Coca-Cola Company" narrative to "Total Beverage Company" — Coca-Cola Energy, Costa ready-to-drink, more water and sports drinks. **Dividend raise slowed to +2.6% — the smallest increase in KO history for the preceding 50+ years.** This was not distress; it was Quincey holding back dividend growth to preserve flexibility for M&A.

### 2020: COVID-19 Response
Away-from-home beverage channels (restaurants, stadiums, cinemas, airports) collapsed in March–May 2020. Total case volume fell ~9% in 2020 — the largest single-year decline in KO history. Revenue fell 11%. **Yet the dividend was raised +2.5% (58th consecutive year).** Additionally, KO completed the fairlife acquisition (January 2020) for the remaining ~57.5% it didn't own — nominal headline of $980M but with a performance earnout that would ultimately balloon to $6.2B by 2025.

**Management's COVID playbook:** protect marketing spend, cut discretionary costs, preserve dividend, suspend buybacks temporarily, draw on commercial paper for liquidity cushion. FCF for 2020 was $8.7B vs $7.0B dividends paid — 1.24x coverage despite the disruption.

### 2021: BODYARMOR Acquisition
In November 2021, KO acquired full control of sports drink maker BODYARMOR for $5.6B — a move to challenge Gatorade (Pepsi). **Dividend raise stayed small at +2.4%.** BODYARMOR proved to be the acquisition that worked least well: two impairments (2024 $760M, 2025 $960M) have knocked $1.72B off its carrying value. The lesson: even disciplined operators like KO can overpay in competitive category plays.

### 2022–2024: Post-COVID Normalization + Margin Expansion
Unit case volume recovered; price/mix became the dominant revenue lever. Operating margin reached 28.7% in 2025 — an all-time high and ~800 bps above 2016. **Dividend growth re-accelerated to +4.8%, +4.5%, +5.4%, +5.2%** over 2022–2025 as FCF normalized.

### 2024: IRS Tax Litigation Deposit
In September 2024, KO paid a $6.0B deposit related to transfer-pricing litigation with the IRS (2007–2009 assessment years, with tax court methodology that could extend through 2025). Management characterized it as refundable if KO wins on appeal. **Dividend raise in Feb 2025 was +5.2% — largest in 5 years.** The October 1, 2025 Eighth Circuit 3M ruling in 3M's favor dramatically improved KO's probability of winning on appeal.

### 2025–2026: Braun Transition
March 31, 2026: Henrique Braun (COO since Jan 2025, Brazilian, 25+ year KO lifer) takes over as CEO. Quincey moves to Executive Chairman. **64th consecutive raise announced February 2026** — $0.51 → $0.53 quarterly, $2.12 annual.

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~10% CAGR (high-growth dividend phase, rising payout ratio)
- **2010–2015:** ~7% CAGR (post-CCE digestion, refranchising start)
- **2015–2020:** ~4% CAGR (refranchising peak, Quincey slowdown)
- **2020–2025:** ~4% CAGR (COVID, M&A, IRS overhang) — stable despite turbulence

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
S&P 500 dividend growth averaged ~6% over 2000–2025. **KO averaged ~4.5%** — slightly below the index average, because KO entered 2000 with an already high payout ratio (~40%) and the index was buoyed by tech companies initiating and rapidly growing dividends. However, **KO's dividend stability was top-decile**: zero cuts, zero freezes, zero misses.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought KO at the 2005 year-end price of ~$40.31 (pre-split equivalent = $20.16 post-split) at a then-forward annual dividend of $1.12 pre-split ($0.56 post-split) had a starting yield of ~2.8%.
- 2010 YOC: 1.76 / 20.16 = **8.7%**
- 2015 YOC: 1.32 / 20.16 = **6.6%** (post-split)
- 2020 YOC: 1.64 / 20.16 = **8.1%**
- 2025 YOC: 2.04 / 20.16 = **10.1%**
- 2026e YOC: 2.12 / 20.16 = **10.5%**

A 2005 buyer earns their cost basis back every 10 years in dividends alone at today's rate.

### Buyback vs dividend mix
KO has been a meaningful buyback company but second-priority. Over 2010–2025:
- Cumulative dividends paid: ~$135B
- Cumulative buybacks: ~$55B
- Ratio: dividends are ~2.5x buybacks

Shares outstanding went from ~4.60B (2010) to ~4.31B (2025) — a 6% cumulative reduction. **KO is a "dividend-first, buybacks-secondary" capital allocator** — the archetype for a Dividend King.

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made KO a reliable dividend grower
1. **Brand is the moat, not the recipe.** Coca-Cola as a trademark is essentially impossible to replicate through volume alone. Every generation of management has understood that the core asset is the trademark, and the dividend is funded by that trademark's pricing power.
2. **Asset-light franchise model.** By selling concentrate to independent bottlers rather than manufacturing finished products, KO captures the high-margin IP royalty while bottlers absorb cyclical commodity and labor risk. When bottling wobbled (CCE 2010 → fix refranchising 2013–2017), the model was restored.
3. **Dividend is cultural.** Every CEO since Woodruff (1923–1985) has treated the dividend as a sacred obligation. The 63-year streak (soon 64) becomes self-reinforcing — no CEO wants to be the one who breaks it, so the organization runs with "protect the streak" as an unwritten constraint.
4. **Emerging-market diversification.** By 2025, North America is only ~37% of operating income — EMEA 31%, Latin America 27%, Asia 15%, Bottling 3%. This geographic spread means a single-region crisis (e.g., Latin American FX, Europe recession) never threatens consolidated cash flow.
5. **Pricing power exceeds inflation.** KO has raised prices ahead of inflation every year of the last decade. The "incidence-based" pricing model (concentrate price tracks bottler finished-goods pricing) compounds at 4–5% per year without volume growth.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2001–2005 Channel Stuffing / SEC Investigation.** Under Ivester and early Daft, KO was pushing concentrate inventory onto Japanese bottlers to meet quarterly targets — accounting gimmick that inflated reported revenue. SEC settled in 2005 (no financial penalty but a consent decree). **Dividend was unaffected** — but if the investigation had spiraled to restatements, the dividend could have been at risk. Lesson: governance and audit quality matter even at dividend kings.
- **2019 "Beverages for Life" — strategy drift.** Quincey signaled a pivot that, if taken too far, could have diluted the core franchise. Under Braun, expect recommitment to Coca-Cola Classic as the crown jewel.
- **BODYARMOR impairments (2024, 2025).** A $5.6B acquisition writing down to $2.44B within three years is a real capital-allocation miss. Not dividend-threatening, but a reminder that even KO overpays in category wars.
- **IRS Transfer Pricing Litigation (2015–present).** Up to $14B aggregate liability if KO loses on appeal. The Eighth Circuit 3M ruling (Oct 2025) improved KO's odds materially, but this remains the single largest below-the-line risk to the balance sheet. A loss would not require a dividend cut — KO has ~$10B of buyback / M&A flex to redirect first — but it would compress growth.

### What to watch looking forward
- **GLP-1 adoption rate.** Ozempic/Wegovy/Zepbound reduce sugared-beverage consumption among users by ~20–40% in early studies. If GLP-1 penetration reaches 15–20% of US adults by 2030, it could slow volume by 2–3 percentage points of CAGR — manageable but not zero. Watch North America unit case trends.
- **Eleventh Circuit ruling on IRS appeal (expected 2026 or early 2027).** The binary event. Win: $6.4B refunds, tail disappears. Lose: $14B liability materializes over 2007–2025 assessments. Dividend survives either outcome but growth rate differs.
- **Braun's first CAGNY presentation (likely Feb 2027).** Will set the tone for capital allocation under the new CEO. Look for continued dividend priority + potential buyback re-acceleration if IRS clears.
- **Currency.** KO gets ~65% of revenue outside the US. USD strength is a multi-year headwind. A secular DXY decline from 105 to 95 would add ~1–2 points to reported dividend growth.

**Conclusion:** KO has cut the dividend zero times since 1963. Through two world recessions, a global pandemic, two major M&A transactions, and a multi-billion-dollar tax dispute, the streak has held. The business underlying the dividend is structurally higher-margin in 2025 than in 2010. Expect the 64-year streak to become a 70-year streak by 2032, barring catastrophe.
