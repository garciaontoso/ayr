## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### Pre-History 2001–2013: Private REIT Formation
Rexford was founded by Howard Schwimmer and Michael Frankel in 2001 as a private investor in Southern California industrial real estate. The strategy from day one: **buy small (50,000–250,000 sq ft) infill warehouses in the LA/Inland Empire markets.** These "last-mile" industrial properties serve e-commerce, consumer distribution, and related logistics tenants — markets with limited supply due to zoning constraints and high barriers to entry.

The private company grew to ~90 properties by mid-2013 when it IPO'd.

### 2013: IPO and Initial Growth Phase
July 2013: REXR IPO'd at $14/share, raising ~$160M. The public thesis was straightforward:
- Southern California has the tightest industrial market in the US (vacancy <4%, rent growth 8%+ annually)
- Barriers to new supply (coastal land scarcity, NIMBY opposition, strict zoning)
- E-commerce acceleration drives demand for last-mile distribution
- **Rexford has unique local sourcing** via relationships with private owners

Initial dividend: $0.07/share quarterly (partial 2013).

### 2014–2017: Scale Phase
Annual acquisition volume grew from $200M (2014) to $800M+ (2017). Portfolio reached 156 properties / 17M sq ft by end of 2017. **Dividend raises of 10–20% per year reflected strong organic growth + accretive acquisitions.** Rent growth in the Southern California industrial market accelerated to 10%+ per year.

### 2018: Step-Up Raise +27%
February 2018: REXR declared a 27% dividend raise, signaling confidence in the outlook and continued accretion from acquisitions. The market's response was positive — stock up 15% in the following quarter.

### 2019: 2-for-1 Stock Split
In November 2019, REXR executed a 2-for-1 stock split to improve retail accessibility. Pre-split share price ~$50; post-split $25. **No impact on dividend policy — each holder's total income was unchanged.**

### 2020: COVID-19 — Industrial Real Estate Boom
Unlike retail REITs (which struggled with tenant closures) or office REITs (which faced WFH), **industrial REITs THRIVED during COVID.** E-commerce penetration accelerated from 15% of US retail (pre-COVID) to 22% (peak 2021), driving unprecedented demand for last-mile distribution space.

REXR specifically:
- Same-property NOI growth of 8%+ in 2020 (industry-leading)
- Acquisition pipeline expanded
- **Dividend raise of +14.3% in 2020** — a bold statement during a global pandemic

### 2021–2022: Peak Industrial Cycle
2021 and 2022 were the best two years in REXR's history:
- Same-property NOI growth of 10%+ for two consecutive years
- Acquisition volume exceeded $1.5B per year
- Rent spreads (new-lease rent vs expiring rent) of 30–40%
- **Dividend raises of +29.7% (2021) and +41.6% (2022)** — extraordinary for any REIT
- Stock price peaked at ~$87 (Dec 2021), ~6x IPO level on split-adjusted basis

### 2023: Rate Shock, Valuation Reset
As the Fed raised rates from 0% to 5%+ during 2022–2023, industrial REIT cap rates expanded from ~4.0% to ~5.5%+. REXR's stock declined ~40% from 2021 peak. **However, fundamentals remained strong — dividend raise of +23.0% in 2023** (reflecting 2022 cash flow strength, declared before the market slowdown was fully felt).

### 2024: Cycle Moderation
Industrial rent growth decelerated from 10%+ to 5–6% in 2024. Vacancy in Southern California ticked up from 3% to 5% as supply (delivered from 2021–2022 development pipelines) caught up to demand. **Dividend raise of +12.5% — still strong but clearly decelerating.**

### 2025: Continued Deceleration
**Dividend raise of +4.6%** — the lowest in REXR's public history. Reflects:
- Slower same-property NOI growth (~3–4%)
- Higher cost of debt (REXR refinancing at 5.5%+ vs legacy 3%)
- Reduced acquisition spread (cap rates on new deals ~5%, vs REXR's cost of capital ~5.5%)
- Management shifting to a more conservative posture

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by period
- **2014–2019:** ~14% CAGR (early public scaling)
- **2019–2022:** ~30% CAGR (COVID tailwind + peak cycle)
- **2022–2025:** ~13% CAGR (deceleration — but still very strong)

REXR's long-term ~17.5% CAGR is **one of the highest in the entire public REIT universe** — driven by a unique market (Southern California infill industrial) at a specific cycle peak (e-commerce acceleration 2018–2022).

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
REXR has outpaced the S&P 500 dividend growth by ~12 points annually over 2014–2025 — an extraordinary performance. However, the deceleration is real: 2025's +4.6% raise is roughly in-line with S&P 500 averages.

### Yield-on-cost from 2015 purchase
A holder who bought REXR at year-end 2015 (~$14.40, post-split equivalent) with the forward $0.335 dividend had a starting yield of ~2.3%.
- 2020 YOC: 0.64 / 14.40 = **4.4%**
- 2022 YOC: 1.175 / 14.40 = **8.2%**
- 2025 YOC: 1.70 / 14.40 = **11.8%**

A 2015 REXR buyer now earns ~12% on cost — higher than all consumer staples peers (KO 10%, PEP 10%, PG 7%) and most REITs — reflecting the extraordinary DGR over this window.

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made REXR a reliable dividend grower (2014–2024)
1. **Unique, hard-to-replicate market position.** Southern California infill industrial is among the tightest markets in the US. Barriers to entry (land, zoning, NIMBY) mean new supply can't quickly respond to demand.
2. **Local sourcing advantage.** REXR has cultivated relationships with private property owners over 20+ years. Most of its acquisitions are off-market or limited-auction deals — which means they close at lower cap rates than marketed deals.
3. **E-commerce secular tailwind.** 2018–2022 saw structural demand growth from Amazon, Target, Walmart, and smaller 3PLs all building last-mile networks.
4. **Low-leverage, high-liquidity balance sheet.** Investment-grade, low net debt / EBITDAre (~4.5x), large revolver capacity. This is NOT a highly-leveraged REIT.
5. **Accretive acquisitions.** REXR's cost of capital was below industrial cap rates for most of 2014–2022, making each deal dividend-accretive.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2022–2023 rate shock.** REXR's stock fell 40% from peak as rates compressed valuations. The *dividend was not affected* (2022–2023 raises stayed strong), but the share price declined dramatically — a reminder that REIT dividend safety ≠ REIT price stability.
- **2024–2025 deceleration.** The dramatic slowdown from +41% raises (2022) to +4.6% (2025) is a major signal. REXR's best years are likely behind it. Future DGR of 3–6% is plausible.
- **Market concentration risk.** REXR is 100% Southern California. If the LA/Inland Empire market enters a prolonged downturn (e.g., import tariff shift reducing port activity), the entire portfolio is exposed.
- **No downturn test.** Unlike O, NNN, or VICI, REXR has not yet faced a true industrial recession. The 2020 pandemic was a tailwind, not a stress.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Same-property NOI growth.** Historical ~10% is decelerating to 3–4%. If it falls below 2%, dividend growth flattens further.
- **Acquisition pipeline.** If cost of capital stays above cap rates, REXR may shift to a retrenchment mode (net disposition rather than net acquisition).
- **LA Port activity.** As a leading indicator of West Coast distribution demand. Watch TEU (container volume) trends.
- **E-commerce normalization.** If e-commerce penetration plateaus at ~22% of US retail (rather than continuing to grow), industrial demand growth moderates.
- **Interest rate trajectory.** A Fed pivot back to easing would restore REXR's cost-of-capital advantage and potentially reaccelerate dividend growth.
- **Amazon specifically.** Amazon is ~3% of REXR's revenue and a major price-setter in industrial markets. Any strategic shift by Amazon toward self-ownership would be a concern.

**Conclusion:** REXR has cut the dividend zero times in 12 years of public history, with extraordinary ~17.5% long-term DGR. However, this is a cyclical story: the 2018–2022 peak is clearly past, and 2025's +4.6% raise signals normalization. Dividend safety at current yield (~3.5% on 2025 price) is strong given the low payout ratio (~70% AFFO) and investment-grade balance sheet. The short public history and market concentration are the main structural risks — REXR has not been through a meaningful recession cycle yet. Expect 3–6% DGR over the next 5 years, with potential upside if the rate environment normalizes.
