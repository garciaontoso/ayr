# PG — The Procter & Gamble Company
## Historical Dividend Evolution (2000–2025)

**Status:** Dividend King (69 consecutive annual increases as of April 2025)
**Founded:** 1837 · **First dividend:** 1890 (paid dividends for 135 consecutive years)
**First annual increase:** 1956
**Sector:** Consumer Staples / Household & Personal Care
**Report date:** 2026-04-17

## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000: The Durk Jager Disaster
In January 2000, CEO Durk Jager (appointed 1999) was pushed out after only 17 months. P&G had missed three consecutive quarters of earnings guidance, and the stock crashed 50%+ in March 2000 (the infamous one-day 31% drop). Jager's aggressive reorganization and M&A strategy (failed bids for Warner-Lambert and American Home Products) alienated both employees and Wall Street. **A.G. Lafley was named CEO in June 2000.** Despite the turbulence, **PG still raised the dividend by 12.3%** — demonstrating the cultural priority of the streak even during the worst management crisis in decades.

### 2001–2009: The Lafley Era — Dividend Golden Age
Lafley's tenure (2000–2009) is widely considered one of the best-managed CEO periods in S&P 500 history:
- Focus on core categories (health, beauty, home care)
- Divested food business (Folgers coffee → Smucker 2008 spin-off)
- Acquired Gillette (October 2005, $57B — PG's largest ever)
- Revenue grew from $38B (2000) to $83B (2009)
- **Annual dividend increases averaged 10.5% — every single year.**

The 2005 Gillette acquisition was transformative. Funded primarily with stock (and limited cash), it added men's grooming, batteries (Duracell), and small appliances (Braun) — while preserving the dividend flexibility. Bob McDonald (then head of North America) noted the deal was structured specifically to avoid any dividend disruption.

**2006: 50th Consecutive Annual Increase — Dividend King.** PG crossed the 50-year threshold and was one of the small group of companies inducted into the Dividend Kings cohort.

### 2008–2009: GFC Response — Double-Digit Raises Continued
2008 DPS growth: +13.3%. 2009 DPS growth: +13.1%. **PG accelerated dividend growth during the Global Financial Crisis** — a signal of management confidence that was, in retrospect, fully justified. Free cash flow actually grew in 2009 ($14.9B vs $12.5B in 2008) because commodity costs collapsed (oil, pulp, fragrances).

**Why PG was resistant:** household and personal care consumption is among the most income-inelastic in the consumer world. Detergent, shampoo, and razors don't get deferred in a recession. Unit sales declined <2% in the worst of 2009.

### 2010–2013: Bob McDonald Era — Execution Drift
McDonald became CEO in 2009. His tenure was mediocre: over-expansion into emerging markets (Venezuela FX, Ukraine, Russia), a too-broad product portfolio (~180 brands at peak), and persistent market-share losses in core categories. **Dividend growth decelerated from ~13% (Lafley) to ~7-10% (McDonald) as EPS stagnated.**

### 2013: Lafley Returns
In May 2013, the board forced McDonald out and brought Lafley back as interim CEO — a rare second-act. Lafley's mandate: simplify the portfolio, shed non-core brands.

### 2014–2016: The Massive Divestiture — 100 Brands Exit
Under Lafley's second term and then successor David Taylor (CEO from 2015), PG divested approximately 100 brands over 2014–2016, including:
- **Duracell battery business** (→ Berkshire Hathaway, 2016 for $4.7B)
- **Beauty brands** (→ Coty, 2016 for $12.5B — 43 brands including Clairol, Wella, CoverGirl, Max Factor)
- Pet food (Iams, Eukanuba → Mars, 2014 for $2.9B)
- Various smaller divestitures

**Revenue shrank from $83B (FY2014) to $65B (FY2016).** Operating margin expanded from ~18% to ~21% as low-margin brands exited.

**Critical dividend policy shift:** The FY2016 dividend raise was **only +2.7% — the smallest PG raise in decades.** This reflected management's desire to preserve cash during the disruptive portfolio reset, not an EPS concern. Similarly, FY2017–2019 raises were only +3.0%, +3.6%, +4.2%. **This 4-year period of sub-5% raises is the weakest dividend growth era in PG's Dividend King history.** Some analysts quietly worried the streak might be breakable; it was not.

### 2017: Nelson Peltz / Trian Proxy Fight
In 2017, activist Nelson Peltz launched the most expensive proxy contest in US corporate history ($60M+) against PG, arguing for three things: a decentralized operating model, cost discipline, and better innovation. He narrowly won a board seat (by ~0.0016% of votes in the closest proxy fight on record). PG ultimately adopted many of Peltz's recommendations — decentralization, category-level P&L accountability. **This re-energized dividend growth starting in FY2020.**

### 2020: COVID-19 Response — Household Staples Boom
Unlike discretionary retailers, PG *thrived* in COVID: panic buying of Charmin toilet paper, Bounty paper towels, Tide detergent drove revenue up 5% in FY2020 and 7% in FY2021. **Dividend raise of +5.4%** — meaningfully accelerating from the 3% era of 2016–2019.

### 2021–2023: Pricing Power Returns, Then Inflation Pass-Through
From 2021 through mid-2023, PG raised prices ~10% cumulatively. FY2022 dividend raise of +7.5% was the highest since 2013. **Operating margin expanded 200+ bps as pricing outpaced cost inflation.**

### 2024–2025: Volume Elasticity Reset, Deceleration
Post-inflation pricing created elasticity headwinds. Volume declined 1–3% in most FY2024 quarters. **Dividend growth moderated to +4.6% (FY2024) and +4.1% (FY2025)** — reflecting balance between shareholder returns and reinvestment in innovation (Tide evo tablets, Bounty smart, Pampers Pure).

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~10.5% CAGR (Lafley era — the golden decade)
- **2010–2015:** ~8% CAGR (McDonald era — deceleration)
- **2015–2020:** ~4% CAGR (Portfolio reset + Peltz disruption — the weakest half-decade)
- **2020–2025:** ~5.4% CAGR (Post-COVID normalization)

Long-term **~7.6% CAGR from 2000–2025** outpaces KO (~4.5%) meaningfully despite PG starting with a higher 2000 yield. PG's margin expansion (via divestitures) offset the absolute lower revenue base.

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
S&P 500 dividend growth averaged ~6% over 2000–2025. **PG outpaced by ~1.6 points per year** despite the 2015–2019 dividend austerity period.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought PG at year-end 2005 (~$57.86, post 2004 split) with the then-forward $1.15 dividend had a starting yield of ~2.0%.
- 2010 YOC: 1.800 / 57.86 = **3.1%**
- 2015 YOC: 2.590 / 57.86 = **4.5%**
- 2020 YOC: 3.120 / 57.86 = **5.4%**
- 2025 YOC: 4.050 / 57.86 = **7.0%**

YOC trajectory is slower than KO or PEP because PG started with higher absolute yield (~2.0% vs KO's ~2.8% and PEP's ~2.0%). The dividend growth rate over 20 years has been respectable but not spectacular.

### Buyback vs dividend mix
PG is famously one of the most aggressive buyback companies in the staples sector:
- Cumulative dividends 2010–2025: ~$85B
- Cumulative buybacks 2010–2025: ~$110B
- Ratio: buybacks are ~1.3x dividends

Share count declined from ~2.89B (FY2010) to ~2.36B (FY2025) — a ~18% cumulative reduction. This is a much heavier buyback posture than KO or PEP, and it's partially why DPS growth (~7.6%) outpaces underlying revenue growth (~2–3%).

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made PG a reliable dividend grower
1. **Category leadership across dozens of essential household products.** Tide (laundry), Pampers (diapers), Charmin (bathroom tissue), Gillette (shaving), Head & Shoulders (shampoo), Dawn (dish soap) — each is the #1 or #2 brand in its category. Category leadership drives pricing power, shelf space, and co-marketing with retailers.
2. **Direct-to-retail relationships.** PG's Cincinnati Walmart "house" (the dedicated team embedded at Bentonville HQ) is emblematic of deep retailer integration. This relationship cannot be replicated by private-label or startup brands.
3. **Innovation cadence.** PG spends ~$2B/year on R&D — among the highest in consumer staples. Roughly every 3–5 years, each category gets a meaningful new product (Tide Pods 2012, Always Infinity 2008, Pampers Pure 2018) that refreshes pricing and margins.
4. **Capital allocation discipline.** PG has historically been a "dividend + buyback" returner, with M&A as a tertiary priority. Major acquisitions (Gillette 2005, Clairol 2001, Wella 2003, Merck consumer health 2018) were primarily stock-funded, preserving dividend capacity.
5. **Resilience to FX and EM turbulence.** PG has taken major FX hits (Venezuela 2015, Russia 2022, Egypt 2023) but has never allowed them to force a dividend cut. The company books FX translation impacts below the line and manages reported vs organic metrics carefully.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2000 Jager crisis.** EPS missed three consecutive quarters; stock fell 50%. Dividend was never at risk because Lafley arrived quickly.
- **2012–2013 McDonald drift.** Organic growth stalled at 2%; market-share losses mounted. Board replaced him promptly.
- **2016–2019 weak dividend growth (3–4%).** The only period in PG's modern history when the raise didn't outpace inflation. If this had persisted another 3–4 years, the company's standing as a "dividend growth" name would have been questioned.
- **2017 Peltz proxy fight.** The closest proxy contest in history (won by ~0.0016%). If Peltz had lost, PG might have kept a less-decentralized structure; some analysts argue the post-Peltz operating model is strictly better.
- **2022 Russia exit.** PG suspended new investment and wound down operations (~3% of revenue at the time). Managed without drama.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Volume reset.** After 3+ years of 8–12% cumulative pricing, unit volumes are soft. If recovery stalls (no return to +1–2% volume growth by FY2026), dividend growth could moderate to 3–4%.
- **Private-label threat.** In some categories (diapers, laundry, dishes), private-label market share has grown 2–4 points in 2023–2024. PG must accelerate innovation or accept modest share loss.
- **China.** PG China is ~8% of revenue and has been under pressure from local brands (Proya, Pechoin). Recovery is not certain.
- **Peltz tenure ending (2027).** Peltz is unlikely to stand for re-election; post-Peltz, PG's board composition and strategic focus could evolve.
- **Emerging-market growth.** Long-term, Africa and South Asia are the biggest demographic tailwinds for consumer staples. PG has been investing selectively; this could be the next leg of dividend growth.

**Conclusion:** PG has cut the dividend zero times since the 1890s (135+ consecutive years of payment, 69 consecutive years of increases). The business is structurally higher-margin in 2025 than in 2005 due to the portfolio cleanup. The 2015–2019 "deceleration" period is the clearest historical illustration that PG will *slow* dividend growth long before it would ever cut — and that's the behavior we want from a Dividend King. Expect the 69-year streak to become a 75-year streak by 2032.
