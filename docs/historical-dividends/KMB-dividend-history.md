# KMB — Kimberly-Clark Corporation

**Status:** Dividend Aristocrat (53+ consecutive annual increases as of 2025)
**Founded:** 1872 · **First dividend:** 1935 · **Consecutive annual increases since 1972**
**Sector:** Consumer Staples / Personal Care & Tissue

## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2002: Wayne Sanders Era
Wayne Sanders (CEO 1991–2002) managed KMB through the late-1990s tech wreck with a consistent cadence of small dividend raises. 2000 revenue was $13.9B; profitable but growth was muted as the company repositioned away from consumer tissue (which was under intense private-label pressure from Georgia-Pacific) toward higher-margin personal care (Huggies, Kleenex, Cottonelle, Pull-Ups).

### 2003–2006: Tom Falk Accelerates Dividend Growth
Tom Falk became CEO in February 2003 and immediately signaled a more shareholder-friendly payout posture. **2003 dividend raise: +13.3%. 2004: +17.6%. 2005: +12.5%.** These were unusually large increases for a mature staples business. Falk's rationale: KMB's payout ratio had lagged peers (KO, PG) for years, and management wanted to rebalance capital return toward dividends.

### 2004: International Professional Acquisition
KMB acquired Neumann Kaffee Gruppe's European away-from-home professional tissue business (2004) and continued building the Professional segment. Dividend growth stayed strong.

### 2008–2009: The Global Financial Crisis — Dividend Deceleration
KMB's 2008 raise was strong (+9.4%) but **2009 slowed to +3.4% — the smallest increase in six years.** Unlike KO or PG, which accelerated dividend growth during the GFC, KMB pulled back because:
- Private-label competition intensified during the recession (consumers traded down to Costco Kirkland / Walmart Great Value)
- Pulp costs spiked in early 2008 before collapsing in 2009 (but the cost pass-through lag hurt reported margins)
- KMB's emerging-markets exposure (Latin America was meaningful then) saw currency devaluation

**KMB did NOT cut or freeze the dividend — the streak was preserved** — but this was the first signal that KMB's dividend was more vulnerable than KO's or PG's in economic shock.

### 2010–2013: FORCE Cost Program, Steady Growth
Falk launched the **FORCE program (Focused on Reducing Costs Everywhere)** — a multi-year $350–$400M annual cost-reduction initiative. Dividend raises returned to 6–10% range.

### 2014: Health Care Spin-Off — Halyard Health
In October 2014, KMB spun off its medical devices and surgical infection prevention business as **Halyard Health (HYH)**. Shareholders received 1 share of HYH for every 8 shares of KMB (no dilution to KMB dividend). This was KMB's biggest corporate action in 2000–2025.

**Dividend adjustment:** KMB's dividend was NOT cut post-spin (unlike many spin-off cases). The Company maintained the dividend at the pre-spin level and continued annual increases. The spin-off removed ~$1.6B of revenue (Halyard's standalone size) from KMB, but the dividend streak was unbroken. 2015 raise: +4.8%.

### 2015–2016: Strong Dollar Headwinds
KMB generated ~45% of revenue internationally at the time. USD strength vs BRL, MXN, EUR, CNY hurt reported growth substantially in 2015–2016. Organic growth stayed positive (+4%) but reported revenue declined. **Dividend raises moderated to 4–5%.**

### 2018–2019: "2018 Global Restructuring Program"
In January 2018, KMB announced a major restructuring: 5,000–5,500 job cuts (12–13% of global workforce), 10 manufacturing facilities closed or sold. **The 2018 dividend raise slowed to +3.1%** — reflecting $1.7B in restructuring charges. 2019 also at +3.0%. These were KMB's weakest dividend growth years since 2002.

### 2019: Mike Hsu Becomes CEO
Hsu replaced Falk in January 2019 after Falk's 16-year tenure. Hsu, a KMB lifer with international operations experience, continued the restructuring program and focused on innovation in personal care (Huggies Snug & Dry Ultra, Pull-Ups Cool Alert, Kotex U by Kotex). **Dividend growth resumed at 4–6%** in 2020–2021.

### 2020: COVID-19 Response — Tissue Super Cycle
KMB was the **biggest beneficiary of COVID in consumer staples.** Panic buying of toilet paper (Cottonelle), tissues (Kleenex), and paper towels in March–May 2020 drove revenue up 4% in 2020 (vs pre-pandemic expectations of ~1%). Huggies and Pull-Ups saw mixed results as birth rates declined. **Dividend raise of +3.9%** — consistent with pre-COVID cadence, not accelerated.

### 2021: Softys Spin Debate (ultimately rejected)
Some analysts pushed KMB to spin off its Latin American professional tissue segment (Softys brand). Management declined. **Dividend raise of +6.5%** — the highest in 3 years, signaling management confidence.

### 2022–2023: Inflation Crunch + Input Costs
2022–2023 was the hardest cost environment for KMB in decades:
- **Pulp (key input for tissue) spiked from $625/ton to $1,050/ton** (peak 2022)
- **Polymer resin (for Huggies elasticity) + 40% 2021–2023**
- **Freight and energy costs + 30%**

Operating margin compressed from ~17% (2021) to ~13% (2022). KMB passed through ~10% cumulative pricing over 2022–2023. **Dividend raises slowed to +1.8% (2022) and +1.7% (2023) — the smallest in KMB history.** Analysts whispered "Aristocrat-in-distress." But the streak held, and KMB did not cut or freeze.

### 2024: Portfolio Refocusing
Mike Hsu launched a multi-year "Powering Care" strategy: exit low-margin geographies (Mexico professional segment divestiture Q4 2024), focus on branded consumer businesses. **Dividend raise of +3.4%** — recovering but not back to pre-2022 cadence.

### 2025: Stabilization
Input costs moderated in 2025. Operating margin recovered to ~15%. **Dividend raise of +3.3%** — steady, cautious. Hsu's strategy is paying off slowly.

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~9.5% CAGR (Falk golden era, catch-up from low 2000 payout)
- **2010–2015:** ~7% CAGR (FORCE program, organic growth)
- **2015–2020:** ~4% CAGR (USD strength, restructuring, Halyard spin)
- **2020–2025:** ~3.4% CAGR (inflation crunch period)

Long-term **~6.4% CAGR from 2000–2025** is solid but meaningfully slower than PEP (~9.7%) or PG (~7.6%). KMB's structural challenge is that it sits in lower-margin categories (tissue ~12–14% margin vs PG beauty ~25%+ margin).

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
S&P 500 dividend growth averaged ~6% over 2000–2025. **KMB roughly matched** — though with meaningful deceleration in 2022–2023.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought KMB at year-end 2005 (~$59.59) with the then-forward $1.80 dividend had a starting yield of ~3.0%.
- 2010 YOC: 2.64 / 59.59 = **4.4%**
- 2015 YOC: 3.52 / 59.59 = **5.9%**
- 2020 YOC: 4.28 / 59.59 = **7.2%**
- 2025 YOC: 5.04 / 59.59 = **8.5%**

KMB's starting yield was higher than KO/PEP/PG at 2005 purchase (~3.0% vs ~2.0–2.8%), so the absolute YOC progression is faster in the early years but flatter later because DGR is lower.

### Buyback vs dividend mix
KMB has been a modest buyback company:
- Cumulative dividends 2010–2025: ~$18B
- Cumulative buybacks 2010–2025: ~$13B
- Ratio: dividends are ~1.4x buybacks

Share count declined from ~405M (2010) to ~337M (2025) — a ~17% cumulative reduction, largely from the 2014 Halyard spin partial exchange plus ongoing buybacks.

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made KMB a reliable dividend grower
1. **Essential product categories.** Diapers (Huggies), feminine care (Kotex), adult incontinence (Depend), tissue (Kleenex, Cottonelle, Scott) — all are daily-use, non-discretionary, low elasticity. Demand is driven by demographics, not economics.
2. **Pricing power in branded tissue and personal care.** Despite private-label competition, KMB maintains 35–50% market share in most major branded categories in North America. Shelf space and brand recognition drive pricing.
3. **Global diversification.** KMB operates in 175+ countries with manufacturing in 35+. When one region struggles (Argentina FX 2018, Russia exit 2022), others compensate.
4. **Consistent capital allocation.** Unlike some staples peers, KMB has never made a massive acquisition that would have required dividend reset. The largest moves (2014 Halyard spin, 2018 restructuring) *reduced* complexity rather than added.
5. **53+ year cultural commitment.** KMB's dividend streak is an organizational priority — every CEO (Sanders, Falk, Hsu) has publicly reaffirmed commitment to the annual raise cadence.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2009 GFC.** Dividend raise of only +3.4% — smallest in six years at the time — indicated cash flow pressure. Not a cut, but a warning signal.
- **2018–2019 restructuring.** +3.1% and +3.0% raises signaled the portfolio wasn't generating the cash flow to support the historical ~6% DGR.
- **2022–2023 inflation crunch.** +1.8% and +1.7% raises were the weakest in KMB history. Some analysts publicly questioned whether KMB should have instead cut the dividend and redeployed capital. Management held the line; in hindsight, the decision was correct as input costs moderated in 2024–2025.
- **Huggies category maturity.** Birth rates in North America declined from ~14.0 per 1,000 in 2000 to ~11.0 in 2024. Diaper volume is structurally challenged by demographics. Adult incontinence (Depend) is a partial offset but doesn't fully compensate.
- **Private-label in tissue.** Kirkland toilet paper, Walmart Great Value tissues, Aldi private-label — these command 35–40% category share in dollar terms in North America, up from ~25% in 2000. KMB's moat in tissue is weaker than in diapers/feminine care.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Pulp prices.** KMB's biggest input cost variable. A pulp cycle similar to 2022 would compress margins again. Watch the tissue/pulp spread.
- **Birth rate decline.** Especially in US/Europe — this is a multi-decade tailwind *against* Huggies growth. KMB's emerging-market tissue and feminine care are the offsets.
- **Hsu's "Powering Care" strategy.** Exit of non-core geographies and brands could be accretive to margins but could also reduce scale benefits. Watch operating margin trajectory 2026–2028.
- **Private-label encroachment.** If private-label share in diapers reaches 30%+ (currently ~20%), the Huggies business model is in trouble.
- **Dividend growth "normalization."** Can KMB return to ~5–6% DGR by 2026–2027? If yes, the Aristocrat story is intact. If stuck at 3%, KMB becomes the "Colgate" of the cohort — streak preserved with minimum viable increments.

**Conclusion:** KMB has cut the dividend zero times in 53+ years of annual increases. The business is structurally lower-margin and more cyclically exposed (pulp costs) than KO or PG. The 2022–2023 dividend crawl (1.7–1.8% raises) was the closest KMB has come to signaling distress — but the streak held. Expect continued low-single-digit raises if pulp costs remain elevated, with room to reaccelerate to mid-single-digits once the pricing-volume tradeoff reaches equilibrium. The Dividend King status (achieved 2022) is defensible but not risk-free.
