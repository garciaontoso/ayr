## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2007: Arthur Weinbach Era — Consistent Growth
Arthur Weinbach was CEO 1998–2006. Under his leadership, ADP was a classic growth compounder:
- Revenue grew from $6.0B (2000) to $9.0B (2007)
- Operating margin expanded from 15% to 20%
- **Dividend growth of 8–20% per year consistently**

ADP's business model — employer payroll and HR services — is extraordinarily sticky. Clients rarely switch providers once integrated.

### 2007: Broadridge Financial Services Spin-Off
March 2007: ADP spun off its Brokerage Services Group as **Broadridge Financial Solutions (BR)**, a provider of investor communications and securities processing services. Shareholders received 1 BR share for every 4 ADP shares. **The ADP dividend was NOT cut — it was held at $0.90 quarterly and then raised to $0.23 (adjusted for timing).** Total shareholder income (ADP + BR combined) increased post-spin.

### 2008–2009: The Global Financial Crisis — ADP Accelerated Dividend
During the GFC, employment in the US fell 8.7M from peak to trough. ADP's business is directly tied to employment — but unlike commodity services, HR administration is a SaaS-like annuity. **Despite the recession, ADP raised dividend +27% in 2008 and +18% in 2009.**

**Why:**
- Clients rarely churn during recessions
- ADP's per-employee fees are small ($10-100/month per worker) and embedded in operations
- Operating margin held above 20% through the crisis
- Free cash flow remained strong

Carlos Rodriguez became CEO in November 2011 (having been CFO 2010–2011). Under Rodriguez:
- Focus on higher-margin SaaS offerings (RUN for small business)
- Expansion into professional employer organization (PEO) services
- Capital allocation discipline

### 2014: CDK Global Spin-Off
September 2014: ADP completed the spin-off of its **Dealer Services Group** (auto retail software) as **CDK Global (CDK)**. Shareholders received 1 CDK share per 1 ADP share.

**Key facts:**
- CDK had ~$2B revenue, ~20% of ADP's total
- The spin focused ADP on HR services (larger TAM, stickier clients)
- ADP's dividend was not cut; the quarterly rate was held at $0.485 and calendar adjustments maintained the streak
- **2015 FY (post-spin) dividend growth was moderated to +2.1%** as ADP re-based its payout ratio

### 2015–2019: The Pershing Square Activist Campaign + Response
In August 2017, **Bill Ackman (Pershing Square) launched an activist campaign** against ADP, arguing:
- ADP had excessive operating costs (margins should be 35%+ vs actual 20%)
- Capital allocation was inefficient
- Management was entrenched

Ackman lost a proxy contest in November 2017 but continued pressuring ADP publicly. Rodriguez responded by accelerating margin expansion — operating margin grew from 21% (2017) to 26% (2020). **Dividend growth reaccelerated to +7.7% (2017), +12.5% (2018), +21.4% (2019).**

### 2020: COVID-19 Response
US employment crashed in March–April 2020 (–21.6M jobs in 2 months). ADP's per-employee fees temporarily declined as clients reduced workforce counts. **But ADP raised the dividend +15.7% in FY 2020** — reflecting management's confidence in the SaaS-like annuity model.

Employment recovery was rapid (V-shaped). By FY2021, employment was already near pre-COVID levels. ADP's revenue recovered fully.

### 2021–2024: Maria Black Era
Maria Black became CEO in January 2023 (replacing Rodriguez). Previously President since 2019. Continues margin expansion and SaaS focus. **Dividend growth remained strong at 7–16% per year.**

### 2024: 50-Year Milestone — Dividend King
FY2024 (ending June 2024): ADP declared its 50th consecutive annual dividend increase ($1.25 → $1.40 quarterly), becoming a formal **Dividend King**.

### 2025: Continued Growth
FY2025 dividend raise of +10.2% to $1.54 quarterly ($6.16 annualized). Operating margin reached 27%+. Revenue exceeded $20B for the first time.

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~15% CAGR (including GFC raises, Broadridge spin effect)
- **2010–2015:** ~7% CAGR (post-CDK spin normalization)
- **2015–2020:** ~12% CAGR (Ackman-influenced margin expansion)
- **2020–2025:** ~11% CAGR (continued compounding)

Long-term **~11.9% CAGR from 2000–2025** is one of the highest in the S&P 500 Dividend King cohort — higher than KO (~4.5%), PG (~7.6%), PEP (~9.7%).

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
ADP has outpaced the S&P 500 dividend growth by ~6 points annually over 2000–2025 — one of the strongest relative performances of any dividend-growth stock.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought ADP at year-end 2005 (~$44.96) with the forward $0.62 dividend had a starting yield of ~1.4%.
- 2010 YOC: 1.39 / 44.96 = **3.1%**
- 2015 YOC: 1.98 / 44.96 = **4.4%**
- 2020 YOC: 3.54 / 44.96 = **7.9%**
- 2025 YOC: 5.84 / 44.96 = **13.0%**

A 2005 ADP buyer now earns 13% on cost — among the highest YOCs in this cohort, driven by the strong DGR. Note: this holder would also have received CDK and Broadridge shares from spins, adding material additional income.

### Buyback vs dividend mix
ADP is a heavy buyback company:
- Cumulative dividends 2010–2025: ~$20B
- Cumulative buybacks 2010–2025: ~$25B
- Share count reduced from ~510M (2010) to ~410M (2025) — ~20% reduction

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made ADP a reliable dividend grower
1. **SaaS-like business model.** Payroll and HR services are embedded in clients' operations. Once integrated, switching costs are enormous (tax reporting, employee data, system integration). Client retention >95%.
2. **Scale advantages.** ADP processes 1 in 6 US paychecks. Economies of scale in compliance, tax reporting, and systems investment create cost advantages smaller competitors can't match.
3. **High cash conversion.** 95%+ of net income converts to free cash flow. No capital-intensive factories, limited physical assets, low working capital requirements.
4. **No debt, net cash.** ADP historically maintains net cash position — zero financial leverage. Dividend safety is maximum possible.
5. **Float.** ADP holds client payroll funds briefly in float accounts, generating several hundred million annually in interest income. Rising rates are a tailwind.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2017 Ackman proxy fight.** Could have disrupted management and strategy. ADP won decisively.
- **2020 COVID employment shock.** Per-employee fees declined temporarily. Revenue grew through despite headcount declines as clients used more ADP services.
- **Competitive threats.** Workday, Paychex, Gusto, Rippling all compete in HR tech. ADP's enterprise scale moat holds up, but mid-market is competitive.
- **AI disruption.** Generative AI could automate some ADP services (basic payroll). Management is investing heavily in AI but upside-downside is uncertain.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Operating margin expansion.** Can ADP reach 30%+ operating margin? Ackman argued for 35%. Current path is 27–29%.
- **Revenue growth.** Currently mid-single-digits. Acceleration to high-single-digits would support continued 10%+ DGR.
- **Client win rates.** ADP competing against Workday in enterprise, Gusto/Rippling in SMB. Watch net new bookings.
- **Float income.** Tailwind at current rates (~5%). Compression if rates fall below 3%.
- **Dividend growth sustainability.** The current 10%+ DGR is hard to sustain long-term without accelerating revenue. Management may moderate to 7–9% over the next decade.

**Conclusion:** ADP has cut the dividend zero times in 51 years of consecutive annual increases. The ~11.9% long-term DGR is among the best in the S&P 500 Dividend King cohort. The business model (HR/payroll SaaS with enormous switching costs) is exceptionally durable. No freeze risk even in severe recessions, as demonstrated during the 2008 GFC (when ADP raised +27%) and 2020 COVID (when ADP raised +15.7%). The 50-year streak will become 60+ years by 2034 barring catastrophic disruption. This is one of the highest-quality dividend-growth stocks in the S&P 500 — high starting yield (~2.5%), high DGR (~10%+), minimal balance sheet risk.
