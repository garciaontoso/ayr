## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2003: Post-IPO Compounder, Tom Lewis Era
CEO Tom Lewis (1994–2009) built the original "Monthly Dividend Company" playbook: freestanding single-tenant retail properties, triple-net leases with 10–20 year terms and 1–2% annual rent escalators, funded with ~50% equity / 50% unsecured debt. **Early 2000s dividend growth of ~4–5% annually.** The portfolio was under ~1,000 properties in 2000, growing to ~2,300 by 2003.

### 2008–2009: The Global Financial Crisis — Net-Lease Resilience Proven
The GFC was the stress test that made O's reputation. Despite broad commercial real estate distress:
- **Rent collection stayed above 97% throughout 2008–2009**
- Occupancy dipped slightly (98.5% → 96.9%) but recovered within 18 months
- O never drew on its revolver credit facility
- **2008 dividend raise: +6.9%. 2009: +5.6%.** Higher than many S&P 500 companies during the GFC.

**Why O was resistant:** tenants like convenience stores, dollar stores, drug stores, quick-service restaurants operated through the recession. Freestanding properties in middle-market retail corridors had demand from multiple potential tenants if any one failed. The NNN structure meant tenants paid taxes, insurance, and maintenance, insulating O from operating cost inflation.

### 2009: John Case Era Begins (Acquisitions Head)
John Case joined in 2009 as head of acquisitions. He would become COO in 2013, CEO in 2015. His era coincides with major scale expansion.

### 2010–2012: Slow Growth Period
Post-GFC, O's dividend growth moderated to ~1% — the lowest in its history. This reflected a deliberate posture: tenant underwriting tightened, occupancy was restored, cash was conserved. **Dividend raises of +0.9%, +1.0%, +1.8% in 2010–2012.** Some analysts questioned whether O was losing its growth edge.

### 2013: American Realty Capital Trust (ARCT) Acquisition
In January 2013, O acquired American Realty Capital Trust for $2.95B in stock — the company's first transformative M&A. The deal added 500+ properties and modestly expanded geographic diversification. **The 2013 dividend annual figure jumped 23% year-over-year due to integration timing** (ARCT shareholders received O's dividend timing from Q2 2013, versus a half-year under ARCT before).

### 2015: Sumit Roy Becomes CEO
Roy (who joined 2013 as head of acquisitions) succeeded John Case as CEO in October 2015. Roy's era is marked by accelerating scale: European expansion (first UK deal 2019, continental Europe 2022), multi-billion dollar M&A (VEREIT 2021, Spirit 2024), and diversification beyond US retail.

### 2019: First UK Acquisition
O acquired a portfolio of Sainsbury's grocery stores in the UK (£452M, 2019) — its first international deal. This opened the European expansion runway.

### 2020: COVID-19 Response
COVID hit retail real estate broadly, but net-lease structures held up:
- Rent collection dipped to ~85% in April–June 2020 (theaters, gyms, casual dining)
- Recovered to 94% by Q3 and >97% by Q4
- O deferred but did not forgive rent for most tenants; deferrals were repaid over 12–24 months
- **2020 dividend raise: +2.7% (51st consecutive raise)** — smaller than recent years but unbroken

**Tenant casualties:** theaters (Regal was a challenging tenant), fitness (LA Fitness, 24 Hour Fitness), restaurants (TGIF Friday's). Combined impact <3% of rent.

### 2021: VEREIT Merger — Transformative Scale Step
November 2021: O closed the all-stock merger with VEREIT, valued at $11B. Post-close, O's portfolio grew from ~7,000 to ~11,000 properties, and enterprise value crossed $50B. **Dividend raise of +1.4% in 2021 reflected merger integration phase.** Some analysts worried VEREIT's lower-quality portfolio (more restaurant tenants, higher vacancy risk) would dilute O's dividend safety.

### 2022: Standalone Office Business Spin-Off — Orion Office REIT
Just before the VEREIT close, O spun off the office portion of VEREIT's portfolio as **Orion Office REIT (ONL)** — distributed to shareholders at a 1:10 ratio. This was a strategic move to keep O focused on retail/industrial NNN rather than inherit VEREIT's office book.

### 2022–2023: Reaccelerated Raises After Merger Digestion
Post-VEREIT, dividend growth resumed: **+4.8% in 2022, +3.0% in 2023.** Property count grew to 13,400+ by end-2023.

### 2024: Spirit Realty Acquisition
January 2024: O closed the all-stock acquisition of Spirit Realty Capital for ~$9.3B. Added ~2,000 properties. **2024 raise of +2.2%** reflected another integration phase.

### 2025: Minimum Viable Increments Era
Five dividend increases in 2025, each in the range of $0.0005–$0.0080 per month:
- February 2025: $0.2640 → $0.2680 (+1.5% — the "annual" raise)
- April 2025: +$0.0005 (to $0.2685)
- July 2025: +$0.0005 (to $0.2690)
- October 2025: +$0.0005 (to $0.2695)
- Early 2026: +$0.0005 (to $0.2700)

**Full-year 2025 DPS of $3.217 = +2.9% YoY.** Critics note that $0.0005 per month is "ceremonial" — enough to preserve the streak but not meaningful income growth.

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~4.8% CAGR (disciplined early growth)
- **2010–2015:** ~2.8% CAGR (post-GFC conservatism + ARCT digestion)
- **2015–2020:** ~3.8% CAGR (Roy era scaling)
- **2020–2025:** ~2.8% CAGR (COVID, VEREIT, Spirit digestion)

Long-term **~4.5% CAGR from 2000–2025** — modest for a growth REIT but stable. O's growth is increasingly size-limited: with a $50B+ enterprise value and 15,000+ properties, each incremental $1B of acquisitions adds <2% to AFFO per share.

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
S&P 500 dividend growth averaged ~6% over 2000–2025. **O has underperformed by ~1.5 points annually** — reflecting REIT structural constraints (90% taxable income distribution requirement means less retained cash for reinvestment vs non-REITs).

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought O at year-end 2005 (~$22.19) with the then-forward $1.335 dividend had a starting yield of ~6.0% — far higher than the consumer staples names.
- 2010 YOC: 1.725 / 22.19 = **7.8%**
- 2015 YOC: 2.271 / 22.19 = **10.2%**
- 2020 YOC: 2.794 / 22.19 = **12.6%**
- 2025 YOC: 3.217 / 22.19 = **14.5%**

A 2005 O buyer now earns 14.5% cash yield on cost — higher than any of the consumer staples names. This is the REIT advantage: start with high yield, compound modestly, end with very high YOC.

### Equity issuance vs reinvestment
Unlike KO/PEP/PG which reduce share count via buybacks, **O has grown share count from ~55M (2000) to ~900M (2025)** — 16.4x more shares — through consistent ATM equity issuance and merger stock swaps (ARCT, VEREIT, Spirit). AFFO per share growth (~4% per year) has been modest; total AFFO dollars have grown ~15% per year. **O is a "growth-by-dilution" REIT, not a buyback compounder.**

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made O a reliable dividend grower
1. **Triple-net lease structure.** Tenants pay taxes, insurance, and maintenance. O's cost base is minimal; rent flows almost directly to cash flow. 10–20 year lease terms with 1–2% escalators create a near-bond-like income stream.
2. **Tenant diversification.** 1,500+ tenants, top tenant <4% of rent. No single bankruptcy can impair O's dividend.
3. **Industry diversification.** Convenience stores (Dollar General, 7-Eleven), dollar stores (Dollar Tree), grocery (Walmart, Kroger), drug (CVS, Walgreens), QSR (Taco Bell, McDonald's), fitness (LA Fitness), theaters (Regal), auto (AutoZone), home improvement (Home Depot). Recession-resistant mix.
4. **Investment-grade balance sheet.** A3/A- credit rating, 93% fixed-rate debt, weighted-average cost of ~3.9%, average maturity ~6+ years. O can weather any refinancing cycle without dividend pressure.
5. **Cultural commitment to monthly payment.** The "Monthly Dividend Company" brand requires the monthly raise cadence. Management has stated unambiguously that the dividend is priority #1 in capital allocation.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2010–2012 GFC hangover slow growth.** 1% raises made some analysts question the growth profile. In hindsight, conservatism was correct.
- **2013 ARCT integration.** A newly public (then delisted) REIT had governance concerns; O conducted extensive due diligence. Integration was smooth.
- **2020 COVID tenant distress.** Theaters, gyms, and some restaurants struggled. Rent collection dipped but recovered. Dividend never at risk.
- **2021 VEREIT merger criticism.** Analysts worried about lower-quality VEREIT portfolio (higher restaurant mix, some sub-IG tenants). O managed the Orion spin to exit VEREIT's office book; retail/industrial integration went well.
- **2024 Spirit merger + rate-hike pressure.** Higher interest rates compressed cap rate spreads, making new acquisitions accretive only at the margin. This is the structural challenge facing O today.
- **2025 $0.0005 raises = ceiling signal.** The tiny increments signal management is operating at the edge of organic AFFO per share growth. Either AFFO growth reaccelerates or dividend growth stays at ~2–3% indefinitely.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Interest rate trajectory.** O's cost of debt averages ~3.9% but new issuance is at 5.5–6%. If rates stay elevated, O must acquire at higher cap rates or accept spread compression.
- **AFFO per share growth.** Target ~4–5% per year would support ~4–5% dividend growth. Current ~2% growth is the warning signal.
- **European expansion success.** The UK and continental European portfolio is smaller but higher-cap-rate. Successful scaling here could be the next growth leg.
- **Tenant credit quality post-recession.** If a recession arrives in 2026–2027, watch tenant concentration and bankruptcy rates carefully.
- **M&A pause?** Roy has hinted the VEREIT-Spirit era of big M&A is largely done; O may focus on smaller bolt-ons and organic growth. If so, 3% DGR may be the long-term run-rate.

**Conclusion:** O has not cut the dividend since 1994 (31 consecutive annual increases, 133 cumulative monthly/quarterly raises). The current 2–3% DGR is a deceleration from the 5% historical average, reflecting both size constraints and a difficult rate environment. Dividend safety is strong (AFFO payout ratio ~75%, investment-grade balance sheet), but growth will likely remain in the 2–4% range for the foreseeable future. This is a "pay-me-now" dividend vehicle — high starting yield, modest growth, reliable monthly income.
