## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2003: The Early Net-Lease Era
NNN was a small-cap retail-focused REIT in 2000 (~$500M market cap, ~300 properties). The focus was single-tenant freestanding retail with 15–20 year NNN leases. **Dividend raises were modest 1–4%** reflecting slow organic growth during the tech-bust era.

### 2004–2007: Craig Macnab Era, Quality Focus
Craig Macnab became CEO in February 2004 and immediately re-focused NNN on high-quality retail real estate. His strategic shift:
- Sold over 200 lower-quality properties (mostly shopping center pads and land parcels)
- Transitioned the portfolio to freestanding single-tenant only
- Emphasized IG-rated tenants (Walgreens, CVS, 7-Eleven)

**Dividend growth remained consistent at 3–5% through 2007.** Property count moved toward ~1,200 by 2007.

### 2008–2009: The Global Financial Crisis — Dividend Deceleration
Unlike O, which grew the dividend at 5–7% during the GFC, **NNN moderated growth to +2.4% (2009) and +1.3% (2010)** — the slowest raises in its history at that point. This reflected:
- Tenant credit concerns (especially in restaurant-heavy concentration)
- Higher cost of capital (NNN issued preferred stock and equity during 2009 to preserve liquidity)
- Property acquisition volume down 70% in 2009

**But the streak was unbroken.** NNN never missed a year, never froze, never cut.

### 2010–2014: Slow Rebuild, Aristocrat Milestone
NNN continued low-single-digit dividend growth (1–3%) through 2014, the year it achieved **25 consecutive annual increases — Dividend Aristocrat status.** Property count grew from ~1,000 (2009) to ~2,200 (2014). Occupancy stayed above 98%.

### 2017–2019: Dividend Growth Reacceleration
With the US retail real estate market stable and cap rates widening, NNN found accretive acquisitions. **Dividend growth accelerated to +5.7%, +5.1%, +5.1%** in 2017–2019. Properties reached ~3,000 by 2019.

### 2020: COVID-19 Response
NNN's tenant mix included some categories hit hard by COVID:
- Fitness (LA Fitness, 24 Hour Fitness) — distressed
- Theaters (Regal) — distressed
- Casual dining (Ruby Tuesday, Bar Louie) — some bankruptcies

**But net-lease economics held:** rent collection dipped to ~84% in April 2020, recovered to 96% by Q4 2020. **Dividend raise of +1.2% (31st consecutive year)** — the smallest increase in 5 years.

NNN's fitness and theater exposure was higher than O's, and some tenants eventually went through bankruptcy (24 Hour Fitness filed June 2020; Regal's parent Cineworld filed Sept 2022). NNN had to re-lease or sell affected properties, which created temporary vacancy/NOI disruption.

### 2021: Continued Recovery
**Dividend raise of +1.9%.** Rent collection normalized above 99%. Occupancy returned to pre-COVID ~99.0%.

### 2023: Renamed NNN REIT
In May 2023, the company rebranded from "National Retail Properties" to "NNN REIT, Inc." to emphasize the triple-net focus and modernize the brand. Ticker remained NNN. No operational or capital structure change.

### 2024–2025: Normalization at ~3% DGR
**Dividend raises of +2.7% (2024), +2.6% (2025).** With property count at ~3,500+ and a mature portfolio, NNN's dividend growth is tied closely to AFFO per share growth and cap rate spreads on new acquisitions.

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~3% CAGR (slow, conservative)
- **2010–2015:** ~2% CAGR (post-GFC recovery)
- **2015–2020:** ~4.2% CAGR (reacceleration)
- **2020–2025:** ~2.8% CAGR (COVID digestion + rate headwinds)

Long-term **~3.0% CAGR from 2000–2025** is the lowest among this 14-ticker comparison set. NNN is explicitly a "high-starting-yield / low-growth" vehicle, not a compounder.

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
S&P 500 dividend growth averaged ~6% over 2000–2025. **NNN underperformed by ~3 points annually** — consistent with REIT structural constraints plus NNN's smaller scale limiting capital allocation flexibility.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought NNN at year-end 2005 (~$20.96) with the then-forward $1.320 dividend had a starting yield of ~6.3%.
- 2010 YOC: 1.530 / 20.96 = **7.3%**
- 2015 YOC: 1.695 / 20.96 = **8.1%**
- 2020 YOC: 2.075 / 20.96 = **9.9%**
- 2025 YOC: 2.380 / 20.96 = **11.4%**

A 2005 NNN buyer now earns 11.4% on cost — slightly lower than O (14.5%) reflecting NNN's slower long-term DGR. Still very respectable for a conservative income vehicle.

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made NNN a reliable dividend grower
1. **Simple, focused strategy.** Freestanding single-tenant retail NNN leases. No forays into office, apartments, or complex structures. Simplicity = predictability.
2. **Conservative balance sheet.** Average net debt / EBITDAre of 5.0–5.5x through cycles. Below many peers (O runs 5.4x, WPC higher). Lower leverage = dividend safety margin.
3. **Tenant discipline.** NNN won't chase yield by underwriting sub-IG tenants. This costs them some growth but protects the streak.
4. **Management continuity.** Craig Macnab (CEO 2004–2017) and Steve Horn (CEO 2017–present) provided 20+ years of continuous strategy. Both have been public about protecting the streak.
5. **Retail net-lease subsector resilience.** Like O, NNN benefits from tenants who operate through recessions (QSRs, drug stores, convenience, auto services).

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2009–2012 GFC slow growth.** Four consecutive years of <2.5% dividend raises. NNN was smaller and more capital-constrained than O, so recovery took longer.
- **2014 (tenant issue).** Some theater/restaurant concentrations caused concerns; NNN gradually reduced exposure over 2015–2020.
- **2020 COVID.** Tenant distress in fitness/theaters. ~2% of rent was at risk. Dividend still raised.
- **Higher rates 2022–2024.** Cap rate spreads compressed. NNN is a cost-of-capital-sensitive business; if rates stay elevated, future AFFO per share growth could slow to 1–2%.

### What to watch looking forward
- **AFFO per share growth.** Must exceed dividend growth by 1–2 points to maintain payout ratio and dividend flexibility.
- **Tenant credit trends.** Restaurant concentration (~25% of rent) is the main vulnerability. Watch QSR vs casual dining performance.
- **Interest expense roll.** Average debt cost ~4% today; new debt issues at 5–6%. Drag on AFFO growth over 2025–2028.
- **M&A appetite.** NNN has been disciplined but could be pressured to consolidate or be acquired by a larger REIT (e.g., Agree Realty, O) if its own growth continues sub-3%.

**Conclusion:** NNN has cut the dividend zero times in 36 years of annual increases. It is the cleanest "high-starting-yield, low-but-consistent-growth" dividend vehicle in this cohort. At a ~5–6% current yield with ~3% DGR, total return expectations are ~8–9% per year — modest but very reliable. The 36-year streak will become a 40-year streak by 2029 at current cadence.
