## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2003: Tobacco Litigation Overhang
Philip Morris Companies (the predecessor to MO) faced existential legal risk:
- **1998 Master Settlement Agreement** with 46 state attorneys general required $206B in payments over 25 years from US tobacco industry
- Multiple class-action lawsuits and individual trials
- Stock price fell 75% from 1998 high to 2000 low

**Yet Philip Morris NEVER cut the dividend.** Dividend growth continued at 8–14% annually through the litigation uncertainty. This was the historical foundation for MO's later dividend-growth reputation.

In 2003, Philip Morris Companies restructured and renamed to "Altria Group" — signaling the parent's intent to diversify beyond tobacco (it owned Kraft Foods at the time).

### 2007: Kraft Foods Spin-Off
March 2007: Altria spun off Kraft Foods Inc. (KFT), distributing Kraft shares to Altria shareholders on a 0.692024:1 basis. Kraft would pay its own dividends post-spin. Altria's dividend was adjusted downward to reflect the now-smaller (post-Kraft) business — but total combined dividend (MO + KFT) was flat or higher for shareholders who held both.

### 2008: Philip Morris International Spin-Off
March 2008: Altria spun off Philip Morris International Inc. (PM), distributing PM shares to Altria shareholders on a 1:1 basis. PM took the international tobacco business; MO kept the US business. Post-spin:
- MO (US only): Marlboro USA, Skoal/Copenhagen (via UST 2009), Black & Mild, cigars, e-cigarettes (later)
- PM (international): All other Philip Morris brands globally

Again, MO's dividend declined post-spin, but PM began paying its own dividend. **Combined MO+PM dividend for a pre-spin holder was unchanged or higher.**

### 2009: UST Acquisition
January 2009: MO closed its $10.4B acquisition of UST Inc., adding Skoal, Copenhagen (the dominant US smokeless tobacco brands), and Ste. Michelle wine. **2009 MO dividend held flat at $1.32** (effectively a freeze) as the Company digested the UST acquisition, managed credit rating impacts, and began the post-spin dividend re-calibration.

### 2010–2017: The Ste. Michelle / Menthol Era
Under CEO Mike Szymanczyk (2008–2012) and Marty Barrington (2012–2018), MO focused on:
- Operational excellence in Marlboro USA (the single most profitable consumer brand in the world)
- Smokeless expansion via UST
- Resisting FDA menthol regulation (cigarettes ~25–30% menthol mix)
- Consistent 8% annual dividend raises

### 2018: Juul Investment — A Major Pivot
December 2018: MO announced a $12.8B investment for a 35% stake in Juul Labs, the dominant e-cigarette brand. Strategic rationale: MO needed exposure to next-generation tobacco products as combustible cigarette volumes declined. **MO funded the investment with debt and dividend raise slowed modestly (from 10% to 18% in 2018 but then decelerated to 9.3% in 2019).**

The Juul investment was a disaster. By 2020, Juul faced:
- FDA Marketing Denial Orders (2022)
- Class action lawsuits
- Massive write-downs: MO wrote down Juul from $12.8B to ~$1.4B, then to near-zero (2022)
- MO exchanged its Juul stake for a smaller non-competing position in 2023

### 2019: Cronos Investment
MO also invested $1.8B in Cronos Group (Canadian cannabis). This was another marginal return; stake later written down.

### 2020: COVID-19 Response
Cigarette volumes declined ~7% (vs typical 3–4%) in 2020 due to COVID-related factors. But MO's pricing power (average Marlboro price increases of 5–7% per year) offset volume declines. **Dividend raise of +3.7%** — smaller than historical 8% but the streak was preserved.

### 2021–2022: Post-Juul Pivot, Resource Allocation
MO shifted focus away from e-cigarettes toward on! nicotine pouches (acquired 2019) and traditional cigarettes. **Dividend raises of 4–5% per year** — stabilized but markedly lower than pre-Juul.

### 2023: NJOY Acquisition
March 2023: MO acquired NJOY Holdings for $2.75B (e-cigarette products with FDA pre-market authorization — a key regulatory advantage over Juul). This was MO's attempt to "get e-cigarettes right" after the Juul debacle. **Dividend raise of +4.3%.**

### 2024–2025: Normalization at 3–4% DGR
Consistent ~3.5–4% dividend raises. Operating cash flow resilient at $7B+ per year on ~$25B revenue. US cigarette volume decline continues at 4–6% per year, offset by pricing. **2025 annual DPS of $4.16.**

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade (MO-only)
- **2000–2010:** ~9% CAGR (including spin adjustments)
- **2010–2015:** ~8% CAGR (UST + Marlboro pricing)
- **2015–2020:** ~6% CAGR (Juul disruption + slower growth)
- **2020–2025:** ~4% CAGR (post-Juul, lower growth)

Clear DECELERATION pattern: MO's historical 8% DGR has moderated to ~4% over the past 5 years. This reflects structural cigarette volume decline and the failure of Juul to provide an e-cigarette growth alternative.

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500 (with spin adjustments)
Including PM and KFT spin dividends, MO's effective dividend growth for a pre-2007 holder has been approximately **10% CAGR** — materially outperforming S&P 500 (~6%). For MO-only holders (i.e., anyone who sold the PM/KFT spins or bought after 2008), dividend growth has been ~6.4% CAGR, approximately matching the S&P.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought Altria (then "MO" ticker) at year-end 2005 (~$74.79 pre-spin) with the $1.60 dividend had a starting yield of ~2.1%.

However, this holder would have received:
- PM shares in 2008 (worth ~$50 at spin)
- KFT shares in 2007 (worth ~$30 at spin)
- Continued MO dividends
- Continued PM and KFT dividends (if held)

**If all spin-offs were held:**
- Total 2025 dividend income from the 2005 MO purchase = MO dividends ($4.16) + PM dividends (~$5.35) + KFT/MDLZ dividends (~$1.70 combined post all spins) = approximately $11.21 per original MO share
- YOC on original $74.79 investment: **~15%**

**If only MO was held (spins sold):**
- 2025 MO-only DPS: $4.16 on $74.79 cost = **5.6% YOC**
- Plus capital from PM/KFT spin sales

### Buyback vs dividend mix
MO is a strong buyback company:
- Cumulative dividends 2010–2025: ~$55B
- Cumulative buybacks 2010–2025: ~$30B
- Share count reduced from ~2.1B (2010) to ~1.7B (2025) — 19% reduction

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made MO a reliable dividend grower
1. **Marlboro pricing power.** The single most profitable consumer brand in the world. Average US retail cigarette prices rose from ~$4.00/pack (2000) to ~$9.00/pack (2025) — 5–6% annual pricing despite volume declines.
2. **Essential product elasticity.** Nicotine is addictive; demand is highly inelastic. Volume declines are gradual (~3–4% per year), not cliff-like, allowing pricing to offset.
3. **Dividend culture.** Like KO and PG, MO's culture is built around the dividend. Philip Morris Companies / Altria has paid dividends for nearly 100 years (since 1928) without interruption.
4. **Asset-light operations.** Manufacturing is efficient; capital expenditure is modest (~$200M/year). Free cash flow approaches net income. Cash distribution to shareholders is the #1 priority.
5. **Litigation and regulatory risk management.** MO has faced existential legal threats (MSA 1998, FDA menthol ban proposals, thousands of lawsuits) but has never let them force a dividend cut. Legal reserves are managed below the operating line.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **1999–2002 tobacco litigation overhang.** Stock fell 75%. Dividend was NEVER cut — a defining test.
- **2008 PM spin-off complexity.** For a year, many dividend investors were confused whether MO had "cut" the dividend. Education took time. No actual cut occurred.
- **2009 UST digestion + freeze.** Dividend flat for one year — the closest MO has come to a formal freeze. It was a voluntary pause, not a distress signal.
- **2018–2022 Juul fiasco.** $12.8B acquisition written down to near-zero. Dividend growth decelerated from ~9% to ~4% as MO absorbed the loss. This was the worst capital allocation decision in MO history. Dividend survived.
- **FDA menthol proposal.** FDA proposed banning menthol cigarettes in 2022. 25–30% of US cigarette volume is menthol. A ban would materially pressure revenue. Status as of 2025: politically delayed, uncertain outcome.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Cigarette volume decline acceleration.** Current ~4% per year. If GLP-1 or other factors accelerate decline to 8%+, pricing power becomes inadequate.
- **FDA menthol ruling.** A binary risk with 20%+ revenue at stake.
- **Nicotine pouch growth (on!).** MO's on! brand competes with Zyn (Philip Morris) for US pouch leadership. Growth here is critical.
- **e-Cigarette authorization.** NJOY has FDA pre-market authorization — rare among e-cigarettes. Regulatory protection is a material asset.
- **Buyback sustainability.** MO's FCF supports ~$7B annual returns (dividends + buybacks). If volume decline accelerates, one of these levers must moderate.

**Conclusion:** MO has never cut its dividend over 56+ years of consecutive annual increases. The 2007 and 2008 apparent "decreases" were spin-off adjustments, not cuts. The current trajectory (4% DGR) is meaningfully slower than the historical 8% — reflecting Juul damage and structural cigarette decline. At current yield (~7–8%), MO is a "pay-me-now" dividend vehicle. For a long-horizon dividend investor, expect 3–5% DGR if management maintains discipline, with downside risk tied to FDA regulation and accelerated volume decline.
