## Section 2 — Notable Events Chronology

### 2000–2001: The Tech Bust — First Dividend Freeze
TROW is an asset manager; its revenue is highly correlated with AUM (assets under management), which in turn is correlated with equity market performance. The 2000–2002 tech bust slammed TROW's fundamentals:
- AUM declined from ~$180B (peak 1999) to ~$140B (trough 2002)
- Revenue declined 20%+
- Operating margin compressed from ~32% to ~25%

**TROW held the quarterly dividend flat at $0.085 for all of 2001** (full calendar year, 4 consecutive quarters with no raise). The Aristocrat streak was preserved on a fiscal-year-over-year basis (2000 and 2001 both totaled $0.34 annual), but this was effectively a FREEZE.

**Management's stated logic:** TROW is a high-compensation, high-operating-leverage business. During market downturns, it retains flexibility by pausing dividend growth rather than using debt. The 2001 freeze was preceded by a 21% raise in 2000 and followed by a 24% raise in 2003 — reflecting the "bank" approach: raise big in good times, pause in bad times.

### 2003–2007: Post-Bust Recovery, Dividend Super-Cycle
Equity markets recovered strongly 2003–2007. TROW's AUM grew from $140B to $400B. Revenue doubled. Operating margin expanded to 45%+. **Dividend growth reflected this:**
- 2003: +24%
- 2004: +22%
- 2005: +21%
- 2006: +12% (plus 2-for-1 split)
- 2007: +26%

Five consecutive years of 12–26% dividend raises. This is among the strongest dividend growth sequences of any S&P 500 company during 2003–2007.

### 2008: The Global Financial Crisis — Second Freeze
The 2008 GFC was an even bigger shock to TROW than the tech bust:
- AUM declined from peak ~$400B to trough ~$250B
- Revenue declined 25%+
- Stock price fell 55% from peak

**TROW held the dividend flat at $0.24 quarterly for all of 2008.** The 2007 raise (+26%) had occurred BEFORE Lehman; TROW then pivoted immediately to conservation mode. The 2008 freeze was the second dividend pause in the modern era.

The streak was preserved on a calendar-year basis because TROW had raised the quarterly rate to $0.24 in late 2006/early 2007, so calendar 2007 and 2008 both totaled $0.96 annually — but the quarterly rate was flat. This is effectively identical to the 2001 pattern.

### 2009–2014: Post-GFC Recovery + Growth
AUM recovered to $730B by end of 2014. Revenue and operating income set new all-time highs. **Dividend growth was 4–16% per year** — less dramatic than 2003–2007 but consistent and strong.

### 2015: First Special Dividend ($2.00)
December 2015: TROW paid a $2.00 special dividend (in addition to regular quarterly dividends). Total 2015 distribution: $4.08/share. This was management's way of returning excess capital without permanently raising the dividend base (which would have been harder to sustain in a future market downturn).

### 2016–2019: Strong Growth
AUM reached $1.2T by end of 2019. Bull market tailwind. **Dividend raises of 3.8–22.8% per year** — with the 2018 +22.8% raise reflecting post-tax-reform EPS acceleration.

### 2020: COVID-19 Response — Rapid Dividend Raise
When COVID crashed markets in March 2020, TROW initially saw AUM decline. But the subsequent V-shaped recovery pushed AUM above $1.6T by year-end 2020. **TROW raised the dividend +18.4% in 2020** — one of the highest Aristocrat raises during COVID. This was explicitly management signaling business-model resilience.

### 2021: Peak AUM + Second Special Dividend
AUM peaked at $1.69T in December 2021. TROW announced a **second $3.00 special dividend** in Q4 2021, plus ongoing regular quarterly raises. Total 2021 distribution: $7.28/share — a peak.

### 2022: Market Decline + Raise Moderation
Equity markets declined 18–25% in 2022. TROW AUM declined from $1.69T to $1.27T. Revenue declined 8%. **Dividend raise moderated to +12.1%** — still meaningful but below peak years.

### 2023–2025: The "Aristocrat-in-Slowdown" Era
**2023 raise: +1.7%. 2024: +1.6%. 2025: +1.6%.** Three consecutive years of sub-2% raises — the weakest dividend growth sequence in TROW history outside the 2001/2008 freezes.

**Why:**
- AUM stagnant at $1.5–1.6T (vs 2021 peak $1.69T)
- Net outflows: TROW lost ~$70–100B in net client redemptions over 2023–2024 (active management losing share to passive/ETFs)
- Operating margin compressed from peak 47% to ~33%
- Revenue declined from peak $7.0B (2021) to $7.2B (2025) — flat for 4 years

Management has preserved the Aristocrat streak with minimum viable increments (1–2% raises), similar to Realty Income's $0.0005 monthly moves. The business-model question: is active management structurally impaired?

## Section 3 — Pattern Analysis

### DGR by decade
- **2000–2010:** ~12% CAGR (including 2001 freeze)
- **2010–2015:** ~14% CAGR (post-GFC bull + special dividend)
- **2015–2020:** ~12% CAGR (steady growth)
- **2020–2025:** ~7% CAGR (including peak year boost + recent deceleration)

Long-term **~11.4% CAGR** is among the highest in the Aristocrat cohort — but deceleration is clear.

### Dividend growth vs S&P 500
TROW has meaningfully outperformed the S&P 500 in dividend growth (~11.4% vs ~6%) over 2000–2025 — but recent years (+1.6% in 2025) are substantially underperforming.

### Yield-on-cost from 2005 purchase
A holder who bought TROW at year-end 2005 (~$35.65 pre-split = $17.83 post-split) with the then-forward $0.68 dividend had a starting yield of ~3.8%.
- 2010 YOC: 1.08 / 17.83 = **6.1%**
- 2015 YOC: 4.08 (incl. special) / 17.83 = **22.9%** (one-time)
- 2020 YOC: 3.60 / 17.83 = **20.2%**
- 2025 YOC: 5.04 / 17.83 = **28.3%**

TROW has one of the highest YOC trajectories in this cohort — driven by the combination of strong DGR and starting yield.

### Buyback vs dividend mix
TROW has been a consistent buyback company:
- Cumulative dividends 2010–2025: ~$10B
- Cumulative buybacks 2010–2025: ~$6B
- Share count reduced from ~257M (2010) to ~230M (2025) — ~11% reduction

## Section 4 — Lessons Learned

### What made TROW a reliable dividend grower
1. **High operating leverage to equity markets.** When markets rise, AUM grows, revenue grows, and margins expand non-linearly. This generates enormous cash flow in bull markets, enabling strong dividends + buybacks + special dividends.
2. **Asset-light business.** No balance sheet risk. Zero debt historically. Net cash position of $2–3B typically. This is a pristinely clean financial profile.
3. **Pragmatic freeze policy.** TROW management has never hesitated to freeze the dividend in market stress years (2001, 2008) while preserving the Aristocrat streak via calendar arithmetic. This is actually GOOD stewardship — pausing in bad years preserves the payout for good years.
4. **No debt, no defined-benefit pension.** TROW has zero financial liability pressure that could force a dividend cut.
5. **Excess capital return via special dividends (2015, 2021).** Rather than permanently inflating the base dividend (which would then be harder to cut), TROW uses specials to return "temporary" excess cash.

### Close calls / red flags over the years
- **2001 and 2008 freezes.** TROW is the ONLY name in this 14-ticker cohort that has actually frozen the dividend in modern times. Twice. This should be flagged as a risk pattern — TROW is more cyclical than its Aristocrat label suggests.
- **2023–2025 sub-2% raises.** The third-longest period of sub-2% raises in TROW history. Signal that the active-management business model is facing structural decline.
- **Active management outflows.** TROW lost ~$150B+ cumulative net redemptions over 2019–2024 as clients shifted to passive/ETFs. If this trend continues another 3–5 years, revenue pressure could force another freeze.
- **Margin compression.** Operating margin declined from peak 47% (2020) to ~33% (2025) — a massive compression reflecting both revenue pressure and cost inflation.
- **Founder/family ownership dilution.** The T. Rowe Price founding family's influence on capital allocation has diminished over the past decade. Current management is professional CEO-driven.

### What to watch looking forward
- **Net flows.** Can TROW stabilize outflows and return to neutral or positive? 2023 and 2024 showed improvement but still negative. 2025–2026 trajectory is critical.
- **AUM growth.** Target $2T+ by 2027. Current pace is slow due to outflows offsetting market returns.
- **Active-to-passive shift.** Structural trend. TROW has responded with ETFs, SMA growth, and retirement solutions. Can these offset?
- **Margin trajectory.** 33% is below the 45%+ peak. Stabilizing at 35–38% would be success; further compression would pressure dividends.
- **Dividend freeze risk.** If AUM declines 20%+ in any future recession, TROW will likely freeze again (as it did in 2001 and 2008). This is built into the business model.

**Conclusion:** TROW has "technically" not cut the dividend in 39 years of annual increases, but the actual history includes TWO dividend freezes (2001 and 2008) where the quarterly rate was held flat for a full year. The Aristocrat streak was preserved by calendar-year arithmetic. This makes TROW the most cyclical and fragile name in the cohort's dividend profile — not a weakness in itself but an important pattern to understand. The current 1.6% DGR is the third year of near-freeze territory and signals structural business-model stress. Dividend safety at current yield (~5%) is adequate given the strong balance sheet, but future growth is uncertain. A 2026–2027 market correction could produce a third freeze.
